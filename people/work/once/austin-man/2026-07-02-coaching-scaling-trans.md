## Inside a coaching session

**Austin:** Can you run me through a typical coaching session?

**Alejandro:** There's an intake form: tell us some things you'd like to delegate, some stats about what you usually do, how much you use AI, and what you think I could help you with. On the call I have that context, which helps me think about what this person does and how much AI they use already. I spend the first 10–15 minutes "trying to make a really vivid image in my mind of what it looks like to do their job day to day". The rest of the call, we pick something that would be immediately valuable for them and they just do it on the call. Often in the process we set up other affordances that are really nice for them to have — Wispr Flow, or how projects work in Cowork, or how to give context to the AI. When the calls go well — "maybe half of them" — or really well — "maybe a third of them" — at the end people have done maybe three things where they're like "whoa, this is immediately [valuable], thank you so much". In the others, you usually get stuck on something and don't manage, in the 30 minutes you have, to do the thing that actually delivers the final value — "but still I think people like them anyway".

**Alejandro:** The first ones I did at like "$20 an hour". ## Scaling ideas

**Austin:** These coaching things seem interesting. I'm wondering if you have thoughts about how to scale it up — there's only one of you and so many people who'd want it.

**Alejandro:** There are a few ways. The idea I've floated more is an AI uplift consultancy org, where scaling is just getting more uplift consultants — and it doesn't necessarily have to look like coaching. There's another notion "I'm not sure I quite believe, but it would be great if it were true": you get people past a certain baseline — Claude Code installed, GitHub, a few other things — and then there are scalable interventions you can do from there. Right now my best attempt at that is: I do the coaching, get them excited, then get them on a Slack channel and spam things they could do with AI. You could also think there might be a more natural progression where you only steer a bit — blog posts giving them tips and such — but "I'm not sure what fraction of the value that captures". And I'm not sure I "have a very scalable idea" for what to do. Oftentimes the blockers keeping people from doing more seem so simple — if they prompted just slightly differently they might get the thing — and "I really don't have a good model of" it.

**Austin:** Say that last bit one more time — oftentimes the thing they're trying to do is easy, but they're not getting results by one-shot prompting Claude, and you suspect if they changed the prompt a bit or used a different scaffold they'd get what they wanted?

**Alejandro:** It's more like: if you take an individual task, oftentimes the difference between success and failure comes down to a few factors. You could think of it as tweaking a prompt, though "it's probably not as easy as tweaking the prompt", because you do see substantial differences in the behavior of power users versus normal people. But maybe you can reduce it to a small set of factors and make those more available to people to get initial traction. Then at least you get a community of AI power users here and there, get really good at identifying who those are, make them as good as they can be, and maybe standardize the process of giving them the affordances to act within the organization to bring about change. That's one version, maybe.
