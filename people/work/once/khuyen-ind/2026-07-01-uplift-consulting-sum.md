## Part 3: The consulting playbook

Alejo asked in what capacity Khuyen imagines working in the space, then argued "a bunch more people should be becoming AI uplift consultants." His method, developed over a few weeks and showing "great product-market fit":

1. Run a workshop or one-on-one call; have them fill an intake form ("I stole [it] from Peter Hartree").
2. Understand their role well enough to imagine doing their job yourself.
3. Within 30 minutes, unblock them on something genuinely valuable — walking them through it on the call, not doing it for them.
4. Use the resulting "whoa, this is making me so productive" moment as initial trust for more consulting or ongoing help.

His diagnostic approach is deliberately loose — "I'm not too systematic about it" — just ask about day-to-day responsibilities and go from there. When Khuyen apparently raised a more systematic/productized offering, Alejo pushed back: while iterating, give small things ("here's a skill for automating your invoice"), not a full package.

---

## Part 4: Who to target and what predicts success

On client selection: "you just go for any CEO or person on an executive team. That's my take. Everyone has pain points." The filter that matters is attitude, not experience:

- People who never got on with computers (where even downloading apps is painful) aren't worth pursuing.
- For everyone else, the predictor is "not how much experience they have using Claude Code or Codex — it's what's their attitude towards it." Many people resist for "identity reasons," e.g. hating the idea of delegating cognitive work to Claude.

On tooling and onboarding friction: installing Claude "still kind of sucks" (close-and-reopen-the-terminal issues), while "the landing experience for Codex is much better." But EA organizations "married early to Claude, because everyone kind of hates OpenAI," making a ChatGPT/Codex sales pitch a big barrier there. His go-to recommendations include the Codex app, Cowork, and Wispr Flow for voice input.

---

## Part 5: Pricing and building trust

Alejo shared an experiment he plans for upcoming engagements: "what I think I'll do is just charge more" — clients who have "emptied [their] wallet" are baseline-committed, aligning incentives. He conceded "I'm not sure how it will work out" and that some initial trust must precede the commitment.

His own trust came through Ambitious Impact: recommended by one of their charities and by "a really good AI coach," AIM simply said "come in, we'll pay you." Nearly all his work so far is within AIM and its charities, where accumulated trust lets him charge from the first call.

His advice to Khuyen: start in a community where you already have built trust, then let word of mouth compound — "word of mouth should take you really, really far." Prestige signals and stats can come later; he doesn't yet know what convinces audiences beyond high-trust EA circles.
