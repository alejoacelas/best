## Part 5: Skill audit and choosing a demographic

Leo asked directly how good Alejandro's core uplift skill is, and whether he has sat with "extremely cracked" people with crazy agent setups. Alejandro: "Basically not." His clients so far are beginners; his strengths are "extreme caring", coach energy, and quickly forming a picture of someone's work — but call value is "somewhat high variance... at least not consistently excellent". His own words: "I basically have no really good uplift skills for the top tier of talent."

This set up the call's central strategic question — who to serve:

- **Leo's view:** technical researchers are "a more important target to shoot for, just objectively" ("I feel very strongly about wanting to uplift technical researchers as much as possible") but the hardest: partially uplifted already, yet not maxing and unable to keep up with the field's pace. So "maybe not the best one to shoot for initially" — build credibility first. If pressed, AI safety policy/governance researchers "might be an equally or even more high-impact group", and easier to help.
- **Leo's proposed model:** be a *mediator* — absorb what one cracked Claude Code power user (e.g., at a lab or Apollo) knows, then install it in each new MATS/LASR cohort. Requires either a more experienced co-founder/employee or upskilling himself — "a very worthwhile upskilling". Alejandro: "sounds kind of exciting to me."
- **Alejandro's lean:** he feels strong at field-building and imagines a broad movement getting everyone (designers, writers) enthusiastic about AI — "the rest of the world is possibly the more valuable target, given my prior beliefs".

Leo illustrated the low-hanging fruit in non-technical domains: reviewing his girlfriend's book with Claude the day before publisher submission, he found "like 20 pretty crucial errors" in an hour (including a misspelled Bogotá neighborhood her editors missed) — "it's too easy", and her publisher uses no AI.

## Part 7: Making consulting scale

Alejandro asked Leo to unpack his "higher level" strategy, sharing his own worry that consulting "scales only with your time" (Peter is skeptical AI can make consulting a scalable good). Leo's answer: one-on-one workflow discovery is the *input*; after a handful of engagements you see the one or two bottleneck tasks shared across researchers, and "a tool starts to take shape". His concrete example: researchers scattered across different ad-hoc ways of tinkering with evals environments to mitigate eval awareness, each re-reading papers after every gap between projects — build a background CLI tool that proposes environment modifications based on the latest research. Alejandro: "the concrete example was useful. This was very useful."

## Part 8: Focus strategy and the power-user pitch

On splitting time across orgs (Dewey and Peter had both said "focus on a small number of VIPs early on"; Alejandro's default is one org for ~3 months, five days there plus one day exploring), Leo advised:

1. First, deeply understand how heavy AI users (like W [name unclear]) work — build a list of "the 20 things these VIPs are doing that are extremely effective" (often conceptually basic but setup-heavy, e.g., Claude connected to Linear and Notion org-wide).
2. Interface with one org by building org-level systems — that way you serve everyone at once.
3. Avoid "stretching yourself too thin"; but maximize reps: give one VIP 3–4 new methods, leave, schedule a follow-up in a month. Never dump 10 workflow changes at once.
4. Read each person's "appetite for change" — capitalize on the hungry ones, let the saturated ones settle. Overall: "I would recommend having a somewhat dynamic strategy here."

For pitching power users (Alejandro's open question, since his "I'm the expert" pitch inverts with them), Leo suggested framing it as learning: "I've heard that you're someone who's super tapped into this... at the moment I'm really wanting to just learn as much as possible" — ask for a minimally intrusive day (or virtual hour) watching their system, get permission to record, and make clear the purpose is to "pass down their knowledge to other high-impact people", offering to come back and help later. Side benefits of 2–4 such meetings: network growth, plus borrowed credibility with less-advanced VIPs ("so-and-so uses this").

## Appendix 3: Underlying dynamics

**Mutual evaluation beneath the advice:** The call doubled as a soft two-way interview. Alejandro openly floated joining Leo; Leo pre-emptively (and kindly) closed the near-term door — not first or second hire — while keeping a future path open. Both left with an explicit keep-tabs agreement, which suggests genuine rather than polite interest.

**Candor as rapport:** Leo asked the hard question ("how good is your core skill?") and Alejandro answered with unusual frankness ("basically not"). That honesty shifted the conversation from pitch mode to genuinely useful mentoring — most of the call's concrete value (the mediator model, the power-user pitch, the dynamic pacing strategy) came after it.

**Small, dense network:** Everyone relevant — Peter, Dewey, Luca De Leo, Olivia, Marius, Jacques — appears in both people's recent conversations; Alejandro even independently re-derived Leo's ex-co-founder as a candidate. Credibility and dealflow in this space clearly travel through the same few nodes, which supports Leo's advice to lead with borrowed credibility and testimonials.
