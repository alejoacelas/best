# Meeting summary: Impact Ops visa sponsorship enquiry (Josh Axford)

**Date:** 2026-06-29

**Participants:** Alejandro (Alejo) Acelas; Joshua (Josh) Axford (Impact Ops founder)

---

## Summary

First call between Alejo and Josh Axford of Impact Ops, prompted by Jorgen at 80,000 Hours suggesting Impact Ops as the route to a UK visa for Alejo's AI-consultancy-for-EA-orgs plan. The call converged quickly on a concrete scheme:

1. Setting up Alejo's own UK company with a sponsorship license would take "three or four months" best case, "nine to 12 months" most likely — so instead, Impact Ops (which already holds a license) would sponsor Alejo's visa and employ him as a team member.
2. Alejo would work essentially on secondment with 80,000 Hours as his first client, folded into Impact Ops' operational infrastructure (systems) team.
3. Josh's only real condition is commercial: cover Alejo's salary, systems overheads, and visa cost, with a client commitment in place for roughly the first three months.
4. Josh will do the math and send a figure plus a proposal; Alejo will take a concrete proposal to Jorgen next Monday.

---

## Part 1: Context — a UK visa for EA-org AI consultancy

After small talk (Josh had just put his kids to bed; Alejo had arrived that day at a friend's house in Münster, Germany), Josh confirmed the premise: Alejo wants a UK visa to do consultancy work for EA orgs. Asked whether he'd start a company or find a fiscal sponsor, Alejo said "I think for-profit makes the most sense" — orgs should pay for AI-uplift help because "incentives work best if they pay for it". But he admitted "I think I'm very impatient": he wants to be working with the 80k team at their offices as soon as possible, and wondered about doing something fast first and transitioning to a company later.

A short aside on note-takers (Fathom vs. Granola) followed while Josh added his recorder.

---

## Part 2: Why the DIY route is slow — and the Impact Ops alternative

Josh managed expectations on the self-owned-company route: to get a UK visa sponsorship license you must be operational — UK bank account (high-street, slower without UK directors) and a real office lease (a WeWork membership doesn't count, though Impact Ops knows workarounds). The Home Office now investigates new applicants with little activity and few UK people involved. Best case "three or four months"; most likely "nine to 12 months". Pivotal Research got theirs in about two and a half months, but "that's very much an anomaly".

Then the pivot: Impact Ops has a sponsorship license, already does systems implementation for clients, and AI implementation "is sort of on our roadmap anyway". Josh's question: does Alejo want his own company, or to collaborate with / join Impact Ops?

Alejo's answer set the tone for the rest of the call: for "at least the next six months" his plan is to pick one org — one team — and "accelerate them as much as I can"; "any setup that allows me to do that really well, I think I'm very keen to explore", deferring on structure to people with higher context.

---

## Part 3: How Impact Ops picks work, and what it does now

Josh explained his filter: as long as there's a strong impact case and the costs of hiring are covered, it's a viable engagement. They've only turned away work with a weak impact case (e.g. Manchester City Council's climate assessment — local impact plus government bureaucracy) or messy leadership teams spotted during entity setup. EA orgs, "especially AI safety organizations", are effectively "a free pass" — the binding constraint is capacity, and for the last couple of months "across the board we're slammed" and not taking new clients.

Current systems work he sketched: automating ERA AI's fellowship visa onboarding (fellow data in Airtable, horrendous manual forms from the "Intern Visa" provider; they explored an AI agent, settled on pre-populated forms), and an Airtable impact dashboard for Food Policy Pathways. The systems team is Mikey, Cherry, and Varun, and their work increasingly looks like AI work.

---

## Part 4: Working format and the commercial threshold

Alejo floated his preferred format: he chooses projects (starting with 80k), spends five days a week doing whatever most helps that org, and one day a week with Impact Ops — helping clients directly or spreading new AI techniques to the team. Josh: "That's totally fine. I think the key thing really from my side is just making sure that we cover the costs."

On evidence of willingness to pay: Alejo hasn't had real commercial discussions yet. With AIM he discussed ratcheting his rate up — "maybe the rate should be like a hundred dollars an hour", which AIM found reasonable though they might fundraise separately for it. He also noted Longview is starting an AI-uplift grantmaking stream, so an upfront grant is plausible: "I think I could quite possibly arrange that."

Josh named the offer explicitly: "Impact Ops could sponsor your visa" — Alejo joins as a team member in the UK, pending commercial viability.

---

## Part 5: Salary, margins, and Impact Ops' finances

The base commercial requirement is Alejo's salary. Alejo, confident he can raise money, offered to take "the minimum wage in the UK"; Josh noted the Skilled Worker visa has its own salary floor. Impact Ops targets a "15 to 20% margin", driven by resilience: they took a startup loan from an EA donor, "paid that back last year", and are now building a reserve — after which Josh is "pretty confident" they'll start making grants to effective charities (they take the Giving What We Can pledge) by end of year.

Alejo framed the ask memorably: give me your "happy figure" — "the cash amount at which you'd be very excited to do this" — "and I'll figure out some way to get that", as close to a fixed upfront commitment as possible. Josh agreed: "I guess I can pull together a proposal."

Brief tangent: Alejo is solo but "looking for a cofounder", ideally someone exceptional at using AI — he rates two 80k people [names unclear in transcript] as "so much better than me".

---

## Part 6: What Alejo does at AIM; Impact Ops' shelved AI-consultancy plan

Alejo described his AIM work: one-on-one fifty-minute coaching calls — reconstruct the person's day-to-day from the intake form and ten minutes of questions, then deliver immediate wins on-call (Cowork, Wispr Flow voice dictation) plus usually something specific to them, and a narrative for compounding productivity. "The standard consulting playbook, but with AI you can deliver value so much more and so much faster."

This triggered Josh to dig up (musing he could "just get Claude to dig it up") Impact Ops' Q4-last-year project plan for "AI consultancy as a service": validate demand via ~20 surveys and interviews (Ambitious Impact, BlueDot, Meridian and others responded), identify adoption barriers, 3–5 pilot offerings, 2–3 case studies, content library, pricing model, and a delivery model not dependent on Josh and Adrian. It stalled on capacity and Adrian's limited progress — but the demand signal was real and Longview was "really interested".

On pricing, Josh explained their model: project fees (e.g. an org-wide AI audit ending in a report, recommendations, and priced next steps) plus ongoing subscription support for systems they build — hourly billing "doesn't incentivize efficiency". Alejo pushed back gently: "in my ideal world I just teach them to use AI so good that they don't need us." Josh countered from experience that clients keep coming back; Alejo's synthesis — automate the support desk itself, feeding requests to Claude for a first draft: "yes, this is totally doable, Alejo just did it, here's how you can test it."

---

## Part 7: Client pipeline — 80k first

Josh asked how real 80,000 Hours is. Alejo: he spoke with Jorgen, who both suggested contacting Impact Ops and — Alejo's "my sense", not verbatim — indicated 80k has "already scoped things that we would be very excited to just contract you kind of right away to do", size undiscussed. Longview contacts at Trajan House hinted at both funding him and possibly hiring him ("maybe maybe us"); another party was vaguely interested. But: "I think I have decided that I want 80k to be kind of my first client" — they're already moving fast on letting AI do more, he loves the org and its people, and insiders are advocating for him.

On model, Alejo flagged a tension: the old Impact Ops plan scoped multi-org productized offerings, while he wants three months deep in one org, much of it learning. Josh dissolved it: "I think they're the same thing, really" — make the 80k deep dive the case study ("this is what we did at 80,000 Hours"), publish it on the EA Forum, and roll forward to similar engagements. The next question to resolve: "is 80,000 Hours happy to pay for it?"

The closing exchange nailed the division of labor. Alejo probed for scale — "250k USD or something" for three months? Josh: "No, it wouldn't be that much" — he'll compute the real figure. Alejo left "very excited": "I expected this call to be great", and it delivered a workable scheme.

## Appendix 3: Underlying dynamics

**Complementary constraints:** Impact Ops has license, legal infrastructure, and a validated-but-shelved AI-consultancy roadmap, but zero spare capacity; Alejo has capacity, AI skill, and likely funding, but no visa. Each is precisely the other's missing piece, which is why the call converged so fast.

**Alejo signaling money-abundance:** Repeatedly ("happy to get minimum wage", "happy to give you all the money", "just give me a figure and I'll get it") Alejo positioned funding as a solved problem to remove Josh's only stated blocker. Josh never had to negotiate — his caution stayed procedural (do the math, meet the team).

**Team-fit as the soft gate:** Beyond the money, Josh deliberately inserted a mutual-fit step (meeting Mikey and Varun, "and vice versa") — a reminder that this is a hire into his team, not just a visa transaction.

**80k's warm pull:** The plan rests heavily on secondhand enthusiasm from 80k (Jorgen's scoped work, internal advocates). Alejo flagged his own uncertainty about the exact wording — the Monday meeting is where that assumption gets tested.
