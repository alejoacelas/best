# Meeting summary: Joshua <> Alejandro — Impact Ops team fit

**Date:** 2026-07-06

**Participants:** Alejandro Acelas; Joshua Axford (Impact Ops, founder/lead)

---

## Summary

A candid negotiation call about whether Alejandro should join Impact Ops or stay independent. Key developments:

1. Joshua pushed back hard early on: nothing Alejandro said convinced him Impact Ops would be more than "a stepping stone", and he is uncomfortable sponsoring a visa "just for like a three month thing".
2. The core misunderstanding surfaced and resolved: Joshua "thought you were the AI power user that's going to help organizations", while Alejandro sees himself as "maybe top five percent" and believes the project needs an extreme (top 0.5%) power user as a partner.
3. Despite the mismatch, Joshua remains open to a bounded three-or-six-month arrangement (a six-month skilled worker visa has precedent via BlueDot), structured as a commercial arrangement between Impact Ops and 80,000 Hours, who are "willing to pay 36,000 pounds for three months" of Alejandro's time.
4. Next steps: Joshua will talk to Jorgen (80k) and email Peter Hartree; Alejandro takes his English test Wednesday morning to keep the visa process moving.

---

## Part 1: Alejandro's update — the 80k uplift decision

After brief small talk (Alejandro is in Münster, Germany), Alejandro shared that he'd just come off a call with Jorgen and was "very excited": he had resolved the recent found-an-org-now question in favor of "pick an org, understand how to uplift orgs first, then maybe found an org later". He plans to work on the 80k uplift, might rope in Peter Hartree to help, and believes Jorgen is "pretty on board" and moving quickly.

Joshua probed the co-founder angle. Alejandro clarified: Peter Hartree is his "ideal co-founder", but when he asked, Peter said "we've barely worked together" — "totally fair". The near-term plan is to do some work together for 80,000 Hours (Peter is already loosely an 80k advisor). Co-founding is "not on the short term, but maybe if things go like amazingly well in like three months".

Pressed on long-term plans, Alejandro described his commitment philosophy: scope commitments to be either "pretty short term" or "easy to say no" to. If things go well in three months, he would go full-time on an AI-uplift org, ideally with Hartree. But he stressed the goal isn't an org per se: "people who are doing high impact work are not using [AI] enough" — that's the problem, and he'll do "whatever it takes to solve it". How to scale beyond himself and Peter: "I don't know yet"; working at 80k is partly "an attempt to figure out how to go about it".

On visas: 80k would sponsor him but only via being hired, which carries complications; short-term, being in the UK in person is "way easier if it's not through being hired at 80k".

---

## Part 2: Joshua's pushback — stepping stone vs. team member

Joshua delivered blunt feedback: "Nothing you've said there has convinced me that working at Impact Ops is a long term thing that you want to do. It's more that Impact Ops would just be like a stepping stone for you." He wants people who want to be Impact Ops contributors — they work with "60 EA organizations at the moment that will need AI support". He's "not that comfortable sponsoring the visa just for like a three month thing" given costs and risks. He framed Alejandro's options as binary: join the team already doing the thing and commit, or do your own thing with Peter Hartree. "So I don't know if this is going to be a good fit."

Alejandro accepted he "probably did like a poor job before" communicating, and restated his position: he is "really passionate about just like solving this specific problem" — getting high-impact people to use AI more — as his "core goal"; organizational arrangements are "pretty much optional". He offered a lighter-touch version: being around in the Impact Ops Slack, coaching calls, answering questions in his own Slack channel — "I'm happy for Impact Ops to keep that kind of like soft commitment". But he refused to feed a false expectation: if Joshua assumes Alejandro will still be at Impact Ops in six months, "I'm not sharing" that view, and "things move fast".

Joshua explained his original reasoning: Impact Ops' mission is to "empower high-impact projects to scale and flourish", flourish increasingly meaning AI uplift; the systems team (Varun, Mikey, Sherry) already does this work. He'd seen Alejandro as a way to skip a hiring round. But: "I just get a sense that you're not committed to joining a team that's working on this problem — you kind of want to be doing your own thing."

---

## Part 3: The power-user disagreement

Alejandro named a deeper reason for hesitation: Impact Ops doesn't have "extreme AI power users" on staff, which is "really important" to him. Joshua challenged the basis of that judgment — so far Alejandro had only pointed at Anthropic subscription tiers ($20 vs $100 seats and whether staff hit rate limits). Alejandro conceded he'd only get real evidence when he meets the systems team ("I'll get to meet them tomorrow... I'll be able to tell with more certainty").

Joshua argued the team is delivering regardless — e.g., currently helping Giving What We Can use AI for grantmaking and due diligence — and revealed he was planning to recruit an AI power user in the next six months anyway; Alejandro joining would have replaced that hiring round.

Alejandro explained his strategy doc (two-three weeks of thinking): among the "top two, top three most repeated pieces of feedback" from people "whose judgment I trust" was that he should "pair with someone who's an extremely high power user" — a big determinant of project success. "I don't expect to easily change my mind on this."

This produced the call's pivotal clarification. Joshua: "Okay, so that clears it up. I thought you were the AI power user that's going to help organizations." Alejandro: "Maybe I'm top five percent and you want top 0.5% or something." So hiring Alejandro would not fill Impact Ops' power-user gap — "then I need to hire an AI power user".

They then haggled over the bar: Joshua doubts making 80k 10% more productive "needs to be a top 0.5% AI user"; Alejandro countered top-0.5% people command "Anthropic-like salaries", cited Leo (recently hired by 80k, making projects "substantially more productive"), and proposed the criterion "someone that Anthropic would be really keen to hire" — noting Coefficient Giving "is excited to pay for something like this". Joshua flagged the tension with Alejandro's earlier for-profit framing: "if you're going to get funding from CG, it's no longer a commercial arrangement". Alejandro allowed he may have "changed my mind somewhat" seeing CG's speed.

Notable moment of self-awareness from Alejandro mid-exchange: "maybe I'm just letting my pride speak right now and I just let my ego be hurt a little when you say oh, you're not a power user".

---

## Part 4: What a bounded arrangement could look like

Joshua asked the decisive question: would Alejandro consider himself an Impact Ops team member — Slack, systems team meetings, team retreats, listed on the website as a consultancy specialist? The full version: work with 80k for three months, write a case study and EA Forum post, then pick follow-on projects from Impact Ops' "50, 55 clients" (Longview, BlueDot, Catalyze Impact, Impact Academy all named).

Alejandro asked for concreteness, then the key question: "Is there a version of this where all my commitments with Impact Ops are bounded either at three months or six months?"

Joshua's answer: probably yes. He recently did a six-month skilled worker visa via BlueDot sponsorship, so "we could probably do a six month skilled worker visa", with 80,000 Hours covering the cost — though after six months Alejandro would have to leave the UK. Structurally it would be a commercial arrangement between Impact Ops and 80k (already an Impact Ops recruitment client). Joshua was honest that this isn't his preference: he wants "a team of people that are going to work together for the long term", and "what you're basically saying is I'll join the three, six months and then I'm getting off the boat".

Joshua also floated an alternative that bypasses Impact Ops: a fiscal sponsor (Sparkwell, in the EA space with Anti Entropy — Alejandro knows Jeffrey) or Alejandro's own 501(c)(3) funded by Coefficient Giving — though no visa sponsorship that way, and it means being "beholden to Coefficient Giving".

The re-anchoring point Joshua kept returning to: "The fact still stands that 80,000 Hours are willing to pay 36,000 pounds for three months of your time, so they must see some value" — so an arrangement may still exist.

## Appendix 3: Underlying dynamics

**A negotiation, not a catch-up:** Joshua opened with deliberate space-giving ("I sort of gave you a bit of space there to talk through what you want to do") and judged the answer against a hiring decision he was implicitly making. Much of the call was Joshua testing commitment and Alejandro refusing to overstate it — both stayed candid rather than smoothing it over.

**The misunderstanding was load-bearing:** Joshua's interest was premised on Alejandro *being* the power user who'd replace a planned Q4 hire. Once Alejandro placed himself at "top 5%" and said he needs a top-0.5% partner, the original deal logic collapsed — but it also depersonalized the rejection: the issue became role definition, not Alejandro's value.

**Alejandro's expectation-setting discipline:** He repeatedly chose honesty at the cost of the deal ("I'm not sharing" the six-month assumption), consistent with his stated policy of commitments that are short-term or easy to decline. Joshua seemed to respect this even while it undercut his team-building preference.

**Ego moment handled openly:** Alejandro flagged in real time that the "you're not a power user" exchange stung, which defused it; Joshua responded factually ("you said you're just top 5%").

**Leverage:** 80k's willingness to pay £36,000 for three months is the anchor keeping the arrangement alive from Joshua's side; Coefficient Giving's enthusiasm (and money) is Alejandro's outside option, which Joshua acknowledged by sketching the fiscal-sponsor route himself.
