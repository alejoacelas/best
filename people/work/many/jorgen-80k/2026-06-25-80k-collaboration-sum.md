## Part 3: Alejandro's vision and why 80K

Asked to talk through the vision and legal structure for a new company, Alejandro gave the arc:

- Motivation: "I'm just very enraged that EA is not using AI more" — EA "basically invented differential technology progress" (Forethought has a piece on it), so some EAs should work on this.
- A year ago a similar attempt "did not go well," which he attributes to lacking validated user demand. This time demand is his guiding light: workshops and especially one-on-one coaching are validated — "most people say this is useful… and a few people say this is just life changing."
- Coaching VIPs is his scaling theory: help a leader, and the leader pushes it to their team through official channels and cultural influence.
- Advice from Peter Hartree and others (one ex-Palantir): pick a small set of people, go as deep as you can, accelerate them, then see what transmits. Hence: pick one org.
- Visa logic: the Global Talent visa (exceptional promise) is a future option — "I don't have quite the profile for that yet" and it needs groundwork that can't "appear out of nowhere one week before the submission." So a Skilled Worker visa through a single org fits, and he prefers the UK over the US.
- Why 80K: which org is "a big amorphous question," but the dominant factor is talent density — "it's great advice to just go join groups of very talented people," and 80K is full of people he's "really incredibly excited by." Talking to Peter Hartree (next Friday; he's in an offline week) is the thing that would make him "much more certain."

## Part 4: Alternative legal setups (Jørgen's ops lens)

Jørgen, drawing on his ops background, raised options Alejandro hadn't considered:

1. **Launch as an AIM startup.** "My quick impression is that this is just the thing they do" — AIM fiscally sponsors people who work legally under them for some months, then spin out. It fits Alejandro's envisioned timeline, and he could still work with 80K and other orgs as a consultant.
2. **Launch under Impact Ops** or another supporting-EA umbrella org. Alejandro had reached out to Impact Ops long ago without reply and admitted he'd under-explored this.
3. **Starting his own org to sponsor his visa won't work:** a UK org needs a sponsor license, onboarding takes time, and "I don't think it would work in your specific case — it would take too long."

Jørgen stressed he was "not implying that it's impossible for 80K to find a solution," but that these options might fit Alejandro's long-term plans better. Alejandro was delighted: the best advice is often "have you considered this option?" when you had no clue it existed.

## Part 6: Is one team for 3–6 months the right scope?

Jørgen probed Alejandro's stated plan of going deep with one org/project for "three to six months" then moving on: if Alejandro worked with one 80K program team, would moving to another 80K team replicate the effect — or should he leave for a different org? He noted 80K plans to hire substantially over the next couple of years, possibly adding teams.

Alejandro is partly deferring to Adam Jones on this and wants to ask Peter Hartree more. His deeper uncertainty: he doesn't understand "what would be the most promising mechanism for widespread transmission of knowledge of how to use AI." Threads:

- Adam's take: the software involved is lightweight — like Peter Hartree's blog posts, "just a few CLIs and a good connector."
- His own enablement style is fixing whatever most blocks a specific person, unlike Luca's org-wide infrastructure projects (e.g. a database migration).
- One transmission mechanism is productizing an internal insight for the world, Asana-style (Dustin Moskovitz building the to-do tool that didn't exist) — but he hasn't mapped what would get built by default and where value accrues.
