# Meeting summary: AI coaching session — using Claude on Sam's personal problems

**Date:** 2026-07-03

**Participants:** Alejandro (Alejo) Acelas; Samantha (Sam) Kagel (Ambitious Impact / AIM, recently moved to CEO)

---

## Summary

A weekly coaching session that Sam chose to spend on personal rather than work use of AI. After surfacing the "should I have kids" question as a candidate topic, Sam settled on her core problem: chronic stress and negative self-view despite strong evidence her life is going well. Alejandro coached her live through prompting Claude Fable against her journal and therapy-note context, and shared his own patterns:

1. Break chats out of stale patterns by restarting with a fresh prompt.
2. Run two to three parallel threads.
3. Encode durable preferences as global instructions.
4. Build the reflex to externalize recurring problems the moment they surface.

They closed by putting Sam's two biggest problems on the record — miscalibrated mood/self-view and no routine for continuous learning — so Alejandro can run the transcript through Fable and follow up on Slack.

---

## Part 1: Catching up and picking a focus

Sam opened by asking where Alejandro is now — Münster, Germany, living with friends "a week and a half with each". The twins in the house prompted his musing on children, which Sam matched instantly: "I want kids so bad. I'm, like, so ready to be a mom." Alejandro proposed the session pattern he'd been decomposing that day: take your biggest problems or hardest decisions to AI. Sam sketched her kids question — her partner (Hugo) is quite a lot younger with a longer timeline and not fully convinced, "but I think probably"; "whether I should get my eggs frozen this year, which I think I probably should"; and at what point in a relationship you need confidence you'd want to raise children the same way.

But when offered the choice, she redirected: work topics were hitting diminishing returns, and "it would be kind of cool to use this one for a personal thing". The personal thing that surfaced: "I am, like, so constantly, like, stressed or negative about myself, even though, like, all the data is pointing to, like, things going well... I just, like, really ruminate and focus on the negative things."

---

## Part 2: Setting up the live session

Alejandro suggested two approaches: tackle it directly with full context, or run a behavioral analysis of what patterns predict the feelings — using Claude as a non-demanding thought partner. He flagged that friends still beat Claude here ("Claude is not yet as good as someone who has known you for five years"), but Claude is always reachable when you're anxious and don't feel like doing anything else.

Sam's journal chat already holds about six months of entries, therapy notes (therapist: Natalie), and goals. Alejandro nudged her toward her personal account — "otherwise Ambitious Impact is the owner of that data" — but she proceeded in the work account for now. Both celebrated Fable's return ("Everything's easy with Fable"). On prompting, Alejandro's advice: don't treat it as a polished conversation — tell Fable your uncertainty itself ("I'm not sure if this is the thing that I want, help me figure it out"). Sam dictated exactly that: read the therapy-session context, then propose how to attack her self-limiting beliefs.

---

## Part 3: Alejandro's personal-use patterns

While Sam's chats "cooked", Alejandro shared his own routine uses:

- **Explainers:** frictionless quick-version/deep-version wikis for things he wants to learn — most recently how Claude Cowork differs from Claude Code, budgeted at "an hour and a half".
- **The lithium story:** after a bipolar 2 diagnosis, his psychiatrist suggested lithium. Claude's first description ("drops your IQ... makes you gain weight") "doesn't sound like the right reference class", so he spent "like six hours" learning how psychiatric drugs are evaluated, built a website from the chat, and landed on lamotrigine — most drugs had big downsides except it, and a friend with bipolar 2 takes it. "The upside-downside calculation looks good enough." Three weeks in.

---

## Part 4: Steering the chats

Sam's first response was an analysis-plus-menu that felt generic. Alejandro's diagnosis: chats settle into patterns — her journal chat was constrained to "respond to your entry" mode. Fix: copy the prompt to a fresh chat and reference the old one for context. His broader steering principle: "you know the thing you want... you should just like try to repeatedly steer it to get the thing you want."

He also described running two to three parallel threads — not the same prompt tweaked, but genuinely different angles — because processing each answer takes minutes and "two to three conversations feels kind of like the right amount". Sam's fresh chat came back "much more useful", offering a menu of engagement modes she started selecting from.

When Sam worried about wasting his time while she was heads-down in chat, Alejandro reframed: "my purpose here for the next 25 minutes is to be the most useful I can to you" — citing Peter Hartree's AI consultant, whose value is partly just that a booked call forces people to actually do the thing.

---

## Part 5: Durable instructions and habits

Asked about life-side habits and automations, Alejandro showed his new global Claude instructions (written the previous weekend): principles cemented through back-and-forths with Fable — "this is really important for me, I don't expect this to change, how can we articulate it in a way that I kind of endorse you acting on?" His thesis: "a good unlock of value is being able to identify durable things that you want your Claude — or yourself — to do, and then encoding those in some principle."

Second habit: as an impulsive person, he's built his life so every recurring important thing has an immediate outlet — a Claude chat, a message to a friend, a walk. Sam compressed it well: "building that reaction impulse to take it to something that can help you solve it rather than just holding it."

Sam then hit her session limit, which turned into practical account logistics: move personal/therapy chats to her personal account ("I'll stop using my work one for therapy, which is probably the right call"), run one account per browser (Alejandro's Safari trick), and consider the $200 Max plan personally.

## Part 6: Sam's biggest problems, spoken into the transcript

Alejandro proposed using the last 10 minutes to get Sam's biggest problems on the record so he could run the transcript through Fable afterward. Sam: "Crazy coaching. I love it."

**Problem 1 — mood miscalibration:** "My mood and view of myself does not match reality. So I'm, like, much more stressed, anxious and self-critical than I should be given my circumstances." Key texture:

- It's gotten worse as her life improved: "almost like the better my life has gotten", the more distant reality is from internal feeling — this year she has a healthy relationship and an objectively better-fitting job, yet still feels "not good enough".
- "Moving to CEO was quite hard for me and destabilizing", though the last two to three months brought a lot of progress, as has feeling safe with Hugo.
- Costs: internal experience, energy (and thus work productivity), and — the one she cares about most — the relationship: bringing low mood when they could be "playful and silly", plus increased withdrawal from friends over the last year.
- Who understands it: her therapy notes and Claude are "pretty close to understanding, like, the root of where this comes from and why"; Hugo; and her dad, a therapist — sparking the idea of having AI prepare a question brief for a conversation with her dad.

Alejandro's take: she's done the obvious good things already, so AI's marginal value is rapid sampling of strategies — 10-minute versions of things like exercise, and unexpected angles. He illustrated with his own case: feeding Claude his blog while thinking about finding a partner led to discovering Substacks of people he might want to marry — a step he "could not have anticipated". Sam translated it for herself: ask Claude to find people on the internet who've worked through similar challenges in public writing. The general pattern he wanted to convey: capture fleeting intuitions about your problems with very low friction, and "through sheer volume" you land on things worth building on.

**Problem 2 — continuous learning:** lots of subject-matter content she wants to learn, no low-friction routine. "This is, like, a really obvious area where AI would be clearly very good."

## Appendix 3: Underlying dynamics

**Coaching format is working:** Sam repeatedly named the meta-value — "thank you so much for just, like, sitting with me, encouraging me" — and Alejandro explicitly invoked the Peter Hartree model: the booked call itself forces action. Much of the session was Sam silently working in chat while Alejandro stood by, and both treated that as success, not dead air.

**Sam's guardedness eased over the call:** she started hesitant about sharing personal content ("maybe I just won't share my screen", muting to dictate), then ended by speaking her biggest problems aloud specifically so they'd be captured in the transcript for Alejandro's follow-up — a notable trust shift within one hour.

**Work/personal data boundary:** Alejandro twice steered Sam away from keeping therapy content in the AIM-owned account. She agreed in principle ("probably the right call") but had been defaulting to the work account for convenience; the session-limit incident is what actually forced the migration decision.
