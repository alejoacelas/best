# Meeting summary: Hugo's Catalyze incubation pitch

**Date:** 2026-07-01

**Participants:** Alejandro (Alejo) Acelas; Hugo Walrand (co-founder, Catalyze Impact)

---

## Summary

Hugo reached out after Coefficient Giving (CG) named "AI uplift for AI safety orgs" one of the top three things Catalyze should get founders to launch, and pitched Alejo on founding a scaled AI-uplift org rather than executing his current plan (join Impact Ops for a UK visa, then a three-month secondment at 80k). His core arguments:

1. CG money is flowing fast to first-time founders (a 1.4M commitment two weeks prior), so Alejo could "raise extremely quickly a lot of money."
2. Money plus a CG stamp is what convinces risk-averse people to join a team — mission alignment alone isn't enough.
3. Co-founder search and hiring can run in parallel with finding product fit, and Catalyze's network (co-founder matching, intros to MATS/METR-tier orgs, free coaches, small grants) exists to compress exactly this zero-to-one phase.

Alejo was receptive but unresolved — "scared and weird... but I think this is really exciting" — and left with concrete next steps: joining Hugo's Friday call with Peter Hartree, booking Alexandra's Calendly, and reconsidering his Impact Ops and AIM-coaching commitments.

---

## Part 1: Why Hugo called, and Alejo's current plan

Hugo opened with the trigger: a message from CG the day before. Catalyze had pushed CG to name the top three things founders should "absolutely launch" — "being quite hardcore in terms of prioritization" — and "AI uplift for AI safety orgs" made the list. Someone mentioned Alejo; Hugo asked directly for his contact.

Alejo laid out where he stands after two weeks in "strategy mode": he wants to do enablement work (service mentality, the "so, so strong" case for differential technological development, love of coaching). So far that's been one-on-one coaching at AIM and elsewhere, plus a view that AI safety / EA orgs should import the private sector's tech-adoption playbook — "no need to reinvent the wheel." The plan: "I think I'll join Impact Ops, mostly because I think they just want to let me do anything I want and they will give me a UK visa" (confirmation still pending from Joshua; "quite possible" sorted before July 20), then a three-month secondment at 80k. He hasn't "figured out the recipe" for org-level uplift consultancy — coaching works for individuals, but changing an org is a different question, and 80k is where he wants to answer it.

---

## Part 2: Criteria, strategy doc, and background

Asked for his decision criteria, Alejo shared his ~500-word strategy doc mid-call. Hugo's immediate reaction: "Would love to help with the co-founder" — which Alejo called "my big request." Alejo flagged that a great co-founder could change his plan substantially: he'd "dump my previous thoughts" and re-derive the plan for two. Within the current strategy, the advice he's gotten (Peter Hartree, Alan Jones) is to pick one org — two "starts feeling like too many" — and go really hard on accelerating it for three months. 80k fits both his skill profile (coaching non-technical-but-useful people, versus Leo's super-technical angle) and his heart — "bizarrely excited" to take orders from Arden, Bella, and Sam.

Background he gave Hugo: ~10 months ago, a vibe-coded ChatGPT MVP for AIM-charity customer screening got validated by Alexandra; he'd been doing EA-type work for an AIM charity using LLMs heavily; then Forethought's "AI Tools for Existential Security" convinced him to quit and spend two months attempting a manifesto to "one-shot everyone in EA" into differential technology. The manifesto failed but the calls built his network (Peter Hartree, Arden Koehler). Before that, teaching — where he learned he loves getting people excited about the ambitious thing.

On funding criteria: "talent plus permission" is what he rates orgs on, and 80k has both. He believes uplift consultancy should be for-profit (clients paying keeps incentives honest). Funding itself isn't a constraint: "if I needed money I could get money" (an offer had already surfaced at Trajan House).

---

## Part 3: The co-founder gap

Hugo probed what Alejo had actually done to find collaborators. Answer: "too little" — an unworked list of names, an idea to "burn all the tokens" scanning the internet for candidates (not executed), and one lukewarm LinkedIn exchange. His obvious first pick is Peter Hartree, who declined ~2 months ago for two reasons: no personal capacity for 80-hour weeks this quarter, and a gradualism intuition Alejo admits he doesn't understand yet — Peter has done uplift consulting intermittently for 6–8 months but feels "it's not quite ripe to take the next step." Alejo sees Peter Friday.

---

## Part 4: Hugo's pitch

Hugo acknowledged the tension in pitching ("I don't want to bias you... it's dangerous for me to do that, but I will still do it") and gave three data points:

1. **CG is deploying fast:** in the prior two weeks, "a commitment of 1.4 million to a team of our founders" and "more than 500k to two other teams" — first-time founders, more than VCs would give.
2. **The signal:** CG putting uplift-for-AI-safety-orgs in Catalyze's top three means Alejo "would be able to raise extremely quickly a lot of money."
3. **Catalyze's machinery:** hundreds of founders guided through zero-to-one, "freaking good" potential co-founders ($3M–$40M ARR track records) looking for something to launch, and network intros (e.g. a quick call with Ryan Kidd at MATS).

The headline vision: "most of the orgs in AI safety as clients in a year and a half," with money and a good co-founder. He was explicit that Catalyze takes no equity ("we are nonprofits").

Alejo's honest first response: money is useful but "I just need to figure out how to spend that money productively" — he'd scale aggressively only if he had more trusted people (currently Peter, Luca at 80k). Otherwise: "I'll put it in a bank account and not look at it until I figure out how to spend it."

---

## Part 5: Hugo's two counterarguments

**Money recruits.** "It's much easier to convince someone to work with you if you have a properly defined stamp of CG... and a lot of money in your bank, plus a set of clients." Alejo's instinct — "aren't you here for the mission?" — met Hugo's observed reality: even mission-aligned people won't leave jobs for a one-person project; they join when money plus an authority's stamp de-risks it.

**Parallel, not sequential.** Finding product fit and building a team aren't sequential phases; the reason Alejo can't take five clients is that he's alone. Hugo's stated goal: get Alejo "as quickly as possible managing a team of people instead of coaching people." Alejo conceded both points were "really appealing propositions" but flagged his inexperience — big strategic decisions go past his trusted advisors first.

This surfaced a live conflict: Alejo committed to coaching AIM charities (coordinated by Hugo's girlfriend, Samantha) and now half-regrets it — "I will do it because I said I would do it, but also kind of maybe not." Hugo framed it as two paths: coach 3–4 AIM CEOs now, or find a co-founder and coach "10 times more people" in a year. "Someone needs to launch something right now... there is way too much money to deploy."

Hugo then sketched the fast path Catalyze could run: co-founder candidates to test in person, travel grants, accelerating the London move, intros to MATS / FAR / METR, building the CG relationship toward a first raise, and pairing with free coaches who built $100M+ ARR companies. His TL;DR: (1) "founders come to us normally, and for you, I'm searching"; (2) multiple people confirm the need; (3) "you are not alone." Scale means a five-FTE team — "bigger than half of the AI safety orgs."

---

## Part 6: The Impact Ops dilemma and UK vs US

Alejo pushed on practicalities: should he decline Impact Ops if Catalyze can get him working in the UK by end of July? Hugo refused to answer directly ("I will not take the risk... I don't have the context") but reframed: "What's the end goal? What's the dream? And how do I, as quickly as possible, arrive at this dream?" — everything between now and the dream is a distraction. He'd "be really surprised" if a competent launch couldn't raise a lot from CG, or if Catalyze couldn't find a co-founder ("this is our bread and butter"). His tentative lean: skip the UK, go to the US / Constellation. Alejo's first reaction: "I love 80k and I love the UK." Hugo's reframe: with the ambitious path "instead of choosing, you have everything" — Constellation, five employees, one of them embedded at 80k.

## Part 8: How Catalyze works, and mutual context

Hugo described the funnel: first call → continued calls where "I will see how you react" (not following recommendations is fine; lack of focus is a bad sign) → active incubation (Catalyze brand, door-opening intros, small grants for e.g. US travel). Alejo offered his frame: he's a utilitarian in theory, so if both sides optimize for good they should converge.

On CG internals, Alejo asked who actually owns this priority — his sense is nobody owns the differential-tech angle (Rohan being closest). Hugo: CG is "teams inside of teams," but it doesn't matter — "CG right now wants to deploy money" and lacks grantees, so one or two internal champions suffice.

Timelines caveat from Alejo, offered as bet-relevant context: "I'm actually less of a believer in short-medium timelines than most people in the AI safety space" (economist by training; closest public proxy Basil Halperin) — "but I still think this is extremely valuable even if there's no very specific crunch time." Hugo's own theory: impact concentrates before/during crunch time, which needs AI-enabled orgs; Catalyze itself is going AI-native (Attio CRM auto-ingesting Granola calls and email, queryable via a Claude Code MCP) — "literally creating something to replace us."

## Appendix 3: Underlying dynamics

**Hugo is both recruiting and evaluating.** The pitch is unusually eager ("for you, I'm searching"), yet he was explicit that the next calls are an assessment — "I will see how you react," with unfocused founders getting dropped. Alejo's follow-through on these next steps is itself the test.

**Self-aware persuasion.** Hugo twice named the risk of biasing Alejo and pitched anyway; Alejo counter-signaled independence ("I can try to be contrarian," decisions go through trusted advisors). Both are managing the influence dynamic in the open.

**Head vs heart on the plan.** Alejo's stated criteria point one way; his repeated "I love 80k and I love the UK" points another. Hugo's "you have everything" reframe targets exactly that split rather than resolving it — the Impact Ops confirmation deadline forces the issue within days.

**Entangled personal ties.** The AIM coaching commitment Alejo wants to shed runs through Hugo's girlfriend Samantha — Hugo took on brokering that himself, which both smooths it and deepens how intertwined the relationship already is.

**Non-public information.** Grant figures and CG details were shared under an explicit agreement: full transcript stays private to Alejo; only the top-three framing and "be more ambitious, build a team" are shareable.
