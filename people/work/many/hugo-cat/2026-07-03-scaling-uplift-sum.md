# Meeting summary: Hugo Walrand & Peter Hartree — scaling AI uplift

**Date:** 2026-07-03

**Participants:** Alejandro Acelas; Hugo Walrand (co-founder, Catalyze Impact); Peter Hartree (independent — uplift consulting, AI blog/newsletter)

---

## Summary

Hugo convened the call to explore how to scale AI uplift in AI safety / GCR orgs, with funder money ready to back it — one funder shortlist item is "creating an org that will be the home... where all the people that are doing AI uplift will live." The conversation covered:
1. Whether a 10–20-person uplift consulting org beats unbundled alternatives (field-building programs, full-time in-org roles, informal knowledge sharing) — Peter's verdict: "worth serious consideration, but it's not a slam dunk".
2. Easier-to-staff subsets: one-on-one uplift coaching calls ("very basic, but very leveraged") and Alejandro's idea of a SPAR-style consultant mentoring stream.
3. Who could coordinate the field — Peter named himself, Chris Webster, and (if not busy) Alejandro; the candidate list runs out fast.
4. Next steps: Peter shares his July plan draft with Hugo next week; Alejandro connects Hugo to Chris Webster and to grantmaking.ai; a Fable-credits regranting fund surfaced as a side idea.

## Part 1: Context-setting

After small talk (Alejandro and Peter had already spoken earlier that day), each gave their situation:

- **Alejandro:** committed to doing the uplift work himself before thinking about scaling — "I just want to do the job myself" of figuring out what an AI consultant would actually do and what format best accelerates orgs. Starting at 80,000 Hours around July 20th; "ultimately I'm a utilitarian... but I feel this is reasonably close to the most valuable thing."
- **Peter:** uplift consulting since last autumn (~60% consulting, 30–40% public goods: blog, newsletter, field building). For July–August he has turned down all consulting — "the demand is extreme" — to give the public-goods work "a real serious run for its money", plus aggressive personal upskilling: "I want to be the person who really knows how to ambitiously delegate just really, really big projects to a group of AI agents" — positioned to get Fable 6 to execute widely valuable stuff.
- **Hugo:** two-time founder (ex-CTO of a 70-person health-tech org), co-founder of Catalyze Impact — an AI safety incubator (no cohorts; money, information, co-founders; governance, bio, cyber as well as technical). Catalyze is running a new idea-finding wave with senior, high-context funders: last month "a bit more than four big grants delivered, between 400k and 1.4 million" went to founders — for projects *less* exciting to the funders than the uplift-org idea on their shortlist. Hence his framing question: "what's the fastest and the biggest way" to get more AI uplift?

---

## Part 2: The core question — more uplift, faster

Alejandro's opening take: make uplift someone's explicit full-time role, not a weekend side-thing. "The org is a reasonably good form factor", but so is the individual-consultant path — "basically I did the uplift consultant playbook in the last four weeks and that got me hired at 80k in some form." The bar is low; encouraging others to run the same playbook is itself an intervention and a filter.

Hugo clarified the goal is **more work delivered**, not necessarily more people — including making existing orgs AI-native.

Peter distinguished two regimes needing different approaches: incrementally uplifting existing staff vs. the coming era of effectively *adding* AI staff ("very ambitious delegation"). He's skeptical orgs are serious: compared to what Shopify or Zapier did, "I don't hear a ton of" CEOs putting ~30% of staff time into upskilling or contemplating org-chart overhauls (his electricity analogy: not useful until workflows were rebuilt). His open question for real consultants: what are the change-management playbooks for radical (not incremental) workflow replacement — e.g. spinning up parallel entrepreneurial teams?

Alejandro flagged that radical versions already exist: he'd heard of a financial org with a "double digit percentage" staff cut where "a bunch of Claudes are now running a bunch of the things" — worth finding a founder or contact from there.

---

## Part 3: Outside experts

Peter noted little outreach has gone to world-class practitioners outside the community — the community is skeptical of outsiders and "a little bit burned" by past fast-onboarding attempts, though it still "could be really, really valuable". His own attempt (emailing Every.to ~6 months ago) went nowhere. Nobody on the call had heard of anyone approaching the OpenAI/Anthropic consulting arms. Peter recalled Coefficient "had a pretty bad time" with non-community consultants.

---

## Part 4: One org vs. unbundled mechanisms

Hugo sketched his vision: a few full-time people exploring "the most ambitious way... to properly scale AI uplift in AI safety and bio" via forward-deployed work with orgs, then a proven scaler founding an org housing "10 to 20 people".

Peter's pivotal question: what do you get from one consulting org that you don't get from 10–20 people in full-time roles at orgs? Hugo conceded frankly: "I have no idea. This is me jumping on the solution too quickly... I expect that the shape will be quite different from what I just said" — but defended the org's advantages: no duplicated work, fast internal spread of discoveries, trusted branding, shared ops, and training capacity ("an org can train five people every three months").

Peter countered that those mechanisms can be unbundled (MATS/Generator-style programs plus informal knowledge sharing) and that the model is "worth serious consideration, but it's not a slam dunk" — "very contingent upon the particular founders you could find." He also suspects "being a great forward deployed consultant is actually quite a lot harder than just having a full time role somewhere": his Coefficient consulting stint was dominated by org-chart politics ("it takes three weeks to get a meeting with the person who's actually going to make the decision"), which he's dispositionally against. Hugo accepted: "the answer will come from this exploration."

---

## Part 5: Easier subsets — coaching calls and a SPAR-style stream

Alejandro floated an idea he could launch in "the month or month and a half": a SPAR-style open call for uplift consultants — he mentors and quality-filters them with a small weekly time commitment, then sends them out to find clients; possibly literally a SPAR work stream (he explained SPAR — the Supervised Program for Alignment Research — as how he got into mech interp).

Peter added that one-on-one uplift coaching calls are a much-easier-to-recruit-for subset: the value is mundane ("have you tried using voice input?") but "it feels very basic, but it's very leveraged, honestly". The biggest headwind is that people in the whirlwind of their jobs can't carve out self-development time — "such a dumb headwind". AIM already built a network doing this (Alejandro was involved), and Peter ran a series of such calls last autumn.

Hugo argued this is exactly why a coordinated, well-funded effort helps: demonstrated wins create pull, and money can buy implementation time ("I pay you the same amount of money you're paid to do your work, but just to implement all those tools right now"). "This is a problem that is as solvable as a technical problem."

## Appendix 3: Underlying dynamics

**Hugo recruiting, Peter positioning:** Hugo's real agenda is scouting who will run the funder-backed uplift effort; he asked the "who is that person?" question directly. Peter named himself first — and his July plan already occupies much of the territory — so the exploration may resolve into Peter-led coordination rather than a new Catalyze-incubated org.

**Solution-first vs. explore-first:** Hugo twice caught himself "jumping too quickly on the solution"; Peter consistently pulled back to mechanisms and unbundling. They converged on funded exploration as the next stage rather than committing to the org form.

**Alejandro as connector, not candidate:** Alejandro has deliberately taken himself off the founder path ("pretty committed" to 80k) and played matchmaker — supplying the Anthropic-staff-cut lead, the Matthew suggestion, grantmaking.ai, and the intro actions — while keeping a small stake via the possible SPAR-style stream and coaching offers to Catalyze founders.
