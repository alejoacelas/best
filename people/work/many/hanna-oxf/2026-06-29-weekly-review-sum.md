## Part 4: Hardship vs. mental health — finding the pattern

Alejo suggested she should feel "kind of invincible" given how she handles what life throws at her. Hanna's correction was precise: "It wasn't very graceful. I was just, like, crying loads" — but "I think I'm actually pretty good at the hard stuff." Her key distinction: "my mental health is pretty decoupled from the actual hardships" — external hardships are challenges to overcome, while her genuinely bad periods usually have no external cause.

Asked how predictable those bad periods are ("Not very"), two candidate predictors emerged:

1. **Season** — "November is bad. And then January is bad" (December is saved by Christmas). Bad periods cluster in autumn; bright lamps sometimes help; this past winter was actually not bad.
2. **Overwork** — "if I'm working a lot, then I just spend less energy on maintaining myself and maintaining my relationships." She'd noticed the outward signs over six months (no climbing, less out of the house) but not the inward one — less energy for people at home — "maybe this is the link between the two."

This connected to the relationship crisis. Alejo pitched the skill of articulating what you want early, so course-correction happens at two weeks rather than six months. Hanna owned her side: her partner *was* communicating — "I think this was mostly my bad... I just didn't spend the energy of actually taking it in" — though the full story was "a bit more than that" and she didn't want to get into it.

Alejo committed to keep "solving season" on his list — he wants it solved for himself too by end of summer.

## Part 6: The visa decision

Alejo laid out where his project stands: people he's consulted are most excited for him to pick a small number of orgs and go full time accelerating them — probably a single org, maybe plus one day for others. That, and doing much better in the UK than in Bogotá, means he needs a visa. Three routes:

1. **80K sponsorship** — work full time with 80K for six months on a skilled worker visa. They "usually don't do skilled worker visas for less than a year", so he'd need to be the exception. "It's my top option right now."
2. **Impact Ops incubation** — Impact Ops helps him incubate an org and runs its standard skilled-worker-visa template; he was booking Joshua (CEO) that night.
3. **The gray area** — stay without receiving payment, as a "business exploration trip", as he'd implicitly done for the past month. An immigration lawyer's contact had told him: "technically, you should not be doing this... We'll let you pass. Just don't do it again."

Alejo framed it as an integrity question: "I think by default I tend to be pretty low integrity, and I kind of want to move to a better equilibrium" — and most people's standard ("just make sure they don't catch you") applied to any other rule is obviously not morality. He also feels a personal commitment to the lawyer who trusted him to do the right thing.

**Hanna's advice was unambiguous:** "I would just try to go the visa route." Her reasons: the gray area "would be kind of an underlying anxiety"; it's worse to sort out after the work succeeds than before; and — self-aware — "I would be anxious about this. And that's also partly why I'm saying this." She noted a Longview-type grant means he could receive payment (Alejo: "more likely than not" he can get a Longview grant quickly), and that 80K brings visibility on top.

On the six-month question: "I think you should try to make a strong case for the six months... I think you're actually not missing that much" even if AI moves fast — "you shouldn't do more than a year." Fellowship programs sponsor for as little as three months, so 80K shouldn't be averse. That gave Alejo a lead: ask the Pivotal organizer (Tobias Häberli, at the LISA offices) what immigration path the fellowships use.

His generalization: some people want a manager, and he slots easily into a light version of that role — she'll now message him Mondays with bullet points of last week and next week's plans. He cited Cassidy's decisiveness ("we are doing this") as the model.

Hanna contrasted her own difficulty: she's fine suggesting what friends *could* do, but feels bad saying what they're doing now is bad — and the hardest case is people with "crap managers", a limbo she knew from her own supervisors: managed enough to need approval, not enough to get direction. She got over it in the past year via good managers and realizing "I can just do things."
