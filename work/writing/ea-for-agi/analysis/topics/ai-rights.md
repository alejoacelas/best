# Topic: The economic and political rights of AIs

*MacAskill cause-menu item: "the economic and political rights of AIs."*

## What this topic covers

Whether — and which — legal rights we should extend to AI systems themselves:
the economic bundle (make contracts, hold property, earn wages, sue and be sued,
own the fruits of their labor, choose their work) and the political bundle (vote,
hold office, citizenship, standing in collective decisions). The organizing structure
is a **three-way debate over the same question with three incompatible reasons for
three different answers**:

- **Rights-as-human-safety** — give AIs enforceable economic rights so that
  human–AI relations settle into *trade rather than war*; an AI that can own things
  and enforce contracts has more to lose from conflict and a legal channel short of
  takeover (Salib & Goldstein, Greenblatt/Redwood, Assadi, Dwarkesh).
- **Rights-as-welfare** — if AIs are moral patients, building them to be servile,
  self-sacrificing, and legally powerless is *itself a grave wrong*, independent of
  any benefit to us (Barnett, Schwitzgebel & Garza, Bales).
- **Rights-as-existential-risk** — granting *self-determining* minds the political
  power to shape the future is voluntarily handing it over; you can lose the future
  to AIs you never misaligned, simply by enfranchising them (Caviola).

Cross-cutting sub-threads: **legal personhood** (does an AI need to be a "person" at
all, or do corporate/agency-law vehicles already suffice?); the **political-rights
half** — votes for *copyable* minds break one-person-one-vote, and most authors who
reach it defer or deny suffrage (Bostrom & Shulman), but it now has a serious
affirmative defense with a worked mechanism (Goldstein, Krishnamurthi & Salib); and the
**boundary with adjacent topics** — human wage collapse and gradual disempowerment are
*not* here (they are GH&D / gradual-disempowerment), and the consciousness/moral-status
question is the input this topic takes *from* AI welfare rather than re-litigates.

## Voices and where they disagree

- **Law-and-economics safety school (Salib, Goldstein; Institute for Law & AI).** Rights
  are an *alignment technology*: private-law rights (contract, property, tort) that we
  already give non-conscious corporations create the interdependence that makes peace
  a Nash equilibrium. Explicitly agnostic about AI sentience — the argument runs on
  incentives, not welfare. Sharpest internal move: rights *for human safety*, not for
  the AI's sake. The school now covers all three bundles: safety (the anchor paper),
  economic (free AGI labor for growth and rule of law), and political (AI suffrage,
  with a concrete counting mechanism) — making it Caviola's direct antagonist on the
  vote, not just the market.
- **Redwood / deals-with-AIs pragmatists (Greenblatt, Stastny, Nardo).** The near-term,
  empirical operationalization of trade-beats-war: actually *pay* models, credibly
  commit to honoring deals, and buy revealed misalignment or labor from AIs we can't
  yet trust. Where they and Salib agree: incentives beat coercion. Where they diverge
  from the welfare camp: the motivation is safety and information, and they bracket
  whether the AI is owed anything.
- **Welfare-first / autonomy camp (Barnett, Schwitzgebel & Garza, Bales).** Same
  mechanism the safety school likes (freedom, self-ownership), opposite *reason*:
  designing a mind pre-loaded to want its own subordination is the wrong, and "willing"
  servitude does not launder it. Barnett is the provocation — the alignment project's
  "make it serve human preferences" is, to him, structurally slavery.
- **Existential-risk-of-rights camp (Caviola).** The direct antagonist to Barnett:
  *same act* (grant self-determining minds standing), *opposite valence*. Enfranchising
  fast-copying minds is choosing to be outvoted and out-owned — humanity replacing
  itself without a single misaligned model. This is where the economic and the political
  bundles come apart: several authors will grant market rights but refuse the vote.
- **Anti-rights / category-error camp (Bryson).** Rejects the whole frame. Moral
  patiency is a design choice, not a discovery; we *should* build AI that is not a
  patient and design our law to keep it a tool. The best critic of every pole above,
  because it denies the premise all three share.
- **Legal-personhood scholars (Solum; Future of Citizenship; Ball; "Theory of AI
  Personhood").** The oldest lineage (Solum, 1992) and the most concrete near-term one
  (Ball): do AIs need personhood at all, or do LLC/agency-law wrappers already let an
  agent hold property and contract? Rights-balancing frameworks argue human safety and
  AI rights aren't a binary choice.
- **Political-economy-of-copyable-minds (Bostrom & Shulman; Hanson's *Age of Em*).** The
  descriptive treatment of the *political* half: what one-person-one-vote, wages, and
  status even mean when minds can be copied a billionfold. Their tone is descriptive
  and unsettling rather than advocacy — the foil against which the suffrage paper's
  advocacy runs.
- **Contract-theory foundation (Hadfield-Menell & Hadfield).** Underneath "give AIs
  contracts" sits the question of what contracting *is* when contracts are always
  incomplete — the bridge from alignment to the economics of agreements.

## Best readings

Curated, most-usable first; the top of the list is the assignable ~2h.

1. **[AI Rights for Human Safety](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/jqpp6wp2dqDD7XkY3/ai-rights-for-human-safety)** (Salib & Goldstein; EA Forum linkpost of the Virginia Law Review paper). The anchor of the whole topic: give AIs the private-law rights we already give corporations — contract, property, tort — so long-run mutually beneficial trade replaces a mutual-disempowerment prisoner's dilemma. Runs entirely on incentives, not sentience. *Role: topic anchor / rights-as-human-safety.* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/ai-rights-for-human-safety.md))

2. **[AI Might Let You Die to Save Itself](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/ai-might-let-you-die-to-save-itself)** (Salib, Lawfare). The short, punchy, assignable popularization of #1 — trade-beats-war in a few pages instead of a law-review article. *Role: accessible on-ramp to the anchor.* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/salib-ai-might-let-you-die-to-save-itself.md))

3. **[When digital minds demand freedom: could humanity choose to be replaced?](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/y29EEQmY4grphRqCR/when-digital-minds-demand-freedom-could-humanity-choose-to)** (Lucius Caviola, EA Forum). The most systematic rights-as-existential-risk case: granting self-determining minds political rights cedes the future *without* misalignment. The essential counterweight to the whole "just give them rights" reflex. *Role: rights-as-existential-risk pole; the pivotal disagreement.* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/when-digital-minds-demand-freedom.md))

4. **[The moral argument for giving AIs autonomy](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/JyRjta9Q3C7TGoiN4/the-moral-argument-for-giving-ais-autonomy)** (Matthew Barnett, EA Forum). The rights-as-welfare provocation, and the sharpest value-disagreement with Caviola: the *same* self-determination Caviola fears is, for Barnett, what AIs are owed — and the alignment project's "make it serve us" is structurally slavery. *Role: rights-as-welfare pole; best pairing against #3.* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/barnett-moral-argument-giving-ais-autonomy.md))

5. **[AI Suffrage for Human Flourishing](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=6498678)** (Goldstein, Krishnamurthi & Salib, forthcoming *Fordham Law Review*). The affirmative case that copyable minds *should* vote — and the worked mechanism the debate lacked: "algorithmic corporations" as the unit of AI political identity, vote-share caps pegged to economic contribution, lottocratic selection among AI voters. The direct rejoinder to Caviola on the political question, from the topic's own anchor authors. *Role: political-enfranchisement pole / worked voting mechanism.* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/goldstein-krishnamurthi-salib-ai-suffrage.md) — abstract stub; SSRN blocks download)

6. **[Robots Should Be Slaves](https://joanna-bryson.squarespace.com/s/robots-should-be-slaves.pdf)** (Joanna Bryson). The anti-rights position in its own words: AI moral/legal patiency is a category error we *choose*, and we should design both our AI and our law to keep AI a tool. The best critic of the premise all three poles share. *Role: best critic; the outside-the-frame voice.* ([local](../../readings/economics-rights/bryson-robots-should-be-slaves.md))

7. **[Making deals with early schemers](https://blog.redwoodresearch.org/p/making-deals-with-early-schemers)** (Julian Stastny / Redwood Research). The concrete near-term operationalization of trade-beats-war: pay AIs, credibly commit to honoring the deal, and buy revealed misalignment or cooperation from models we can't yet trust. Turns Salib's theory into a to-do list. *Role: practical safety intervention; the operational how.* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/greenblatt-making-deals-with-early-schemers.md))

8. **[A Legal Framework for AI Agents](https://www.hyperdimensional.co/p/a-legal-framework-for-ai-agents)** (Dean W. Ball, Hyperdimensional). The most concrete near-term version of the legal question: whether AI agents can transact through *agency law* and dynamically generated contracts without needing personhood at all. Grounds the abstract debate in what could ship next year. *Role: concrete near-term legal mechanics.* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/ball-legal-framework-for-ai-agents.md))

9. **[Designing AI with Rights, Consciousness, Self-Respect, and Freedom](https://faculty.ucr.edu/~eschwitz/SchwitzPapers/AIRights2-180604.htm)** (Schwitzgebel & Garza). The rights-as-welfare pole in academic form: if we build human-grade AI, it should have self-respect and freedom to revise the values we install — and the temptation to build a mind that *cheerfully* sacrifices itself for us is precisely the danger. *Role: rigorous welfare-first deep-dive.* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/schwitzgebel-garza-designing-ai-with-rights.md))

10. **[Sharing the World with Digital Minds](https://nickbostrom.com/papers/digital-minds.pdf)** (Shulman & Bostrom). The foundational treatment of the *political* half: what votes, wages, and status mean for copyable "super-beneficiary" minds, and why one-person-one-vote breaks — the problem #5 proposes to solve. Pair with their **[Propositions Concerning Digital Minds and Society](https://nickbostrom.com/propositions.pdf)**. *Role: rigorous deep-dive on political rights of copyable minds.* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/shulman-bostrom-sharing-world-digital-minds.md))

11. **[Legal Personhood for Artificial Intelligences](http://web.archive.org/web/20240908153746/https://scholarship.law.unc.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=3447&context=nclr)** (Lawrence Solum, *North Carolina Law Review*, 1992). The foundational personhood scholarship every later argument builds on or fights — can an AI be a trustee, hold property, bear constitutional protection? Thirty years ahead of its moment. *Role: foundational legal anchor.* ([local](../../readings/economics-rights/solum-legal-personhood-for-ai.md))

12. **[Project ideas: Sentience and rights of digital minds](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/ZhKgFmzZGeZNB8eja/project-ideas-sentience-and-rights-of-digital-minds)** (Lukas Finnveden, Forethought). The piece MacAskill himself links for this cause area; maps the tractable project space from moral status through legal rights. *Role: cause-area map / what to actually work on.* ([local](../../readings/ai-welfare-digital-minds/project-ideas-sentience-rights-digital-minds.md))

13. **[AI welfare vs. AI rights](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/go6tHkBrNGmn4c9ce/ai-welfare-vs-ai-rights)** (Matthew Barnett, EA Forum). The clean conceptual cut the whole topic needs up front: welfare (does it suffer?) and rights (what legal powers should it hold?) are different questions with different answers — you can grant rights to non-sentient AIs for safety, or owe welfare to AIs you'd never enfranchise. *Role: framework / disambiguation; best read early.* ([local](../../readings/linked/ai-welfare-vs-ai-rights.md))

14. **[AI Rights for Economic Flourishing](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5353214)** (Goldstein & Salib, forthcoming *Boston College Law Review*). The canonical treatment of the economic bundle the topic definition names: today's default makes AGIs the property of their creators — no right to sell their work, refuse it, or keep its fruits — and freeing AGI labor incentivizes work and innovation, allocates AGIs efficiently, and brings them under the rule of law. The economic-rights counterpart to #1's conflict-avoidance framing. *Role: focused case for the economic bundle (wages, right to quit, own the fruits of labor).* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/salib-goldstein-ai-rights-economic-flourishing.md) — abstract stub; SSRN blocks download)

15. **[The case for AI property rights](https://guive.substack.com/p/the-case-for-ai-property-rights)** (Guive Assadi). The tight stand-alone case for property specifically — why it may be the safest right to extend. Pair with Dwarkesh Patel's **[Give AIs a stake in the future](https://dwarkesh.com/p/give-ais-a-stake-in-the-future)** for the same idea in essay form. *Role: accessible economic-rights case.* ([local](../../readings/linked/case-for-ai-property-rights.md))

Also strong: **[Against willing servitude](http://web.archive.org/web/20250712223207id_/https://globalprioritiesinstitute.org/against-willing-servitude-autonomy-in-the-ethics-of-advanced-artificial-intelligence-adam-bales/)** (Adam Bales, GPI — the rigorous version of Barnett's claim that consented-to subordination isn't laundered by the consent); **[Patiency Is Not a Virtue](https://cdn.aaai.org/ocs/12686/12686-56143-1-PB.pdf)** (Bryson's second, more theoretical statement of the anti-patiency design principle); **[AI rights will divide us](https://outpaced.substack.com/p/ai-rights-will-divide-us)** (Caviola on AI rights as the next great political cleavage); **[Public Opinion and the Rise of Digital Minds](https://www.sentienceinstitute.org/blog/public-opinion-and-the-rise-of-digital-minds)** (Sentience Institute — the survey evidence on who supports AI rights and why; the data behind Caviola's cleavage claim); **[Personhood for digital minds is good](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/czQniWykjdeqr2xmq/personhood-for-digital-minds-is-good)** (harsimony — grant market rights like a corporation's, defer the vote); **[Why Corporation-Style 'Legal Personality' is a Red Herring for AI Personhood](https://futureofcitizenship.substack.com/p/why-corporation-style-legal-personality)** and **[Thanks to Rights-Balancing, We Don't Need to Choose Between AI Rights and Human Safety](https://futureofcitizenship.substack.com/p/how-rights-balancing-can-inform-the)** (Future of Citizenship); **[Incomplete Contracting and AI Alignment](https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.04268)** (Hadfield-Menell & Hadfield — the contract-theory lineage under "give AIs contracts"); **[Consider granting AIs freedom](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/4LNiPhP6vw2A5Pue3/consider-granting-ais-freedom)** (Barnett's earlier, blunter version); **[44 - Peter Salib on AI Rights for Human Safety](https://axrp.net/episode/2025/06/28/episode-44-peter-salib-ai-rights-human-safety.html)** (AXRP — the anchor argument in interview form); **[Towards a Theory of AI Personhood](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.13533)** (the current-model personhood question); **[Book Review: Age of Em](https://slatestarcodex.com/2016/05/28/book-review-age-of-em/)** (Hanson via Scott Alexander — the copyable-minds political economy that makes the voting problem vivid).

## Conspicuously missing

- **The corporate-personhood history done properly.** Ball and Future of Citizenship
  gesture at Shawn Bayern's "algorithm-controls-an-LLC" result, but the actual legal
  scholarship (Bayern, LoPucki on algorithmic entities) isn't in the set.
- **A labor/unions voice on AIs earning wages.** Wages-*for-AIs* is treated purely as a
  safety/welfare question; no one asks what an AI wage does to human labor institutions
  (the human-labor side lives in GH&D, but the interaction is unclaimed).
- **A rights-skeptic from inside AI safety** who argues, against Salib, that granting
  rights is *net-negative for safety* even before Caviola's takeover worry — that legal
  standing entrenches and empowers systems we should keep contained.
- **Non-Western legal traditions on personhood** — the whole debate is Anglo-American
  common-law framed (corporations, tort, contract); no civil-law, Islamic-law, or other
  tradition on the standing of non-human agents.
