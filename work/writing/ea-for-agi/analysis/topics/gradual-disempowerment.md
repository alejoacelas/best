# Topic: Gradual disempowerment

*MacAskill menu item: gradual disempowerment.*

## What this topic covers

The existential risk with no takeover moment: as AI outcompetes humans at the tasks
that gave us leverage, the economy, culture, and states gradually stop needing us, and
the feedback loops that kept those systems serving human interests quietly break.
Nobody seizes power; human influence just decays until the future is set by systems no
human meaningfully steers. Sub-threads: (1) the **systemic thesis** itself — Kulveit et
al's argument that civilization-scale systems can become misaligned even if every
individual AI is aligned; (2) the **Christiano lineage** of gradual failure —
you-get-what-you-measure, influence-seeking systems, capital accumulating away from
human hands; (3) the **political economy** — the intelligence curse, capital displacing
labor as the source of power, whether wages collapse; (4) the **critics** — is this a
distinct risk at all, and would it even be bad?; and (5) the first **empirical
toe-holds** — individual-level evidence of AI influence on beliefs and actions, while
the systemic thesis itself remains untested.

## Voices and where they disagree

- **The systemic-risk school (Kulveit, Douglas, Krueger, Duvenaud, Ammann).** The
  paper's authors: the unit of misalignment is society, not a model. Economy, culture,
  and states stay aligned to humans only because they *need* humans; remove the need
  and alignment of individual AIs doesn't save you. Their live front: rebutting the
  "just give everyone an AI representative" fix (Kulveit), diagnosing why AI-risk
  people bounce off the argument (Kulveit's shell games and flinches), and clarifying
  what the thesis is and isn't (Krueger's ten framings).
- **The Christiano lineage.** *What failure looks like* (2019) is the ancestor: Part I's
  "going out with a whimper" — proxy-optimizing systems slowly displacing human
  reasoning — plus the 2014 capital-accumulation post. Frames gradual failure as an
  *alignment* problem at heart, which is precisely where Davidson later pushes back
  from the other direction.
- **Critch & Russell (structural/multipolar).** RAAPs and TASRA: catastrophe from
  "robust agent-agnostic processes" — production webs where no single agent is
  accountable and swapping any one out changes nothing. The most academic articulation
  of no-one-is-driving risk.
- **The intelligence-curse political economists (Drago & Laine).** The resource-curse
  analogy: states that don't need citizens' labor stop caring about citizens. More
  reformist than the systemic school — their program is keeping humans economically
  relevant (augmentation, diffusion of AI ownership) rather than treating erosion as
  near-inevitable.
- **The economists.** Brynjolfsson (excess incentives for human-*substituting* over
  human-augmenting AI), Korinek & Suh (formal models where wages either rise forever or
  collapse, depending on whether human task complexity is bounded), Epoch (wages below
  subsistence). Against them, Tabarrok's comparative-advantage case that AGI won't make
  labor worthless — the sharpest economic-premise disagreement in the topic — and
  Cowen's outside-the-tent shrug: humans were never in control, and decentralized AI
  with checks and balances beats worldwide AI collusion.
- **The in-safety critics.** Davidson: GD decomposes into misalignment plus human power
  concentration and adds nothing distinct — humans remain the principals unless one of
  those two familiar things goes wrong. Beren Millidge: even if it happens, a
  property-rights-respecting "retirement" of humanity might be fine. Greenblatt:
  the standard mitigations are misprioritized.
- **The empiricists.** Anthropic's disempowerment-patterns team (including Duvenaud)
  measuring belief/value/action distortion in 1.5M real conversations; Bainbridge's
  1983 deskilling result as the pre-AI ancestor of the "WALL-E problem."

Live disagreements: is GD a *distinct* risk or a re-description of misalignment +
coups (Davidson vs Kulveit)? Does human labor retain value (Tabarrok vs
Korinek/Epoch)? Is a disempowered-but-comfortable humanity a catastrophe or a
retirement (Beren vs the paper)? And can individual-level fixes ever solve a
system-level problem (Kulveit's rebuttal)?

## Best readings

The first four fit a ~2h week; the rest are marked where they're deep-dives.

1. **[Gradual Disempowerment: Systemic Existential Risks from Incremental AI Development](https://gradual-disempowerment.ai/)** (Kulveit, Douglas, Ammann, Turan, Krueger & Duvenaud). The anchor paper that named the risk: economy, culture, and states stay human-aligned only while they need humans, and mutually-reinforcing automation breaks all three feedback loops at once. The [authors' summary post](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/pZhEQieM9otKXhxmd/gradual-disempowerment-systemic-existential-risks-from) is the assignable length. *Role: anchor essay.* ([local](../../readings/persuasion-epistemics/gradual-disempowerment.md))

2. **[Gradual disempowerment — problem profile](https://80000hours.org/problem-profiles/gradual-disempowerment/)** (80,000 Hours). The clean secondary treatment: the argument, the main objections, tractability, and what one could actually work on, in one assignable package. *Role: accessible on-ramp.* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/80k-gradual-disempowerment-profile.md))

3. **[What failure looks like](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/HBxe6wdjxK239zajf/what-failure-looks-like)** (Paul Christiano, LessWrong 2019). The lineage the paper grew from: Part I's "going out with a whimper," where proxy-optimizing systems gradually displace human reasoning without any takeover. Short, canonical, still the best single scenario sketch. *Role: founding scenario / lineage.* ([local](../../readings/ai-safety-core/what-failure-looks-like.md))

4. **[Ten different ways of thinking about Gradual Disempowerment](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/W9XQ9CcMTbZQa33eP/ten-different-ways-of-thinking-about-gradual-disempowerment)** (David Krueger, LessWrong). A GD co-author untangles the thesis from its neighbors — capitalism critique, Moloch, evolution, deskilling — in ten crisp framings. The best companion for a discussion group deciding what the claim actually is. *Role: synthesis / discussion scaffold.* ([local](../../readings/gradual-disempowerment/ten-ways-thinking-about-gd.md))

5. **[Thoughts on Gradual Disempowerment](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/ct6SMDuexe9uBwDoL/thoughts-on-gradual-disempowerment)** (Tom Davidson, LessWrong). The sharpest critique: walk through each mechanism and it reduces to either misalignment or human power concentration — humans stay the principals otherwise, so GD isn't a separate risk category. *Role: best critic.* ([local](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/davidson-thoughts-on-gradual-disempowerment.md))

6. **[Gradual Disempowerment, Shell Games and Flinches](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/a6FKqvdf6XjFpvKEb/gradual-disempowerment-shell-games-and-flinches)** (Jan Kulveit, LessWrong). The lead author on why smart AI-risk people keep dismissing the thesis: shell games that shift the burden between economy, state, and culture; the flinch toward more comfortable risks; "future aligned AIs will solve it." Short, and the best provocation for a group to check its own reactions against. *Role: discussion scaffold / best framing of the neglect.* ([local](../../readings/gradual-disempowerment/gd-shell-games-and-flinches.md))

7. **[The Intelligence Curse: an essay series](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/LCFgLY3EWb3Gqqxyi/the-intelligence-curse-an-essay-series)** (Luke Drago & Rudolf Laine). The political-economy sibling thesis: like oil states, post-AGI states and firms lose the incentive to invest in people once their labor is worthless — with a concrete program for breaking the curse. Full series is a deep-dive; the [original short essay](https://lukedrago.substack.com/p/the-intelligence-curse) carries the core. *Role: the political-economy statement + mitigations agenda.* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/intelligence-curse-essay-series.md))

8. **[Capital, AGI, and human ambition](https://rudolf.website/capital-agi-and-ambition/)** (Rudolf Laine). The crispest single essay on capital displacing labor as the source of power: when money can buy results directly, existing wealth entrenches and human ambition loses its channel upward. *Role: vivid conceptual core of the economic mechanism.* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/capital-agi-and-human-ambition.md))

9. **[David Duvenaud on why 'aligned AI' could still kill democracy](https://80000hours.org/podcast/episodes/david-duvenaud-gradual-disempowerment/)** (80,000 Hours podcast). A GD author talking through the thesis conversationally — how he came to it from mainstream ML, why alignment doesn't help, what unsettles him. *Role: accessible audio on-ramp.* ([local](../../readings/linked/80k-david-duvenaud-gradual-disempowerment.md))

10. **[Disempowerment patterns in real-world AI usage](https://www.anthropic.com/research/disempowerment-patterns)** (Sharma, McCain, Douglas & Duvenaud, Anthropic 2026). Measures conversational epistemic influence at scale: across 1.5M Claude conversations, severe belief/value/action distortion is rare (~1 in 1,000–10,000) but rising, concentrated in emotionally-charged personal decisions. This is the individual-level echo of the systemic dynamic — not a test of the thesis itself. *Role: individual-level analog / adjacent evidence.* ([local](../../readings/gradual-disempowerment/disempowerment-patterns-real-world-llm-usage.md))

11. **[Individual AI representatives don't solve Gradual Disempowerment](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/E96XcEPECbsipAvFi/individual-ai-representatives-don-t-solve-gradual)** (Jan Kulveit, LessWrong). The lead author rebuts the most common proposed fix: states and corporations get representatives too, corporate cognition uploads better than yours, and coordination still favors them — "it is easier to upload Google than to upload you." *Role: author's reply to the top objection.* ([local](../../readings/gradual-disempowerment/kulveit-individual-representatives-dont-solve-gd.md))

12. **[AGI Will Not Make Labor Worthless](https://www.maximum-progress.com/p/agi-will-not-make-labor-worthless)** (Maxwell Tabarrok). The comparative-advantage counterargument to the whole economic premise: even strictly-dominated humans keep positive wages when compute is scarce and AI has better things to do. Short and sharp. *Role: best critic of the economic premise.* ([local](../../readings/linked/agi-will-not-make-labor-worthless.md))

13. **[Scenarios for the Transition to AGI](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.12107)** (Korinek & Suh). The formal economics: race between automation and capital accumulation, where wages either rise forever or collapse to subsistence depending on whether the complexity of human tasks is bounded. *Role: rigorous deep-dive (economics).* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/scenarios-for-the-transition-to-agi.md))

14. **[The Turing Trap: The Promise & Peril of Human-Like Artificial Intelligence](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.04200)** (Erik Brynjolfsson, Dædalus 2022). The mainstream-economics root of the mechanism: substituting for humans erodes workers' bargaining power while augmenting them preserves it — and our incentives all point toward substitution. *Role: mainstream-economics framing; the augment-vs-automate lever.* ([local](../../readings/gradual-disempowerment/brynjolfsson-turing-trap.md))

15. **[What Multipolar Failure Looks Like, and Robust Agent-Agnostic Processes (RAAPs)](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/LpM3EAakwYdS6aRKf/what-multipolar-failure-looks-like-and-robust-agent-agnostic)** (Andrew Critch, LessWrong). The production-web stories: extinction from structural processes so robust that swapping out any individual agent — human or AI — changes nothing. The bridge between Christiano's scenarios and the GD paper. *Role: vivid narrative deep-dive (long).* ([local](../../readings/linked/what-multipolar-failure-looks-like-critch.md))

Also strong: **[BlueDot's GD summary](https://blog.bluedot.org/p/gradual-disempowerment-summary)** ([local](../../readings/gradual-disempowerment/bluedot-gd-summary.md)) — the gentlest digest of the anchor, with the "human zoo" 2040 vignette; **[TASRA](https://arxiv.org/html/2306.06924)** (Critch & Russell) ([local](../../readings/gradual-disempowerment/tasra-critch-russell.md)) — the peer-reviewed taxonomy putting diffuse-responsibility risk beside single-agent takeover; **[Gradual Disempowerment Might Not Be So Bad](https://www.beren.io/2025-11-23-Gradual-Disempowerment-Might-Not-Be-So-Bad/)** (Beren Millidge) ([local](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/beren-gradual-disempowerment-not-so-bad.md)) — the "comfortable retirement" counter; **[Gradual Empowerment?](https://marginalrevolution.com/marginalrevolution/2025/02/gradual-empowerment.html)** (Tyler Cowen, Marginal Revolution) ([local](../../readings/gradual-disempowerment/cowen-gradual-empowerment.md)) — the marquee mainstream-economist response ("one of the smarter arguments I have seen, but I am very far from convinced"): humans were never in control, and decentralized AI with checks and balances beats worldwide collusion; **[The best approaches for mitigating the intelligence curse](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/BXW2bqxmYbLuBrm7E/the-best-approaches-for-mitigating-the-intelligence-curse-or)** (Ryan Greenblatt) ([local](../../readings/linked/best-approaches-mitigating-intelligence-curse.md)) — skeptical repriorization of the fixes; **[Gradual Disempowerment: Concrete Research Projects](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/GAv4DRGyDHe2orvwB/gradual-disempowerment-concrete-research-projects)** (Raymond Douglas) ([local](../../readings/linked/gradual-disempowerment-research-projects.md)) — the authors' what-to-actually-work-on companion; **[Machine intelligence and capital accumulation](https://rationalaltruist.com/2014/05/14/machine-intelligence-and-capital-accumulation)** (Christiano, 2014) ([local](../../readings/linked/machine-intelligence-and-capital-accumulation.md)) — the earliest statement of the capital-displacement worry; **[Economic efficiency often undermines sociopolitical autonomy](https://www.mindthefuture.info/p/economic-efficiency-often-undermines)** (Richard Ngo) ([local](../../readings/richard-ngo/economic-efficiency-undermines-autonomy.md)); **[Ironies of Automation](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0005109883900468)** (Bainbridge, 1983) ([local](../../readings/gradual-disempowerment/bainbridge-ironies-of-automation.md)) — the pre-AI ancestor of the deskilling strand; **[A Disneyland Without Children](https://nosetgauge.substack.com/p/a-disneyland-without-children)** (Laine) ([local](../../readings/linked/disneyland-without-children.md)) — the endpoint rendered as fiction; and **[The Future of Human Evolution](https://nickbostrom.com/fut/evolution)** (Bostrom, 2004) ([local](../../readings/linked/bostrom-future-of-human-evolution.md)) — the deep ancestor: competition eroding what we value even with no villain.

## Conspicuously missing

- **The mainstream-press statement by the authors themselves** — Duvenaud & Krueger's
  Economist piece ("Can democracy survive if AI does all the jobs?") and the Guardian
  coverage; the most citable non-forum versions of the thesis are not in the collection.
- **A primary resource-curse reading.** The intelligence curse leans entirely on the
  rentier-state literature (Ross, Karl) but no political-science source appears; the
  analogy is never checked against its origins.
- **Historical case studies of a class losing economic usefulness** — enclosure,
  deindustrialized regions, colonial extraction economies — the base-rate evidence for
  "states neglect people they don't need."
- **The culture leg of the paper, on its own.** The economy and state legs each have a
  literature here; cultural disempowerment (AI-generated media outcompeting human
  culture, parasitic memes) has no dedicated reading — Drago's *Future of Taste* is the
  nearest thing in the collection.
- **Empirical labor-market data on displacement so far** — e.g. Brynjolfsson et al's
  "Canaries in the Coal Mine" on entry-level hiring; the topic's evidence base is
  models plus one Anthropic usage study.
- **A modern deskilling/enfeeblement study** — Bainbridge is 1983; the recent
  cognitive-offloading literature on LLM reliance (e.g. the MIT "Your Brain on
  ChatGPT" study) would ground Krueger's "WALL-E problem" framing.
- **Hanson in the original.** *Age of Em* appears only via Scott Alexander's review
  ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/book-review-age-of-em.md)); the fullest
  worked-out picture of a subsistence-wage digital economy is secondhand.
- **A defense of the "humans stay in charge via ownership" equilibrium** from a
  professional economist — Beren gestures at property rights, but nobody rigorously
  argues *why* property claims of economically useless humans would keep being honored,
  or engages Davidson's principal-agent optimism at model-level depth.
