# Topic: Democracy preservation

*MacAskill cause-menu item: "democracy preservation."*

## What this topic covers

Keeping democratic institutions functional through the AI transition — the week that
asks whether one-person-one-vote survives a technology that decouples power from
popular support. Four sub-threads run through it: (1) **election integrity and
institutional resilience** — whether electoral administration, the legitimacy of
results, and trust in the process survive AI (the "liar's dividend"); the persuasion
mechanics themselves — deepfakes, micro-targeting, synthetic media — belong to the
*AI-driven persuasion and epistemic disruption* week; (2) **checks and balances under
concentrated AI power** — coup-proofing the state when a small group can command loyal,
militarized AI, and the institutional countermeasures against executive power grabs;
(3) **autocratization dynamics** — the ongoing global backsliding wave and the
digital-repression toolkit (surveillance, censorship, "informational autocrats") that AI
supercharges; and (4) **the social contract when labor and consent become optional** —
what democracy is for once states no longer need citizens' work or armies, and whether
benefit-sharing (UBI, windfall clauses) or structural power redistribution can hold the
bargain together. A fifth, forward-looking thread runs underneath all of these:
**institutional designs that survive — or upgrade — the transition**, from Taiwan-style
digital deliberation to AI-mediated collective decision-making.

## Voices and where they disagree

- **Democracy-erosion pessimists** (Harari, Garfinkel, Hammond). AI erases the practical
  advantages that made democracy outcompete autocracy — chiefly its superior
  information-processing — and lets power detach from the mass base that once forced
  rulers to bargain. Democracy, on this view, was a contingent blip, not an equilibrium.
- **Comparative-politics / autocratization scholars** (V-Dem, Guriev & Treisman, *Journal
  of Democracy*). Supply the pre-AI baseline: a 25-year backsliding wave and a shift from
  "fear dictators" to "spin/informational autocrats" who rule by manipulating information
  rather than by mass terror. Their live disagreement with the AI crowd: is AI a *new*
  mechanism, or just an amplifier of dynamics already well underway?
- **Digital-repression empiricists** (Freedom House, Polyakova & Meserole, Andersen).
  Document AI-enabled surveillance, censorship, and the export of authoritarian tech as
  things happening now, not forecasts — grounding the week in observed evidence.
- **Post-labor political economists** (Drago & Laine's *Intelligence Curse*, Korinek,
  Ngo). When AI removes the economic and military need for ordinary people, the incentive
  to keep them enfranchised evaporates. They split on the fix: cash redistribution (UBI,
  windfall) versus structural power — the *Intelligence Curse* insists money isn't enough
  because it leaves citizens dependent rather than powerful.
- **Benefit-sharing designers** (O'Keefe's windfall clause, Altman, Justen). Concrete
  pre-commitment mechanisms to distribute AI wealth. Critics call them voluntary,
  unenforceable, and beside the point if power — not income — is what democracy needs.
- **Democratic-upgrade optimists / the Plurality school** (Tang, Weyl, Landemore, CIP,
  Ovadya, the *Digital Public Squares* collective). Don't just defend democracy from AI —
  use AI to *strengthen* it through deliberation at scale, bridging systems, and open
  mini-publics. They disagree with the "preservationists" who want mainly to protect
  existing institutions.
- **Checks-and-balances / coup-proofing** (Davidson–Finnveden–Hadshar, GovAI, Hadshar).
  The institutional-design countermeasures against concentrated AI power: internal
  controls, transparency, distributed command over militarized AI. Shades into the coups
  week but supplies this week's constructive core.
- **Decentralizers** (Buterin's d/acc). Defensive, distributed technology as the
  alternative to concentrating AGI in one authority — explicitly against the
  "one safe project" centralizers, on the grounds that the cure is worse than the disease.
- **The just-relax skeptics** (Cochrane; on the narrower misinformation front, Simon,
  Altay & Mercier). Nobody has ever correctly forecast a technology's social and
  political effects — Malthus, Marx, the Club of Rome — so confident "AI ends democracy"
  stories deserve no policy weight; the real danger is the panic itself, which invites
  captured, preemptive regulation that entrenches incumbents. The direct rebuttal to the
  erosion pessimists' claim that technology now favors tyranny.

## Best readings

Most-usable first; the top of the list is assignable within a ~2h week, deep-dives marked.

1. **[Why Technology Favors Tyranny](https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/2018/10/yuval-noah-harari-technology-tyranny/568330/)** (Yuval Noah Harari, The Atlantic). The best-known statement of the topic's central thesis: democracy's 20th-century edge was better information-processing, and AI hands that edge back to centralized power — "democratic politics will mutate into an emotional puppet show." *Role: anchor essay / vivid first reading.* ([local](../../readings/democracy-preservation/harari-why-technology-favors-tyranny.md))

2. **[How will advanced AI systems impact democracy?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.06729)** (Summerfield, Argyle, Bakker et al., arXiv — 23 authors across Google DeepMind, Oxford, and academia). The rigorous, balanced survey that maps the whole week: AI's *epistemic* impacts on informed voting, *material* impacts on elections and institutions, and *foundational* impacts on democracy itself — plus the upgrade opportunities. Read alongside #1 to see the same terrain without the fatalism. *Role: rigorous survey / week map.* ([local](../../readings/democracy-preservation/summerfield-how-will-ai-impact-democracy.md))

3. **[AI, Society, and Democracy: Just Relax](https://www.grumpy-economist.com/p/ai-society-and-democracy-just-relax)** (John Cochrane, The Digitalist Papers). The direct rebuttal to #1: nobody — Malthus, Marx, the Club of Rome — has ever correctly forecast a technology's political effects, and the panic invites the captured, preemptive regulation that would do the real damage. Assign against Harari and Garfinkel. *Role: best critic (of the erosion thesis).* ([local](../../readings/democracy-preservation/cochrane-ai-democracy-just-relax.md))

4. **[How AI Will Change Democracy](https://www.schneier.com/essays/archives/2024/05/how-ai-will-change-democracy.html)** (Bruce Schneier, essay). The assignable-length distillation of *Rewiring Democracy*: AI's effects sorted across five arenas — politics, lawmaking, administration, the courts, and citizens themselves — with the "reform / resist / responsibly use / renovate" framing. *Role: checks-and-balances survey.* ([local](../../readings/democracy-preservation/schneier-how-ai-will-change-democracy.md))

5. **[Is Democracy a Fad?](https://benmgarfinkel.blog/2021/02/26/is-democracy-a-fad)** (Ben Garfinkel). The deep framing question underneath the whole topic: was democracy a technologically contingent phase — sustained by mass warfare and mass economies that needed the population — now that AI is removing both conditions? *Role: rigorous framing deep-dive.* ([local](../../readings/linked/is-democracy-a-fad.md))

6. **[Democratising AI: Multiple Meanings, Goals, and Methods](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.12642)** (Seger, Ovadya, Garfinkel, Siddarth & Dafoe, GovAI / AIES). The conceptual map that keeps the week honest: "democratizing AI" splits into democratizing *use*, *development*, *profits*, and *governance* — four goals that routinely conflict. Read early to disambiguate every other reading. *Role: conceptual anchor.* ([local](../../readings/democracy-preservation/seger-democratising-ai-meanings.md))

7. **[AI-Enabled Coups: How a Small Group Could Use AI to Seize Power](https://www.forethought.org/research/ai-enabled-coups-how-a-small-group-could-use-ai-to-seize-power)** (Davidson, Finnveden & Hadshar, Forethought). Read here for its mitigations chapters — the constructive core of the checks-and-balances thread: internal controls, transparency about model specs, and distributed command over militarized AI. The threat model itself is the coups week's anchor; this week takes the countermeasures. *Role: institutional countermeasures (coup-proofing).* ([local](../../readings/forethought/ai-enabled-coups-how-a-small-group-could-use-ai-to-seize-power.md))

8. **[Democracy Report 2025: 25 Years of Autocratization](https://www.v-dem.net/documents/60/V-dem-dr__2025_lowres.pdf)** (V-Dem Institute). The empirical baseline the AI transition plugs into: autocracies now outnumber democracies for the first time in over two decades, and the average person's freedom is back to mid-1980s levels. *Role: data/evidence (autocratization baseline).* ([local](../../readings/democracy-preservation/vdem-democracy-report-2025.md))

9. **[Informational Autocrats](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jep.33.4.100)** (Sergei Guriev & Daniel Treisman, *Journal of Economic Perspectives*). The mechanism V-Dem measures: modern autocrats rule by manipulating information and simulating democracy — propaganda and co-optation over mass terror — exactly the lever AI cheapens. The scholarly core of the "spin dictators" argument. *Role: rigorous deep-dive (autocratization mechanism).* ([local](../../readings/democracy-preservation/guriev-treisman-informational-autocrats.md))

10. **[Freedom on the Net 2023: The Repressive Power of Artificial Intelligence](https://freedomhouse.org/report/freedom-net/2023/repressive-power-artificial-intelligence)** (Freedom House). The observed record across 70 countries: AI scaling up censorship, surveillance, and disinformation — the digital-repression thread as documented fact rather than forecast. *Role: data/evidence (AI repression today).* ([local](../../readings/democracy-preservation/freedom-house-repressive-power-of-ai.md))

11. **[The Intelligence Curse: an essay series](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/LCFgLY3EWb3Gqqxyi/the-intelligence-curse-an-essay-series)** (Luke Drago & Rudolf Laine). Read here for the "shaping the social contract" chapters: when states no longer need citizens' labor or armies, cash redistribution leaves people dependent rather than powerful — the argument that benefit-sharing without structural power fails. The full disempowerment case is the gradual-disempowerment week's anchor; take only the social-contract slice here. *Role: focused deep-dive (post-labor social contract).* ([local](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/intelligence-curse-essay-series.md))

12. **[The Windfall Clause: Sharing the Benefits of Advanced AI](https://www.effectivealtruism.org/articles/cullen-okeefe-the-windfall-clause-sharing-the-benefits-of-advanced-ai)** (Cullen O'Keefe, GovAI / EA Global). The canonical concrete benefit-sharing mechanism: an ex-ante commitment by AI firms to redistribute windfall profits. The thing to argue for and against — #11 supplies the against. *Role: benefit-sharing mechanism / discussion pivot.* ([local](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/windfall-clause.md))

13. **[Economic Policy Challenges for the Age of AI](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.13168)** (Anton Korinek, NBER). The rigorous economics of the social-contract thread: as AGI substitutes for labor, work-based social insurance fails and income concentrates; the tax base and the mechanisms of distribution have to be rebuilt. Data/evidence for what benefit-sharing is up against. *Role: rigorous deep-dive (distribution economics).* ([local](../../readings/democracy-preservation/korinek-economic-policy-challenges-age-of-ai.md))

14. **[AI and the Future of Digital Public Squares](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.09988)** (Goldberg, Landemore, Tang, Ovadya, Siddarth et al.). The leading positive program: four ways LLMs could upgrade rather than degrade democratic deliberation — collective-dialogue systems, bridging, community moderation, proof-of-humanity. A rare constructive, empirically-grounded deep-dive. *Role: rigorous deep-dive (institutional design that upgrades).* ([local](../../readings/democracy-preservation/ai-future-of-digital-public-squares.md))

15. **[Audrey Tang on what we can learn from Taiwan's experiments with how to do democracy](https://80000hours.org/podcast/episodes/audrey-tang-what-we-can-learn-from-taiwan/)** (80,000 Hours podcast). The vivid, in-the-course's-voice version of the upgrade program: vTaiwan, Polis, and alignment assemblies — democracy strengthened with technology, told by the person who ran it. *Role: accessible on-ramp (positive / institutional design).* ([local](../../readings/linked/80k-audrey-tang-taiwan-digital-democracy.md))

Also strong: **[Misinformation Reloaded?](https://misinforeview.hks.harvard.edu/article/misinformation-reloaded-fears-about-the-impact-of-generative-ai-on-misinformation-are-overblown/)** (Simon, Altay & Mercier — the misinformation-alarm rebuttal; the deepfake/microtargeting readings themselves live in the persuasion week, [local](../../readings/democracy-preservation/simon-altay-genai-misinformation-overblown.md)); **[Sense-making about extreme power concentration](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/tnkEoTxdGCsBFRQty/sense-making-about-extreme-power-concentration)** (Hadshar — how worried to be, on reflection, [local](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/sense-making-extreme-power-concentration.md)); **[d/acc: one year later](https://vitalik.eth.limo/general/2025/01/05/dacc2.html)** (Buterin — the decentralization alternative to a single guarded AGI project, [local](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/vitalik-dacc-one-year-later.md)); **[Collective Constitutional AI](https://www.anthropic.com/research/collective-constitutional-ai-aligning-a-language-model-with-public-input)** (Anthropic & CIP — the live experiment in public input to model values, [local](../../readings/ai-character/collective-constitutional-ai.md)); **[Exporting Digital Authoritarianism](https://www.brookings.edu/research/exporting-digital-authoritarianism/)** (Polyakova & Meserole — how the repression toolkit spreads, [local](../../readings/democracy-preservation/polyakova-meserole-digital-authoritarianism.md)).

## Conspicuously missing

- **A first-rate account of AI-assisted lawmaking and administration.** Schneier gestures
  at it, but there is no deep piece on AI writing legislation, adjudicating benefits, or
  running the bureaucracy — the least-covered of the "checks and balances" arenas, and the
  one most likely to reshape the state quietly.
- **The military / security-services coup-proofing detail.** The countermeasures thread
  leans on the coups week; missing is a standalone treatment of keeping AI-enabled armed
  force under distributed, democratically-accountable command.
- **A serious critic of benefit-sharing from the left.** The windfall clause and UBI are
  presented and gently doubted, but no one argues that redistributing AI rents *entrenches*
  a rentier-and-dependent structure — the natural political-theory counter to O'Keefe.
- **Non-Western democratic experience beyond Taiwan.** India, Indonesia, Brazil, and the
  African cases run huge AI-era elections; the set has Taiwan (Tang) and China (repression)
  but little from the large, contested democracies of the Global South.
- **Empirical political science on whether AI actually changed a real election outcome.**
  Simon & Altay argue "not yet," but there is no rigorous post-mortem of a specific 2024
  race establishing the magnitude either way.
- **The pro-centralization rebuttal to d/acc, in its own voice.** "One Western AGI project"
  and soft-nationalization are cited elsewhere, but the case that concentrating AGI is the
  *safer* path for democracy never speaks directly against Buterin here.
- **A legitimacy / political-theory treatment of who governs the model.** Collective
  Constitutional AI and CIP run the experiments, but no political theorist asks by what
  right ~1,000 sampled citizens (or a lab) get to set values for everyone.
