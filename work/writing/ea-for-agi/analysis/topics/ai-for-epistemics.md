# Topic: AI for better reasoning, decision-making and coordination

*MacAskill cause menu — "AI for better reasoning, decision-making and coordination"
(the opportunity side he flags as cross-cutting: forecasting, truthful AI, collective
intelligence and bridging systems, prediction markets and info-finance, and AI tools
for negotiation, decision-making and coordination).*

## What this topic covers

The optimistic mirror-image of the persuasion/epistemic-disruption topic: instead of
asking how AI wrecks our collective ability to track truth, it asks whether AI could
*upgrade* human reasoning and institutions past anything we manage unaided. The bet is
that the same capabilities that make AI a persuasion weapon — superhuman forecasting,
argument evaluation, mediation, information aggregation — are also the raw material for
better epistemics, if we build the tools and adopt them before the bad uses lock in.
Four intertwined sub-threads: (1) **AI forecasting and decision advice** — LLMs that
predict, weigh evidence, and counsel on hard choices; (2) **truthful AI** — norms and
institutions for AI that reliably does not lie, as epistemic infrastructure; (3)
**collective intelligence and bridging systems** — AI that mediates deliberation,
surfaces common ground, and ranks for what unites rather than inflames (Habermas
Machine, Community Notes, Polis); (4) **prediction markets and info-finance** — markets
and futarchy as truth-machines, and AI agents as their traders. The cross-cutting
Forethought framing (Finnveden) treats this as a *differential* project: race the good
uses of AI-for-epistemics ahead of the bad, because whoever's epistemics compound
fastest wins the transition.

## Voices and where they disagree

- **Forethought / EA macrostrategy (Finnveden, Cotton-Barratt, MacAskill).** The
  cause-area architects. Epistemics is a neglected, high-leverage lever on how well
  humanity steers the transition; the work is to build AI decision-advice, forecasting,
  and coordination tools *differentially* faster than the disruptive uses. Optimistic
  but hedged — the same series (good/bad/ugly) catalogs how it backfires.
- **Truthful-AI school (Evans, Owain; Cotton-Barratt).** Frame the whole thing as
  *institutions for honesty*: define lying, build truthfulness evaluators, create norms
  and standards so AI advisers are auditable. The disagreement with the pure-capability
  camp is where leverage sits — norms/governance vs. raw forecasting skill.
- **Forecasting empiricists (Halawi et al., Schoenegger/"Silicon Crowd", Gooen/QURI,
  Metaculus).** Show the capability is real and measurable. Internal live crux (Gooen's
  cruxes piece): how close are LLMs to *actually useful* public forecasting, and does
  scale close the gap or plateau?
- **Info-finance / futarchy tradition (Buterin, Hanson, Tabarrok).** Markets, not
  models, are the truth-machine; AI's role is to make markets thick by trading the long
  tail of questions. Hanson's futarchy pushes hardest — bet on beliefs, vote on values,
  put markets *in charge*. **Best critics:** Rethink Priorities (structural problems
  with futarchy), Works in Progress (why markets stay niche), Scott Alexander (even a
  proponent flags the mispricings and limits).
- **Collective-intelligence / bridging school (CIP — Siddarth & Huang; Ovadya &
  Thorburn; DeepMind's Tessler et al.; Audrey Tang / Polis).** The most institutionally
  serious opportunity camp: AI as *mediator and aggregator* of many humans, not oracle.
  Empirical wins (Habermas Machine, Birdwatch bridging-ranking, vTaiwan) anchor it.
  Even the Habermas Machine team's own follow-up (Tessler et al., Knight Institute
  symposium) concedes the open challenges: deliberation's trilemma — participation,
  equality, depth — and whether optimizing statements for agreement flattens what
  deliberation is for.
- **Factored-cognition / debate / process-supervision tradition (Ought — Elicit; Khan
  et al.).** The worked engineering examples: decompose reasoning into checkable steps
  so tools amplify good thinking rather than launder bad, and set AIs to argue so that
  human judges track truth better than they could alone. Bridges to the AI-safety
  scalable-oversight debate.
- **Deskilling / epistemic-autonomy critics (Aboodi).** The only camp that attacks the
  core thesis rather than a sub-thread: habitually outsourcing critical thinking to AI
  erodes the capacities the tools claim to augment, and the erosion compounds across
  generations — so "AI upgrades our epistemics" may be a category error even if every
  tool works as advertised.

The deepest fault line runs between the **market/aggregation** optimists (truth emerges
from many agents — markets, crowds, bridging) and the **model/adviser** optimists (truth
comes from one very good reasoner you can audit). And over all of it hang two
rejoinders: the disruption topic's (every tool here is dual-use, so the opportunity is
real only if adoption of the good uses is *differentially* faster) and the deskilling
camp's (a civilization that reasons through AI may forget how to check it).

## Best readings

Curated, most-usable first. The top of the list is assignable within a ~2h week; items
tagged *deep-dive* are optional depth.

1. **[What's important in "AI for epistemics"?](https://www.forethought.org/research/whats-important-in-ai-for-epistemics)** (Lukas Finnveden, Forethought). The closest thing to an anchor essay for this exact MacAskill menu item — maps the whole opportunity space (forecasting, truthful AI, decision advice, coordination) and the case for racing the good uses ahead of the bad. *Role: topic anchor / cause-area map.* ([local](../../readings/forethought/whats-important-in-ai-for-epistemics.md))

2. **[Using AI to enhance societal decision-making](https://80000hours.org/problem-profiles/ai-enhanced-decision-making)** (80,000 Hours problem profile). The accessible, structured on-ramp: why AI-for-reasoning is a high-impact cause, what the concrete bets are, and how to work on it. *Role: accessible on-ramp; best single first reading.* ([local](../../readings/linked/80k-ai-enhanced-decision-making.md))

3. **[AI, epistemics, and the good, the bad, and the ugly](https://www.forethought.org/research/ai-impacts-on-epistemics-the-good-the-bad-and-the-ugly)** (Forethought). The balanced framing that keeps the optimism honest — the same capabilities that upgrade epistemics also enable manipulation and lock-in. Read alongside #1 to see both sides of the same coin. *Role: framing / built-in skeptic.* ([local](../../readings/forethought/ai-impacts-on-epistemics-the-good-the-bad-and-the-ugly.md))

4. **[The Worrisome Potential of Outsourcing Critical Thinking to Artificial Intelligence](https://philarchive.org/rec/ABOTWP)** (Ron Aboodi, *Educational Theory*, open access). The adversary the whole topic needs: habitually turning to AI for guidance erodes the critical-thinking capacities the tools claim to augment, compounding across generations — a worry Aboodi argues survives *any* level of AI capability. Every other critic here bites a sub-thread; this one bites the thesis. *Role: best critic of the core thesis.* ([local](../../readings/ai-for-epistemics/aboodi-outsourcing-critical-thinking.md))

5. **[AI can help humans find common ground in democratic deliberation](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.adq2852)** (Tessler et al., Google DeepMind, *Science* 2024). The "Habermas Machine": an LLM mediator that produced group statements UK citizens endorsed *more* than human-mediated ones, reducing division on Brexit, immigration, climate. The best hard empirical result in the whole topic. *Role: data/evidence + vivid; the flagship "it actually works" study.* ([local](../../readings/ai-for-epistemics/tessler-habermas-machine-common-ground.md))

6. **[Debating with More Persuasive LLMs Leads to More Truthful Answers](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.06782)** (Khan et al., ICML 2024 best paper). The argument-evaluation mechanism, tested: non-experts judging two debating LLMs reach 88% accuracy on questions where naive judges get 60% — and making the debaters *more persuasive* makes the judges *more* accurate. The direct empirical answer to "does AI-mediated argument help humans track truth?", and the bridge to AI-safety debate/scalable oversight. *Role: data/evidence for the argument-evaluation thread.* ([local](../../readings/ai-for-epistemics/khan-debating-persuasive-llms-truthful-answers.md))

7. **[Truthful AI: Developing and governing AI that does not lie](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.06674)** (Evans, Cotton-Barratt et al.). The canonical "truthful AI" proposal the definition names: defining lying vs. honesty for AI, truthfulness evaluators, and the norms/institutions to enforce them. The governance backbone of the topic. *Role: rigorous anchor for the truthful-AI thread.* ([local](../../readings/persuasion-epistemics/truthful-ai-evans-cotton-barratt.md))

8. **[Approaching human-level forecasting with language models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.18563)** (Halawi et al.). The capability datapoint: a retrieval-augmented LLM system that nears the human crowd on real forecasting questions. Pair with the "Silicon Crowd" result in the list below — a twelve-LLM ensemble that statistically matches the human crowd. *Role: data/evidence for the forecasting thread.* ([local](../../readings/linked/arxiv-2402.18563.md))

9. **[From prediction markets to info finance](https://vitalik.eth.limo/general/2024/11/09/infofinance.html)** (Vitalik Buterin). Defines the "info-finance" framing named in the topic definition — prediction markets and Community Notes as the two flagship social epistemic technologies, and the AI hook that makes the old markets debate current: AI agents as cheap traders that make the long tail of questions liquid. *Role: anchor for the markets/info-finance thread.* ([local](../../readings/persuasion-epistemics/buterin-info-finance.md))

10. **[The Collective Intelligence Project whitepaper](https://www.cip.org/whitepaper)** (Siddarth & Huang, CIP). The main institutional statement of AI-augmented collective intelligence — steering transformative tech via better collective decision-making, not just better individual models. *Role: anchor for the collective-intelligence thread.* ([local](../../readings/persuasion-epistemics/cip-collective-intelligence-whitepaper.md))

11. **[Birdwatch: Crowd Wisdom and Bridging Algorithms can Inform Understanding and Reduce the Spread of Misinformation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.15723)** (Wojcik et al., Twitter/X). The most-deployed bridging system in the world: notes selected for cross-viewpoint agreement rather than engagement cut resharing of false posts. For the concept it instantiates, see Ovadya & Thorburn's definitional "Bridging-Based Ranking" in the list below. *Role: data/evidence; the bridging-systems thread at planetary scale.* ([local](../../readings/ai-for-epistemics/wojcik-birdwatch-bridging-algorithms.md))

12. **[AI tools for existential security](https://www.forethought.org/research/ai-tools-for-existential-security)** (Finnveden & Cotton-Barratt, Forethought). The coordination/opportunity framing MacAskill flags as cross-cutting: using AI to build the negotiation, verification, and coordination tools that make the transition survivable. *Role: anchor for the coordination-tools thread.* ([local](../../readings/forethought/ai-tools-for-existential-security.md))

13. **[My current claims and cruxes on LLM forecasting & epistemics](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/EykCuXDCFAT5oGyux/my-current-claims-and-cruxes-on-llm-forecasting-and)** (Ozzie Gooen, EA Forum). The inside-baseball state-of-play from someone building the tools: how close LLM forecasting actually is to being useful, and where the live cruxes sit. The honest practitioner counterweight to the capability papers. *Role: deep-dive; practitioner's cruxes on the forecasting thread.* ([local](../../readings/persuasion-epistemics/llm-forecasting-epistemics-cruxes.md))

14. **[Shall We Vote on Values, But Bet on Beliefs?](https://mason.gmu.edu/~rhanson/futarchy2013.pdf)** (Robin Hanson, *Journal of Political Philosophy*). The primary futarchy text — put prediction markets in charge of policy, with 25 objections answered. Pre-AI, but it is the institution AI traders would power: Hanson's perennial answer to thin markets and missing questions is exactly what Buterin's AI-agent traders (#9) claim to supply. Read against its critic (#15). *Role: deep-dive; primary source for prediction-markets-as-governance.* ([local](../../readings/linked/hanson-futarchy.md))

15. **[Issues with futarchy](https://rethinkpriorities.org/research-area/issues-with-futarchy/)** (Lizka Vaintrob, Rethink Priorities). The structured critique of prediction-markets-as-institutions — Goodharted welfare measures, wealth-weighted influence, manipulation, thin markets — the essential counterweight to Buterin and Hanson, and a checklist AI traders only partially answer. *Role: best critic of the markets thread.* ([local](../../readings/persuasion-epistemics/rethink-priorities-issues-with-futarchy.md))

## Conspicuously missing

- **The automation-of-alignment-research overlap.** "AI for better reasoning" and "use
  AI to do our safety homework" are nearly the same bet; a Christiano/Carlsmith piece on
  automating alignment (both local elsewhere) would sharpen the highest-stakes version.
- **A Metaculus/ForecastBench-style live benchmark.** The forecasting evidence here is
  2024-vintage papers; a current leaderboard of AI vs. human forecasters would ground
  the capability claim in *now*, not 18 months ago.
- **Negotiation and bargaining tools, concretely.** The definition names "AI tools for
  negotiation," but the coverage is mostly deliberation/mediation; the game-theoretic
  bargaining-agent and automated-treaty-verification literature is absent.
- **A non-Western collective-intelligence voice beyond Taiwan.** vTaiwan is the one
  deployed non-US case; nothing from India (CIP works there), Africa, or the EU citizens'
  assemblies that also ran Habermas-style experiments.
- **Cost, capture, and legitimacy critiques of bridging systems.** Community Notes is
  celebrated here; missing is the harder look at how bridging ranking gets gamed, slowed,
  or captured — and who decides what "common ground" counts.
- **The demand-side problem.** Nearly everything here is supply-side (build the tool);
  almost nothing addresses *why decision-makers would adopt* an AI adviser or market that
  tells them things they don't want to hear — the adoption gap that sinks most
  epistemic-infrastructure dreams.
