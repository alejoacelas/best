# Topic: Space governance

*MacAskill menu item: **space governance** — who claims the cosmos after an
intelligence explosion; space resources and settlement governance; lock-in of the
long-term future.*

## What this topic covers

The readings ask a linked pair of questions: *will humanity's descendants spread into
space, and if so, who decides how they behave when they get there?* — with the AGI-era
twist that makes it urgent: an intelligence explosion could compress the whole
race for space resources into years, letting whoever leads it convert a temporary
technological edge into permanent material superiority (MacAskill, Forethought). The
topic runs from that lock-in argument, through the physics of whether interstellar
settlement is even possible (Sandberg & Armstrong, Ord, Beckstead) and the
astronomical-stakes case that how space is governed could shape near-all of the
future's value (Bostrom, Baumann, Moorhouse), to the competitive and evolutionary
dynamics that decide *what kind* of civilization fills the cosmos (Hanson, Shulman,
Buhler), and finally to the near-term policy surface where those decisions get made or
locked in today (Outer Space Treaty, the Artemis-vs-ILRS bloc split, orbital data
centers, lunar property). Two live fault lines cut across all of it: **is a spacefaring
future good or an existential trap** (Bostrom-style astronomical waste vs. Deudney's
*Dark Skies*), and **is "space governance" a real cause area or a speciality/meta-cause**
(Baumann's ITN case vs. JordanStone's reframing vs. kbog's skepticism).

## Voices and where they disagree

The topic is a debate, not a consensus. Six distinct voices:

1. **EA cause-prioritization (the house view).** Tobias Baumann's ITN post, Fin
   Moorhouse's 80k profile, MacAskill's Better Futures essays, Carson Ezell's UN and
   field-building pieces, the Center for Space Governance / Space Futures Initiative
   launches. Claim: long-term space governance is important, time-sensitive, tractable,
   neglected — settle a fair paradigm *before* colonization begins because vast distances
   make later enforcement impossible, and because post-AGI resource grabs could entrench
   whoever moves first. Notably self-critical: Moorhouse's own profile rates it only
   "sometimes recommended" and stacks up the objections.

2. **Grand-futures physics (scale & feasibility).** Sandberg & Armstrong's *Eternity in
   Six Hours*, Ord's *Edges of Our Universe*, Beckstead's FHI colonization review,
   Ćirković's cosmological forecast. Establishes the premise the house view rests on:
   intergalactic settlement is physically feasible and the reachable universe is vast but
   bounded. This is the "awe and scale" material.

3. **Hansonian evolutionary economics.** Robin Hanson (*Burning the Cosmic Commons*,
   *Hardscrapple Frontier*), Carl Shulman (*catch-up growth*, *spreading happiness*), the
   grabby-aliens model (Cook) and grabby-values thesis (Buhler). Claim: competitive
   selection at the colonization frontier, not anyone's intentions, decides the outcome —
   frontier colonists come to value only whatever keeps them at the frontier. **Internal
   disagreement:** Hanson/Buhler expect a value-impoverished "burned" cosmos; Shulman
   argues eudaimonic civilizations can colonize *nearly as fast* as pure "locusts" and
   then convert resources to welfare, so a good future is barely harder than a big one.

4. **Feasibility skeptics.** Charles Stross (Beckstead conversation, *High Frontier
   Redux*), Zach Weinersmith (the "space bastard" turn), Tom Murphy (galactic-scale energy
   limits), kbog's informal review. Claim: settlement by biological humans is far harder
   than enthusiasts admit (radiation, minimum viable civilization size, no economic
   motive), and the whole cause may be premature. Stross concedes it works *given* uploads
   or advanced AI — which quietly hands the question back to the AI topics; the Forethought
   AGI-era pieces (MacAskill, Parrack & Moorhouse) pick that hand-off up explicitly.

5. **Anti-expansionists (desirability skeptics).** Daniel Deudney's *Dark Skies* is the
   flagship: space expansion is *itself* an existential and totalitarian risk (asteroid
   redirection as weapon, ungovernable diverging colonies, Earth's deep gravity well as a
   target), so humanity should stay "Oasis Earth" for centuries or forever. Baumann's
   s-risk essay and the "why settlement is controversial" paper map the same ethical fault
   lines more neutrally. This tradition directly contradicts traditions 1–3.

6. **IR / security / space law (the near-term surface).** Kohler's *Cosmic Anarchy*
   (latency makes anarchy the default cosmic order), the Outer Space Treaty, Bilal's
   analysis of the Artemis Accords vs. China–Russia ILRS bloc split, lunar
   peaks-of-eternal-light property, orbital-data-center feasibility. Where the abstract
   stakes meet actual treaties, alliances, and launch manifests.

The productive tension: traditions 2–3 say the stakes are astronomical and the outcome
is up for grabs *now*; traditions 4–5 say it's either infeasible or actively dangerous
to try; tradition 6 is where a reader could actually get their hands on a lever — and
Moorhouse plus JordanStone both question whether pulling it does anything durable.

## Best readings

Most-usable first; the top of the list is assignable within a ~2h week, with
deep-dives marked as such.

1. **[Space governance — problem profile](https://80000hours.org/problem-profiles/space-governance/)** (Fin Moorhouse, 80,000 Hours). The canonical EA case *and* its own best critic — lays out scale/neglectedness/solvability, then a full section of counter-arguments (early efforts wash out, maybe wait, maybe AI dominates). *Role: anchor essay.* ([local](../../readings/space-governance/moorhouse-80k-space-governance-profile.md))

2. **[How to Make the Future Better](https://www.forethought.org/research/how-to-make-the-future-better)** (William MacAskill, Forethought). The AGI hook, from the essay series the founding post cites: space governance matters because solar-system resource grabs could turn a temporary AI lead into permanent dominance, and extrasolar allocation is a decision about "almost everything that will ever happen." Assign §2.2 and §3.4 — concrete asks included (if-then treaty clauses, OST ambiguities to fix now). *Role: AGI-era bridge / second anchor.* ([local](../../readings/forethought/how-to-make-the-future-better.md))

3. **[Space governance is important, tractable and neglected](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/QkRq6aRA84vv4xsu9/space-governance-is-important-tractable-and-neglected)** (Tobias Baumann, EA Forum). The short, clean origin statement of the cause, with the crux made memorable: settle a fair paradigm before colonizing, because "space is an endless desert with oases extremely far apart" and enforcement across light-years is impossible. *Role: accessible on-ramp.* ([local](../../readings/space-governance/space-governance-important-tractable-neglected.md))

4. **[Space Governance is not a cause area](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/6kwbebYw2eHKCqyCL/space-governance-is-not-a-cause-area)** (JordanStone, EA Forum). The sharpest internal reframe — space governance is a *meta*/risk-factor speciality (like community-building or international policy), not a standalone cause. A draft-amnesty post that visibly changes its own mind. *Role: best internal critic / reframing.* ([local](../../readings/space-governance/space-governance-is-not-a-cause-area.md))

5. **[Summary of *Dark Skies* by Daniel Deudney](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/YKqKv8tckMHkmFB4z/summary-of-and-thoughts-on-dark-skies-by-daniel-deudney)** (Cody Fenwick, EA Forum). The strongest anti-expansion case — space expansion as *itself* an existential and totalitarian risk. Note the filter: this is a longtermism-sympathetic summary with weaknesses flagged (ignores digital minds, hand-waves the coercion of keeping humanity Earth-bound), not Deudney's own voice. *Role: best external critic (secondhand).* ([local](../../readings/space-governance/dark-skies-deudney-summary.md))

6. **[The advent of astropolitical alliances](https://spacenews.com/advent-astropolitical-alliances/)** (Mustafa Bilal, SpaceNews). The near-term flashpoint analyzed: lunar governance is splitting into a US-led Artemis Accords bloc and a China–Russia ILRS bloc, precedent-setting happening *now* by alliance rather than treaty. The concrete great-power surface under all the cosmic abstractions. *Role: near-term case study / data-evidence.* ([local](../../readings/space-governance/advent-astropolitical-alliances-artemis-ilrs.md))

7. **[Will We Really Put Data Centers in Space?](https://www.forethought.org/research/will-we-really-put-data-centers-in-space)** (Avi Parrack & Fin Moorhouse, Forethought). Sober feasibility assessment of orbital AI compute — the first place the intelligence explosion and the space economy physically meet, and a live governance question (Musk: space is "the lowest-cost place to put AI" within years). *Role: concrete space×AI case study.* ([local](../../readings/forethought/will-we-really-put-data-centers-in-space.md))

8. **[Astronomical Waste](https://nickbostrom.com/papers/astronomical-waste/)** (Nick Bostrom). The foundational text the rest of the list references constantly: every second of delayed colonization forgoes vast potential value, yet safety dominates speed. Cross-listed from the macrostrategy cluster because the whole topic rests on it. *Role: foundational premise (short, dense).* ([local](../../readings/macrostrategy/astronomical-waste.md))

9. **[Spreading happiness to the stars seems little harder than just spreading](http://reflectivedisequilibrium.blogspot.com/2012/09/spreading-happiness-to-stars-seems.html)** (Carl Shulman, Reflective Disequilibrium). The cruxy optimistic rebuttal to Hanson: a good future may be *barely* costlier than a big one, because a eudaimonic civilization can colonize at near-max speed and convert resources to welfare only once past the frontier. Short, and the comment thread (Hanson, Pearce) is a live debate. *Role: rigorous deep-dive on "big vs. good."* ([local](../../readings/space-governance/shulman-spreading-happiness-to-stars.md))

10. **[Burning the Cosmic Commons](https://mason.gmu.edu/~rhanson/filluniv.pdf)** (Robin Hanson). The evolutionary-economics warning: uncoordinated colonization races "burn" cosmic resources and select for frontier-fixated values. The formal engine behind grabby aliens and grabby values. *Role: rigorous deep-dive (dense; pair with Shulman).* ([local](../../readings/space-governance/hanson-burning-the-cosmic-commons.md))

11. **[The Edges of Our Universe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2104.01191)** (Toby Ord, arXiv). Maps the affectable universe — the physical bound on everything the cause is about. Vivid, quantitative, and short on prose. *Role: data/evidence, scale anchor.* ([local](../../readings/space-governance/ord-edges-of-our-universe.md))

12. **[Eternity in Six Hours](https://www.aleph.se/papers/Spamming%20the%20universe.pdf)** (Armstrong & Sandberg). The feasibility keystone: intergalactic colonization via self-replicating probes is shown to be technically possible with foreseeable tech. Establishes the premise the skeptics attack. *Role: rigorous deep-dive (long PDF — skim/excerpt).* ([local](../../readings/space-governance/armstrong-sandberg-eternity-in-six-hours.md))

13. **[Cosmic Anarchy and Its Consequences](https://medium.com/@KevinKohlerFM/cosmic-anarchy-and-its-consequences-b1a557b1a2e3)** (Kevin Kohler, Medium). Turns the enforcement crux into a crisp thesis: light-speed latency caps the size of any government, so a galactic state is impossible and anarchy is the default cosmic order — 100+ billion independent star systems. The IR-theory lens on the whole topic. *Role: vivid mechanism / framing.* ([local](../../readings/linked/cosmic-anarchy-and-its-consequences.md))

14. **[The Colonization of Space](https://nss.org/the-colonization-of-space-gerard-k-o-neill-physics-today-1974/)** (Gerard K. O'Neill, Physics Today 1974). The original pro-settlement vision — rotating habitats, energy and land without limit — that the whole skeptic tradition is rebutting. Read before Stross so the debunk has its claim. *Role: primary-source origin claim.* ([local](../../readings/space-governance/oneill-colonization-of-space-1974.md))

15. **[A conversation with Charlie Stross on space colonization](https://www.nickbeckstead.com/conversations/stross)** (Beckstead & Stross). The best-articulated feasibility skeptic in interview form: radiation, minimum viable civilization (~hundreds of thousands of people), no economic motive — but "feasible given uploads or AI," which routes the debate back to AI. *Role: accessible skeptic (pair with O'Neill).* ([local](../../readings/space-governance/beckstead-stross-space-colonization.md))

Also strong, in supporting roles: **[AGI and Lock-in](../../readings/forethought/agi-and-lock-in.md)** (Forethought — the general mechanism behind the topic's "lock-in" framing; assign if the group hasn't met it in the macrostrategy week); **[AI Creation and the Cosmic Host](../../readings/linked/bostrom-ai-creation-cosmic-host.md)** (Bostrom — what a cosmos already containing other superintelligences implies for how we should behave in it); **[The Outer Space Treaty](../../readings/linked/outer-space-treaty-intro.md)** (UNOOSA — the primary-source legal status quo everything argues around); **[The Peaks of Eternal Light](../../readings/space-governance/peaks-of-eternal-light-moon-property.md)** (Elvis & Milligan — an already-here lunar-property lock-in case, good discussion seed); **[Zach Weinersmith on becoming a "space bastard"](../../readings/linked/80k-zach-weinersmith-space-settlement.md)** and **[Anders Sandberg on war in space](../../readings/space-governance/sandberg-80k-war-in-space-best-things.md)** (both 80k podcasts — the accessible audio routes into the skeptic and grand-futures threads respectively).

## Conspicuously missing

- **The anti-expansionist case in its own primary voice.** #5 is a sympathetic summary;
  Deudney's book isn't freely readable, and the current outside-the-tent critique of
  this exact worldview — Adam Becker's *More Everything Forever: AI Overlords, Space
  Empires, and Silicon Valley's Crusade to Control the Fate of Humanity* (Basic Books,
  2025) — has no assignable excerpt in the collection yet.
- **A humanist / awe opener with a named human.** Nothing like Sagan's *Pale Blue Dot* or
  a Carl Sagan / Kim Stanley Robinson passage — the set is analytic and treaty-heavy;
  it lacks an emotional on-ramp.
- **A decolonial / Global South voice.** The collection is almost entirely Anglo-American.
  Missing: scholarship on "space colonialism" and equity (e.g. Danielle Wood on space
  sustainability & the Global South, or Deganit Paikowsky / non-Western space-law
  scholars). The "whose voices govern the commons" concern is raised by Ezell but never
  voiced *by* a Global South author.
- **Whose welfare fills space — digital minds / directed panspermia.** The Shulman–Pearce
  hedonium exchange is only in a comment thread; missing is a dedicated piece on spreading
  digital minds or on directed-panspermia ethics (spreading life = spreading wild-animal
  suffering, e.g. Ng / Hauskeller), which is the AI-welfare bridge.
- **A near-term space-economics voice.** No economist on the actual commercial space
  economy and its governance (e.g. Matthew Weinzierl's "Space, the Final Economic
  Frontier") — the set jumps from cosmic stakes to treaties with no market layer.
- **SETI / METI contact governance.** Gestured at (downloading alien AI, grabby aliens)
  but no dedicated reading on whether/how to govern messaging or responding to
  extraterrestrial contact.
- **Planetary protection / contamination governance (COSPAR).** The one existing
  near-term, technical governance regime with real teeth — absent entirely.
