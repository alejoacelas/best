# Cluster map: s-risks

## What this cluster covers

S-risks — "risks of astronomical suffering," futures that contain vastly more
suffering than has existed on Earth, sometimes framed as outcomes *worse than
extinction*. The collection runs along four braided threads: (1) the **concept and
taxonomy** — what s-risks are, how they're classified (agential / incidental /
natural), and why their probability isn't negligible; (2) the **value theory** that
motivates prioritizing them — suffering-focused ethics, negative utilitarianism,
tranquilism, and the objections to these; (3) the **s-risk-from-AI** mechanism —
near-miss alignment, suffering subroutines, mindcrime, and the claim that solving
alignment isn't enough; and (4) the **cooperation/conflict** research program — CLR's
multi-agent agenda, commitment races, surrogate goals, and evidential cooperation,
where s-risk reduction becomes a concrete technical field. A fifth practical thread
runs underneath: **risk factors and interventions** (malevolent actors, moral circle
expansion, better politics, movement-building).

## Intellectual traditions and where they disagree

Three organizational "schools," plus a mainstream bridge and a popularizer layer:

- **CRS (Center for Reducing Suffering)** — Tobias Baumann, Magnus Vinding, Simon
  Knutsson. The values-and-advocacy wing: suffering-focused ethics as the moral
  foundation, broad interventions (moral circle expansion, better institutions),
  s-risks framed as robust across many value systems. Baumann's *Avoiding the Worst*
  is the field's first book.
- **CLR (Center on Long-Term Risk, formerly Foundational Research Institute)** —
  Lukas Gloor, David Althaus, Jesse Clifton, Caspar Oesterheld, Anthony DiGiovanni,
  Daniel Kokotajlo, Max Daniel. The AI-and-decision-theory wing: cooperative AI,
  game/decision theory, commitment races, downside-focused cause prioritization.
  More technical, more AI-conflict-centric.
- **Brian Tomasik / reducing-suffering.org** — the intellectual grandfather. Negative-
  leaning utilitarianism, wild-animal suffering, suffering subroutines, near-miss AI,
  values-spreading over extinction-risk. Idiosyncratic, foundational, cited everywhere.
- **Mainstream EA bridge** — 80,000 Hours problem profile, Cooperative AI Foundation
  (Hammond, Dafoe et al.). Legible framing, lower probability estimates, ties s-risk
  to accepted AI-safety and great-power-conflict work.
- **Popularizers** — Rational Animations (Allen Liu), LessWrong explainers. Vivid
  on-ramps.

Live disagreements the curriculum can teach *with*:
- **Does the case depend on suffering-focused ethics?** CRS/CLR insiders (Baumann,
  DiGiovanni) argue s-risk reduction is robust under moral uncertainty; critics see
  it as leaning on negative utilitarianism. This is the load-bearing crux.
- **How probable?** 80k judges worst-case s-risks "very low, much lower than
  extinction"; CLR/CRS argue "non-negligible" and disjunctive. Same facts, different
  bottom line.
- **NU vs classical utilitarianism** — the Ord ↔ Knutsson exchange (world-destruction
  argument, procreation asymmetry, minimalist axiologies).
- **Should we even prioritize AI?** Vinding's lengthy critique argues values-spreading
  beats AI-specific work — an internal dissent.
- **Extinction vs quality of future** — DiGiovanni's critique of "the expected value
  of extinction-risk reduction is positive"; Tomasik's values-spreading-over-extinction.
- **Can moral circle expansion backfire?** A contrarian thread argues wider circles
  could *increase* suffering (more beings deemed worth simulating/creating).

## Conspicuously missing

Specific enough to go search for:

1. **Toby Ord's original "Why I'm not a negative utilitarian."** The collection has
   Knutsson's *reply* and the world-destruction argument, but not the canonical
   philosophical critique they answer. You can't run the debate with only one side.
2. **Nick Bostrom primaries on "mindcrime" and infinite/hyperexistential ethics.**
   Mindcrime, "astronomical waste," and infinite ethics are cited across nearly every
   reading but no Bostrom text is in the collection.
3. **A non-Western / Buddhist primary source.** The SFE intro *mentions* Shantideva
   and Mahayana suffering-focus, but every actual text is Western-analytic. A primary
   Buddhist-ethics reading (or a scholarly piece on Buddhism and suffering) would add
   the tradition the collection claims but doesn't show.
4. **A digital-minds / artificial-sentience primary tying s-risk to AI welfare.** The
   "suffering subroutines / mindcrime" link leans on this but there's no Jeff Sebo,
   Bostrom & Shulman ("Propositions Concerning Digital Minds and Society"), or NYU
   digital-minds primary here — it lives entirely in the AI-welfare cluster.
5. **Stable totalitarianism as an s-risk.** 80k's "risks of stable totalitarianism"
   profile and Bryan Caplan's "The Totalitarian Threat" are referenced as the
   canonical worse-than-extinction lock-in scenario but neither is collected.
6. **A skeptical / outside-EA critique.** The only dissent is in-house (Vinding). A
   piece arguing s-risk prioritization is Pascalian, intractable, or fringe — from
   someone outside the CRS/CLR/Tomasik orbit — would sharpen the fellowship debate.
7. **Great-power-conflict / international-relations primary.** Agential s-risks and
   the whole cooperative-AI agenda rest on conflict dynamics, but there's no primary
   on war/escalation as the driver (only CLR's abstract game-theory framing).
8. **Any quantitative probability or forecasting estimate.** The field's genuine gap:
   nobody offers a defended number. If a Metaculus/forecasting or explicit-estimate
   piece exists, it would counter the "purely speculative" objection.
9. **A voice outside the CRS/CLR/Tomasik triangle.** Jonathan Leighton (OPIS, *The
   Battle for Compassion*) or David Pearce (Hedonistic Imperative / abolitionist
   project) — suffering-focused thinkers with a different institutional lineage.
10. **A 2024–25 piece connecting s-risks to concrete frontier developments** (agentic
    AI deployments, AI-welfare policy at labs). The newest collected framing pieces
    are mostly pre-2024 except the multi-agent report.
