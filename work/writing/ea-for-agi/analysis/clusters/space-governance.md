# Cluster map: space governance

## What this cluster covers

The readings ask a linked pair of questions: *will humanity's descendants spread into
space, and if so, who decides how they behave when they get there?* The cluster runs
from the physics of whether interstellar settlement is even possible (Sandberg &
Armstrong, Ord, Beckstead), through the astronomical-stakes argument that how space is
governed could shape near-all of the future's value (Baumann, Moorhouse), to the
competitive and evolutionary dynamics that decide *what kind* of civilization fills the
cosmos (Hanson, Shulman, Buhler), and finally to the near-term policy surface where those
decisions get made or locked in today (Outer Space Treaty, Artemis Accords, lunar
property, space debris, anti-satellite arms). Two live fault lines cut across all of it:
**is a spacefaring future good or an existential trap** (Bostrom-style astronomical waste
vs. Deudney's *Dark Skies*), and **is "space governance" a real cause area or a
speciality/meta-cause** (Baumann's ITN case vs. JordanStone's reframing vs. kbog's
skepticism).

## Intellectual traditions and where they disagree

The cluster is a debate, not a consensus. Six distinct voices:

1. **EA cause-prioritization (the house view).** Tobias Baumann's ITN post, Fin
   Moorhouse's 80k profile, Carson Ezell's UN and field-building pieces, the Center for
   Space Governance / Space Futures Initiative launches. Claim: long-term space
   governance is important, time-sensitive, tractable, neglected — settle a fair paradigm
   *before* colonization begins because vast distances make later enforcement
   impossible. Notably self-critical: Moorhouse's own profile rates it only "sometimes
   recommended" and stacks up the objections.

2. **Grand-futures physics (scale & feasibility).** Sandberg & Armstrong's *Eternity in
   Six Hours*, Ord's *Edges of Our Universe*, Beckstead's FHI colonization review,
   Ćirković's cosmological forecast. Establishes the premise the house view rests on:
   intergalactic settlement is physically feasible and the reachable universe is vast but
   bounded. This is the "awe and scale" material.

3. **Hansonian evolutionary economics.** Robin Hanson (*Burning the Cosmic Commons*,
   *Hardscrapple Frontier*), Carl Shulman (*catch-up growth*, *spreading happiness*), the
   grabby-aliens model (Cook) and grabby-values thesis (Buhler). Claim: competitive
   selection at the colonization frontier, not anyone's intentions, decides the outcome —
   frontier colonists come to value only whatever keeps them at the frontier. **Internal
   disagreement:** Hanson/Buhler expect a value-impoverished "burned" cosmos; Shulman
   argues eudaimonic civilizations can colonize *nearly as fast* as pure "locusts" and
   then convert resources to welfare, so a good future is barely harder than a big one.

4. **Feasibility skeptics.** Charles Stross (Beckstead conversation, *High Frontier
   Redux*), Zach Weinersmith (the "space bastard" turn), Tom Murphy (galactic-scale energy
   limits), kbog's informal review. Claim: settlement by biological humans is far harder
   than enthusiasts admit (radiation, minimum viable civilization size, no economic
   motive), and the whole cause may be premature. Stross concedes it works *given* uploads
   or advanced AI — which quietly hands the question back to the AI clusters.

5. **Anti-expansionists (desirability skeptics).** Daniel Deudney's *Dark Skies* is the
   flagship: space expansion is *itself* an existential and totalitarian risk (asteroid
   redirection as weapon, ungovernable diverging colonies, Earth's deep gravity well as a
   target), so humanity should stay "Oasis Earth" for centuries or forever. Baumann's
   s-risk essay and the "why settlement is controversial" paper map the same ethical fault
   lines more neutrally. This tradition directly contradicts traditions 1–3.

6. **IR / security / space law (the near-term surface).** Kohler's *Cosmic Anarchy*
   (latency makes anarchy the default cosmic order), the Outer Space Treaty and Artemis
   Accords primary sources, anti-satellite arms race and UNSC-space-security analyses,
   lunar peaks-of-eternal-light property, Forethought on space debris, the Wired piece on
   libertarian ideology in space. Where the abstract stakes meet actual treaties, arms
   control, and the US–China/Russia Accords split.

The productive tension for a classroom: traditions 2–3 say the stakes are astronomical
and the outcome is up for grabs *now*; traditions 4–5 say it's either infeasible or
actively dangerous to try; tradition 6 is where a student could actually get their hands
on a lever — and Moorhouse plus JordanStone both question whether pulling it does
anything durable.

## Standout readings

- **[Space governance — 80k problem profile](../../readings/space-governance/moorhouse-80k-space-governance-profile.md)** (Fin Moorhouse).
  The canonical EA case *and* its own best critic — lays out scale/neglectedness/
  solvability, then a full section of counter-arguments (early efforts wash out, maybe
  wait, maybe AI dominates). **Role: anchor essay.**

- **[Space governance is important, tractable and neglected](../../readings/space-governance/space-governance-important-tractable-neglected.md)** (Tobias Baumann).
  The short, clean origin statement of the cause, with the crux made memorable: settle a
  fair paradigm before colonizing, because "space is an endless desert with oases
  extremely far apart" and enforcement across light-years is impossible. **Role:
  accessible on-ramp.**

- **[Space Governance is not a cause area](../../readings/space-governance/space-governance-is-not-a-cause-area.md)** (JordanStone).
  The sharpest internal reframe — space governance is a *meta*/risk-factor speciality (like
  community-building or international policy), not a standalone cause. A draft-amnesty post
  that visibly changes its own mind, which models the epistemic humility the course wants.
  **Role: best internal critic / reframing.**

- **[Summary of *Dark Skies* by Daniel Deudney](../../readings/space-governance/dark-skies-deudney-summary.md)** (Cody Fenwick).
  The strongest anti-expansion case, fairly summarized *with* its weaknesses flagged
  (ignores digital minds and astronomical waste, hand-waves the coercion needed to keep
  humanity Earth-bound). The designated antagonist to the whole longtermist premise.
  **Role: best external critic.**

- **[Spreading happiness to the stars seems little harder than just spreading](../../readings/space-governance/shulman-spreading-happiness-to-stars.md)** (Carl Shulman).
  The cruxy optimistic rebuttal to Hanson: a good future may be *barely* costlier than a
  big one, because a eudaimonic civilization can colonize at near-max speed and convert
  resources to welfare only once past the frontier. Short, and the comment thread (Hanson,
  Pearce) is a live debate. **Role: rigorous deep-dive on "big vs. good."**

- **[Burning the Cosmic Commons](../../readings/space-governance/hanson-burning-the-cosmic-commons.pdf)** (Robin Hanson).
  The evolutionary-economics warning: uncoordinated colonization races "burn" cosmic
  resources and select for frontier-fixated values. The formal engine behind grabby
  aliens and grabby values. **Role: rigorous deep-dive (dense; pair with Shulman).**

- **[The Edges of Our Universe](../../readings/space-governance/ord-edges-of-our-universe.pdf)** (Toby Ord).
  Maps the affectable universe — the physical bound on everything the cause is about.
  Vivid, quantitative, and short on prose. **Role: data/evidence, scale anchor.**

- **[Eternity in Six Hours](../../readings/space-governance/armstrong-sandberg-eternity-in-six-hours.pdf)** (Armstrong & Sandberg).
  The feasibility keystone: intergalactic colonization via self-replicating probes is
  shown to be technically possible with foreseeable tech. Establishes the premise the
  skeptics attack. **Role: rigorous deep-dive (long PDF — skim/excerpt).**

- **[Cosmic Anarchy and Its Consequences](../../readings/linked/cosmic-anarchy-and-its-consequences.md)** (Kevin Kohler).
  Turns the enforcement crux into a crisp thesis: light-speed latency caps the size of any
  government, so a galactic state is impossible and anarchy is the default cosmic order —
  100+ billion independent star systems. The IR-theory lens on the whole cluster. **Role:
  vivid mechanism / framing.**

- **[A conversation with Charlie Stross on space colonization](../../readings/space-governance/beckstead-stross-space-colonization.md)** (Beckstead & Stross).
  The best-articulated feasibility skeptic in interview form: radiation, minimum viable
  civilization (~hundreds of thousands of people), no economic motive — but "feasible
  given uploads or AI," which routes the debate back to AI. **Role: accessible skeptic.**

- **[Zach Weinersmith on becoming a "space bastard"](../../readings/linked/80k-zach-weinersmith-space-settlement.md)** (80k podcast).
  A researcher who set out pro-settlement and came out skeptical — vivid, funny, and
  covers the governance/law problems of near-term settlement for a general audience.
  **Role: accessible narrative / media diversity (audio).**

- **[The Outer Space Treaty](../../readings/linked/outer-space-treaty-intro.md)** (UNOOSA).
  The primary source: no national appropriation, space free for all, the actual legal
  status quo everything else argues around. **Role: primary-source grounding.**

- **[The Peaks of Eternal Light: lunar property](../../readings/space-governance/peaks-of-eternal-light-moon-property.pdf)** (Elvis & Milligan).
  A concrete, near-term, *already-here* lock-in case — a handful of permanently-lit lunar
  sites that first-movers could effectively monopolize. Makes "govern it before it's
  locked in" tangible rather than cosmic. **Role: concrete case study / discussion seed.**

- **[Anders Sandberg on war in space and the best things possible](../../readings/space-governance/sandberg-80k-war-in-space-best-things.md)** (80k podcast).
  Accessible, wide-ranging on defense-dominance in space, whether conflict is likely, and
  the best futures physically achievable — pairs the awe of tradition 2 with the security
  of tradition 6. **Role: accessible on-ramp / two-voice balance (audio).**

## Conspicuously missing

- **Bostrom's *Astronomical Waste* itself.** The foundational text for the entire "stakes
  are astronomical, delay is costly" premise sits in the macrostrategy cluster
  (`readings/macrostrategy/astronomical-waste.md`), not here. The space cluster references
  it constantly but never includes it — it should at least cross-link, arguably be a core
  reading.
- **A humanist / awe opener with a named human.** Nothing like Sagan's *Pale Blue Dot* or
  a Carl Sagan / Kim Stanley Robinson passage — the cluster is analytic and treaty-heavy;
  per the Handbook's "open with awe" virtue it lacks an emotional on-ramp.
- **The original pro-settlement vision.** Only Stross's rebuttal *High Frontier Redux* is
  present; Gerard K. O'Neill's *The High Frontier* (space habitats, the actual case being
  rebutted) is absent, so students hear the debunk without the claim.
- **A decolonial / Global South voice.** The collection is almost entirely Anglo-American.
  Missing: scholarship on "space colonialism" and equity (e.g. Danielle Wood on space
  sustainability & the Global South, or Deganit Paikowsky / non-Western space-law
  scholars). The "whose voices govern the commons" concern is raised by Ezell but never
  voiced *by* a Global South author.
- **The Artemis Accords vs. China–Russia ILRS split, analyzed.** We have NASA's own
  Accords page and a UNSC piece, but not a critical analysis of the emerging two-bloc lunar
  governance order — the single most concrete great-power space-governance flashpoint.
- **Space × AI lock-in, made explicit.** Multiple readings punt to "given advanced AI…"
  but no piece directly connects space governance to AI value lock-in / singleton dynamics
  (Forethought's *AGI and Lock-in* lives in another cluster). This is the bridge the whole
  AGI-era framing needs.
- **Whose welfare fills space — digital minds / directed panspermia.** The Shulman–Pearce
  hedonium exchange is only in a comment thread; missing is a dedicated piece on spreading
  digital minds or on directed-panspermia ethics (spreading life = spreading wild-animal
  suffering, e.g. Ng / Hauskeller), which is the AI-welfare bridge.
- **A near-term space-economics voice.** No economist on the actual commercial space
  economy and its governance (e.g. Matthew Weinzierl's "Space, the Final Economic
  Frontier") — the cluster jumps from cosmic stakes to treaties with no market layer.
- **SETI / METI contact governance.** Gestured at (downloading alien AI, grabby aliens)
  but no dedicated reading on whether/how to govern messaging or responding to
  extraterrestrial contact.
- **Planetary protection / contamination governance (COSPAR).** The one existing
  near-term, technical governance regime with real teeth — absent entirely.
