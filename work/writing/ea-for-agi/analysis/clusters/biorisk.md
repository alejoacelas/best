# Cluster map: biorisk

## What this cluster covers

Global catastrophic biological risk — engineered pandemics as the near-term
extinction-scale threat, and how AI changes the picture. The collection runs on four
sub-threads: (1) the **threat model** — why a deliberately-released engineered pathogen
could exceed any natural one, and whether that's imminent or overstated; (2) the
**AIxBio question** — do LLMs and biological design tools actually uplift a bad actor,
and by how much, measured now; (3) the **defense portfolio** — the concrete,
pathogen-agnostic megaprojects (metagenomic early warning, far-UVC, super-PPE, DNA
synthesis screening, refuges) that make civilization robust regardless of the pathogen;
and (4) **governance, norms, and field-building** — information hazards, the toothless
Biological Weapons Convention, dual-use research policy, and how a newcomer enters the
field. The center of gravity is the MIT/SecureBio "delay, detect, defend" program;
almost everything else either feeds it, funds it, or disputes its urgency.

## Intellectual traditions and where they disagree

Seven distinct voices, with two live disagreements running through them.

- **The Esvelt / SecureBio "delay-detect-defend" school** (`delay-detect-defend`,
  `esvelt-mitigating-catastrophic-biorisks`, `global-nucleic-acid-observatory`,
  `securebio-ai-team-biorisk-evaluations`, linked `nao-*`, `blueprint-far-uvc`). Threat
  model: within a decade tens of thousands can single-handedly ignite a pandemic, so
  the answer is technical defense plus strict information-hazard discipline (Esvelt
  won't name the candidate viruses). The organizing frame the rest of the cluster
  orbits.
- **EA cause-prioritization / longtermism** (`80k-preventing-catastrophic-pandemics`,
  `concrete-biosecurity-projects`, `10-big-projects-reducing-bio-x-risk`,
  `five-tractable-biosecurity-projects`, linked `openphil-biosecurity-cause-report`,
  `founders-pledge-gcbr-report`, `xpt-results-biorisk`). ITN analysis, scalable
  megaprojects, "biosecurity is ~10–100x less important than AI but far more
  tractable" (Snyder-Beattie & Alley say so explicitly).
- **The Johns Hopkins CHS / NTI / Bipartisan-Commission policy establishment** (linked
  `apollo-program-for-biodefense`, `nti-preventing-gcbr`, `chs-*`,
  `gcsp-securing-civilisation-pandemics`). All-hazards public-health framing, tabletop
  exercises, tech-priority roadmaps. Overlaps heavily with EA but predates and outsizes it.
- **AIxBio security / governance studies** (`cset-ai-and-biorisk-explainer`,
  `sandbrink-ai-biological-misuse`, `csr-aixbio-landscape`, `rand-operational-risks-*`,
  linked `nsceb-aixbio-policy-options`, `bulletin-ai-biological-design-regulation`).
  Sandbrink's LLM-vs-BDT distinction (lowers barriers vs. raises the ceiling) is the
  load-bearing concept; CSET and RAND are the sober "don't over-index on AI" wing.
- **The skeptics / "germy paradox"** (linked `eukaryote-germy-paradox-intro`,
  `eukaryote-book-review-barriers`, `bulletin-myths-realities-synthetic-bioweapons`,
  `cnas-aum-shinrikyo`, `warontherocks-biological-weapons-taboo`). Tacit knowledge and
  organizational failure — not information — have historically stopped bioweapons; Aum
  Shinrikyo had money and scientists and still failed.
- **Techno-optimist / d/acc** (`vitalik-my-techno-optimism-dacc`, linked
  `gans-andreessen-techno-optimism`). Defensive acceleration: out-build the offense with
  better air, vaccines, and detection rather than restrict.
- **Frontier labs, self-reporting** (linked `anthropic-activating-asl3`,
  `anthropic-bio-report`, `openai-bio-early-warning-system`, `time-*-bio-risk`).

**Disagreement 1 — how much does AI uplift biorisk *right now*?** RAND's red-team found
"no statistically significant difference" (`rand-operational-risks-ai-bio-attacks`) and
CSET says AI isn't needed for biorisk at all; Esvelt, Sandbrink, the frontier labs
(Anthropic activating ASL-3), and the MIT classroom exercise say the trajectory is
alarming. `state-of-bio-uplift-research-2026` and `epoch-biorisk-evaluations-critique`
adjudicate on current evidence and ask whether the evals even measure the real thing.

**Disagreement 2 — are bioweapons hard?** The germy-paradox skeptics (Ben
Ouagrham-Gormley, Georgia Ray, Lentzos/Marris) say the tacit-knowledge barrier is real
and durable; the delay-detect-defend school says AI is precisely what dissolves that
barrier, so the historical comfort is obsolete. This is the cluster's deepest fault line
and the natural spine of a discussion session.

A third, quieter tension: **natural vs. engineered framing** — `biodefense-blind-spot-washington`
and linked `statnews-mother-nature-not-ultimate-bioterrorist` argue institutions built
for natural outbreaks are blind to deliberate, surveillance-evading design.

## Conspicuously missing

- **A non-Western / Global South voice.** The reading list admits its US-Berkeley bias
  and nothing corrects it. No African, Indian, or Chinese perspective on pandemic
  preparedness or on what AGI-bio means for countries without a Strategic National
  Stockpile. Concrete targets: Shruti Rajagopalan on India's COVID response (an existing
  80k episode, uncollected); an African CDC / IFBA voice on LMIC biosecurity.
- **A vivid named-hero opener, Handbook-style.** There's no bio equivalent of "500
  Million, But Not a Single One More" — smallpox eradication as awe, or a Pardis Sabeti
  Ebola-genomics field narrative (the Sabeti/Sentinel podcast is linked but not the
  narrative telling). The cluster opens on threat, never on triumph.
- **A full-throated critic of the EA/securitization framing itself.** Filippa Lentzos,
  Claire Marris, and Kathleen Vogel argue that the securitized "GCBR" frame overstates
  risk and militarizes the life sciences. Only the "myths" piece is gestured at; the
  collection lacks the sociology-of-science critique that a criticism session needs.
- **The 2011–12 H5N1 gain-of-function controversy as a two-sided narrative.**
  Fouchier/Kawaoka, the NSABB decision, Inglesby–Relman vs. the pro-publication camp —
  the founding dual-use debate. `newyorker-deadliest-virus` is linked but the primary
  pro/con exchange (`fhi-`, `nti-between-publishing-and-perishing`) isn't foregrounded
  as a debate.
- **The COVID-19 lab-leak / origins debate.** The most politically salient biosecurity
  event of the era and a live case in GOF governance — entirely absent. Needs at least
  one careful, non-partisan treatment.
- **Shown-arithmetic cost-effectiveness.** Millett & Snyder-Beattie's "Existential Risk
  and Cost-Effective Biosecurity" (the actual $/life-year figure) is named in the
  reading list but uncollected. The Handbook loves the estimate-in-a-footnote move; bio
  currently has none.
- **AI-as-actor, not just AI-as-tool.** Every AIxBio reading treats AI as something that
  uplifts a *human* bad actor. For an AGI course, the missing piece is a misaligned or
  autonomous AI *itself* pursuing a bio-pathway — the scenario that connects this cluster
  to AI safety proper.
- **A benefits-side / cost-of-caution voice from within bioscience.** The collection is
  overwhelmingly risk-framed. Missing: a synthetic-biology researcher arguing that
  screening mandates and infohazard norms carry large real costs to beneficial biotech
  (a progress-studies rebuttal sharper than Vitalik's).
- **A playable tabletop exercise.** Clade X and Event 201 are referenced as videos but
  no scenario is collected in a form a cohort could actually run — the obvious
  participatory exercise for this cluster.
- **DNA-synthesis-screening governance primary source.** IBBIS (International Biosecurity
  and Biosafety Initiative for Science) and the NTI common-mechanism work — the concrete
  institution behind "screen DNA synthesis." `securing-benchtop-dna-synthesizers` and
  `asimov-dna-screening` are linked but the governing body itself is missing.
