# Cluster map: power concentration & democracy

## What this cluster covers

This is the "who ends up holding the power once AI can do the work" cluster. It runs
from the mechanisms by which advanced AI could let a small group seize or quietly
accumulate unprecedented control (coups, corporate consolidation, an economy that no
longer needs its citizens), through the *durability* problem (why such an outcome
could lock in for millennia rather than self-correcting), to the defensive agenda
(democratic reform, decentralization, benefit-sharing, and institutions that survive
the transition). Six recurring sub-threads:

1. **AI-enabled coups / power grabs** — deliberate, fast seizure by a human or small
   group using singularly-loyal or exclusively-held AI (Davidson, secretly-loyal AIs,
   Luttwak, "human takeover worse than AI takeover").
2. **Gradual disempowerment** — emergent, slow erosion of human influence over
   economy, culture, and state, with no coordinated betrayal (Kulveit et al.,
   Duvenaud, Christiano's capital accumulation).
3. **The intelligence curse** — the economic engine of concentration: once labor is
   automated, powerful actors lose the incentive to invest in people (Drago/Laine,
   pyramid replacement).
4. **Lock-in & stable totalitarianism** — the durability multiplier: why AI removes
   the historical forces (succession, value drift, resistance) that ended past
   regimes (Finnveden/Riedel/Shulman lineage, Lamerton, Caplan, 80k profiles).
5. **State transformation** — how the *shape* of the state itself changes, between a
   "despotic Leviathan" and an "absent Leviathan" (Hammond's institutional economics).
6. **The defensive agenda** — decentralization and d/acc (Buterin), plurality and
   digital democracy (Tang, Weyl, Ovadya), democratic-innovation experiments,
   institutional reform, and concrete anti-concentration mechanisms (windfall clause,
   MAGIC, compute governance, A Narrow Path).

## Intellectual traditions and where they disagree

- **Longtermist x-risk / EA governance** (Bostrom, MacAskill, Ord, Finnveden, Shulman,
  Riedel, Forethought/Davidson, Hadshar, 80k, GovAI). Owns the lock-in and coups
  framing; treats durable value lock-in as a distinct existential catastrophe.
- **Gradual-disempowerment / multi-agent systems** (Kulveit, Ammann, Duvenaud,
  Krueger, Christiano). Reframes the risk as emergent and systemic — no villain
  required — which puts it partly at odds with the coup framing's focus on a
  power-seeker.
- **Institutional-economics / state-capacity techno-optimism** (Hammond / *Second
  Best*). Neither doomer nor accelerationist; asks how Coasean transaction-cost shocks
  reshape institutions, and argues *robust state capacity* is itself a check on
  private concentration — a stance the decentralizers distrust.
- **Crypto / d/acc / Plurality** (Buterin, Tang, Weyl, RadicalxChange, Ovadya). The
  optimistic, build-the-alternative camp: decentralized and defensive technology,
  mechanism design (quadratic voting, bridging-based ranking, platform democracy) as
  the antidote to centralization.
- **Democracy scholarship / political science** (*Journal of Democracy*, Cambridge
  Elements, Garfinkel). Empirical grounding on backsliding and on whether democracy is
  even the historical default — a sober counterweight to speculative AI scenarios.
- **Intelligence-curse / young-writer economics** (Drago, Laine). Vivid,
  resource-curse-analogy popularization aimed at a broad audience.
- **Geopolitics / great-power & compute** (Amodei, Sarah Paine, CSET, CNAS). Frames
  concentration as a race and a chips story; internally split on whether the
  "arms race" is real (Amodei's export-control hawkishness vs. arms-race skeptics).

**The load-bearing disagreements a curriculum should stage:**

- **Power-seeking vs. emergent** — is the core risk a deliberate grab or an
  incentive-driven drift? Hadshar's 2x2 makes this the central axis.
- **Human takeover vs. AI takeover** — Forethought argues a human seizing power via AI
  could be *worse* than misaligned-AI takeover; cuts against the standard alignment
  hierarchy.
- **Centralize vs. decentralize AGI development** — the cluster's sharpest fault line.
  One-project / MAGIC / soft-nationalization / export-controls (concentrate to control
  the race) vs. Buterin's d/acc and open-source (distribute to prevent capture). Each
  side's solution is the other side's nightmare.
- **Despotic vs. absent Leviathan** — does AI produce an over-mighty surveillance
  state or a hollowed-out one displaced by private governance? (Hammond).
- **Is the worry overblown?** — Caplan takes totalitarianism seriously; Cowen's
  "paradox of libertarianism," the arms-race skeptics, and Hanson's
  decentralized-evolution alternative to MacAskill's lock-in push back that power
  stays distributed by default.

## Standout readings

Anchors and on-ramps:

- [80k: Extreme power concentration](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/80k-extreme-power-concentration.md)
  — the cause-area anchor: scenario → mechanism → harm → *five counterarguments* →
  what to do. **Role: anchor essay / week spine.**
- [Rose Hadshar: Sense-making about extreme power concentration](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/sense-making-extreme-power-concentration.md)
  — the disambiguating map (AI-vs-human × power-seeking-vs-emergent; concentration as
  the undermining of checks and balances). Short, clarifying. **Role: conceptual
  orientation, read early.**
- [The Intelligence Curse](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/intelligence-curse-original.md)
  — vivid resource-curse analogy: automate labor and elites stop investing in people.
  Accessible, quotable, names a mechanism. **Role: accessible on-ramp.**

Rigorous deep-dives:

- [Gradual Disempowerment (Kulveit et al.)](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/gradual-disempowerment-full.md)
  — the seminal systemic-risk paper; six claims about economy/culture/state eroding in
  mutually reinforcing loops with no coordinated betrayal. **Role: rigorous deep-dive
  (assign an excerpt + the accessible [Duvenaud 80k interview](../../readings/linked/80k-david-duvenaud-gradual-disempowerment.md)).**
- [AXRP: Tom Davidson on AI-enabled coups](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/axrp-tom-davidson-ai-enabled-coups.md)
  — the definitive mechanism walkthrough of coups via singularly-loyal / exclusively-held
  AI. Long; pair with the short [80k write-up](../../readings/linked/80k-tom-davidson-power-grabs.md)
  as the core slot. **Role: rigorous deep-dive on the coups sub-thread.**
- [Hammond: AI and Leviathan, Part I](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/hammond-ai-and-leviathan-part-i.md)
  — best institutional-economics voice; the x-ray-glasses metaphor and the despotic-vs-absent
  Leviathan frame, grounded in the printing-press/English-Civil-War analogy. **Role:
  the "how the state itself changes" reading; distinct tradition.**

Durability / lock-in:

- [What is Lock-In?](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/what-is-lock-in.md)
  + [Lock-In Threat Models](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/lock-in-threat-models.md)
  (Lamerton) — clean primer synthesizing Bostrom/MacAskill/Finnveden/Riedel/Hanson/Christiano
  and enumerating concrete routes. **Role: the lock-in concept primer + literature map.**
- [80k: Stable totalitarianism](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/stable-totalitarianism-an-overview.md)
  — names the 20th-century horrors, then argues why AI could make a regime *permanent*.
  High wow-density, honestly hedged on probability. **Role: emotional-stakes reading
  for the durability thread.**
- [Karnofsky: lock-in as the weak point](../../readings/linked/karnofsky-lock-in-weak-point.md)
  — accessible "most important century" framing of why the transition is the moment
  that could get frozen. **Role: accessible on-ramp to lock-in.**

Provocations and the defensive agenda:

- [Forethought: Human takeover might be worse than AI takeover](../../readings/linked/human-takeover-worse-than-ai-takeover.md)
  — the counterintuitive reframe that inverts the usual alignment priority. **Role:
  best discussion-starter / steelman fodder.**
- [Buterin: d/acc, one year later](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/vitalik-dacc-one-year-later.md)
  (or the [80k transcript](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/vitalik-defensive-acceleration-transcript.md))
  — the decentralization counter-thesis: defensive, distributed tech instead of one
  controlling authority. **Role: the opposing solution paradigm; two-voice pairing
  against centralize-to-control proposals.**
- [Audrey Tang: Taiwan's digital democracy](../../readings/linked/80k-audrey-tang-taiwan-digital-democracy.md)
  — the concrete, non-Western positive existence proof: AI/mechanism design used to
  *strengthen* collective self-government. **Role: positive vision / hope beat.**
- [The Windfall Clause](../../readings/power-concentration-democracy/windfall-clause.md)
  — a concrete, legally-grounded anti-concentration mechanism (pre-commit to share
  windfall profits). **Role: "boring tractability" solution reading.**
- [Garfinkel: Is democracy a fad?](../../readings/linked/is-democracy-a-fad.md)
  — argues democracy may be a contingent product of industrial-era conditions AI could
  reverse; sober grounding for why "democracy preservation" is a live cause. **Role:
  the political-science reality check.**

## What's conspicuously missing

- **The primary lock-in report.** Finnveden, Riedel & Shulman's *AGI and Lock-In*
  (2022) is cited by nearly every lock-in piece here but is not in the collection as
  its own file — the load-bearing source is absent.
- **A pointed rebuttal of Gradual Disempowerment.** The paper admits no one has a plan;
  the cluster has no piece arguing the mechanism is wrong (the "markets and
  democratic feedback will adapt, this is neo-Luddism" critique). Hanson's
  decentralized-evolution view and a direct GD critique both belong here.
- **A Global South / non-Western analysis of concentration** beyond Tang's Taiwan case
  — what AGI power concentration means for developing economies whose leverage was
  cheap labor (an Indian, African, or Latin American governance voice; the intelligence
  curse *between* countries, not just within them).
- **China as the live authoritarian-AI existence proof.** The collection has
  surveillance-spread (Carnegie) and China's *external* AI strategy, but nothing
  deep on the CCP's domestic AI-governance / social-credit apparatus as the concrete
  model of what "digital totalitarianism" already looks like.
- **The Plurality / Radical Markets primary text.** Weyl and Tang's agenda appears only
  refracted through Buterin and a "contra Weyl" post; the actual *Plurality* book or a
  Weyl primary source is missing, as is a serious treatment of quadratic funding/voting
  beyond the popular-press Colorado piece.
- **The redistribution / UBI answer to the intelligence curse.** The economic-solutions
  side is thin: no dedicated reading on UBI, sovereign-wealth / citizen-dividend
  models, or post-labor distributive politics as the antidote (only the corporate
  windfall clause).
- **The critical / left tech-power tradition.** Kate Crawford, Meredith Whittaker,
  Amba Kak / AI Now's structural critique of corporate AI power is present only as one
  landscape report; the "power" scholarship outside the EA/rationalist orbit is
  underweighted.
- **AI rights as a check on concentration.** MacAskill's menu lists "economic and
  political rights of AIs," and stable-totalitarianism gestures at "digital
  totalitarianism," but no reading develops AI legal/economic personhood as either a
  diffusing check or a new concentration vector.
- **A vivid, human-centered narrative.** Per the handbook's wow-density-per-named-human
  virtue, the cluster is framework-heavy and story-poor. It needs one concrete
  scenario or first-person narrative dramatizing life under AI-enabled concentration
  (the AI-CEO-quiet-takeover scenario is the closest; a more human, less strategic
  telling is missing).
- **A constitutional / checks-and-balances design proposal specific to AI.** Hadshar
  frames concentration as eroded checks and balances, but no reading proposes concrete
  constitutional or oversight architecture (AI in the separation of powers, on-chip
  governance of state AI) to rebuild them.
