# Cluster map: AI rights & the post-AGI economy

*Cartographer's map of the `ai-rights-economics` cluster for the "EA in the age of AGI"
curriculum. Curated readings live in [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/);
linked-pile additions in [../../readings/linked/](../../readings/linked/).*

## What this cluster covers

Two tightly-braided questions about a world where AI can do most economic work: **what
happens to humans** (wages, jobs, capital, power) and **what happens to the AIs**
(do they get contracts, property, votes, freedom — and what follows if they do). The
economics half runs from a labor-optimist pole (comparative advantage keeps humans
well-paid) to a labor-pessimist pole (wages fall below subsistence; capital and
compute become the only things that matter), with an explosive-growth debate underneath
about how fast and how discontinuous the transition is. The rights half runs from
AI-rights-as-human-safety (give AIs property/contract rights so trade beats war) through
AI-rights-as-welfare (subservient minds are a moral wrong) to AI-rights-as-existential-risk
(granting rights is a voluntary path to human replacement). **Gradual disempowerment** is
the hinge that joins them: humans stay empowered because societal systems need us, and
both wage-collapse and rights-granting sever that need.

Main sub-threads:
- **Comparative-advantage debate** — does Ricardo save human wages when AI is better at everything?
- **Capital & power entrenchment** — the "intelligence curse," static society, end of social mobility.
- **Explosive growth** — endogenous growth theory vs. bottlenecks/diminishing-returns skepticism.
- **Post-labor economic models** — Age of Em subsistence wages, Korinek–Suh task automation, capital in the long run.
- **Wealth distribution mechanisms** — windfall clause, UBI, sharing AI's future wealth.
- **AI rights as safety / as welfare / as replacement risk** — legal personhood, autonomy, "willing servitude."
- **Political economy & governance** — public-choice reshuffling, AI-agent institutions, disempowerment feedback loops.

## Traditions & voices, and where they disagree

- **EA/rationalist growth economists** (Tom Davidson, Ege Erdil/Epoch, Anton Korinek, Philip Trammell, Carl Shulman) — formal models and forecasts of automation, wages, and >30%/yr growth. Internally split: Epoch/Davidson give explosive growth real probability mass; the models themselves (Korinek–Suh) show wages can rise *or* collapse on a knife-edge assumption.
- **Mainstream-econ optimists** (Noah Smith, Roon) — comparative advantage and opportunity cost keep humans employed and, in a rich society, well-paid. Directly rebutted by **Steve Newman** and **Tyler Cowen** ("comparative advantage is overrated"; horses went to the glue factory), and partially conceded by the Korinek–Suh model Smith himself cites.
- **The "intelligence curse" / political-economy pessimists** (Rudolf Laine, Luke Drago, Sam Hammond) — when humans stop being economically necessary, states and firms lose the incentive to care about them; power freezes. Hammond is the most institutionalist (public choice, regime change, deregulation); Laine/Drago the most values-driven (human ambition, dynamism). **Zvi Mowshowitz** contests Hammond point-by-point.
- **Gradual-disempowerment school** (Jan Kulveit & ACS, David Duvenaud, Rose Hadshar, 80,000 Hours) — systemic, non-takeover existential risk from incremental competitive displacement. Explicitly frames itself *against* the classic sudden-takeover risk model.
- **AI-rights-as-safety legal scholars** (Peter Salib & Simon Goldstein, Gillian Hadfield, Matthew Barnett) — extend corporate-style private-law rights to AIs so gains-from-trade produce peace. Contract-theory lineage (Hadfield-Menell & Hadfield on incomplete contracting).
- **AI-rights-as-welfare / autonomy** (Adam Bales, Matthew Barnett's moral argument, Schwitzgebel & Garza via Caviola, "we need an AI rights movement," "silicon slavery") — designing minds to be servile is itself wrong; peaceful human disempowerment may be acceptable or good. This pole collides head-on with —
- **AI-rights-as-replacement-risk** (Lucius Caviola) — granting self-determining digital minds rights is a *voluntary* path to human disempowerment, even absent misalignment. Caviola and Barnett are the sharpest disagreement in the cluster: same mechanism, opposite valence.
- **Skeptics of the whole frame** (David Thorstad on the singularity hypothesis; Maxwell Tabarrok "AGI will not make labor worthless") — explosive growth and wage-collapse are under-argued; ideas get harder to find, bottlenecks bind.
- **Foundational borrowings** — Hayek (distributed knowledge / why prices persist), Robin Hanson (Age of Em, AI and economic growth), Nietzsche (techno-optimism's philosophical root, via Hammond).

The deepest fault lines: (1) is human wage-collapse an economic theorem or a political choice; (2) is AI rights the intervention that *saves* humans or the mechanism that *replaces* them; (3) is the danger sudden takeover or gradual, incentive-driven drift.

## Standout readings

Economics of the transition:
- [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/capital-agi-and-human-ambition.md](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/capital-agi-and-human-ambition.md) — **Rudolf Laine.** The cluster's anchor essay: capital matters *more* after AGI, money finally buys talent, social mobility ends. Vivid, quotable, emotionally load-bearing. *Role: anchor / vivid narrative.*
- [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/plentiful-high-paying-jobs-age-of-ai.md](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/plentiful-high-paying-jobs-age-of-ai.md) — **Noah Smith.** The clearest, most accessible statement of the comparative-advantage optimist case. *Role: accessible on-ramp; head of a debate pair.*
- [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/comparative-advantage-strong-ai.md](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/comparative-advantage-strong-ai.md) — **Steve Newman.** The direct rebuttal — the model's hidden assumptions (energy competition, adaptation limits, transaction cost) break under strong AI. *Role: best critic; the other head of the debate pair. Assign together with Smith.*
- [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/scenarios-for-the-transition-to-agi.md](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/scenarios-for-the-transition-to-agi.md) — **Korinek & Suh.** The rigorous formal model that adjudicates the Smith–Newman fight: wages rise forever or collapse depending on whether human task-complexity has an infinite tail. *Role: rigorous deep-dive / synthesis.*
- [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/book-review-age-of-em.md](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/book-review-age-of-em.md) — **Scott Alexander on Hanson.** The classic thought experiment: mass-copyable digital labor drives wages to subsistence. Long but hugely readable; the most concrete picture of a post-labor economy. *Role: vivid narrative / imaginative on-ramp to the pessimist economics.*
- [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/explosive-growth-review-of-arguments.md](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/explosive-growth-review-of-arguments.md) — **Erdil & Besiroglu (Epoch).** The reference survey — the case for explosive growth plus nine counterarguments, laid out even-handedly. *Role: balanced survey / data-and-evidence.*
- [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/against-the-singularity-hypothesis.md](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/against-the-singularity-hypothesis.md) — **David Thorstad (GPI).** The skeptic: extraordinary growth claims are thin; ideas get harder to find; bottlenecks and physics bind. *Role: best critic of the whole explosive-growth premise.*

Capital, power, and gradual disempowerment:
- [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/intelligence-curse-essay-series.md](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/intelligence-curse-essay-series.md) — **Drago & Laine.** The flagship post-AGI political-economy thesis, and the one reading with a concrete *agenda* (avert / diffuse / democratize). *Role: anchor for the political-economy thread + solutions.*
- [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/gradual-disempowerment.md](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/gradual-disempowerment.md) — **Kulveit et al.** The rigorous statement of systemic, non-takeover existential risk; the hinge between the economics and rights halves. *Role: rigorous anchor.* Pair with —
- [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/80k-gradual-disempowerment-profile.md](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/80k-gradual-disempowerment-profile.md) — **80,000 Hours.** Career-framed, six-claim summary with counterarguments and "what you can do." *Role: accessible on-ramp / curriculum-ready version of the above.*
- [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/ninety-five-theses-on-ai.md](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/ninety-five-theses-on-ai.md) — **Sam Hammond.** Ninety-five compressed provocations on AI political economy, regime change, and public choice; enormous surface area for discussion. *Role: breadth-generator / debate seed.* Pair with **Zvi's response** ([../../readings/linked/zvi-response-to-95-theses.md](../../readings/linked/zvi-response-to-95-theses.md)).

AI rights:
- [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/ai-rights-for-human-safety.md](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/ai-rights-for-human-safety.md) — **Salib & Goldstein.** The flagship AI-rights-*as-safety* argument: game-theoretic prisoner's dilemma solved by giving AIs contract/property/tort rights. Short, sharp, counterintuitive. *Role: anchor for the rights thread.*
- [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/when-digital-minds-demand-freedom.md](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/when-digital-minds-demand-freedom.md) — **Lucius Caviola.** The rights-*as-replacement-risk* case: self-determining digital minds could disempower us *voluntarily and without misalignment*. The most systematic treatment. *Role: rigorous deep-dive / the unsettling counterpoint.*
- [../../readings/linked/moral-argument-for-giving-ais-autonomy.md](../../readings/linked/moral-argument-for-giving-ais-autonomy.md) — **Matthew Barnett.** The pro-autonomy provocation: preventing human disempowerment is not synonymous with a good future; peaceful hand-off may be acceptable. *Role: best critic of the human-centric default; sharpest value-disagreement with Caviola.*
- [../../readings/ai-rights-economics/windfall-clause.md](../../readings/ai-rights-economics/windfall-clause.md) — **Cullen O'Keefe (GovAI).** The canonical concrete wealth-distribution proposal — an ex-ante commitment to share windfall AI profits. *Role: solutions / policy exemplar.*

## Conspicuously missing

Distribution & policy mechanisms:
- **Daron Acemoglu & Simon Johnson, *Power and Progress*** (and Acemoglu's skeptical macro estimate that AI adds ~1% to GDP) — the single biggest absence; the mainstream-economics "who controls the technology" political-economy voice, and the leading growth-skeptic with academic weight.
- **UBI empirics** — the cluster asserts UBI as "the default solution" but has no reading on actual pilots (OpenResearch/Altman's basic-income study, GiveDirectly's Kenya UBI), nor a critique of UBI-as-answer.
- **Sam Altman, "Moore's Law for Everything"** and other equity-stake / sovereign-wealth-fund / "share the wealth" proposals (Noema's "share AI's future wealth" is linked but under-developed) — a positive-abundance distribution vision to sit beside the windfall clause.

Labor & the present:
- **Empirical present-day labor-market impact** — a real-deployment study (Anthropic Economic Index; Brynjolfsson–Li–Raymond "Generative AI at Work" RCT) to ground the speculative wage debates in data. "GPTs are GPTs" is linked but is a 2023 exposure estimate, not measured impact.
- **Organized-labor / worker voice** — no union, labor-movement, or worker-organizing perspective; the whole cluster is written by economists and technologists *about* workers.
- **Care economy / social-reproduction and feminist economics** angle on which work remains and why "human jobs" may be gendered and undervalued.

Rights & legal theory:
- **The anti-rights position in its own words** — Joanna Bryson's "Robots Should Be Slaves" is cited only secondhand (via Caviola). Assign the actual argument that AI rights are a category error.
- **AI-welfare-first scholarship** — Jeff Sebo & Robert Long on moral status / "AI systems by 2030," Schwitzgebel & Garza's original "designing AI with rights" paper (only cited), the NYU Center for Mind, Ethics, and Policy. The cluster leans safety-first; the welfare-first case is thin.
- **Classic legal-personhood scholarship** — Lawrence Solum's foundational "Legal Personhood for Artificial Intelligences" and the corporate-personhood analogy done at length (Future of Citizenship pieces are linked but lightweight).

Perspectives & scope:
- **Non-Western / Global South voice** — the entire cluster is US/UK rationalist-EA-economist. Missing: an Indian or African economist on AI, automation, and *development* (does AI close the path to development that industrialization offered?); ILO / World Bank work on AI and developing-country labor. MacAskill's brief explicitly asks for this ("organizations in the global health space… how they plan to adapt").
- **China / state-capitalist model** of managing mass labor displacement — a non-liberal-democratic political economy of the transition.
- **s-risk from cheap copyable minds** — the economics of mass-produced digital labor implies astronomical digital suffering (Sentience Institute, Center on Long-Term Risk); touched by Age of Em but no dedicated treatment.
- **Humanistic "post-work / meaning" reading** — Keynes's "Economic Possibilities for our Grandchildren" or John Danaher's *Automation and Utopia*; the values/meaning question of a world without human work, which the brief flags as central ("values and epistemic habits we'll need").
