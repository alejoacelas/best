I recently argued that it’s [time for our community to be more ambitious](https://80000hours.org/2021/11/growth-of-effective-altruism/).

And when it comes to [preventing pandemics](https://80000hours.org/problem-profiles/global-catastrophic-biological-risks/), it’s starting to happen.

Andrew Snyder-Beattie is a programme officer at [Open Philanthropy](https://www.openphilanthropy.org/), a foundation which has more than $1 billion available to fund big pandemic-prevent projects. He and Ethan Alley (co-CEO at [Alvea](https://www.alvea.bio/)) recently wrote an exciting [list of projects](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/u5JesqQ3jdLENXBtB/concrete-biosecurity-projects-some-of-which-could-be-big-1) they’d like to see get founded, including:

  * An international early detection centre
  * Actually good PPE
  * Rapid and broad-spectrum antivirals and vaccines
  * A bioweapons watchdog
  * Self-sterilising buildings
  * Refuges



One of these ideas is already happening. Alvea aims to produce a cheap, flexible vaccine platform using a new type of vaccine (DNA vaccines), starting with an Omicron-specific shot. In two months, they [hired 35 people and started preclinical trials](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/67awq5ozeYSjsYchk/announcing-alvea-an-ea-covid-vaccine-project).

The above are technical solutions, which make it possible for a relatively small number of people to make a significant difference to the problem. But policy change is also an important angle.

[Guarding Against Pandemics](https://www.againstpandemics.org/) was an effort to lobby the US government for $30 billion in funding for pandemic prevention. Unfortunately the relevant bill didn’t pass, but the sum at stake made it [clearly worth trying](https://80000hours.org/articles/expected-value/).

Efforts in the UK might be more successful — the country is currently refreshing its biosecurity policy.
