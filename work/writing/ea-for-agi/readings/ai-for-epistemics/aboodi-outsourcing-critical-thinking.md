# The Worrisome Potential of Outsourcing Critical Thinking to Artificial Intelligence

**Ron Aboodi** — *Educational Theory* 75(4), pp. 626–645, 2025.
DOI: [10.1111/edth.70037](https://doi.org/10.1111/edth.70037)
Open-access copies: [PhilArchive record](https://philarchive.org/rec/ABOTWP) ·
[Wiley (open access)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/edth.70037)

## Abstract

> As Artificial Intelligence (AI) keeps advancing, Generation Alpha and future
> generations are more likely to cope with situations that call for critical thinking
> by turning to AI and relying on its guidance without sufficient critical thinking.
> The author defends this worry and argues that it calls for educational reforms that
> would be designed mainly to (a) motivate students to think critically about AI
> applications and the justifiability of their deployment, as well as (b) cultivate
> the skills, knowledge, and dispositions that will help them do so. Furthermore, the
> author argues that these educational aims will remain important in the distant
> future no matter how far AI advances, even merely on outcome-based grounds.

## Why it is in this cluster

The canonical statement of the deskilling objection to the whole
"AI for better reasoning" bet: habitual reliance on AI guidance erodes the very
critical-thinking capacities the opportunity framing claims to augment, and the
erosion compounds across generations. Aboodi argues the worry survives even arbitrary
AI progress — even on purely outcome-based grounds, humans must retain the capacity to
critically evaluate AI applications and the justifiability of deploying them. Read as
the adversary to Finnveden's "What's important in AI for epistemics" and the
80,000 Hours decision-making profile.
