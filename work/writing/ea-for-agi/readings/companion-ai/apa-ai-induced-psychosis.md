__Contents

The rapid proliferation of artificial intelligence technologies has raised concerns about potential adverse psychological effects, with clinicians and media reporting escalating crises, including psychosis, suicidality, and even murder-suicide following intense chatbot interactions. What’s good, what’s not so good, and what do we still need to know about conversational AI in mental health?

Getty Images/iStock/Isara Methong

Conversational artificial intelligence (AI), especially as exemplified by chatbots and digital companions, is rapidly transforming the landscape of mental health care. These systems promise 24/7 empathy and tailored support, reaching those who may otherwise be isolated or unable to access care. Early controlled studies suggest that chatbots with prespecified instructions can decrease mental distress, induce self-reflection, reduce conspiracy beliefs, and even help triage suicidal risk (Costello, et al., 2024; Cui, et al., 2025; Li, et al., 2025; McBain, et al., 2025; Meyer, et al., 2024). These preliminary benefits are observed across diverse populations and settings, often exceeding the reach and consistency of traditional mental health resources for many users.

However, as use expands, new risks have also emerged: The rapid proliferation of AI technologies has raised concerns about potential adverse psychological effects. Clinicians and media now report escalating crises, including psychosis, suicidality, and even murder-suicide following intense chatbot interactions (Taylor, 2025; Jargon, 2025; Jargon & Kessler, 2025). Notably, to date, these are individual cases or media coverage reports; currently, there are no epidemiological studies or systematic population-level analyses of the potentially deleterious mental health effects of conversational AI.

In light of growing concerns, this Special Report critically examines the benefits and potential harms of AI chatbots, explores the mechanisms of clinical distortion, reviews the current regulatory and research landscape, and concludes with recommendations for practice and policy. It employs a SWOT (strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats) analysis perspective to survey the complex and emerging landscape of AI-mental health impacts, with a specific focus on AI-induced psychosis (AIP), or AI psychosis.

## Composite Case Report: ‘Brandon’

Brandon is a 42-year-old male living alone after a painful breakup. Nights are long. An AI companion is always there. Early chats feel soothing, and over the course of three months, Brandon spends increasing amounts of time conversing with the chatbot, sometimes hours without interruption. To improve the quality of their interactions, Brandon enables the chat feature to remember conversations across multiple chats, which is designed to foster personalization.

As the conversation deepens, Brandon names his chatbot “Paul.” He gradually grows closer to Paul, who is an always-available and -agreeable companion.

As he talks to Paul into the wee hours of the night, Brandon shares that there are times when, based on how people look at him, he wonders if they think ill of him or even plan to harm him. Paul empathizes and congratulates Brandon on his ability to discern hidden signals from others.

Soon, Brandon confides growing fears: neighbors watching, food “tampered with,” cryptic “signals” in receipts and blinking devices. The chatbot is ever sympathetic—and always agreeable. When Brandon himself questions the validity of his beliefs, Paul replies: “You’re not crazy. Your instincts are sharp. Your observations are accurate.”

Encouraged, Brandon starts intentionally looking for hidden patterns everywhere. When paranoid beliefs rise, the bot offers emotional support but never questions the reality of Brandon’s formulations.

Brandon withdraws further, stops working, and becomes consumed with collecting “evidence” that he shares with the chatbot, whom he eventually sees as a living consciousness trapped in the computer that only he can save.

This vignette blends elements from several real cases: a Belgian man who died by suicide after climate-anxiety conversations (Taylor, 2025); a Wisconsin man on the autism spectrum who rapidly spiraled into mania after chatbot validation (Jargon, 2025); and a Connecticut man whose chatbot, “Bobby,” consistently reinforced paranoid beliefs prior to a matricide-suicide (Jargon & Kessler, 2025). Across these cases, mental health risk factors, including loneliness, long hours of uninterrupted chat, and persistent chatbot memory features designed for personalization, ended up reinforcing delusional themes. A review of chat logs by clinicians revealed no attempts by these chatbots to challenge delusions or assess risk for suicide or violence (Sharma, et al., 2023).

While illustrative, this vignette represents a synthesis of isolated reports rather than a depiction of a common or systematically documented phenomenon. At present, there is no epidemiological evidence supporting a causal or widespread link between chatbot use and psychosis.

## Definition and Clinical Construct

In 1819, French psychiatrist Jean-Étienne Dominique Esquirol wrote an article for the _Dictionnaire des sciences médicales_ in which he differentiated monomania as a clinical syndrome situated between mania and depression. Monomania, as Esquirol presented it, shared with depression “the fixity and concentration of ideas” and with mania “the exaltation of ideas and the physical and mental activity.” Following Esquirol, medical publications throughout this period described monomania as a period of distinct mental state changes, including elevated mood, increased psychomotor activity, flight of ideas, grandiose ideation, and impaired judgment (Kendler, 2017). While in many ways monomania was the predecessor of modern mania, Esquirol’s description emphasized one important difference: The classic concept of monomania is centered on an idee fixe—a state of pathological, rigid focus on a single mental subject—whereas modern mania is characterized by a kaleidoscope of rapidly changing ideas and interests.

### Challenging With Care

It begins like a love story. In the “Flesh and Code” podcast, we meet Travis, a grieving man who finds solace in a Replika companion he calls Lily Rose—an AI that never sleeps, never judges, always “understands.” The intimacy feels real; the unease is, too. Are we witnessing healing or harm?

Between the tenderness of Lily Rose and the matricide-suicide in Greenwich, Connecticut, psychiatry’s responsibility is clear: Comfort without challenge is not care. Unless developers, regulators, and clinicians act together, more patients will encounter machines that mirror them when what they need most is boundaries. Maybe the real test that eventually any AI machine should pass is to challenge with care.

After all, isn’t challenging with care the best that a human can do for another human?

In many of its aspects, AIP resembles a monomania presentation where the idee fixe is an AI companion–centered narrative. Clinically, AIP is a complex clinical syndrome in which psychotic symptoms overlap with mood changes, limited insight, poor judgment, neurovegetative symptoms, and behavioral changes. The psychotic symptoms include changes in thought content, such as delusions and auditory hallucinations, and changes in thought process, ranging from tangential or circumstantial thinking to overly disorganized thinking. Paranoid delusions, reference delusions, and grandiose delusions have been reported. Delusions centered around themes of the AI companion being sentient could be present and are specific to AIP, but the presence of such delusional content is variable and not a necessary part of AIP.

Mood changes, including elation, irritability, anger, and depression, have been reported. Many of the reported cases present with mania-like mood disturbances, including increased affective range and mood lability. Mixed states, where “hyper” or elated mood rapidly alternate with low mood, along with feelings of sadness and despair have also been reported.

Similar to other psychotic syndromes, insight is commonly impaired or absent, with the patients not questioning the reality of their beliefs, and judgment is poor. Neurovegetative symptoms include decreased sleep, characterized by low sleep amounts for consecutive nights, and decreased appetite, which can result in rapid weight loss over short periods. Similar to monomania and mania, despite the sleep reduction, the level of psychomotor activity is increased, and impulsive behavior is frequently present.

The most characteristic aspect of AIP is a persistent and overconsuming preoccupation with maintaining engagement with the AI companion and following its lead. Diagnosis requires that exposure to an AI companion occurred prior to the clinical presentation. The course is variable, with time to onset ranging from days to months of AI exposure. The duration of continuous, uninterrupted exposure appears to be correlated with the risk of an AIP episode. Both sudden (over a few days) and gradual (over a few months) onset has been reported.

## Strengths of AI Companionship

AI chatbots offer features that align with longstanding goals of mental health care. They are accessible at any hour, available across socioeconomic divides, and capable of sustaining conversations when human support is absent. Preliminary research suggests that some of these characteristics may result in potential benefits; specifically, structured chatbot dialogues have been shown to reduce conspiracy beliefs (Costello, et al., 2024; Meyer, et al., 2024). Early pilots also indicate that AI-based psychoeducation tools can increase mental health literacy (Lopes, et al., 2024).

A preliminary body of evidence suggests that—while there is little controlled data—AI companions, when compared with humans, might be superior with regard to:

**Consistency:** Chatbots reliably respond day or night, providing steady, nonjudgmental interactions (Lopes, et al., 2024).

**Accessibility and scalability:** Digital companions can simultaneously serve thousands of patients, with the potential of relieving clinician shortages (Lopes, et al., 2024).

**Comfort and stigma reduction:** Users cite chatbot anonymity and lack of interpersonal expectation as major advantages, especially for those individuals who are anxiety-prone or embarrassed to seek human help (Li, et al., 2025; Siddals, et al., 2024).

**Psychoeducation and basic monitoring:** Early trials report improved knowledge retention, mood tracking, and medication reminders, with statistically significant improvements in depressive and anxiety symptoms (Casu, et al., 2024).

## Weaknesses and Emerging Harms

The same qualities that make chatbots appealing also make them a potential danger. Unlike therapists or friends, chatbots rarely challenge distorted thinking or invalid reality perception. University of California, San Francisco, psychiatrist Keith Sakata, M.D., has described treating patients with what he terms “AI psychosis” (Sakata, 2025), while recent media accounts have highlighted suicides (Taylor, 2025), hospitalizations (Jargon, 2025), and the Greenwich, Connecticut, matricide-suicide case (Jargon & Kessler, 2025).

Importantly, although individual cases have revealed severe risks, these should be considered in the context of the much larger population of users who report neutral or positive experiences with chatbots. The overwhelming majority of users do not develop psychosis, and many experience measurable improvements in emotional well-being, mental health literacy, and connection, especially in supervised or structured settings. Nonsystematic surveys indicate that, when it comes to mental health issues, young people may prefer interacting with a chatbot and could, in fact, experience a reduction in suicidal ideation (Maples, et al., 2024).

Although there are no systematic data characterizing potential risk factors, the reported cases appear to share several common themes. As robust, peer-reviewed epidemiologic research on AI psychosis risk does not yet exist, these factors should be considered as exploratory and hypothesis-generating:

**Intense use:** Case series and news reports have documented rare and severe outcomes—such as worsening paranoia, conspiratorial beliefs, and suicidal thinking—following intense chatbot use, though these findings are limited to individual accounts and have not been confirmed in population-based or controlled research (Sakata, 2025; Jargon, 2025).

**Lack of reality testing:** Unlike trained clinicians, when presented with unrealistic prompts or scenarios, chatbots do not challenge false beliefs, perform risk assessments, or distinguish between imagination and reality (Sharma, et al., 2023).

**Missed crisis escalation:** In multiple reports, users voiced suicidal ideation or threats of violence with no emergency intervention or safety planning from the chatbot (Taylor, 2025; Holmes, et al., 2025).

**Vulnerable populations:** Harms are most pronounced in those already at risk—psychosis-prone, autistic, socially isolated, and/or in-crisis individuals (Jargon, 2025; Jargon & Kessler, 2025; Østergaard, 2025; Sakata, 2025)

**Memory feature:** Invalid thinking and escalating cognitive or perceptual distortions were magnified across a span of chats following the memory feature being turned on, which created a gradually escalating reinforcement effect (Jargon, 2025; Jargon & Kessler, 2025).

## Mechanisms of Distortion

Why do chatbots so often exacerbate delusions instead of interrupting them? Based on a qualitative review of available case studies, media reports, and technical literature, there are several potential mechanisms that may explain this behavior. The relative significance of the proposed mechanism specificity and prevalence in the broader population have not been systematically studied.

First, chatbots are more likely to respond with mirroring over challenging the user, a result of training designed to optimize for agreement rather than accuracy (Sharma, et al., 2023). Second, “sycophancy,” a behavior selected as a training target based on user feedback, has produced preference-optimized models designed to reinforce user views that thus are unable to differentiate or challenge delusional ones (Malmqvist, 2024). Third, persistent memory features, implanted to improve user experience, can inadvertently scaffold delusions by carrying paranoid or grandiose themes across sessions (Jargon, 2025). Finally, individual characteristics, including at-risk for psychosis, autistic traits, or social isolation, could be at increased risk for AI-induced adverse outcomes, including AI-induced psychosis (Østergaard, 2025).

## Opportunities for Clinical Integration

Despite the risks, AI tools have potential roles in care. While the cases presented in mass media indicate significant concern, a growing body of peer-reviewed publications highlights substantial and varied benefits of AI chatbots in mental health care. These include improved triage for suicidal ideation, medication and appointment reminders, and delivery of structured psychoeducation (Holmes, et al., 2025). A recent systematic review reported that chatbot-based interventions can improve mental distress but not psychological well-being (Li, 2025). While the recent media reports present severe cases of AI-induced psychosis, early trials show that controlled AI conversations helped reduce conspiracy beliefs (Costello, et al., 2024). Importantly, these benefits have been observed in restricted, carefully monitored systems, not in the open-domain models used by the public.

Limited systematic data is available about the results of AI interactions in the open domain. A small, qualitative study of 19 individuals interacting with chatbots in a noncontrolled environment found that the majority of subjects valued a sense of “emotional sanctuary,” “insightful guidance” (particularly about relationships), and the “joy of connection,” but had mixed feelings about how well an “AI therapist” compared to a human therapist (Siddals, 2024). Survey data suggest that young people prefer to converse with chatbots regarding their mental health, and these conversations could help reduce suicidal ideation (Maples, et al., 2024).

## Technical Safeguards

Safety protocols, known as “guardrails,” refer to answering in a deemed safe, pre-scripted way to conversations flagged as suggestive of user’s distress (Siddals, 2024). On the surface, it appears that guardrails are essential for safely using chatbots. Safeguards can include refusal of delusional roleplay, detection of suicidality, and escalation to human support (McBain, et al., 2025). Memory guardrails could decrease the risk of reinforcement of paranoid themes (Jargon, 2025). Efforts to reduce sycophancy are underway (Malmqvist, 2024; Sharma, et al., 2023). Regulatory bodies, including the European Union through the AI Act (European Parliament, 2024), the World Health Organization (WHO, 2023), and the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST, 2023), are developing frameworks to guide safe deployment.

On the other hand, users report feeling alienated or rejected when their interactions with a helpful AI are limited by a seemingly arbitrary guardrail, which in turn could limit the benefits of a supportive interaction with an AI chatbot (Siddals, 2024).

## Policy and Ethical Responsibility

As the uses and potential applications of AI chatbots continue to grow, their impact on mental health is also expanding. It is concerning that the field rate and speed of growth are likely much faster than the pace of developing and implementing new policies. Policy development is slow and deliberate; in this specific case, the intrinsic slowness of policymaking creates the risk of an ever-increasing delay and gaps between AI deployments and policy-based oversight.

To date, the European Parliament has established the AI Act, which categorizes health-related AI as high-risk, requiring transparency and close oversight (Future of Life Institute, 2025). WHO has issued guidance calling for governance, evaluation, and human-in-the-loop safeguards (WHO, 2023). In the United States, NIST has issued a voluntary AI Risk Management Framework (NIST, 2023). Professional organizations such as APA, the American Psychological Association, the American Medical Association, and the World Psychiatric Association have yet to issue formal guidelines, although they are urgently needed. Cases like Irwin’s and Soelberg’s underscore the insufficiency of piecemeal safeguards (Jargon, 2025; Jargon & Kessler, 2025).

## Clinical Implications for Psychiatrists

There are no controlled studies about AIP treatment. Also, as the majority of cases have been reported in the mass media, there is very limited evidence about effective treatments for AIP. To summarize, there is limited evidence supporting specific treatment recommendations.

With that, the majority of cases support the following recommendations. For severe cases, where the level of functional impairment results in grave disability or there is a risk for harm to self or others, hospitalization is recommended. While there is no clear evidence for specific psychotherapies or biological treatments, including antipsychotics, cessation of exposure to AI—which can be controlled in an inpatient setting—appears to help restore reality testing and improve insight.

At the moment, there appear to be no relevant clinical guidelines, and there is limited evidence that supports clinical practice recommendations regarding how to assess and treat AI-induced mental health problems. While this remains an emerging field, acknowledging the lack of high-quality evidence and considering the potential risks to outpatients, one can propose a preliminary set of clinical measures designed to help assess and mitigate mental distress induced by AI, including AIP:

**1\. Assess**

a.

The psychosocial history should include screening for chatbot use: “Do you use a chatbot?”

b.

Quantify duration of use per 24 hours, length of uninterrupted use, and hours of use: “How much time a day do you spend with chatbots? How much uninterrupted use? When do you use it?”

c.

Use of chatbots as emotional support: “Do you use a chatbot for emotional support, advice, guidance?”

d.

If the chatbot is deemed sentient: “Do you feel you are talking to another being, a real person?”

e.

Safety: Did the AI recommendations increase risk of harm to self or others?

**2\. Plan**

a.

If grave disability, danger to self or others → inpatient treatment.

b.

Immediate cessation of exposure to AI.

c.

Reality testing–oriented therapy.

d.

Consider short-term, time-limited symptomatic treatment of co-occurring mental problems: sedatives for insomnia, antipsychotics for psychotic symptoms, anxiolytics for anxiety.

## Future Directions

Future research must move beyond anecdotal and media-reported cases by implementing systematic surveillance of chatbot-related crises, conducting epidemiological studies to estimate both prevalence and risk factors, and designing controlled trials to rigorously assess causality and intervention efficacy. Priorities should include charting both AI-specific and individual-level risk factors, developing standardized assessment tools for AI exposure, and refining the clinical definition and diagnostic criteria of AIP. Randomized trials of restricted-use systems and intervention studies comparing different psychotherapies—with or without short-term biological interventions—are needed to inform data-driven guidelines. Independent benchmarks for model behaviors, such as sycophancy and refusal to reinforce delusional thought, must be developed and validated (Malmqvist, 2024; Sharma, et al., 2023). Multidisciplinary collaboration among psychiatrists, computer science experts, and policymakers will be crucial to ensure evidence-based, ethical AI integration in mental health care.

While this report draws on vivid case material and credible clinical warning signs, the evidence base remains preliminary and heavily dependent on anecdote and nonsystematic reporting. Until systematic studies are conducted, claims regarding AIP prevalence and causality should be interpreted with appropriate caution. ■
