A **nuclear close call** is an incident that might have led to at least one [nuclear explosion](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_explosion "Nuclear explosion"), but did not. They can be split into intentional use and unintentional use close calls.

Intentional use close calls may occur during increased military tensions involving one or more [nuclear states](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_states "Nuclear states"). They may be a threat made by the state, or an attack upon the state. They may also come from [nuclear terrorism](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_terrorism "Nuclear terrorism").

Unintentional use close calls may occur due to equipment failure. Common examples are [strategic bombers](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_bomber "Strategic bomber") accidentally dropping or crashing with nuclear bombs, or [early warning systems](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_warning_systems "Early warning systems") mistaking phenomena such as weather events or non-nuclear rocket launches for an [ICBM](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ICBM "ICBM") [first strike](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_strike_\(nuclear_strategy\) "First strike \(nuclear strategy\)") and therefore recommending a [second strike](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_strike "Second strike").

Though exact details on many nuclear close calls are hard to come by, the analysis of particular cases has highlighted the importance of a variety of factors in preventing accidents. At an international level, this includes the importance of context and outside mediation; at the national level, effectiveness in government communications, and involvement of key decision-makers; and, at the individual level, the decisive role of individuals in following intuition and prudent decision-making, often in violation of protocol.[1]

A possible example of an accident that did result in a nuclear explosion is the 2019 [Nyonoksa radiation accident](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nyonoksa_radiation_accident "Nyonoksa radiation accident") in Russia.

Any nuclear exchange carries the possibility of rapid [climate change](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_variability_and_change "Climate variability and change"), threatening global food production: [nuclear famine](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_famine "Nuclear famine").[2]

Despite [reduction of nuclear arms](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arms_control "Arms control") and lower tensions after the end of the [Cold War](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), estimated nuclear warhead stockpiles total roughly 15,000 worldwide, with the [United States](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and [Russia](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia") holding 90% of the total.[3]

## Intentional use close calls

Intentional close calls may occur during increased military tensions involving one or more [nuclear states](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_states "Nuclear states"). They may be a threat made by the state, or an attack upon the state. They may also come from [nuclear terrorism](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_terrorism "Nuclear terrorism").

### 1950–1953: Korean War

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: 1950–1953: Korean War")]

See also: [Korean War § U.S. threat of atomic warfare](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_War#U.S._threat_of_atomic_warfare "Korean War"), and [Operation Hudson Harbor](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Hudson_Harbor "Operation Hudson Harbor")

During the [Korean War](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_War "Korean War"), the U.S. considered nuclear attacks on [North Korea](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Korea "North Korea") and [Manchuria](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchuria "Manchuria"), in response to involvement from the [People's Volunteer Army](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People's_Volunteer_Army "People's Volunteer Army") from China. [Mark 4 nuclear bombs](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_4_nuclear_bomb "Mark 4 nuclear bomb"), lacking their [fissile pits](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pit_\(nuclear_weapon\) "Pit \(nuclear weapon\)"), were deployed to Guam and Okinawa. [Boeing B-29 Superfortresses](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing_B-29_Superfortress "Boeing B-29 Superfortress") were stationed in [Kadena Air Base](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kadena_Air_Base "Kadena Air Base"), Okinawa, and flew practice bombing runs with dummy nuclear or conventional bombs against North Korean targets.

The U.S. also considered strikes on [Soviet Air Force](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Air_Forces "Soviet Air Forces") bases in the [Russian Far East](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Far_East "Russian Far East").[4] The [Artem air base](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladivostok_International_Airport "Vladivostok International Airport") was the most immediate.

### 1954: First Indochina War

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: 1954: First Indochina War")]

Main article: [Operation Vulture](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Vulture "Operation Vulture")

During the [Battle of Dien Bien Phu](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu "Battle of Dien Bien Phu"), at the request of the French, the U.S. considered [tactical nuclear weapons](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tactical_nuclear_weapon "Tactical nuclear weapon") use against the [Viet Minh](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viet_Minh "Viet Minh"). U.S. strategic bombers stationed in the Philippines or Okinawa, as well as carrier aircraft in the [Seventh Fleet](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Seventh_Fleet "United States Seventh Fleet"), were considered for a strike using three tactical nuclear bombs, possibly [Mark 7s](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_7_nuclear_bomb "Mark 7 nuclear bomb").

### 1956: Suez Crisis

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: 1956: Suez Crisis")]

See also: [Suez Crisis](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suez_Crisis "Suez Crisis")

During the [Suez Crisis](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suez_Crisis "Suez Crisis"), the [North American Aerospace Defense Command](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_Aerospace_Defense_Command "North American Aerospace Defense Command") (NORAD) received a number of simultaneous reports, including unidentified aircraft over [Turkey](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkey "Turkey"), Soviet [MiG-15](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-15 "Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-15") fighters over [Syria](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Syrian_Republic "Second Syrian Republic"), a downed British [Canberra medium bomber](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Electric_Canberra "English Electric Canberra"), and unexpected maneuvers by the Soviet [Black Sea Fleet](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea_Fleet "Black Sea Fleet") through the [Dardanelles](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dardanelles "Dardanelles") that appeared to signal a Soviet offensive. Considering previous Soviet threats to use conventional missiles against [France](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Fourth_Republic "French Fourth Republic") and the [United Kingdom](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom"), U.S. forces believed these events could trigger a [NATO](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO "NATO") nuclear strike against the Soviet Union. In fact, all reports of [Soviet](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Armed_Forces "Soviet Armed Forces") action turned out to be erroneous, misinterpreted, or exaggerated. The perceived threat was due to a coincidental combination of events, including a wedge of swans over Turkey, a fighter escort for [Syrian President](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Syria "President of Syria") [Shukri al-Quwatli](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shukri_al-Quwatli "Shukri al-Quwatli") returning from [Moscow](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moscow "Moscow"), a British bomber brought down by mechanical issues, and scheduled exercises of the Soviet fleet.[5]

### 1958: Second Taiwan Strait Crisis

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: 1958: Second Taiwan Strait Crisis")]

[U.S. Secretary of State](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Secretary_of_State "U.S. Secretary of State") [Christian Herter](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Herter "Christian Herter") characterized the [Second Taiwan Strait Crisis](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Taiwan_Strait_Crisis "Second Taiwan Strait Crisis") as "the first serious nuclear crisis".[6] In this conflict, the [People's Republic of China](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People's_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") (PRC) shelled the islands of [Kinmen](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinmen "Kinmen") (Quemoy) and the [Matsu Islands](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matsu_Islands "Matsu Islands") along the east coast of [mainland China](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainland_China "Mainland China") (in the [Taiwan Strait](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_Strait "Taiwan Strait")) in an attempt to probe the extent of the [United States](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") military defense of Taiwan's sovereign territory. This was an ultimately failed preemptive strike prior to an attempted invasion of Taiwan, where the [Republic of China](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China "Republic of China")'s (ROC) [military forces](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwanese_Armed_Forces "Taiwanese Armed Forces") and political apparatuses, known as the [Kuomintang](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuomintang "Kuomintang") (KMT), had been exiled since the end of the [Chinese Civil War](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Civil_War "Chinese Civil War") in 1949. A naval battle also took place around [Dongding Island](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dongding_Island "Dongding Island") when the ROC Navy repelled an attempted [amphibious landing](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amphibious_warfare "Amphibious warfare") by the PRC Navy.[7][8][9][10]

### 1962: Cuban Missile Crisis

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: 1962: Cuban Missile Crisis")]

Main article: [Cuban Missile Crisis](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuban_Missile_Crisis "Cuban Missile Crisis")

#### Soviet nuclear weapons in Cuba

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Soviet nuclear weapons in Cuba")]

Under [Operation Anadyr](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Anadyr "Operation Anadyr"), the [Soviet Union](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") deployed both [strategic](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_nuclear_weapon "Strategic nuclear weapon") and [tactical nuclear weapons](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tactical_nuclear_weapons "Tactical nuclear weapons") in Cuba, totalling 158 warheads. Of these, 80 were for use by the [9K52 Luna-M](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/9K52_Luna-M "9K52 Luna-M") [nuclear](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_artillery "Nuclear artillery") [rocket artillery](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_artillery "Rocket artillery")/[short-range ballistic missile](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short-range_ballistic_missile "Short-range ballistic missile") system, and 12 were for use by the [FKR-1](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FKR-1_cruise_missile "FKR-1 cruise missile") [cruise missile](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cruise_missile "Cruise missile"). At least one unit of the latter was targeted at the U.S. [Guantanamo Bay Naval Base](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_Naval_Base "Guantanamo Bay Naval Base") on the island of Cuba.[11][12]

#### U.S. false alarm at interceptor airbase

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: U.S. false alarm at interceptor airbase")]

[](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Convair_F-106A-1-CO_060928-F-1234S-039.jpg)An [F-106A](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convair_F-106_Delta_Dart "Convair F-106 Delta Dart") during takeoff

During the Cuban Missile Crisis, United States military planners expected that [sabotage](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabotage "Sabotage") operations might precede any [nuclear first strike](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_first_strike "Nuclear first strike") by the Soviet Union. Around midnight on 25 October 1962, a guard at the [Duluth Sector Direction Center](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duluth_Sector_Direction_Center "Duluth Sector Direction Center") in [Minnesota](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minnesota "Minnesota") saw a figure climbing the security fence. He shot at it and activated the sabotage alarm, which automatically set off similar alarms at other bases in the region. At [Volk Field](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volk_Field "Volk Field") in [Wisconsin](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wisconsin "Wisconsin"), a faulty alarm system caused the [klaxon](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klaxon "Klaxon") to sound instead, which ordered [Air Defense Command](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_Defense_Command "Air Defense Command") (ADC) nuclear-armed [F-106A](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F-106A "F-106A") interceptors into the air. The pilots had been told there would be no practice alert drills and, according to political scientist [Scott Sagan](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scott_Sagan "Scott Sagan"), "fully believed that a [nuclear war](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_warfare "Nuclear warfare") was starting".[13] Before the planes were able to take off, the base commander contacted Duluth and learned of the error. An officer in the command center drove his car onto the runway, flashing his lights and signaling to the aircraft to stop. The intruder was discovered to be a [bear](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bear "Bear").[13][14]

Sagan writes that the incident raised the dangerous possibility of an ADC interceptor [accidentally shooting down](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friendly_fire "Friendly fire") a [Strategic Air Command](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_Air_Command "Strategic Air Command") (SAC) bomber.[13] Interceptor crews had not been given full information by SAC of plans to move bombers to dispersal bases (such as Volk Field) or the classified routes flown by bombers on continuous alert as part of [Operation Chrome Dome](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Chrome_Dome "Operation Chrome Dome"). Declassified ADC documents later revealed that "the incident led to changes in the alert Klaxon system [...] to prevent a recurrence".[13]

#### Soviet averted launch of nuclear torpedo

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Soviet averted launch of nuclear torpedo")]

[](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Soviet_b-59_submarine.jpg)_B-59_ near Cuba with a U.S. Navy helicopter circling above, c. October 28–29, 1962

At the height of the Cuban Missile Crisis, Soviet patrol submarine [_B-59_](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_submarine_B-59 "Soviet submarine B-59") almost launched a [nuclear torpedo](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_torpedo "Nuclear torpedo") while under harassment by American naval forces on October 27. One of several vessels surrounded by American [destroyers](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Destroyer "Destroyer") near Cuba, _B-59_ dove to avoid detection and was unable to communicate with [Moscow](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moscow "Moscow") for a number of days.[15] [USS  _Beale_](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USS_Beale_\(DD-471\) "USS Beale \(DD-471\)") began dropping practice [depth charges](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Depth_charge "Depth charge") to signal _B-59_ to surface; however, the captain of the Soviet submarine and its _[zampolit](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_commissar "Political commissar")_ (political officer) took these to be real depth charges.[16] With low [batteries](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_battery "Electric battery") affecting the submarine's [life-support systems](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life-support_system "Life-support system") and unable to make contact with [Moscow](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moscow "Moscow"), the commander of _B-59_ feared that war had already begun and ordered the use of a 10-kiloton nuclear torpedo against the American fleet. The _zampolit_ agreed, but the chief of staff of the [flotilla](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flotilla "Flotilla") (second in command of the flotilla) [Vasily Arkhipov](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasily_Arkhipov "Vasily Arkhipov") refused permission to launch. He convinced the captain to calm down, surface, and make contact with Moscow for new orders.[17][18]

#### U.S. loss of ICBM launch authority

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: U.S. loss of ICBM launch authority")]

According to Scott Sagan, also on 27 October, at [Malmstrom Air Force Base](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malmstrom_Air_Force_Base "Malmstrom Air Force Base") in [Montana](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montana "Montana"), officers of [Strategic Air Command](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_Air_Command "Strategic Air Command") jerry-rigged their launch system on a [Minuteman](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LGM-30_Minuteman "LGM-30 Minuteman") ICBM, bypassing standard procedure and obtaining an independent launch authority.[11]

#### U.S. scramble of interceptors

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: U.S. scramble of interceptors")]

Additionally on 27 October, an American [U-2 spy plane](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_U-2 "Lockheed U-2") was shot down over Cuba, resulting in the death of pilot Major [Rudolf Anderson](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Anderson "Rudolf Anderson") of the [United States Air Force](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Air_Force "United States Air Force") (USAF), and another U-2 flown by Captain Charles Maultsby, USAF, from [Eielson Air Force Base](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eielson_Air_Force_Base "Eielson Air Force Base"), [Alaska](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alaska "Alaska"), strayed 300 miles (480 km) into Soviet airspace. Despite orders to avoid Soviet airspace by at least 100 miles (160 km), a navigational error caused by the [aurora borealis](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora "Aurora") took the U-2 over the [Chukotka Peninsula](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chukchi_Peninsula "Chukchi Peninsula"), causing Soviet MiG interceptors to scramble and pursue the aircraft.[5][19][20] American [F-102A](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convair_F-102_Delta_Dagger "Convair F-102 Delta Dagger") interceptors armed with [GAR-11 Falcon](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AIM-26_Falcon "AIM-26 Falcon") nuclear [air-to-air missiles](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air-to-air_missile "Air-to-air missile") (each with a 0.25 kiloton yield) were then scrambled to escort the U-2 into friendly airspace.[21] Individual pilots were capable of arming and launching their missiles. The incident remained secret for many years.[20]

### 1968–1969: Vietnam War

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: 1968–1969: Vietnam War")]

During the [Vietnam War](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam_War "Vietnam War"), the United States military developed at least two [contingency plans](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contingency_plan "Contingency plan") relating to nuclear weapons in Vietnam. In early 1968, the [Fracture Jaw](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fracture_Jaw "Fracture Jaw") plan called for the deployment of [tactical nuclear weapons](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tactical_nuclear_weapons "Tactical nuclear weapons") to [South Vietnam](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Vietnam "South Vietnam") during the [Battle of Khe Sanh](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Khe_Sanh "Battle of Khe Sanh"), and was approved by General [William Westmoreland](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Westmoreland "William Westmoreland"). It was vetoed by President [Lyndon B. Johnson](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson "Lyndon B. Johnson"), partially out of fear of causing the [People's Republic of China](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People's_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") to enter the conflict.[22] In late 1969, the [Nixon White House](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nixon_White_House "Nixon White House") developed the [Duck Hook](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duck_Hook "Duck Hook"), which involved major escalation of the U.S. war effort via bombing and mining campaigns. The plan was abandoned by Nixon on 1 November due to opposition from the cabinet, lowered public war support, and uncertain effectiveness.

### 1969: DPRK shootdown of U.S. EWAC aircraft

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: 1969: DPRK shootdown of U.S. EWAC aircraft")]

Main article: [1969 EC-121 shootdown incident](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1969_EC-121_shootdown_incident "1969 EC-121 shootdown incident")

[](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:EC-121_PR-21_USN.jpg)The EC-121 shot down in 1969, seen here c. 1960

A United States [Lockheed EC-121 Warning Star](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_EC-121_Warning_Star "Lockheed EC-121 Warning Star") early warning aircraft was shot down by a [Democratic People's Republic of Korea](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_People's_Republic_of_Korea "Democratic People's Republic of Korea") (DPRK, _i.e._ North Korea) [MiG-21](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MiG-21 "MiG-21"), killing all 31 servicemen aboard. [F-4 Phantom](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F-4_Phantom_II "McDonnell Douglas F-4 Phantom II") fighter-bomber jets at [Kunsan Air Base](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kunsan_Air_Base "Kunsan Air Base") were ordered to load [B61 nuclear bombs](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B61_nuclear_bomb "B61 nuclear bomb") and began preparations for a nuclear strike against the DPRK.[23] The attack was to include the airfield from which the North Koreans had attacked the U.S. aircraft.[24] After a few hours, the order to stand down was given and the jets never took off. Reportedly, President [Richard Nixon](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Nixon "Richard Nixon") was [drunk](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_intoxication "Alcohol intoxication") when he gave the order for a nuclear attack against the DPRK.[25] The order to stand down was given on the advice of [Secretary of State](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State "United States Secretary of State") [Henry Kissinger](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Kissinger "Henry Kissinger").[_[citation needed](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### 1969: Sino-Soviet conflict

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: 1969: Sino-Soviet conflict")]

Main articles: [Sino-Soviet border conflict](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Soviet_border_conflict "Sino-Soviet border conflict") and [Sino-Soviet split § Planned Soviet nuclear strike on China](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Soviet_split#Planned_Soviet_nuclear_strike_on_China "Sino-Soviet split")

In 1969, following the border conflict [Battle of Zhenbao Island](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhenbao_Island_incident "Zhenbao Island incident") in March, the USSR [considered a massive nuclear attack on China](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Soviet_split#Planned_Soviet_nuclear_strike_on_China "Sino-Soviet split"), targeting cities and nuclear facilities. It made military activity in the [Russian Far East](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Far_East "Russian Far East"), and informed its allies and the United States of this potential attack. The Chinese government and archives were evacuated from [Beijing](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") while the [People's Liberation Army](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People's_Liberation_Army "People's Liberation Army") scattered from its bases. The crisis abated when U.S. Secretary of State Henry Kissinger informed the Soviet Union that an attack on China would be met by a U.S. nuclear attack on 130 Soviet cities.[26][27][28] According to the [U.S. Department of State](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Department_of_State "U.S. Department of State"), one of the two main "after-the-fact explanations" for the [Joint Chiefs of Staff Readiness Test](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff_Readiness_Test "Joint Chiefs of Staff Readiness Test") conducted by the U.S. military in October 1969 was to deter a possible Soviet nuclear strike against the People's Republic of China.[29][30]

Main article: [Yom Kippur War](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yom_Kippur_War "Yom Kippur War")

During the Yom Kippur War, Israeli officials panicked that the Arab invasion force would overrun Israel after the [Syrian Army](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syrian_Army "Syrian Army") nearly achieved a breakout in the [Golan Heights](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golan_Heights "Golan Heights"), and the [U.S. government](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._government "U.S. government") rebuffed Israel's request for an emergency [airlift](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airlift "Airlift"). According to a former [CIA](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CIA "CIA") official, Defense Minister [Moshe Dayan](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moshe_Dayan "Moshe Dayan") requested and received authorization from Israeli prime minister [Golda Meir](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golda_Meir "Golda Meir") to arm 13 [Jericho missiles](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jericho_\(missile\) "Jericho \(missile\)") and 8 [F-4 Phantom II](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F-4_Phantom_II "McDonnell Douglas F-4 Phantom II") fighter jets with nuclear warheads. The missile launchers were located at [Sdot Micha Airbase](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sdot_Micha_Airbase "Sdot Micha Airbase"), while the fighter jets were placed on 24-hour notice at [Tel Nof Airbase](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tel_Nof_Airbase "Tel Nof Airbase"). The missiles were said to be aimed at the Arab military headquarters in [Cairo](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cairo "Cairo") and [Damascus](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damascus "Damascus").[5]

The United States discovered Israel's nuclear deployment after a [Lockheed SR-71 Blackbird](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird "Lockheed SR-71 Blackbird") reconnaissance aircraft spotted the missiles, and it began [an airlift](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Nickel_Grass "Operation Nickel Grass") the same day. After the [UN Security Council](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council "United Nations Security Council") imposed a ceasefire, conflict resumed when the [Israel Defense Force](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel_Defense_Forces "Israel Defense Forces") (IDF) moved to encircle the [Egyptian Third Army](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_Army_\(Egypt\) "Third Army \(Egypt\)"). According to former [U.S. State Department](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State "United States Department of State") officials, the leader of the USSR, [Leonid Brezhnev](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev "Leonid Brezhnev"), threatened to deploy the [Soviet Airborne Forces](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Airborne_Forces "Russian Airborne Forces") against Israeli forces, and the [U.S. Armed Forces](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces "United States Armed Forces") were placed at [DEFCON](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DEFCON "DEFCON") 3\. Israel also redeployed its nuclear weapons. While DEFCON 3 was still in effect, mechanics repairing the alarm system at [Kincheloe Air Force Base](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kincheloe_Air_Force_Base "Kincheloe Air Force Base") in [Michigan](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michigan "Michigan") accidentally activated it and nearly scrambled the [B-52 bombers](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing_B-52_Stratofortress "Boeing B-52 Stratofortress") at the base before the duty officer declared a false alarm.[5] The crisis finally ended when Prime Minister Meir halted all military action.[31] Declassified Israeli documents have not confirmed these allegations directly, but have confirmed that Israel was willing to use "drastic means" to win the war.[32]

### 1983: Able Archer 83 NATO exercise

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: 1983: Able Archer 83 NATO exercise")]

Main article: [Able Archer 83](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Able_Archer_83 "Able Archer 83")

Able Archer 83 was a [command post exercise](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Command_post_exercise "Command post exercise") carried out by [NATO](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO "NATO") military forces and political leaders between 7 and 11 November 1983.[33] The exercise simulated a Soviet conventional attack on European NATO forces three days before the start of the exercise (D-3), transitioning to a large-scale [chemical war](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_warfare "Chemical warfare") (D-1), and on day one (D+1) of the exercise, NATO forces sought political guidance on the use of [nuclear weapons](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_weapon "Nuclear weapon") to stem the Soviet advance which was approved by political leaders. NATO then began simulating preparations for a transition to [nuclear war](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_warfare "Nuclear warfare").[34]

These simulations included 170 [radio-silent](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_silence "Radio silence") flights to air lift 19,000 U.S. troops to Europe, regularly shifting military commands to avoid nuclear attack, the use of new nuclear weapon release procedures, the use of nuclear [Command, Control, and Communications](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Command_and_control "Command and control") (C3) networks for passing nuclear orders, the moving of NATO forces in Europe through each of the alert phases from DEFCON 5 to DEFCON 1, and the participation of political leaders like [Margaret Thatcher](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher "Margaret Thatcher"), [Helmut Kohl](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmut_Kohl "Helmut Kohl") and [Ronald Reagan](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_Reagan "Ronald Reagan").[35]

The issue was worsened by leaders referring to B-52 [sorties](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sortie "Sortie") as "nuclear strikes",[35] by the increased use of encrypted diplomatic channels between the U.S. and UK,[36] and by the nuclear attack [false alarm](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False_alarm "False alarm") in September.

In response, Soviet [nuclear-capable](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_delivery "Nuclear weapons delivery") aircraft were fueled and armed ready to launch on the runway, and [ICBMs](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile "Intercontinental ballistic missile") were brought up to alert. Soviet leaders believed the exercise was a ruse to cover NATO preparations for a [nuclear first strike](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_strike_\(nuclear_strategy\) "First strike \(nuclear strategy\)") and frantically sent a telegram to its residencies seeking information on NATO preparations for an attack. The exercise closely aligned with Soviet timeline estimations that a NATO first strike would take 7 to 10 days to execute from the political decision being made.[37][38]

Soviet forces stood down after 11 November when the exercise ended. NATO was unaware of the complete Soviet response until [British intelligence](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_intelligence_agencies "British intelligence agencies") asset [Oleg Gordievsky](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oleg_Gordievsky "Oleg Gordievsky") passed on the information.[39]

#### Coalition nuclear weapons

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Coalition nuclear weapons")]

The [Coalition of the Gulf War](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coalition_of_the_Gulf_War "Coalition of the Gulf War") included the [nuclear-armed countries](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_states_with_nuclear_weapons "List of states with nuclear weapons") of the United States and the United Kingdom. They both deployed nuclear weapons to the region during the conflict, ostensibly to allow retaliation in the event of an [Iraqi chemical weapons](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraqi_chemical_weapons_program "Iraqi chemical weapons program") attack. The United States deployed 1,000 [tactical weapons](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tactical_nuclear_weapon "Tactical nuclear weapon") to the region, with 300 land-based weapons in [Turkey](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkey "Turkey"), and 700 aboard warships in the [Persian Gulf](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_Gulf "Persian Gulf") region. The United Kingdom also deployed five nuclear-capable vessels probably carrying between eight and sixteen nuclear weapons. Western [chemical weapons](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_weapon "Chemical weapon") were also mobilized to the region.[40]

Threats of nuclear use by coalition officials came from public statements, private communication with Iraqi officials, and [leaked](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/News_leak "News leak") communications.[40] Among them, U.S. [Secretary of Defense Dick Cheney](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dick_Cheney "Dick Cheney"), General [Norman Schwarzkopf Jr.](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norman_Schwarzkopf_Jr. "Norman Schwarzkopf Jr."), and UK Prime Minister [Margaret Thatcher](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher "Margaret Thatcher") all emphasized that the use of [weapons of mass destruction](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction "Iraq and weapons of mass destruction") (WMD) against coalition forces would lead to a nuclear attack on Iraq.[41]

On January 7, 1991, _[The Washington Post](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Washington_Post "The Washington Post")_ reported that the U.S. military had ruled out the use of nuclear or chemical weapons in the conflict. Cheney had requested [Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff "Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff") [Colin Powell](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colin_Powell "Colin Powell") to produce nuclear strike options against [Iraq](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq "Iraq"). Powell later wrote that "To do serious damage to just one [armored division](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armored_division "Armored division") dispersed in [the desert](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Desert "Arabian Desert") would require a considerable number of small [tactical nuclear weapons](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tactical_nuclear_weapon "Tactical nuclear weapon")…. If I had had any doubts before about the practicality of nukes in the field of battle, this report clinched them".[42]

On January 9, 1991, [U.S. Secretary of State](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US_Secretary_of_State "US Secretary of State") [James Baker](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Baker "James Baker") delivered to Iraqi [Foreign Minister](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(Iraq\) "Minister of Foreign Affairs \(Iraq\)") [Tariq Aziz](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tariq_Aziz "Tariq Aziz") a letter carrying a formal threat, presumably nuclear retaliation, to the following actions:

  1. If [Saddam Hussein](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saddam_Hussein "Saddam Hussein") used [chemical](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_weapon "Chemical weapon") or [biological weapons](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_weapons "Biological weapons")
  2. If Saddam Hussein [destroyed the Kuwaiti oil fields](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuwaiti_oil_fires "Kuwaiti oil fires")
  3. If Saddam Hussein [supported terrorists](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saddam_Hussein_and_al-Qaeda_link_allegations "Saddam Hussein and al-Qaeda link allegations")



Baker warned that the U.S. had "the means to exact vengeance" in the event of an Iraqi resort to WMD.[43] After the war, the [Defense Intelligence Agency](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defense_Intelligence_Agency "Defense Intelligence Agency") credited these threats with deterring Iraq from launching chemical attacks on coalition forces.[41]

Researcher [Hans M. Kristensen](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_M._Kristensen "Hans M. Kristensen") has argued that it is unclear if U.S. nuclear threats were fully understood by Iraqi officials, that they were more concerned by the threat of [regime change](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regime_change "Regime change"), and that their [destruction of the Kuwaiti oil fields](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuwaiti_oil_fires "Kuwaiti oil fires") indicates [deterrence](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deterrence_theory "Deterrence theory") was not successful.[42]

#### Israeli nuclear weapons

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Israeli nuclear weapons")]

During the Persian Gulf War, [Ba'athist Iraq](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ba'athist_Iraq "Ba'athist Iraq") launched [Scud missiles](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scud_missile "Scud missile") at [Saudi Arabia](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia") and [Israel](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel "Israel") and possessed a large cache of WMD in the form of chemical weapons. This, along with Saddam Hussein's previous threat to "burn half of Israel" with chemical weapons, led to fears that Saddam Hussein would order the use of the chemical weapons against the [U.S.-led coalition](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coalition_of_the_Gulf_War "Coalition of the Gulf War") or against Israel (see: [Iraq–Israel relations § Until the 2003 Iraq War](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq–Israel_relations#Until_the_2003_Iraq_War "Iraq–Israel relations")).[44][41] Israeli prime minister [Yitzhak Shamir](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yitzhak_Shamir "Yitzhak Shamir") and [Israeli Air Force](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_Air_Force "Israeli Air Force") Commander-in-Chief [Avihu Ben-Nun](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avihu_Ben-Nun "Avihu Ben-Nun") both warned that an Iraqi chemical attack would trigger "massive retaliation", implying that Israel would retaliate with nuclear weapons. Saddam Hussein had a [contingency plan](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contingency_plan "Contingency plan") to launch WMD-armed warheads against [Tel Aviv](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tel_Aviv "Tel Aviv") in the event that he became cut off from the [Iraqi Armed Forces](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraqi_Armed_Forces "Iraqi Armed Forces") leadership or if the [Iraqi government](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Socialist_Ba'ath_Party_–_Iraq_Region "Arab Socialist Ba'ath Party – Iraq Region") was about to collapse, which almost certainly would have triggered a retaliatory nuclear response from Israel. Saddam Hussein ultimately never deemed this option necessary because he never felt as if his government was about to collapse.[45]

### 2017–2018: North Korea crisis

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: 2017–2018: North Korea crisis")]

Main article: [2017–2018 North Korea crisis](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2017–2018_North_Korea_crisis "2017–2018 North Korea crisis")

[](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kim_and_Trump_shaking_hands_at_the_red_carpet_during_the_DPRK–USA_Singapore_Summit.jpg)Kim Jong-un (left) and Donald Trump in June 2018

Between 2017 and 2018, the [United States](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and [North Korea](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Korea "North Korea"), most particularly [U.S. president](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_the_United_States "President of the United States") [Donald Trump](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump "Donald Trump") and North Korean supreme leader [Kim Jong-un](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kim_Jong-un "Kim Jong-un"), exchanged threats that have been described as on the brink of nuclear war. In August 2017, Trump warned that North Korea would be met with "fire and fury, the likes of which the world has never seen".[46] During his speech in 2017 at the [United Nations General Assembly](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly "United Nations General Assembly"), he warned that if the [United States](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") were forced to defend itself, they would have no choice but to "totally destroy North Korea".[47] North Korea, in response, has referred to Trump as a "[dotard](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dotard "Dotard")", stated that he will "pay dearly", and accused the U.S. of "[declaring war](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declaration_of_war "Declaration of war")".[48][49][50] In January 2018, Trump also tweeted that his country has a "bigger nuclear button" than North Korea.[51]

In January 2018, it was reported that Trump withdrew [Victor Cha](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victor_Cha "Victor Cha") as a pick to be [U.S. Ambassador to South Korea](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_ambassadors_of_the_United_States_to_South_Korea "List of ambassadors of the United States to South Korea") over his opposition to launch a limited strike at North Korea. The withdrawal sparked major concerns for North Korean experts that the U.S. was seriously considering war with North Korea.[52]

The crisis has been described as the "Cuban Missile Crisis in slow motion".[53] In Van Jackson's book _On the Brink: Trump, Kim, and the Threat of Nuclear War_ , he stated that the United States was close as ever to [World War III](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_III "World War III") between 2017 and early 2018.[54] Trump also reportedly considered evacuating Americans from [South Korea](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Korea "South Korea") despite warnings that such a move could lead to war.[55][56] Reports also emerged in 2023 that Trump was considering the use of nuclear weapons against North Korea in 2017.[57]

### 2019 India-Pakistan conflict

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: 2019 India-Pakistan conflict")]

Main article: [2019 India–Pakistan border skirmishes](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_India–Pakistan_border_skirmishes "2019 India–Pakistan border skirmishes")

In 2023, [Mike Pompeo](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mike_Pompeo "Mike Pompeo"), who served as [United States Secretary of State](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State "United States Secretary of State") in 2019, claimed that U.S. diplomacy prevented heightened tensions between [India and Pakistan](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India–Pakistan_relations "India–Pakistan relations") from sparking a nuclear war. According to Pompeo, he was informed by [Sushma Swaraj](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sushma_Swaraj "Sushma Swaraj") in February 2019 that the Indian government believed Pakistan was preparing a nuclear attack, and that India was preparing an escalatory response. Pompeo claimed that he and then [National Security Advisor](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_National_Security_Advisor "United States National Security Advisor") [John Bolton](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Bolton "John Bolton") spoke with then [Pakistani Army](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan_Army "Pakistan Army") Chief [Qamar Javed Bajwa](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qamar_Javed_Bajwa "Qamar Javed Bajwa") in "the tiny secure communications facility in our hotel", who was under the equivalent impression about Indian nuclear preparations. The situation was de-escalated as U.S. intermediaries informed both countries that no nuclear attack preparations were occurring. Neither the [Indian Ministry of External Affairs](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_External_Affairs_\(India\) "Ministry of External Affairs \(India\)") nor [Pakistani Foreign Office](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(Pakistan\) "Ministry of Foreign Affairs \(Pakistan\)") commented on Pompeo's claims.[58][59]

### 2022–present: Russian invasion of Ukraine

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: 2022–present: Russian invasion of Ukraine")]

Main article: [Nuclear risk during the Russian invasion of Ukraine](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_risk_during_the_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine "Nuclear risk during the Russian invasion of Ukraine")

Russia has threatened the use of nuclear weapons throughout its [invasion of Ukraine](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russo-Ukrainian_war_\(2022–present\) "Russo-Ukrainian war \(2022–present\)"), characterized as [nuclear blackmail](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_blackmail "Nuclear blackmail").[60][61]

In late 2022, tensions over Russian nuclear use reached a high point. [Russian Foreign Minister](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minister_of_Foreign_Affairs_\(Russia\) "Minister of Foreign Affairs \(Russia\)") [Sergey Lavrov](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergey_Lavrov "Sergey Lavrov") implied nuclear weapons could be used to defend [annexed Ukrainian territories](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_annexation_of_Donetsk,_Kherson,_Luhansk_and_Zaporizhzhia_oblasts "Russian annexation of Donetsk, Kherson, Luhansk and Zaporizhzhia oblasts").[62] Russian officials, including [Russian defense minister](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_\(Russia\) "Ministry of Defence \(Russia\)") [Sergei Shoigu](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Shoigu "Sergei Shoigu"),[63] publicly and privately accused Ukraine of preparing to use a radioactive [dirty bomb](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirty_bomb "Dirty bomb") on Ukrainian territory.[64] A tweet by the Russian Ministry of Defence, purportedly showing evidence of a Ukrainian dirty bomb in production, was debunked as a collection of old and unrelated photos.[65] On 24 October, [John Kirby](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Kirby_\(admiral\) "John Kirby \(admiral\)") stated that there was no evidence Russia was preparing a dirty bomb strike.[66] At Ukraine's request, the [United Nations](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") sent an [International Atomic Energy Agency](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency "International Atomic Energy Agency") (IAEA) investigation to Ukraine, which found no evidence of a dirty bomb being developed or any other undeclared nuclear activity.[67][68][69] U.S. officials feared that those allegations by Russia may be a confirmation of it preparing for a nuclear strike on Ukraine, using dirty bomb allegations or a [false flag](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False_flag "False flag") attack as a [pretext](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casus_belli "Casus belli").[70] The U.S., as reported by [CNN](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNN "CNN"), has "prepared 'rigorously' for potential Russian nuclear strike in Ukraine". It also engaged U.S. diplomats, as well as asked other countries, namely China and India, to engage diplomatically to persuade Russia to avoid nuclear escalation.[70]

On 25 March 2023, Russian president [Vladimir Putin](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Putin "Vladimir Putin") announced plans to install [Russian tactical nuclear weapons in Belarus](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_military_presence_in_Belarus "Russian military presence in Belarus").[71]

On 19 November 2024, Putin signed a [decree](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decree_of_the_President_of_Russia "Decree of the President of Russia") allowing Russia to use nuclear weapons in response to [conventional](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conventional_weapon "Conventional weapon") attacks by a non-nuclear state supported by a [nuclear power](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_states_with_nuclear_weapons "List of states with nuclear weapons"), lowering the threshold for a nuclear strike in response to a possible conventional attack.[72]

On 21 November 2024, Russia used a conventionally armed [multiple independently targetable reentry vehicle](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_independently_targetable_reentry_vehicle "Multiple independently targetable reentry vehicle") (MIRV) system, on the [Oreshnik](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oreshnik_\(missile\) "Oreshnik \(missile\)") [intermediate-range ballistic missile](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intermediate-range_ballistic_missile "Intermediate-range ballistic missile") to attack the Ukrainian city of [Dnipro](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dnipro "Dnipro"), marking their first usage in combat. The MIRV system was developed by multiple nations for nuclear strategic missiles.[73]

On 7 December 2024, [Russia and Belarus](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_State "Union State") signed an agreement offering security guarantees to [Belarus](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belarus "Belarus") including [nuclear security](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_safety_and_security "Nuclear safety and security") and the possible use of [Russian nuclear weapons](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction "Russia and weapons of mass destruction") to repel aggressions.[74] Two days later, Belarusian president [Alexander Lukashenko](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Lukashenko "Alexander Lukashenko") confirmed the presence of Russian nuclear weapons in Belarus, including Russia's Oreshnik missile system.[75]

On 4 May 2025, in an interview to mark his 25 years of power in Russia, Putin remarked that "he hoped that there would be no need to use nuclear weapons" to bring the Russian invasion of Ukraine "to its logical conclusion".[76][77]

## Unintentional close calls

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Unintentional close calls")]

Unintentional use close calls may occur due to equipment failure. Common examples are strategic bombers accidentally dropping or crashing with nuclear bombs, or [early warning systems](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_warning_systems "Early warning systems") mistaking phenomena such as [weather events](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weather_events_during_wars "Weather events during wars") or non-nuclear rocket launches for an [ICBM](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ICBM "ICBM") [first strike](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_strike_\(nuclear_strategy\) "First strike \(nuclear strategy\)") and therefore recommending a [second strike](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_strike "Second strike").

### 1957: U.S. accidental bomb drop in New Mexico

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: 1957: U.S. accidental bomb drop in New Mexico")]

Main article: [Mark 17 nuclear bomb § 1957 incident](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_17_nuclear_bomb#1957_incident "Mark 17 nuclear bomb")

A [B-36](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B-36 "B-36") accidentally dropped a bomb just south of [Albuquerque](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albuquerque "Albuquerque"), [New Mexico](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Mexico "New Mexico"). Due to safety measures, the plutonium core was not mounted to the bomb at the time but rather stored elsewhere in the plane, preventing a [nuclear detonation](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_explosion "Nuclear explosion"). The conventional explosives created a 7.6 m (25 ft)-wide crater on impact.

### 1958: U.S. accidental bomb drop in Savannah, Georgia

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: 1958: U.S. accidental bomb drop in Savannah, Georgia")]

Main article: [1958 Mars Bluff B-47 nuclear weapon loss incident](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1958_Mars_Bluff_B-47_nuclear_weapon_loss_incident "1958 Mars Bluff B-47 nuclear weapon loss incident")

A bomb was mistakenly dropped by a [U.S. Air Force](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Air_Force "U.S. Air Force") [Boeing B-47E-LM Stratojet](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing_B-47E-LM_Stratojet "Boeing B-47E-LM Stratojet") near [Savannah](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia "Savannah, Georgia"), [Georgia](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georgia_\(U.S._state\) "Georgia \(U.S. state\)"), when a man in the [bomb bay](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bomb_bay "Bomb bay") area grabbed the emergency release pin by accident. Similar to the 1957 incident, safety precautions meant that the plutonium was not mounted to the bomb but rather stored elsewhere on the plane at the time.

### 1960: U.S. false alarm from moonrise

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: 1960: U.S. false alarm from moonrise")]

Radar equipment in [Thule](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thule_Air_Base "Thule Air Base"), [Greenland](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenland "Greenland"), mistakenly interpreted a [moonrise](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonrise_and_moonset "Moonrise and moonset") over [Norway](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norway "Norway") as a large-scale [Soviet](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") missile launch. Upon receiving a report of the supposed attack, [NORAD](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NORAD "NORAD") went on high alert. However, doubts about the authenticity of the attack arose due to the presence of Soviet leader [Nikita Khrushchev](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev "Nikita Khrushchev") in [New York City](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City "New York City") as head of the USSR's [United Nations](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") delegation.[78][79][80]

### 1961: U.S. strategic bomber crash in North Carolina

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: 1961: U.S. strategic bomber crash in North Carolina")]

See also: [1961 Goldsboro B-52 crash](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1961_Goldsboro_B-52_crash "1961 Goldsboro B-52 crash")

[](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Goldsboro_Bomb_Weapon_1_\(LA-UR-20-22180\).jpg)One of Mark 39 nuclear bombs after the 1961 incident near Goldsboro, North Carolina

A [B-52 Stratofortress](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B-52_Stratofortress "B-52 Stratofortress") carrying two 3–4-[megaton](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TNT_equivalent#Kiloton_and_megaton "TNT equivalent") [Mark 39 nuclear bombs](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_39_nuclear_bomb "Mark 39 nuclear bomb") broke up in mid-air near [Goldsboro](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldsboro,_North_Carolina "Goldsboro, North Carolina"), [North Carolina](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Carolina "North Carolina"), dropping its nuclear payload in the process.[81] The pilot in command, Walter Scott Tulloch, ordered the crew to [eject](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ejection_seat "Ejection seat") at 9,000 feet (2,700 m). Five crewmen successfully ejected or bailed out of the aircraft and landed safely, another ejected but did not survive the landing, and two died in the crash.[82]

Information declassified in 2013 showed that "only a single switch prevented the ... bomb from detonating and spreading fire and destruction over a wide area."[83] An expert evaluation written on 22 October 1969 by Parker F. Jones, the supervisor of the nuclear weapons safety department at [Sandia National Laboratories](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandia_National_Laboratories "Sandia National Laboratories"), reported that "one simple, [dynamo](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamo "Dynamo")-technology, low voltage switch stood between the United States and a major catastrophe", and that it "seems credible" that a short circuit in the Arm line during a mid-air breakup of the aircraft "could" have resulted in a nuclear explosion.[84]

### 1961: U.S. strategic bomber crash in California

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: 1961: U.S. strategic bomber crash in California")]

Main article: [1961 Yuba City B-52 crash](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1961_Yuba_City_B-52_crash "1961 Yuba City B-52 crash")

A [B-52 Stratofortress](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B-52_Stratofortress "B-52 Stratofortress") carrying two nuclear weapons experienced a catastrophic [decompression](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncontrolled_decompression "Uncontrolled decompression"), eventually forcing the crew to eject. The crewless plane crashed 15 miles west of [Yuba City](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuba_City "Yuba City"), [California](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California "California"). The bombs did not detonate due to safety features.

### 1961: U.S. false alarm from communications failure

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=29 "Edit section: 1961: U.S. false alarm from communications failure")]

Staff at the [Strategic Air Command](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_Air_Command "Strategic Air Command") Headquarters (SAC HQ) simultaneously lost contact with NORAD and multiple [Ballistic Missile Early Warning System](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ballistic_Missile_Early_Warning_System "Ballistic Missile Early Warning System") sites. Since these communication lines were designed to be redundant and independent from one another, the communications failure was interpreted as either a very unlikely coincidence or a coordinated attack. SAC HQ prepared the entire ready force for takeoff before already overhead aircraft confirmed that there did not appear to be an attack. It was later found that the failure of a single relay station in [Colorado](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colorado "Colorado") was the sole cause of the communications problem.[78]

### 1962: U.S. mistaken order during Cuban Missile Crisis

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=30 "Edit section: 1962: U.S. mistaken order during Cuban Missile Crisis")]

Further information: [United States nuclear weapons in Japan § Nuclear weapons accidents](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_nuclear_weapons_in_Japan#Nuclear_weapons_accidents "United States nuclear weapons in Japan")

According to a technician who served there, a mistaken order was issued by [Kadena Air Base](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kadena_Air_Base "Kadena Air Base") in [Okinawa](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Okinawa_Prefecture "Okinawa Prefecture") to nuclear missile sites in Okinawa to launch all their nuclear missiles. None were launched. A team responsible for four missiles at [Bolo Airfield](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolo_Airfield "Bolo Airfield") in [Yomitan](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yomitan "Yomitan") reported that the order's codes were in order, but the local officer in charge did not trust the order, partly because only one of their four missiles was targeted on Russia, and he saw no logic why missiles would be launched against China too, and because readiness was at [DEFCON](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DEFCON "DEFCON") 2, not DEFCON 1.[85] Others serving there at the time have made statements saying they doubt this incident ever happened.[86]

### 1964: U.S. strategic bomber crash in Maryland

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=31 "Edit section: 1964: U.S. strategic bomber crash in Maryland")]

Main article: [1964 Savage Mountain B-52 crash](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1964_Savage_Mountain_B-52_crash "1964 Savage Mountain B-52 crash")

A B-52 carrying nuclear bombs was severely damaged while flying in turbulence over Western [Pennsylvania](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pennsylvania "Pennsylvania"). The plane crashed in [Garrett County, Maryland](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garrett_County,_Maryland "Garrett County, Maryland"), at [Savage River State Forest](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Savage_River_State_Forest "Savage River State Forest"). Having completed an [Operation Chrome Dome](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Chrome_Dome "Operation Chrome Dome") mission, the plane was traveling from [Massachusetts](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massachusetts "Massachusetts") to [Georgia](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georgia_\(U.S._state\) "Georgia \(U.S. state\)") carrying two nuclear bombs. Reports on the condition of the bombs varied, with [Department of Defense](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense "United States Department of Defense") (DoD) saying they were "relatively intact" while [Sandia National Laboratories](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandia_National_Laboratories "Sandia National Laboratories") engineers saying they "broke apart" and that it was too risky to hastily move them. Within two days, the bombs were retrieved and taken to a local airport for transportation to Air Force facilities. The pilot ordered the crew to eject from the plane when it lost control. Three crew members died in the crash or due to exposure in the snowy winter conditions.

### 1965: U.S. false alarm from blackout computer errors

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=32 "Edit section: 1965: U.S. false alarm from blackout computer errors")]

The Command Center of the [Office of Emergency Planning](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives_Office_of_Emergency_Planning,_Preparedness,_and_Operations "United States House of Representatives Office of Emergency Planning, Preparedness, and Operations") went on full alert after a [massive power outage in the northeastern United States](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northeast_blackout_of_1965 "Northeast blackout of 1965"). Several nuclear bomb detectors—used to distinguish between regular power outages and power outages caused by a nuclear blast—near major U.S. cities malfunctioned due to circuit errors, creating the illusion of a [nuclear attack](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_warfare "Nuclear warfare").[5]

### 1965: U.S. attack aircraft falling off carrier

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=33 "Edit section: 1965: U.S. attack aircraft falling off carrier")]

Main article: [1965 Philippine Sea A-4 incident](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1965_Philippine_Sea_A-4_incident "1965 Philippine Sea A-4 incident")

During a training exercise in the [Philippine Sea](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippine_Sea "Philippine Sea"), a nuclear-armed [Douglas A-4 Skyhawk](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_A-4_Skyhawk "Douglas A-4 Skyhawk") attack aircraft fell off the side of the aircraft carrier [USS _Ticonderoga_](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USS_Ticonderoga_\(CV-14\) "USS Ticonderoga \(CV-14\)"). The jet, pilot and weapon were not recovered.

### 1966: U.S. strategic bomber crash in Spain

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=34 "Edit section: 1966: U.S. strategic bomber crash in Spain")]

Main article: [1966 Palomares B-52 crash](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1966_Palomares_B-52_crash "1966 Palomares B-52 crash")

A B-52G bomber and [KC-135](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KC-135 "KC-135") tanker crashed over the [Mediterranean Sea](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea"). The bomber was carrying nuclear weapons at the time. While the bombs did not detonate, they did [contaminate](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_contamination "Radioactive contamination") the area with radioactive material.

### 1966: French false alarm from weather (likely)

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=35 "Edit section: 1966: French false alarm from weather \(likely\)")]

In the early days of the French [Strategic Air Forces](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_Air_Forces_Command "Strategic Air Forces Command"), electrical transmissions were disrupted due to a thunderstorm, causing a wartime takeoff order to be displayed. The [French Air Force](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Air_and_Space_Force "French Air and Space Force") launched a [Mirage IV](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dassault_Mirage_IV "Dassault Mirage IV") with an [AN-11 atomic bomb](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AN-11_bomb "AN-11 bomb"). The crew was called back by radio, but did not respond, as required by procedure. When they reached their refueling zone, they were unable to find the supply plane, forcing them to abort their mission, turn back, and land.[87]

### 1967: U.S. false alarm from weather

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=36 "Edit section: 1967: U.S. false alarm from weather")]

A powerful [solar flare](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_flare "Solar flare") accompanied by a [coronal mass ejection](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection "Coronal mass ejection") interfered with multiple [NORAD](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NORAD "NORAD") radars over the [Northern Hemisphere](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere "Northern Hemisphere"). These radars included three [Ballistic Missile Early Warning Systems (BMEWS)](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ballistic_Missile_Early_Warning_System "Ballistic Missile Early Warning System") that had been recently upgraded, and only resumed operation eight days prior to the flare.[88] This interference was initially interpreted as [intentional jamming](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar_jamming_and_deception "Radar jamming and deception") of the radars by the Soviets, and thus an act of war. A nuclear bomber [counterstrike](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counterattack "Counterattack") was nearly launched by the United States.[89] The Strategic Air Command had prepared to launch fighters before NORAD alerted them of the solar flare.

### 1968: U.S. strategic bomber crash in Greenland

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=37 "Edit section: 1968: U.S. strategic bomber crash in Greenland")]

Main article: [1968 Thule Air Base B-52 crash](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1968_Thule_Air_Base_B-52_crash "1968 Thule Air Base B-52 crash")

A fire broke out on a nuclear-armed B-52 bomber just off Greenland, and the plane crashed into the sea without causing a detonation.

### 1979: U.S. false alarm from computer training scenario

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=38 "Edit section: 1979: U.S. false alarm from computer training scenario")]

Computer errors at the [NORAD](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NORAD "NORAD") headquarters in [Peterson Air Force Base](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peterson_Air_Force_Base "Peterson Air Force Base"), the [Strategic Air Command](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_Air_Command "Strategic Air Command") command post in [Offutt Air Force Base](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Offutt_Air_Force_Base "Offutt Air Force Base"), the [National Military Command Center](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Military_Command_Center "National Military Command Center") in the [Pentagon](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Pentagon "The Pentagon"), and the [Alternate National Military Command Center](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternate_National_Military_Command_Center "Alternate National Military Command Center") in the [Raven Rock Mountain Complex](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raven_Rock_Mountain_Complex "Raven Rock Mountain Complex") led to alarm and full preparation for a nonexistent large-scale Soviet attack.[5][78] NORAD notified [National Security Advisor](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Security_Advisor_\(United_States\) "National Security Advisor \(United States\)") [Zbigniew Brzezinski](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zbigniew_Brzezinski "Zbigniew Brzezinski") that the Soviet Union had launched 250 [ballistic missiles](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ballistic_missile "Ballistic missile") with a trajectory for the United States, stating that a decision to retaliate would need to be made by the president within three to seven minutes. NORAD computers then placed the number of incoming missiles at 2,200.[90] Strategic Air Command was notified, and nuclear bombers prepared for takeoff. Within six to seven minutes of the initial response, [PAVE PAWS](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PAVE_PAWS "PAVE PAWS") satellite and radar systems were able to confirm that the attack was a false alarm.[5][6][91]

[Congress](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Congress "United States Congress") quickly learned of the incident because Senator [Charles H. Percy](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_H._Percy "Charles H. Percy") was present at the NORAD headquarters during the panic. A [General Accounting Office](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_Accountability_Office "Government Accountability Office") investigation found that a training scenario was inadvertently loaded into an operational computer in the [Cheyenne Mountain Complex](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheyenne_Mountain_Complex "Cheyenne Mountain Complex").[5] Commenting on the incident, [U.S. State Department](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State "United States Department of State") adviser [Marshall Shulman](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshall_D._Shulman "Marshall D. Shulman") stated that "false alerts of this kind are not a rare occurrence. There is a complacency about handling them that disturbs me."[90] [Soviet General Secretary](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union "General Secretary of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union") [Leonid Brezhnev](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev "Leonid Brezhnev") composed a letter to U.S. President [Jimmy Carter](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jimmy_Carter "Jimmy Carter") that the false alarm was "fraught with a tremendous danger" and "I think you will agree with me that there should be no errors in such matters."[92] In the months following the incident, there were three more false alarms at NORAD, two of them caused by faulty [computer chips](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_circuit "Integrated circuit").[78] One of them forced the [National Emergency Airborne Command Post](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Emergency_Air-Borne_Command_Post "National Emergency Air-Borne Command Post") to taxi into position at [Andrews Air Force Base](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrews_Air_Force_Base "Andrews Air Force Base").[92]

### 1980: U.S. false alarm from Soviet missile exercise

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=39 "Edit section: 1980: U.S. false alarm from Soviet missile exercise")]

A Soviet submarine near the [Kuril Islands](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuril_Islands "Kuril Islands") launched four missiles as part of a [training exercise](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_exercise "Military exercise"). American early warning sensors determined one of the four to be aimed towards the United States. In response, the United States convened officials for a threat assessment conference, at which point it was determined to not be a threat and the situation was resolved.[78]

### 1980: Explosion at U.S. missile silo

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=40 "Edit section: 1980: Explosion at U.S. missile silo")]

Main article: [1980 Damascus Titan missile explosion](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1980_Damascus_Titan_missile_explosion "1980 Damascus Titan missile explosion")

An explosion in [Arkansas](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arkansas "Arkansas") ripped the doors off a silo and launched part of a nuclear missile out of the structure. The warhead landed 30m away, but safety features prevented either a detonation or release of radioactive material. The incident caused one death and 21 injuries, and sparked a congressional inquiry.[93]

### 1983: Soviet false alarm from weather (likely)

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=41 "Edit section: 1983: Soviet false alarm from weather \(likely\)")]

Main article: [1983 Soviet nuclear false alarm incident](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1983_Soviet_nuclear_false_alarm_incident "1983 Soviet nuclear false alarm incident")

Several weeks after the downing of [Korean Air Lines Flight 007](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_Air_Lines_Flight_007 "Korean Air Lines Flight 007") over Soviet airspace, a satellite early warning system near Moscow reported the launch of one American [Minuteman ICBM](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LGM-30_Minuteman "LGM-30 Minuteman"). Soon after, it reported that five missiles had been launched. Convinced that a real American offensive would involve many more missiles, Lieutenant Colonel [Stanislav Petrov](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanislav_Petrov "Stanislav Petrov") of the [Air Defense Forces](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Air_Defence_Forces "Soviet Air Defence Forces") refused to acknowledge the threat as legitimate and continued to convince his superiors that it was a false alarm until this could be confirmed by ground [radar](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar "Radar").[6][94][95][96][97][98][99][_[excessive citations](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Bundling_citations "Wikipedia:Citing sources")_]

### 1991: Tornado at U.S. strategic bomber airbase

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=42 "Edit section: 1991: Tornado at U.S. strategic bomber airbase")]

Main article: [1991 Andover tornado](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1991_Andover_tornado "1991 Andover tornado")

On April 26, 1991, a large tornado touched down southwest of the city of [Wichita, Kansas](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wichita,_Kansas "Wichita, Kansas"), heading towards the city of [Andover](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andover,_Kansas "Andover, Kansas"). As the tornado approached Andover, it struck the [McConnell Air Force Base](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/McConnell_Air_Force_Base "McConnell Air Force Base"), where it narrowly missed ten lined up [Rockwell B-1 Lancers](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rockwell_B-1_Lancer "Rockwell B-1 Lancer"), two of them armed with nuclear warheads.[100][101][102]

### 1995: Russian false alarm from Norwegian research rocket

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=43 "Edit section: 1995: Russian false alarm from Norwegian research rocket")]

Main article: [Norwegian rocket incident](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norwegian_rocket_incident "Norwegian rocket incident")

[Russian President](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Russia "President of Russia") [Boris Yeltsin](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boris_Yeltsin "Boris Yeltsin") became the first world leader to activate the [Russian nuclear briefcase](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheget "Cheget") after Russian radar systems detected the launch of what was later determined to be a Norwegian [Black Brant XII](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Brant_XII "Black Brant XII") research rocket being used to study the [northern lights](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora "Aurora").[103] Russian [ballistic missile submarines](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ballistic_missile_submarine "Ballistic missile submarine") were put on alert in preparation for a possible retaliatory strike.[104] When it became clear the rocket did not pose a threat to Russia and was not part of a larger attack, the alarm was cancelled. Russia was, in fact, one of a number of countries earlier informed of the launch; however, the information had not reached the Russian radar operators.[78]

### 2007: Improper transport of U.S. nuclear weapons

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_close_calls&action=edit&section=44 "Edit section: 2007: Improper transport of U.S. nuclear weapons")]

Main article: [2007 United States Air Force nuclear weapons incident](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2007_United_States_Air_Force_nuclear_weapons_incident "2007 United States Air Force nuclear weapons incident")

On 29 August 2007, six nuclear-armed [AGM-129 ACM](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AGM-129_ACM "AGM-129 ACM") [cruise missiles](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cruise_missile "Cruise missile") were mistakenly loaded onto a [United States Air Force](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Air_Force "United States Air Force") (USAF) [B-52H](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B-52H "B-52H") [heavy bomber](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_bomber "Heavy bomber") at [Minot Air Force Base](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minot_Air_Force_Base "Minot Air Force Base") in [North Dakota](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Dakota "North Dakota") and transported to [Barksdale Air Force Base](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barksdale_Air_Force_Base "Barksdale Air Force Base") in [Louisiana](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louisiana "Louisiana"). The nuclear warheads in the missiles were supposed to have been removed before the missiles were taken from their storage bunker. The missiles with the nuclear warheads were not reported missing and remained mounted to the aircraft at both Minot and Barksdale for 36 hours. During this period, the warheads were not protected by the various mandatory security precautions for nuclear weapons, and the government was not aware of their location. The incident was the [first of its kind](//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bent_Spear "Bent Spear") in 40 years in the United States and was later described by the media as "one of the worst breaches in U.S. nuclear weapons security in decades".[105]
