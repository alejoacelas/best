# Please Forgive the Errors in My Ways

I spend too much of my time talking and thinking about extreme versions of honesty. As a result, I often feel compelled to direct my thoughts to friends in ways that may reveal not only my lack of decorum but also my ignorance of their goals, their situation, their emotions, and even of the world itself and its workings.

Why do I do this?

Maybe I have a delusional account of the world, where every extra piece of information can only help you make better decisions. What better way to make all the best decisions than to have all the best information, which you acquire through the perfect honesty of everybody else?

Maybe I fantasize about amassing twenty-six billion dollars and becoming the wealthiest person under thirty. And when the report comes to me—"Sir, crypto prices have come down and our investment arm can't cover its losses. The only way to keep things afloat is to use some of the customer deposits."—I say, "Oh, but wouldn't that involve lying to our users? To EAs?" And I go on and not do that, and still manage to keep most of my wealth, and everyone in EA remains ambitious forever.

Maybe I don't know you at all. Maybe, through blurting an unrequested piece of advice, I've just shown you how little attention I've been paying and how little thought I give to all the vastness inside you. Maybe I've shown you that I have mixed motives beyond your wellness—that I am willing to cause you discomfort just for the thrill of following some abstract, unvalidated ideal.

Maybe I am so exhausted by the complexity of consequentialism in this history-defining moment and every decision feels like a thousand twists and turns I cannot anticipate. Maybe I want to grip tightly the hope that if I follow some simple maxims—be more honest, be more ambitious—I can capture the upside that my poor capacity for consequentialist reasoning would otherwise squander.

Maybe I can't wait to tell you about my day. Maybe if I pour my thoughts through you I won't have to make all the hard decisions alone. Maybe if I say what's in my mind loud enough, someone will see me and think, oh, do I love this kid. Maybe if you know me well enough we'll marry, and I'll know you as much as anyone can be known.

Maybe I've seen the world outside of EA and thought: isn't it great that we're so much more honest here? Maybe I've reached out to someone who months ago was a perfect stranger and said "Can you advise me on building a free competitor to your tool? It's for biosecurity" and have them become one of our most cherished advisors. Maybe I've emailed a famous EA and been enchanted by their candidness. Maybe I appreciated some curt words when they were meant to seek truth.

Maybe I've seen how much of the costs of honesty fall on the sender. Maybe I've seen how much it mortifies me to be rejected, to say a true thing and have the other person realize I'm not what they're looking for (not the right partner, not the right candidate). Maybe I still love them hard enough to feel proud of letting them make their choice.

Maybe I dislike small talk, and would rather ask you why you love your partner than hear which bus you took on the way here.

Maybe every argument I have heard against full honesty sounds like some version of "I just don't like how it feels." We have these pesky emotions, these weird preferences against hearing some things that might be true, and we should aspire to be stronger and see through them.

Though my motives may be impure and my reasoning may be faulty, I want to be honest, and I want it badly. I don't know if I can do this without being some tinge of hurtful and annoying.

So I ask, please forgive the errors in my ways, while I keep doing what I think is most important. I'm just trying to do good, and I'm confused about how, and I mean the best for you, and I mean the best for everyone.
