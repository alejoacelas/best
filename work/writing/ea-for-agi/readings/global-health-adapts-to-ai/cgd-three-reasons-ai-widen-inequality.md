# Three Reasons Why AI May Widen Global Inequality

Artificial intelligence is frequently promoted as capable of fundamentally transforming economies and societies. However, how AI will affect worldwide income distribution remains uncertain. Although AI should theoretically enhance productivity at the macro level, it risks expanding income gaps within nations by favoring highly educated professionals, eliminating routine jobs, and accumulating wealth among technology owners. An even more critical yet understudied concern involves the potential for AI to expand inequality between developed and developing nations.

## Three Key Concerns

**Richer nations possess superior capabilities to leverage AI advantages.** Wealthy countries benefit from advanced digital infrastructure, substantial AI research capacity, and sophisticated data management systems. In 2023, the United States attracted $67.2 billion in AI-related private investments, substantially exceeding other nations. Meanwhile, internet availability in low-income countries stands at merely 27 percent, versus 80 percent in upper-middle-income nations. Fixed broadband expenses consume 31 percent of monthly income in low-income countries compared to just 1 percent in wealthy ones. AI threatens to strengthen wealthy nations' dominance in high-value industries like finance and pharmaceuticals, while automation may undermine developing nations' competitive advantage in low-wage manufacturing and services, eliminating traditional development pathways.

**Poorer countries lack infrastructure to manage AI disruptions.** Advanced economies feature robust social safety nets and retraining initiatives; developing nations struggle with inadequate resources and underdeveloped labor protections. Many lower-income countries already contend with substantial informal employment and unstable labor markets, making workers exceptionally susceptible to economic shocks.

**AI challenges conventional development frameworks.** Export-focused manufacturing has historically absorbed workers transitioning from agriculture. However, AI increasingly requires capital intensity rather than labor, reducing employment capacity. Bangladesh exemplifies the danger: automation could eliminate roughly 60 percent of garment-industry positions by 2030. Service-focused alternatives (business process outsourcing) face comparable threats.

## Policy Solutions

- **Infrastructure investment:** treat internet and electricity as essential services (India's "Digital India").
- **Education emphasis:** integrate AI and data literacy into curricula (Vietnam).
- **International partnerships:** collaboration with advanced nations for knowledge transfer.
- **Local innovation support:** grassroots solutions to regional challenges (Rwanda's coding initiatives).
- **Workforce protection:** safety nets and retraining (Singapore's SkillsFuture).

## Conclusion

AI's trajectory regarding global inequality remains undetermined. Present policy decisions will determine whether AI exacerbates or alleviates worldwide disparities. Strategic investments in education, protective systems, and international cooperation could let developing nations harness AI's benefits while minimizing harmful disruptions.
