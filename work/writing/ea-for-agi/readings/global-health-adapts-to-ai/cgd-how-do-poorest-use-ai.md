# (How) Do the Poorest Use AI?

Major AI companies have begun sharing detailed usage reports, but these focus on employment impacts affecting knowledge workers. This overlooks billions of lower-income people in developing nations who prioritize different needs—health advice, business guidance, and educational access rather than job-displacement concerns.

Bjorkegren notes that poor countries still use AI far less than rich countries, yet current statistics miss whether the poorest populations actually benefit. Early research showed teachers in Sierra Leone used AI more than search engines, primarily for fact-checking.

The author proposes three analytical approaches:

**1. Are the poorest using AI?** Labs should measure device types, language preferences, and geographic distribution. Results likely show wealthy early adopters dominating, with limited rural access and heavy English-language bias despite local-language prevalence.

**2. What do the poor use AI for?** Rather than applying knowledge-worker taxonomies, researchers should develop poverty-specific categories covering agriculture, health, government navigation, and business advice. Early evidence suggests educational and health queries predominate among lower-income users.

**3. Understanding barriers.** Analyzing failure or abandonment rates by language, and comparing actual usage against population demographics, reveals whether adoption reflects capability gaps or access limitations.

Bjorkegren concludes these simple, feasible analyses would clarify whether AI genuinely serves humanity broadly or concentrates benefits among the wealthy.
