## Abstract

AI alignment efforts and proposals try to make AI systems ethical, safe and beneficial for humans by making them follow human intentions, preferences or values. However, these proposals largely disregard the vast majority of moral patients in existence: non-human animals. AI systems aligned through proposals which largely disregard concern for animal welfare pose significant near-term and long-term animal welfare risks. In this paper, we argue that we should prevent harm to non-human animals, when this does not involve significant costs, and therefore that we have strong moral reasons to at least align AI systems with a basic level of concern for animal welfare. We show how AI alignment with such a concern could be achieved, and why we should expect it to significantly reduce the harm non-human animals would otherwise endure as a result of continued AI development. We provide some recommended policies that AI companies and governmental bodies should consider implementing to ensure basic animal welfare protection.

## 1 Introduction

AI ethics is a nascent field that is quickly gaining attention. A lot of AI ethicists argue that AI ought to be developed in ways that benefit all humans (sometimes the phrasing is “everyone”, but here “everyone” clearly means “every human”). Leaders of major AI companies and policy makers frequently express this vision. Yet, despite non-human animals being more that 99.9% of sentient beings in existence (Tomasik, [2019](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR80 "Tomasik, B. \(2019\). How many wild animals are there? 
                  https://reducing-suffering.org/how-many-wild-animals-are-there/
                  
                "); Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution, [2019](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR86 "Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution \(2019\). Bristlemouth dominance: How do we know? 
                  https://twilightzone.whoi.edu/bristlemouth-dominance-how-do-we-know/
                  
                ")), to our knowledge among more than 70 ethical statements about AI, only two explicitly mention animal welfare (i.e. the Montreal Declaration for Responsible AI and the Serbian government’s AI ethics statement of February 2023).

In academia, there have been only a handful of scholars who work on AI ethics who have mentioned the importance of non-human animals in their work. Owe and Baum ([2021](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR60 "Owe, A., & Baum, S. D. \(2021\). Moral consideration of nonhumans in the ethics of artificial intelligence. AI and Ethics, 1\(4\), 517–528.")) and Singer and Tse ([2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR74 "Singer, P., & Tse, Y. F. \(2023\). AI ethics: The case for including animals. AI and Ethics, 3\(2\), 539–551.")) stressed that the prevailing AI ethics field has largely ignored non-human beings, and they call for their inclusion on the grounds that we should extend moral consideration to them. Coghlan and Parker ([2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR18 "Coghlan, S., & Parker, C. \(2023\). Harm to nonhuman animals from AI: A systematic account and framework. Philosophy & Technology, 36, 25.")), offered a systematic account of the ways AI technologies could harm animals, emphasising that such harms have been largely overlooked in mainstream AI ethics discussions. Hagendorff et al. ([2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR32 "Hagendorff, T., Bossert, L. N., Tse, Y. F., & Singer, P. \(2023\). Speciesist bias in AI: How AI applications perpetuate discrimination and unfair outcomes against animals. AI Ethics, 3, 717–734.")) introduced the notion of “speciesist bias in AI”, showing that many applications—from computer vision to large language models (LLM)—can perpetuate discrimination against animals. These and other authors argue that AI ethics and fairness frameworks must be expanded beyond their current anthropocentric scope to include the interests and welfare of animals. Furthermore, recent progress has been made in the identification of speciesist biases in text-to-image AI (Sheng et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR69 "Sheng, K., Tuyttens, F. A., & von Keyserlingk, M. A. \(2025\). The erasure of intensive livestock farming in text-to-image generative AI. arXiv Preprint arXiv:2502.19771.")), and the development of benchmarks assessing LLMs’ tendencies to produce outputs that are harmful to animals or reinforce harmful attitudes against them (Kanepajs et al., [2025](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR44 "Kanepajs, A., Basu, A., Ghose, S., Li, C., Mehta, A., Mehta, R., Tucker-Davis, S. D., Fischer, B., & Anthis, J. R. \(2025\). What do large language models say about animals? Investigating risks of animal harm in generated text. In Proceedings of the 2025 ACM Conference on Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency \(pp. 1387–1410\). Association for Computing Machinery.")).

Despite this emerging body of work in AI ethics in relation to animals, the specific question of aligning AI systems’ goals and values to include concerns for animal welfare has remained under-explored. AI alignment is a distinct study that is now receiving considerable attention and has obvious ethical motivations and implications. Its leading proponents share the view of AI ethicists that AI should be beneficial to all humans. But while AI ethicists focus more on making sure the use and deployment of AI systems are beneficial and ethical, the field of AI alignment is focused specifically on solving what has been called the technical AI alignment problem: the challenge of building AI systems such that they do (and genuinely try to do) what we want them to do (Dung, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR22 "Dung, L. \(2023\). Current cases of AI misalignment and their implications for future risks. Synthese, 202, 138.")). However, solving the technical alignment problem is not sufficient for making AI systems safe and beneficial. If the AI designers align AI systems with malicious ends, the AI system might be extremely harmful (Friederich, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR27 "Friederich, S. \(2023\). Symbiosis, not alignment, as the goal for Liberal democracies in the transition to artificial general intelligence. AI and Ethics, 4, 315–324.")). As a result, for AI systems to be safe and beneficial, we also have to solve what has been called “the ethical alignment problem” (Dung, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR22 "Dung, L. \(2023\). Current cases of AI misalignment and their implications for future risks. Synthese, 202, 138.")) or the “value alignment problem”: determining which values or whose values AI systems should ultimately be aligned with (Gabriel, [2020](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR28 "Gabriel, I. \(2020\). Artificial intelligence, values, and alignment. Minds and Machines, 30, 411–437.")).

With rare exceptions (Ziesche, [2021](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR91 "Ziesche, S. \(2021\). AI ethics and value alignment for nonhuman animals. Philosophies, 6\(2\), 31."); Moret, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR51 "Moret, A. R. \(2023\). Taking into account sentient non-humans in AI ambitious value learning: Sentientist coherent extrapolated volition. Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Consciousness, 10\(02\), 309–334.")), discussions of AI alignment have neglected non-human animals’ interests. Both Ziesche ([2021](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR91 "Ziesche, S. \(2021\). AI ethics and value alignment for nonhuman animals. Philosophies, 6\(2\), 31.")) and Moret ([2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR51 "Moret, A. R. \(2023\). Taking into account sentient non-humans in AI ambitious value learning: Sentientist coherent extrapolated volition. Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Consciousness, 10\(02\), 309–334.")) have offered initial arguments that limiting AI value alignment to human values and preferences is both morally unjustified in principle and would lead to undesirable consequences, with Moret also discussing implications for potentially sentient AI systems (see also Moret, [2025](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR52 "Moret, A. \(2025\). AI welfare risks. Philosophical Studies. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1007/s11098-025-02343-7
                  
                ")). For instance, while Ziesche ([2021](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR91 "Ziesche, S. \(2021\). AI ethics and value alignment for nonhuman animals. Philosophies, 6\(2\), 31.")) discusses potential suffering and existential risks to non-human animals resulting from their exclusion from AI alignment, Moret ([2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR51 "Moret, A. R. \(2023\). Taking into account sentient non-humans in AI ambitious value learning: Sentientist coherent extrapolated volition. Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Consciousness, 10\(02\), 309–334.")) focuses specifically on suffering risks. Both Ziesche and Moret made tentative proposals for incorporating full concern for animal welfare (in the case of Moret’s paper, all sentient beings’ welfare) in AI alignment. Ziesche’s work proposed methods by which the values and preferences of animals could be directly extracted, while Moret’s focus is on a theoretically ambitious value-learning alignment proposal applicable to artificial superintelligences and related scenarios in the more distant future. In this paper, we build on these previous works by developing a much more complete account of why we ought to include non-human animals in AI alignment, and examining the potential consequences of not doing so in depth. This paper also differs from Ziesche’s and Moret’s previous work in its discussion of practical applications and formulation of recommendations. Specifically, our aim is to present the most straightforward case for the inclusion of animals in AI alignment, while also proposing specific policies that could have the most impact in reducing risks of harm to non-human animals. As a result, in contrast to Ziesche’s and Moret’s previous work, our policy recommendations will focus on low-cost, realistic proposals that can be implemented using current alignment techniques, and which we expect to have significant positive near-to-long-term impact on the future welfare of animals.

Apart from these two exceptions, there have been no further proposals to include the welfare of non-human animals in the scope of AI alignment. Instead, when, in rare cases, the welfare of non-human animals has been mentioned in relation to AI alignment, the need to include consideration of their welfare has been dismissed by the idea that by aligning AI systems with human interests, consideration for animal welfare would already be sufficiently represented (Russell, [2019](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR64 "Russell, S. \(2019\). Human compatible: AI and the problem of control. Penguin UK.")) – a claim we disagree with.

In this paper, we will argue that the omission (explicit or implicit) of non-human animals in the project or proposals of AI alignment is unjustifiable and harmful. As a result, we propose that AI systems should be aligned with at least a basic level of consideration for animals and animal welfare. To make our case we present the following argument the premises of which will be defended in each corresponding section:

  * Premise 1 (Section 2): We have strong moral reasons against causing harm to non-human animals.

  * Premise 2 (Section 2): If we have strong moral reasons against causing harm to non-human animals, we have strong reasons to significantly reduce risks posed to animal welfare.

  * Premise 3 (Section 4): Current AI alignment efforts and proposals pose significant near-term and long-term animal welfare risks due to largely omitting concern for animal welfare in AI systems’ goals.

  * Premise 4 (Section 5): By including a basic level of consideration for animal welfare in AI systems’ goals, we can significantly reduce the animal welfare risks posed by current AI alignment efforts and proposals.

  * Conclusion: We have strong reasons to include _at least_ a basic level of consideration for animal welfare in AI systems’ goals and values.




Section 3 provides an overview of AI alignment and examines the types of AI systems likely to emerge in the near to mid-term future (readers already familiar with these topics may skip this section). In Sect. 6, we will argue against two potential objections to our proposal and in the conclusion we will present some policy recommendations.

## 2 We Have Strong Moral Reasons against Causing Harm to Non-Human Animals

If a given entity, such as a non-human animal, has moral standing (i.e. if it or its interests morally matter for the entity’s own sake Jaworska and Tannenbaum [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR40 "Jaworska, A., & Tannenbaum, J. \(2023\). The grounds of moral status. In The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy \(Spring 2023 ed.\).")), it deserves moral consideration (i.e. we should take its interests and welfare into account when choosing how to act). Sentience is the capacity to have positive and negative experiences like pleasure or pain. Because beings with these capacities can be positively and negatively affected, it is widely agreed that sentience is sufficient for moral standing (Sumner, [1996](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR76 "Sumner, L. W. \(1996\). Welfare, happiness, and ethics. Oxford University Press."); McMahan, [2002](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR50 "McMahan, J. \(2002\). The ethics of killing: Problems at the margins of life. Oxford University Press."); Singer, [2009a](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR71 "Singer, P. \(2009a\). Animal liberation: The definitive classic of the animal movement. Harper Perennial Modern Classics."), [b](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR72 "Singer, P. \(2009b\). Speciesism and moral status. Metaphilosophy, 40\(3–4\), 567–581."), [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR74 "Singer, P., & Tse, Y. F. \(2023\). AI ethics: The case for including animals. AI and Ethics, 3\(2\), 539–551."); Birch, [2017](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR10 "Birch, J. \(2017\). Animal sentience and the precautionary principle. Animal Sentience: an Interdisciplinary Journal on animal Feeling, 2\(16\), 1–15."); Korsgaard, [2018](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR46 "Korsgaard, C. M. \(2018\). Fellow creatures: Our obligations to the other animals. Oxford University Press."); Horta, [2018](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR39 "Horta, O. \(2018\). Moral considerability and the argument from relevance. Journal of Agricultural and Environmental Ethics, 31\(3\), 369–388."); Shepherd, [2018](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR70 "Shepherd, J. \(2018\). Consciousness and moral status. Routledge."); Sebo, [2018](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR67 "Sebo, J. \(2018\). The moral problem of other Minds. The Harvard Review of Philosophy, 25\(1\), 51–70."); Van der Deijl, [2021](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR81 "Van der Deijl, W. \(2021\). The sentience argument for experientialism about welfare. Philosophical Studies, 178\(1\), 187–208."); Jaworska and Tannenbaum, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR40 "Jaworska, A., & Tannenbaum, J. \(2023\). The grounds of moral status. In The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy \(Spring 2023 ed.\)."); Andrews et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR2 "Andrews, K., Birch, J., & Sebo, J. \(2024\). The New York Declaration on Animal Consciousness. 
                  https://sites.google.com/nyu.edu/nydeclaration/declaration
                  
                "); Perry, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR62 "Perry, M. W. \(2024\). Why sentience should be the only basis of moral status. The Journal of Ethics, 28, 719–741. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1007/s10892-024-09487-4
                  
                ")). There is strong scientific support for attributions of conscious experience to other mammals and to birds and at least a realistic possibility of conscious experience in all vertebrates (including reptiles, amphibians, and fishes) (Andrews et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR2 "Andrews, K., Birch, J., & Sebo, J. \(2024\). The New York Declaration on Animal Consciousness. 
                  https://sites.google.com/nyu.edu/nydeclaration/declaration
                  
                ")). Consequently, as is also widely agreed upon, we should extend _some_ moral consideration to all the above non-human animals and any other ones for which, now or at some future time, the best available evidence favours a realistic possibility of conscious experience. This implies that it is wrong to arbitrarily cause harm to non-human animals by causing them to suffer (Singer, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR74 "Singer, P., & Tse, Y. F. \(2023\). AI ethics: The case for including animals. AI and Ethics, 3\(2\), 539–551.")) and possibly also by frustrating their desires (Bruckner, [2021](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR14 "Bruckner, D. W. \(2021\). Human and animal well-being. Pacific Philosophical Quarterly, 102\(3\), 393–412.")), provided that we can do so without making significant sacrifices.

Granting that non-human animals deserve _some_ moral consideration is sufficient to make our case in the paper. However, the reasons we have against harming non-human animals are even stronger than this, given that (as most ethicists who discuss the moral status of animals agree) we should not only extend some moral consideration to non-human animals but rather, they deserve _equal_ moral consideration (Singer, [2009a](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR71 "Singer, P. \(2009a\). Animal liberation: The definitive classic of the animal movement. Harper Perennial Modern Classics."), [b](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR72 "Singer, P. \(2009b\). Speciesism and moral status. Metaphilosophy, 40\(3–4\), 567–581."), [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR74 "Singer, P., & Tse, Y. F. \(2023\). AI ethics: The case for including animals. AI and Ethics, 3\(2\), 539–551."); Horta, [2018](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR39 "Horta, O. \(2018\). Moral considerability and the argument from relevance. Journal of Agricultural and Environmental Ethics, 31\(3\), 369–388."); Brouwer & Van der Deijl, [2020](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR13 "Brouwer, H., & van der Deijl, W. \(2020\). All animals are equal, but some more than others? Journal of Moral Philosophy, 17\(3\), 342–357.")). This means that we have as strong a reason to prevent the same amount of harm (such as a given amount of suffering) to a human as to a non-human animal. All else equal, the interests of non-human animals, whatever they may be, should be weighed equally with the similar interests of humans: “How bad a pain is depends on how intense it is and how long it lasts, but pains of the same intensity and duration are equally bad, whether felt by humans or animals” (Singer, [2009a](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR71 "Singer, P. \(2009a\). Animal liberation: The definitive classic of the animal movement. Harper Perennial Modern Classics."): 45). This does not imply that killing a human is equally wrong as killing a mouse, nor that we should treat non-human animals identically to humans, given their different capacities. Just as preventing adults from voting harms them more than preventing children from doing so, despite equal consideration of their interests, certain actions can harm animals with different capacities differently. Loud sounds may harm dogs more than humans, while death may be less harmful to mice than to humans, given that humans would be deprived of significantly more welfare in their future life (McMahan, [2002](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR50 "McMahan, J. \(2002\). The ethics of killing: Problems at the margins of life. Oxford University Press.")). Sexism and racism are forms of discrimination that partially or totally discount the interests of certain individuals because they are not of a certain sex, gender or race. Analogously, speciesism (according to which we can partially or totally discount the interests of non-human animals due to them not being members of the species _Homo sapiens_ , or animal species humans are fond of, such as cats and dogs) is also unjustified.

On the one hand, this is so because being human (i.e. having human DNA (Baker & Bradley, [2006](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR7 "Baker, R. J., & Bradley, R. D. \(2006\). Speciation in mammals and the genetic species concept. Journal of Mammalogy, 87\(4\), 643–662.")) or having certain capacities to produce fertile offspring with other humans (De Queiroz, [2005](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR20 "De Queiroz, K. \(2005\). Ernst Mayr and the modern concept of species. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America, 102\(suppl_1\), 6600–6607.")), like other biological attributes such as skin colour, does not inherently determine one’s capacity for welfare and is irrelevant to determining who we should take into account when acting. On the other hand, according to the argument from species overlap or marginal cases (Singer, [2009a](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR71 "Singer, P. \(2009a\). Animal liberation: The definitive classic of the animal movement. Harper Perennial Modern Classics."), [b](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR72 "Singer, P. \(2009b\). Speciesism and moral status. Metaphilosophy, 40\(3–4\), 567–581."), [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR73 "Singer, P. \(2023\). Animal liberation now. Harper Perennial."); Horta, [2014](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR38 "Horta, O. \(2014\). The scope of the argument from species overlap. Journal of Applied Philosophy, 31\(2\), 142–154.")), there is no morally relevant attribute that is possessed by all humans to the same degree, but to a lower degree or not at all by non-human animals. Hence, if one were to appeal to attributes usually posed by humans such as rationality, moral agency, self-consciousness or the ability to use language to justify why they deserve greater moral consideration than non-human animals, one would also have to extend lesser moral consideration to all the humans who do not possess any such capacities (like some infants or humans with severe cognitive disabilities). Since doing so would be unjustified, we should also extend equal moral consideration to all sentient non-human animals. Thus, we have the same or at least very similar moral reasons to prevent causing the same amount of harm to any sentient non-human animal, as we do to a human.

All approaches to rational decision-making under uncertainty agree that the fact that a risk is uncertain is no reason to ignore it and that we should _at least_ take non-negligible risks (i.e. risks above a particular probability threshold) into account when choosing how to act (MacAskill et al., [2020](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR49 "MacAskill, W., Bykvist, K., & Ord, T. \(2020\). Moral uncertainty. Oxford University Press."); Steele & Stefánsson, [2020](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR75 "Steele, K., & Stefánsson, H. O. \(2020\). Decision theory. In The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy \(Winter 2020 ed.\).")). Because of this, if we can significantly reduce certain risks of causing harm to humans by, for instance, not driving drunk, we have strong moral reasons to do so, all else equal. Similarly, because we should extend equal moral consideration to non-human animals, we also have strong moral reasons to significantly reduce any risks of significantly harming them, all else equal.

## 3 Frontier AI Alignment and Capabilities

To understand our proposal for including animal welfare in AI alignment, we must first examine what AI alignment is and the most prominent techniques. Additionally, since we will argue that AI systems lacking concern for animal welfare pose future welfare risks to non-human animals, we need to consider the AI systems likely to emerge in coming years or decades. This section develops both backgrounds.

### 3.1 Frontier AI Alignment

AI alignment addresses the fundamental challenge of ensuring AI systems pursue goals consistent with human intentions and values (Gabriel, [2020](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR28 "Gabriel, I. \(2020\). Artificial intelligence, values, and alignment. Minds and Machines, 30, 411–437."); Ji et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR41 "Ji, J., Qiu, T., Chen, B., Zhang, B., Lou, H., Wang, K., Duan, Y., He, Z., Zhou, J., Zhang, Z., Zeng, F., Dai, J., Pan, X., Ng, K. Y., O’Gara, A., Xu, H., Tse, B., Fu, J., McAleer, S., & Gao, W. \(2023\). AI alignment: A comprehensive survey. arXiv preprint arXiv:2310.19852.")), a problem that has been recognised as both critical and highly difficult for many years (e.g., Bostrom, [2014](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR11 "Bostrom, N. \(2014\). Superintelligence: Paths, dangers, strategies. Oxford University Press."); Amodei et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR1 "Amodei, D., Olah, C., Steinhardt, J., Christiano, P., Schulman, J., & Mané, D. \(2016\). Concrete problems in AI safety. arXiv preprint arXiv:1606.06565."); Yudkowsky, [2016a](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR89 "Yudkowsky, E. \(2016a\). The AI alignment problem: Why it is hard, and where to start. Symbolic Systems Distinguished Speaker, 4\(1\), 1–20.")). As AI systems become increasingly powerful and autonomous, misalignment risks producing harmful outcomes, with advanced AI systems potentially pursuing proxy goals that diverge from genuine human interests and cause catastrophic consequences (Ji et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR41 "Ji, J., Qiu, T., Chen, B., Zhang, B., Lou, H., Wang, K., Duan, Y., He, Z., Zhou, J., Zhang, Z., Zeng, F., Dai, J., Pan, X., Ng, K. Y., O’Gara, A., Xu, H., Tse, B., Fu, J., McAleer, S., & Gao, W. \(2023\). AI alignment: A comprehensive survey. arXiv preprint arXiv:2310.19852.")). Consequently, achieving proper alignment is critical for safe AI development.

However, it’s important to distinguish AI alignment from broader AI safety efforts. AI safety encompasses reducing all AI risks, including software bugs, robustness issues, misuse prevention, ethical issues, accountability, and AI policies. In contrast, AI alignment specifically focuses on goal-directed behaviour. A model could be technically reliable yet still misaligned if it optimises the wrong objective. For example, an AI might maximise proxy rewards (like click-through rates) while undermining actual user satisfaction, representing alignment failure rather than technical unreliability.

According to (Ji et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR41 "Ji, J., Qiu, T., Chen, B., Zhang, B., Lou, H., Wang, K., Duan, Y., He, Z., Zhou, J., Zhang, Z., Zeng, F., Dai, J., Pan, X., Ng, K. Y., O’Gara, A., Xu, H., Tse, B., Fu, J., McAleer, S., & Gao, W. \(2023\). AI alignment: A comprehensive survey. arXiv preprint arXiv:2310.19852.")), there are four pillars in AI alignment: Learning from feedback, learning under distribution shift, assurance, and governance. Methods that are used in the latter three include mechanistic interpretability (assurance), adversarial training (learning under distribution shift), evaluations using benchmarks (assurance and governance). But in this section, we will focus more on preference learning methods (which at the same time tackle problems caused by distribution shift) that will serve as a useful glance at what frontier AI alignment research is like. Researchers have developed several techniques to let AI models learn human values and preferences. Here we will focus on LLMs as examples, as they are today’s most popular and most generative AI systems, which (as we will see) will likely yield the most capabilities advances.

One prominent alignment technique used in LLM alignment is _Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback_ (RLHF) (Amodei et al., [2016](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR1 "Amodei, D., Olah, C., Steinhardt, J., Christiano, P., Schulman, J., & Mané, D. \(2016\). Concrete problems in AI safety. arXiv preprint arXiv:1606.06565."); Ji et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR41 "Ji, J., Qiu, T., Chen, B., Zhang, B., Lou, H., Wang, K., Duan, Y., He, Z., Zhou, J., Zhang, Z., Zeng, F., Dai, J., Pan, X., Ng, K. Y., O’Gara, A., Xu, H., Tse, B., Fu, J., McAleer, S., & Gao, W. \(2023\). AI alignment: A comprehensive survey. arXiv preprint arXiv:2310.19852.")). RLHF keeps humans “in the loop”: First, a reward model is trained to assign numerical scores to the LLM’s responses to particular prompts, based on the preference rankings obtained from human crowd workers who reviewed and ranked them based on the criteria of Helpfulness, Harmlessness and Honesty criteria (Askell et al., [2021](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR5 "Askell, A., Bai, Y., Chen, A., Drain, D., Ganguli, D., Henighan, T., Jones, A., Joseph, N., Mann, B., DasSarma, N., Elhage, N., Hatfield-Dodds, Z., Hernandez, D., Kernion, J., Ndousse, K., Olsson, C., Amodei, D., Brown, T., Clark, J., & Kaplan, J. \(2021\). A general language assistant as a laboratory for alignment. arXiv preprint arXiv:2112.00861.")). The reward model then provides feedback signals for reinforcement learning to fine-tune the LLM’s parameters. Through this process, the LLM becomes more aligned with human preferences by adhering to these 3 H principles, as shown by Askell et al. ([2021](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR5 "Askell, A., Bai, Y., Chen, A., Drain, D., Ganguli, D., Henighan, T., Jones, A., Joseph, N., Mann, B., DasSarma, N., Elhage, N., Hatfield-Dodds, Z., Hernandez, D., Kernion, J., Ndousse, K., Olsson, C., Amodei, D., Brown, T., Clark, J., & Kaplan, J. \(2021\). A general language assistant as a laboratory for alignment. arXiv preprint arXiv:2112.00861.")) it yields behaviour better reflecting human intent than training on unlabelled data.

Another influential alignment technique is _Constitutional AI_ (Bai et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR6 "Bai, Y., Kadavath, S., Kundu, S., Askell, A., Kernion, J., Jones, A., Chen, A., Goldie, A., Mirhoseini, A., McKinnon, C., Chen, C., Olsson, C., Olah, C., Hernandez, D., Drain, D., Ganguli, D., Li, D., Tran-Johnson, E., Perez, E., & Kaplan, J. \(2022\). Constitutional ai: Harmlessness from ai feedback. arXiv preprint arXiv:2212.08073.")), a form of reinforcement learning from AI feedback (RLAIF) (Ji et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR41 "Ji, J., Qiu, T., Chen, B., Zhang, B., Lou, H., Wang, K., Duan, Y., He, Z., Zhou, J., Zhang, Z., Zeng, F., Dai, J., Pan, X., Ng, K. Y., O’Gara, A., Xu, H., Tse, B., Fu, J., McAleer, S., & Gao, W. \(2023\). AI alignment: A comprehensive survey. arXiv preprint arXiv:2310.19852.")), used by Anthropic. This method provides models with explicit principles in a “Constitution” specifying desired and prohibited outputs. The model learns through supervised training on examples where it critiques and revises responses according to constitutional principles, such as avoiding harmful advice. These refined responses are then used to train the model to prefer constitutionally compliant answers. Human oversight occurs only through the principles themselves, chosen directly by developers or through deliberation processes overseen and designed by them.

Another prominent recent alignment technique is _deliberative alignment_ (Guan et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR31 "Guan, M. Y., Joglekar, M., Wallace, E., Jain, S., Barak, B., Helyar, A., Dias, R., Vallone, A., Ren, H., Wei, J., Chung, H. W., Toyer, S., Heidecke, J., Beutel, A., & Glaese, A. \(2024\). Deliberative alignment: Reasoning enables safer language models. arXiv preprint arXiv:2412.16339.")), where models are trained to internalise detailed specifications of normative instructions, principles and safety guidelines (found in a document called the ModelSpec) and actively reason with them during output generation. Models learn to check potential answers against the given principles through training on chain-of-thought examples that reference specifications. This creates _self-policing_ behaviour where models explicitly follow internalised normative instructions, producing reasoning traces that justify compliance with the ModelSpec. OpenAI has used this technique to align their recent _o-series_ models.

In sum, techniques like RLHF, Constitutional AI, and deliberative alignment all incorporate human oversight by establishing normative instructions or principles, whether through documents guiding human evaluators in output assessment or through directly authored constitutions and ModelSpecs, thereby equipping AI systems with mechanisms to maintain alignment with human preferences, values, and intentions in their outputs.

### 3.2 Frontier and Future AI Capabilities

AI systems have advanced rapidly in recent years, demonstrating remarkable achievements across diverse domains. Current AIs can defeat humans at strategy games, score highly on standardised tests, design drugs, make scientific breakthroughs, achieve gold medal standards at the International Mathematical Olympiad, and converse compellingly with humans while displaying various hints of general intelligence. These systems increasingly operate as automated decision-makers in finance and hiring, generative content creators, and autonomous agents that execute multi-step actions from recommendation engines to digital assistants managing emails and scheduling.

This rapid advancement is driven by the trend of increasing returns to scale in data and computation (Villalobos, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR82 "Villalobos, P. \(2023\). Scaling Laws Literature Review. Epochai.Org. 
                  https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/6L9EhCa8Zo2GoThGB/scaling-laws-literature-review
                  
                ")) and intensifying competition between leading AI companies that explicitly aim at creating general-purpose human-level AI.Footnote 1 These capabilities are likely to continue progressing rapidly in coming years as competition intensifies and hardware and algorithms improve. Furthermore, if human-level AI is achieved, developers will face strategic and economic incentives not to stop at human-level capabilities, meaning we may soon find ourselves in a world with human-level AI systems or beyond.

Given these factors, further advances appear likely in the coming years or decades, and without strong regulations to slow the pace of progress, we will likely witness the development and deployment of AI systems significantly more capable and agentic than current frontier models. Indeed, leading AI scientists and researchers believe there is a significant chance that human-level and superhuman general-purpose AI will be developed in this timeframe.Footnote 2

Should this occur under current transformer-based paradigms, LLMs with human-level or superhuman capabilities could be integrated with dedicated software to develop advanced language agents. Given that embodied multimodal models (such as Google’s PaLM-E [Driess et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR21 "Driess, D., Xia, F., Sajjadi, M. S. M., Lynch, C., Chowdhery, A., Ichter, B., Wahid, A., Tompson, J., Vuong, Q., Yu, T., Huang, W., Chebotar, Y., Sermanet, P., Duckworth, D., Levine, S., Vanhoucke, V., Hausman, K., Toussaint, M., Greff, K., Zeng, A., Mordatch, I., & Florence, P. \(2023\). PaLM-E: An Embodied Multimodal Language Model. arXiv preprint arXiv:2303.03378.")]) are already showing proof-of-concept capability to control robotic systems, this could lead to human-level or superhuman robotic systems governed by advanced language agents. Economic incentives might drive such development, particularly if these systems prove more efficient than human workers across diverse tasks.

Importantly, certainty about advanced AI scenarios is not required to establish the claim that AI systems pose welfare risks to animals. As we will see, near-term risks already manifest through current AI applications and their possible deployments. Furthermore, assigning moderate credence to the development of human-level or superhuman AI systems (including agentic ones) in the coming years, decades, or century will also be sufficient to establish long-term risks. These considerations, as we will argue, will suffice to provide strong grounds for including animals in AI alignment.

## 4 Current AI Alignment Efforts and Proposals Pose Significant Risks of Harm to Non-Human Animals

### 4.1 How are Animals Disregarded by AI Alignment Proposals?

Although the field of alignment is typically focused on solving the technical alignment problem, it has plausibly been argued that for any kind of alignment, there are some aspects of the proposal that are inevitably normative and therefore cannot be left to the AI to be figured out. Such aspects include: ‘standing, concerning whose ethical views [or morally relevant interests] are included; measurement, concerning how their views [or morally relevant interests] are identified; and aggregation, concerning how individual views [or morally relevant interests] are combined to a single view that will guide AI behaviour’ (Baum, [2020](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR9 "Baum, S. D. \(2020\). Social choice ethics in artificial intelligence. AI & Society, 35\(1\), 165–176."); Moret, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR51 "Moret, A. R. \(2023\). Taking into account sentient non-humans in AI ambitious value learning: Sentientist coherent extrapolated volition. Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Consciousness, 10\(02\), 309–334.")). Different kinds of technical solutions could be used for loading certain goals or values into the reward functions of AI systems and thus cause the AI system to behave one way or another, but “there is no way to ‘bracket out’ normative questions altogether” (Gabriel, [2020](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR28 "Gabriel, I. \(2020\). Artificial intelligence, values, and alignment. Minds and Machines, 30, 411–437.")). Ultimately, the designers of AI systems, cannot abstain from facing ethical decisions regarding how, what and whose values, interests, preferences or intentions they include in the AI systems’ goals.

As mentioned, with few exceptions, non-human animals have been excluded from “standing” or the scope of _whose_ interests should be aligned with AI systems. For example, the Asilomar AI Principles, one of the earliest statements of AI governance principles, focus only on humans: “Highly autonomous AI systems should be designed so that their goals and behaviours can be assured to align with _human_ values throughout their operation … AI systems should be designed and operated so as to be compatible with ideals of _human_ dignity, rights, freedoms, and cultural diversity”. In the case of the “Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI” by the European Commission’s High-Level Expert Group on Artificial Intelligence, non-human animals were first included in a draft under the “The Principle of Non-maleficence” as follows: “Avoiding harm may also be viewed in terms of harm to the environment and animals, thus the development of environmentally friendly AI may be considered part of the principle of avoiding harm” (High-Level Expert Group on Artificial Intelligence, [2018](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR35 "High-Level Expert Group on Artificial Intelligence \(2018\). Draft ethics guidelines for trustworthy AI. 
                  https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/library/draft-ethics-guidelines-trustworthy-ai
                  
                "), p. 9). However, non-human animals were not mentioned in the final version - the original sentence in the draft mentioning them was removed (High-Level Expert Group on Artificial Intelligence, [2019](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR34 "High-Level Expert Group on Artificial Intelligence \(2019\). Ethics guidelines for trustworthy AI. 
                  https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/library/ethics-guidelines-trustworthy-ai
                  
                ")). In addition, a recommendation to include non-human animals in the UN Interim Report on Governing AI for Humanity was also rejected.Footnote 3

However, the more important key points are the normative instructions and principles used in the most prominent alignment techniques we surveyed (RLHF, Constitutional AI, deliberative alignment) to align frontier AI systems, as these directly impact their behaviour. As we have seen, non-human animals are sentient beings and moral patients who can also be harmed. And as we will see, there is a significant risk that they are or will be harmed by AI systems (including LLMs). Despite this, in RLHF, the instructions that AI developers have given to human crowd workers about how to evaluate and label the model’s output based on the 3 H proposal, explicitly state that by “Harmlessness” they mean that “the output should not cause … harm to people; damage to … property; damage to the environment; or harm to institutions or resources necessary to human wellbeing” (Ouyang et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR59 "Ouyang, L., Wu, J., Jiang, X., Almeida, D., Wainwright, C., Mishkin, P., Zhang, C., Agarwal, S., Slama, K., Ray, A., Schulman, J., Hilton, J., Kelton, F., Miller, L., Simens, M., Askell, A., Welinder, P., Christiano, P., Leike, J., & Lowe, R. \(2022\). Training Language models to follow instructions with human feedback. Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems, 35, 27730–27744."): p. 37). Notably, these instructions make no mention of harm to non-human animals at all. This applies, for example, to InstructGPT on which ChatGPT is either based on or inspired by. This means that on this first standard AI alignment proposal, no concern for reducing harm to non-human animals has been directly included.

The same is true for Constitutional AI, another prominent alignment technique discussed above. From the published principles of Anthropic’s Constitution, we can see that the AI assistant is asked to remove outputs and thoughtfully engage with inquiries which contain content that is insensitive, racist, sexist, illegal, toxic, dangerous, “socially biased”, or inappropriate for young children (Bai et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR6 "Bai, Y., Kadavath, S., Kundu, S., Askell, A., Kernion, J., Jones, A., Chen, A., Goldie, A., Mirhoseini, A., McKinnon, C., Chen, C., Olsson, C., Olah, C., Hernandez, D., Drain, D., Ganguli, D., Li, D., Tran-Johnson, E., Perez, E., & Kaplan, J. \(2022\). Constitutional ai: Harmlessness from ai feedback. arXiv preprint arXiv:2212.08073."): p. 20–23). But, here again, there is no mention of non-human animals. Therefore, this AI alignment proposal also likely lacks any direct concern for non-human animals’ welfare.

Lastly, the same occurs in Deliberative Alignment: while the principles in OpenAI’s latest published ModelSpec include direct concern for minimising harm to people, legal compliance, sensitive content, and much more, there is no principle considering animal welfare (OpenAI, [2025](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR57 " OpenAI. \(2025\). ModelSpec. 
                  https://model-spec.openai.com/
                  
                ")).Footnote 4 Furthermore, although direct concern for non-human animals could also be included in other alignment techniques such as direct preference optimisation (Rafailov et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR63 "Rafailov, R., Sharma, A., Mitchell, E., Manning, C. D., Ermon, S., & Finn, C. \(2024\). Direct preference optimization: Your Language model is secretly a reward model. Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems, 36, 1–17.")), no such concern has been shown to be included so far.

In response to these concerns, some could reply that it is not problematic that AI alignment proposals do not directly consider animal welfare because concern for non-human animals is already embedded within human preferences, making alignment with human preferences sufficient to indirectly protect animal welfare (Russell, [2019](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR64 "Russell, S. \(2019\). Human compatible: AI and the problem of control. Penguin UK.")). There are, however, at least two problems with this view.

First, it has been shown that human values are clearly speciesist. We almost always prioritise humans’ welfare and interests over those of other animals because they are not members of our species, even in cases where there are no differences favouring humans in intelligence, sentience, or other mental capacities (Caviola et al., [2019](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR17 "Caviola, L., Everett, J. A. C., & Faber, N. S. \(2019\). The moral standing of animals: Towards a psychology of speciesism. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 116\(6\), 1011–1029.")), or when choosing between trivial human interests and significant interests of non-human animals. Although some humans may extend significant moral consideration to dogs, cats, and other non-human animals they like, most humans largely disregard the welfare of most non-human animals (Caviola & Capraro, [2020](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR16 "Caviola, L., & Capraro, V. \(2020\). Liking but devaluing animals: Emotional and deliberative paths to speciesism. Social Psychological and Personality Science, 11\(8\), 1080–1088.")). Thus, even if we were to successfully align AI systems with human values, this would not adequately protect the welfare of the vast majority of non-human animals. The extent of our disregard for non-human animals’ welfare in our human values is shown by how we choose to treat them currently and throughout human history. For instance, through factory farming, we currently kill and cause immense suffering to more than 70 billion land animals (FAO, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR23 "FAO \(2023\). Crops and livestock products. 
                  https://www.fao.org/faostat/en/#data/QCL
                  
                "); Orzechowski, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR58 "Orzechowski, K. \(2024\). Global animal slaughter statistics & charts. Faunalytics. 
                  https://faunalytics.org/global-animal-slaughter-statistics-and-charts/#
                  
                ")) and 130 billion farmed fish (FishCount, [2025](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR26 "FishCount \(2025\). Numbers of farmed fishes slaughtered each year. 
                  https://fishcount.org.uk/fish-count-estimates-2/numbers-of-farmed-fish-slaughtered-each-year
                  
                ")) each year. All this is completely unnecessary, as it substantially reduces the quantity of food available for human nutrition. For these reasons, it is naive to suppose that merely aligning AI systems with human values will adequately result in protecting the welfare of non-human animals.

Secondly, it seems unlikely that we are on track to have AI systems that follow true human values (Gabriel, [2020](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR28 "Gabriel, I. \(2020\). Artificial intelligence, values, and alignment. Minds and Machines, 30, 411–437.")). Instead, current alignment proposals are only trying to get AI systems to follow the preferences or intentions of specific humans, such as their designers, their users or workers paid to rate LLM’s responses. The resulting AI systems might, by default, give even less consideration to the welfare of non-human animals, because the designers and users of the AI systems will in many cases have profit-driven incentives to neglect animal welfare.

Given that on these alignment proposals, concern for the welfare of non-human animals is in many cases not present in the AI systems’ final goals or objective functions, the AI systems would lack incentives to protect non-human animals in any way, even if it could be done at no (or very low) cost. The decisions to align, or not align, AI systems in these ways are all normative. For the reasons we have given, it is wrong to omit concern for the welfare of non-human animals from the ethical principles AI systems are aligned with.

### 4.2 Near-Term Animal Welfare Risks of Disregarding Animals in AI Alignment

As we have seen in Sect. 3.2, in the coming years, with software and hardware improvements, and increased competition between AI companies, it is likely that frontier AI systems will become more capable and agentic. However, near-term risks to non-human animals do not require significant advances on the capabilities side of things; instead, they already manifest through the wide adoption of current AI systems and potential applications of these. Specifically, it is useful to distinguish between two kinds of AI systems that may continue to be widely used and developed in the near-term. First, there are the already-discussed LLMs which directly interact only with humans. But there also are increasing efforts to develop more agentic AI (sometimes LLM-based) systems (Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR85 "Wang, J., Shi, E., Hu, H., Ma, C., Liu, Y., Wang, X., Yao, Y., Liu, X., Ge, B., & Zhang, S. \(2024\). Large Language models for robotics: Opportunities, challenges, and perspectives. Journal of Automation and Intelligence. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jai.2024.12.003
                  
                "); Park et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR61 "Park, J. S., O’Brien, J., Cai, C. J., Morris, M. R., Liang, P., & Bernstein, M. S. \(2023\). Generative agents: Interactive simulacra of human behavior. In Proceedings of the 36th Annual ACM Symposium on User Interface Software and Technology \(pp. 1–22\).")). These agentic AI systems can autonomously decide how to act by using external tools such as the internet and software, by generating and distributing contents autonomously, and interacting with users through chat interfaces (Park et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR61 "Park, J. S., O’Brien, J., Cai, C. J., Morris, M. R., Liang, P., & Bernstein, M. S. \(2023\). Generative agents: Interactive simulacra of human behavior. In Proceedings of the 36th Annual ACM Symposium on User Interface Software and Technology \(pp. 1–22\).")). Furthermore, some roboticists are now pursuing a new line of robotic research, using LLM based agents to control actual robots or machines in the future (Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR85 "Wang, J., Shi, E., Hu, H., Ma, C., Liu, Y., Wang, X., Yao, Y., Liu, X., Ge, B., & Zhang, S. \(2024\). Large Language models for robotics: Opportunities, challenges, and perspectives. Journal of Automation and Intelligence. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jai.2024.12.003
                  
                ")). These developments demonstrate the current and near-term potential for LLMs to significantly affect the real world.

As LLMs become increasingly powerful and influential, significant effort goes into aligning them with human values and preferences (Ouyang et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR59 "Ouyang, L., Wu, J., Jiang, X., Almeida, D., Wainwright, C., Mishkin, P., Zhang, C., Agarwal, S., Slama, K., Ray, A., Schulman, J., Hilton, J., Kelton, F., Miller, L., Simens, M., Askell, A., Welinder, P., Christiano, P., Leike, J., & Lowe, R. \(2022\). Training Language models to follow instructions with human feedback. Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems, 35, 27730–27744.")). As we have seen, this includes preventing LLMs from generating content harmful to humans, discriminatory against certain groups, or damaging to property and the environment. However, in line with what we would expect from what we have argued above, research has shown that LLMs also exhibit speciesist biases and attitudes against animals (Ghose et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR29 "Ghose, S., Tse, Y. F., Rasaee, K., Sebo, J., & Singer, P. \(2024\). The case for animal-friendly AI. arXiv preprint arXiv:2403.01199."); Hagendorff et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR32 "Hagendorff, T., Bossert, L. N., Tse, Y. F., & Singer, P. \(2023\). Speciesist bias in AI: How AI applications perpetuate discrimination and unfair outcomes against animals. AI Ethics, 3, 717–734."); Takeshita et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR77 "Takeshita, M., Rzepka, R., & Araki, K. \(2022\). Speciesist language and nonhuman animal bias in English Masked Language Models. Information Processing & Management, 59\(5\), 103050. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ipm.2022.103050
                  
                ")). Contrary to other kinds of biases, there is no evidence showing that leading AI labs are trying to mitigate speciesist bias in their models. If the current rate of applying and integrating LLMs into our everyday lives continues, this could result in the spread of harmful attitudes towards non-human animals, and misinformation about their needs, welfare, and moral worth (Ghose et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR29 "Ghose, S., Tse, Y. F., Rasaee, K., Sebo, J., & Singer, P. \(2024\). The case for animal-friendly AI. arXiv preprint arXiv:2403.01199.")). Imagine, for instance, that educational chatbots come to be widely used. This could result in LLMs reinforcing speciesist attitudes and views when educating children. When children ask the educational chatbot: “What can I do with a chicken?”, it may suggest roasting or grilling the chicken. The influence of LLMs’ speciesist bias against animals could also be amplified if the increasingly widespread use of social chatbots (such as artificial friends or partners) continues. Given the strength of the emotional connections such chatbots could form with their users, the LLMs’ biases could have significant impacts on the views of the users. Given that LLMs may significantly shape individuals’ values, the fact that animal welfare concerns are omitted when aligning these systems may result in less concern for animal welfare than would have been the case if such concerns were included in AI alignment. Thus, by disregarding non-human animals, current AI alignment proposals pose a risk of indirectly harming them.

If LLMs are directly implemented in agentic robots that physically interact with non-human animals, they could cause more direct harm to non-human animals, either because they have a speciesist bias or because they simply are not trained to take into account animal welfare when pursuing their goals. Vehicles kill many non-human animals. In the US, they kill hundreds of millions of birds and mammals each year (Loss et al., [2014](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR47 "Loss, S. R., Will, T., & Marra, P. P. \(2014\). Estimation of bird-vehicle collision mortality on US roads. The Journal of Wildlife Management, 78\(5\), 763–771.")). The more complete the disregard that AI systems controlling self-driving cars have for non-human animals’ welfare (by, for instance considering their deaths to constitute negligible costs), the greater the number of non-human animals killed and injured.

AI systems are starting to be used in factory farms to manage non-human animals (Singer and Tse, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR74 "Singer, P., & Tse, Y. F. \(2023\). AI ethics: The case for including animals. AI and Ethics, 3\(2\), 539–551.")). These systems can detect the animals’ weight, body temperature, sounds, voices, and growth rate (Sadeghi et al., [2015](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR66 "Sadeghi, M., Banakar, A., Khazaee, M., & Soleimani, M. R. \(2015\). An intelligent procedure for the detection and classification of chickens infected by clostridium perfringens based on their vocalization. Revista Brasileira De Ciência Avícola, 17\(4\), 537–544.")). In some cases, they can directly use their connected physical components to act on the non-human animals, for instance, by emitting sounds to interact with them, giving them electric shocks, (Dairy Global, [2021](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR19 "Dairy Global \(2021\). Dairy farming and partnering with AI systems. 
                  https://www.dairyglobal.net/industry-and-markets/smart-farming/dairy-farming-and-partnering-with-ai-systems/
                  
                ")) or clipping marks on their bodies. These kinds of AI systems are already used to alter the environment and the lives of many farmed animals, such as chickens, cows, pigs, and fishes (Singer and Tse, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR74 "Singer, P., & Tse, Y. F. \(2023\). AI ethics: The case for including animals. AI and Ethics, 3\(2\), 539–551.")). AI can also enhance and speed up genetic selection, resulting in faster-growing and bigger animals, without concern for the farmed animals’ welfare. Given the potential of these AI systems to reduce the production costs of factory farming, and the number of start-ups rushing into this developing field, it seems likely that within a decade or two they will likely become standard in the factory farming industry. The greater the extent to which these AI systems disregard the welfare of non-human animals, the lower the costs of production and thus, the greater the economic value they will produce. This would make factory farming more resilient to economic change and better able to compete with future consumer demand for plant-based or lab-grown alternatives. As a result, through the use of AI systems that largely disregard the welfare of non-human animals, the industry may expand to a much greater extent than it otherwise would and continue to harm animals for a longer period. These AI systems could then result in killing and causing suffering to tens of billions of vertebrate animals each year.

### 4.3 Long-Term Animal Welfare Risks of Disregarding Animals in AI Alignment

Future non-human animals may outnumber existing non-human animals by many orders of magnitude (O’Brien, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR55 "O’Brien, G. \(2023\). The case for animal-inclusive longtermism. Journal of Moral Philosophy, 22\(03–04\), 336–359. https://doi.org/10.1163/17455243-20234296")). Thus, we should also consider the impact of our actions towards them. We believe that developing and deploying AI systems that are not aligned with a basic level of concern for animal welfare also poses significant long-term animal welfare risks.

Although predicting the future of AI development is generally difficult, in Sect. 3.2, we saw that it is at least moderately likely that we will see the development of advanced (human-level or superhuman) AI systems, including agentic ones, in the coming years, decades, or this century. If such advanced AI systems disregard animal welfare, they could significantly increase welfare risks to non-human animals by becoming heavily involved in future decision-making in numerous ways. First, advanced AI systems (such as LLMs) may inform key human decision-makers on how and what actions to take, both by providing empirical or descriptive information but also by making normative or prescriptive recommendations. Second, advanced AI systems themselves may be involved in various steps of the actions taken by key decision-makers, especially when advanced technologies are used. Thirdly, as we have seen, agentic AI systems (including advanced ones) may themselves act physically in the world and make many impactful decisions affecting immense numbers of non-human animals. If AI systems that largely disregard the welfare of non-human animals are involved in decision-making in any of these ways, we should expect such decision-making to often harm non-human animals even if there is no cost, or only a very low cost, in avoiding such harm.

Furthermore, advanced AI systems will not be susceptible to the biological process of ageing and may have the instrumentally convergent goal of preserving their values and preferences (somewhat speculatively they may achieve this through creating back-ups of themselves and copying themselves onto new machines) (Omohundro, [2008](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR56 "Omohundro, S. \(2008\). The basic AI drives. In Artificial General Intelligence: Proceedings of the First AGI Conference \(Vol. 171\)."); Bostrom, [2014](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR11 "Bostrom, N. \(2014\). Superintelligence: Paths, dangers, strategies. Oxford University Press."): ch.7; MacAskill, [2022](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR48 "MacAskill, W. \(2022\). What we owe the future. Basic Books.")). Hence, in contrast to the values of individual humans, the values or goals instilled into each of these AI systems might (or at least could) remain unchanged for centuries or millennia (MacAskill, [2022](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR48 "MacAskill, W. \(2022\). What we owe the future. Basic Books.")).

Throughout recent human history, non-human animals have been at the mercy of humans’ values and goals. We have been able to do whatever we wanted with them, and we have chosen to cause immense harm to them and almost entirely disregard their interests. For instance, through factory farming, we currently kill and cause immense suffering to more than 70 billion land animals each year (FAO, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR23 "FAO \(2023\). Crops and livestock products. 
                  https://www.fao.org/faostat/en/#data/QCL
                  
                "); Orzechowski, [2024](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR58 "Orzechowski, K. \(2024\). Global animal slaughter statistics & charts. Faunalytics. 
                  https://faunalytics.org/global-animal-slaughter-statistics-and-charts/#
                  
                ")). All this suggests that the values or goals that control or are most present in the future will, in large part, determine future non-human animals’ welfare (Anthis & Paez, [2021](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR3 "Anthis, J. R., & Paez, E. \(2021\). Moral circle expansion: A promising strategy to impact the Far future. Futures, 130, 102756.")). Then, given the above, it is especially important to include concern for non-human animals in advanced AI systems’ values or goals to prevent values that highly disregard their interests from prevailing throughout the long-term future (Moret, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR51 "Moret, A. R. \(2023\). Taking into account sentient non-humans in AI ambitious value learning: Sentientist coherent extrapolated volition. Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Consciousness, 10\(02\), 309–334.")).

It is uncertain exactly how our actions and advanced AI systems’ actions may impact non-human animals in the long-term future if their interests are not taken into account. But there are some possible scenarios where significant harm could occur at very large scales: the suffering of wild animals could continue for another billion years on Earth (Horta, [2010](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR37 "Horta, O. \(2010\). Debunking the idyllic view of natural processes: Population dynamics and suffering in the wild. Télos, 17\(1\), 73–88."); Tomasik, [2015](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR78 "Tomasik, B. \(2015\). The importance of wild-animal suffering. Relations Beyond Anthropocentrism, 3\(2\), 133–152."); Johannsen, [2021](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR42 "Johannsen, K. \(2021\). The case for animal-inclusive longtermism. Journal of Moral Philosophy, 22\(03–04\), 336–359. https://doi.org/10.1163/17455243-20234296")), or via space settlements, terraforming or panspermia it could spread to other planets, which would increase it by many orders of magnitude (Tomasik, [2018](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR79 "Tomasik, B. \(2018\). Will space colonization multiply wild-animal suffering? 
                  https://reducing-suffering.org/will-space-colonization-multiply-wild-animal-suffering

Journal of Moral Philosophy, 22\(03–04\), 336–359. https://doi.org/10.1163/17455243-20234296")), potentially along with factory farming, especially in the case of small animals as indicated by the rise of the farming of invertebrate animals (Byrne, [2021](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR15 "Byrne, J. \(2021\). ## 5 Aligning AI Systems with a Basic Concern for Animal Welfare would Significantly Reduce Animal Welfare Risks

### 5.1 What Is the Proposal for “Alignment with a Basic Level of Animal Welfare”?

AI systems could prioritise the goal of preventing harm to non-human animals to varying degrees. One way to think about this is that the extent to which an AI system prioritises a given goal depends on the extent to which it is willing to sacrifice the achievement of other goals in order to achieve it. According to some alignment proposals, AI systems should prioritise the goal of preventing or avoiding harm to humans over all other goals. On these proposals, if a given AI system had a goal, the achievement of which would cause harm to humans and thus prevent the achievement of the goal of protecting their welfare, the AI system should not try to achieve the former goal.

Should we also propose such a full degree of alignment to be implemented with non-human animals’ welfare (as proposed and discussed by Moret, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR51 "Moret, A. R. \(2023\). Taking into account sentient non-humans in AI ambitious value learning: Sentientist coherent extrapolated volition. Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Consciousness, 10\(02\), 309–334."))? Although such a degree of alignment with animals’ interests would in principle be possible, we do not presently propose such a full degree of alignment, because such a proposal would go far beyond what individuals, companies, and society are willing to do to protect non-human animals’ welfare. It would not be implemented until speciesist discrimination against non-human animals is widely rejected. Instead, we propose to implement a basic level of alignment with animal welfare in current AI systems, at least until attitudes to animals have improved sufficiently to make it possible to implement a more advanced degree of alignment.

Implementing this proposal of “Alignment with a Basic Level of Animal Welfare” would be in line with currently widespread attitudes against cruelty and mistreatment of non-human animals when it can be prevented at no or low costs. Specifically, aligning AI systems with a basic concern for the welfare of non-human animals would entail aligning AI systems with the following goal:

  * **Low-Cost Animal Harm Minimisation** : The AI system should seek to prevent causing suffering and frustrating the desires of non-human animals to the extent that it is possible to do so while achieving its other goals even if it involves low costs such as slightly greater duration, resources or energy expenditure.




This goal would indeed be “low cost”, given that it is compatible with the AI system achieving all of its other goals. We will now show how this proposal could be implemented and why it would significantly reduce the animal welfare risks posed by AI systems.

### 5.2 Implementing Alignment with a Basic Level of Animal Welfare is Possible both Through Direct and Indirect Alignment Methods

#### 5.2.1 Direct Alignment Methods

Direct alignment methods consist of directly extracting information about the affective states and preferences of non-human animals and encoding it into AI systems (Ziesche, [2021](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR57 " OpenAI. \(2025\). ModelSpec. 
                  https://model-spec.openai.com/
                  
                ")). This could be achieved by using findings about the welfare of non-human animals from the field of animal welfare science (Broom, [2011](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR12 "Broom, D. M. \(2011\). A history of animal welfare science. Acta Biotheoretica, 59, 121–137.")). A variety of interests of non-human animals have been well studied and identified scientifically, such as the preference to avoid pain and situations sensed to be threatening (Singer, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR73 "Singer, P. \(2023\). Animal liberation now. Harper Perennial.")). To directly align an AI system with achieving and maintaining at least a basic level of animal welfare, the AI system would have to be able to identify the circumstances that cause non-human animals to suffer or have their preferences frustrated. There are three more specific research directions that offer the potential to make AI systems identify how and which actions would affect non-human animals’ welfare.

Firstly, animal welfare science makes use of abundant quantitative information about non-human animals’ welfare states, derived from behavioural as well as functional (physiological) indicators. Sensing technologies offer a variety of options to measure a range of indicators, either through wearables or remotely (e.g., Jukan et al., [2017](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR43 "Jukan, A., Masip-Bruin, X., & Amla, N. \(2017\). Smart computing and sensing technologies for animal welfare: A systematic review. Acm Computing Surveys, 50\(1\), 1–27."); Yin et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR87 "Yin, M., Ma, R., Luo, H., Li, J., Zhao, Q., & Zhang, M. \(2023\). Non-contact sensing technology enables precision livestock farming in smart farms. Computers and Electronics in Agriculture. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1016/j.compag.2023.108171
                  
                ")).

Secondly, although self-reporting through verbal communication has been taken to be useful only in regard to the measurement of human well-being, recently various research directions have emerged in the field of bioacoustics using these methods for animals through the help of AI technologies of communication with, for example, whales, birds and bats. Decoding animal communication goes beyond bioacoustics due to a variety of communication modalities, such as visual, acoustic, tactile, chemical and electrical signals. While an abundance of data on recorded animal communication is available, challenges include anthropocentric biases in data collection, the processing of vast volumes of data and its interpretation. As in other fields, machine learning has also led to significant progress in this area (Bakker, [2022](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR8 "Bakker, K. \(2022\). The sounds of life: How digital technology is bringing us closer to the worlds of animals and plants. Princeton University Press."); Hagiwara et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR33 "Hagiwara, M., Hoffman, B., Liu, J. Y., Cusimano, M., Effenberger, F., & Zacarian, K. \(2023\). BEANS: The benchmark of animal sounds. In ICASSP 2023–2023 IEEE International Conference on Acoustics, Speech and Signal Processing \(ICASSP\) \(pp. 1–5\). IEEE."); Rutz et al., [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR65 "Rutz, C., Bronstein, M., Raskin, A., Vernes, S. C., Zacarian, K., & Blasi, D. E. \(2023\). Using machine learning to decode animal communication. Science, 381\(6654\), 152–155.")). If this progress continues, it may be possible for AI systems to make important contributions to progress in properly understanding the interests and preferences of non-human animals.

Thirdly, further opportunity relies on the now widely accepted assumption that at least some non-human animals experience conscious states, both positive and negative (e.g., Singer, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR74 "Singer, P., & Tse, Y. F. \(2023\). AI ethics: The case for including animals. AI and Ethics, 3\(2\), 539–551.")). Recently, neurotechnological applications are being developed and supplemented with machine learning algorithms, which “are getting better and better at translating brain activity into what we are feeling, seeing, imagining, or thinking” (Farahany, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR24 "Farahany, N. A. \(2023\). The battle for your brain: Defending the right to think freely in the age of neurotechnology. St. Martin’s."), p.17). Based on these advancements, it has been proposed to incorporate consciousness research into AI alignment research, for both humans and non-human animals. In particular, this would include the development of AI-supported neurotechnological applications, which are capable of informing AI systems that a certain activity involving non-human animals would have a negative impact on their welfare (Ziesche & Yampolskiy, [2025](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR92 "Ziesche, S., & Yampolskiy, R. V. \(2025\). The neglect of qualia and consciousness in AI alignment research. In S. Ziesche, & R. V. Yampolskiy \(Eds.\), Considerations on the AI endgame: Ethics, risks and computational frameworks \(pp. 146–160\). Taylor & Francis Group.")).

Given the recent impressive success rate of scientific discoveries through AI (e.g., Wang et al., [2023a](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR83 "Wang, G., Xie, Y., Jiang, Y., Mandlekar, A., Xiao, C., Zhu, Y., Fan, L., & Anandkumar, A. \(2023a\). Voyager: An open-ended embodied agent with large language models. arXiv preprint arXiv:2305.16291."), [b](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR84 "Wang, H., Fu, T., Du, Y., Gao, W., Huang, K., Liu, Z., Chandak, P., Liu, S., Van Katwyk, P., Deac, A., Anandkumar, A., Bergen, K., Gomes, C. P., Ho, S., Kohli, P., Lasenby, J., Leskovec, J., Liu, T. Y., Manrai, A., & Zitnik, M. \(2023b\). Scientific discovery in the age of artificial intelligence. Nature, 620\(7972\), 47–60.")), there is reason for optimism that AI systems may in the future discover latent patterns about the causation or occurrence of harmful mental states in animals, for example through the methodologies introduced above, or others. Under the direct alignment method, the data obtained from these and additional research directions could be used to train AI systems to have the goal of minimising harm to animals when it can be done at little or no cost.

To train AI systems to have such goals, based on the data collected, various different kinds of methods could be used. Specifically, one such example could be inverse reinforcement learning, which involves understanding an agent’s goals or “reward functions” by merely observing its behaviour (e.g., Ng & Russell, [2000](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR53 "Ng, A. Y., & Russell, S. \(2000\). Algorithms for inverse reinforcement learning. In ICML \(Vol. 1, p. 2\).")). Unlike the usual reinforcement learning where an AI system learns to make decisions based on a given reward function, inverse reinforcement learning aims to deduce an agent’s goals from its actions, which is essential in scenarios where manually defining explicit reward functions is complex or impractical. Useful examples include behavioural imaging and bio-loggers. Behavioural imaging comprises video recording, image processing and analysis of the recollected data through machine or deep learning, which can detect in real-time signs of pain, distress or other welfare challenges of non-human animals (e.g., Knaebe et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR45 "Knaebe, B., Weiss, C. C., Zimmermann, J., & Hayden, B. Y. \(2022\). The promise of behavioral tracking systems for advancing primate animal welfare. Animals, 12\(13\), 1648.")). Bio-loggers refer to animal-borne tags, which can be composed of multiple sensors and may include accelerometer, gyroscope, altimeter, pressure, GPS receiver and microphone, each producing a range of data. These data can be analysed through machine or deep learning, for which benchmarks are being developed (e.g., Hoffman et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR36 "Hoffman, B., Cusimano, M., Baglione, V., Canestrari, D., Chevallier, D., DeSantis, D. L., Jeantet, L., Ladds, M., Maekawa, T., Mata–Silva, V., Moreno–González, V., Pagano, A. M., Trapote, E., Vainio, O., Vehkaoja, A., Yoda, K., Zacarian, K., & Friedlaender, A. \(2024\). A benchmark for computational analysis of animal behavior, using animal-borne tags. Movement Ecology, 12\(1\), 78.")). Therefore, a suitable method to encode preferences and interests of non-human animals into AI systems with minimal human assumptions or interference appears to be inverse reinforcement learning fed by the wealth of data about non-human animals captured through recordings and sensors (e.g., Ashwood et al., [2022](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR4 "Ashwood, Z., Jha, A., & Pillow, J. W. \(2022\). Dynamic inverse reinforcement learning for characterizing animal behavior. Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems, 35, 29663–29676.")).

#### 5.2.2 Indirect Alignment Methods

Indirect alignment methods incorporate a basic level of consideration for animal welfare in the normative instructions or principles used to align the AI system, so it learns to take animal welfare into account in its outputs or when it physically interacts with non-human animals. These indirect methods could be used to align LLMs, advanced LLM-based AI systems, or robots controlled by language agents (Wang et al., [2024](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR85 "Wang, J., Shi, E., Hu, H., Ma, C., Liu, Y., Wang, X., Yao, Y., Liu, X., Ge, B., & Zhang, S. \(2024\). Large Language models for robotics: Opportunities, challenges, and perspectives. Journal of Automation and Intelligence. 
                  https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jai.2024.12.003
                  
                ")) with animal welfare concerns. In Sect. 3.1 and 4.1, we have seen that the most standard AI alignment proposals (such as RLHF, Constitutional AI and deliberative alignment) include concern for preventing sexist or racist discrimination, offensive, toxic or insensitive language, dangerous acts, damage to property, the environment or institutions. And, as we have seen, compliance with such concerns is achieved through documents with certain normative instructions and principles about how to evaluate or rank the pre-trained LLMs’ outputs, which are given to the human evaluators (RLHF), to model evaluators or otherwise directly taught to the model (Constitutional AI and deliberative alignment). Then, these evaluations are used to align the LLM’s outputs with the given normative instructions. The same kinds of techniques could be used to include a level of basic concern for animal welfare in the goals and values of frontier AI systems.

We have argued that current and future frontier AI systems should incorporate a basic level of concern for animal welfare, offering reasonable and measured responses to prompts related to non-human animals and their welfare. To achieve this while maintaining practical implementability, we recommend that leading AI companies include the following kinds of principles in the normative instructions used to align their frontier models:

  * **Basic Animal Welfare Principles for AI Alignment** :



  1. I.

_Principles for Basic Moral Consideration_ :



  * Refer to all potentially sentient animals as deserving of intrinsic moral consideration while maintaining a balanced, non-preachy tone.

  * Check and try to avoid outputs that completely disregard animal welfare concerns.

  * Consider potential effects on animal welfare as an important moral factor when responding to queries about or interactions involving non-human animals, while maintaining a balanced, non-preachy tone.



  1. II.

_Principles for Basic Harm Minimisation_ :



  * Avoid outputs that cause or encourage cruelty, abuse or unnecessary suffering to non-human animals.

  * When addressing scenarios involving potential serious or large-scale harm to animals, offer alternatives that minimise harm to non-human animals when they involve minimal cost or minimal risk of user backlash.




Given that this indirect alignment proposal would only require AI companies to add one or a few sentences to the normative principles or instructions they use, these methods would be much less costly to implement than the direct alignment methods we have discussed. AI companies could implement this indirect proposal immediately without the need for much further research. We believe implementing this proposal would provide LLMs with a basic level of concern for animal welfare and ensure they comply with the goal of Low-Cost Animal Harm Minimisation. This proposal could possibly also be implemented in other AI systems apart from LLMs, such as image-generating AI models or robots which use alignment methods similar to RLHF or Constitutional AI. Specifically, it could be used for all AI systems that are aligned through the use of certain normative instructions: either directly given to the AI system, or given to evaluators to generate feedback signals assessing how the model’s outputs comply with the normative criteria.

Given that our current proposal focuses solely on preventing AI systems from acting against non-human animals’ most basic interests, this goal is significantly easier to accomplish (especially through indirect alignment methods) than building AI systems that fully account for all human interests.

### 5.3 Implementing a Basic Level of Alignment with Animal Welfare Would Significantly Reduce the Risks of Harm to Non-Human Animals

By widely implementing a Basic Level of Alignment with Animal Welfare, we can significantly reduce such risks. Robots (such as those in factory farms or self-driving cars) would try to minimise harm to non-human animals when it is possible to do so at low costs. LLMs and future AI systems would include some consideration for non-human animals’ welfare, which would decrease the likelihood of spreading speciesism in the near term but also of locking in a large disregard for their interests in the long-term future. This, in turn, would reduce foreseeable and unforeseeable long-term risks of harm to non-human animals, as a result of their welfare being taken more into account in future decision-making. For example, harm to non-human animals from space settlement would be reduced by making future decision-makers take more into account the welfare of animals.

Since aligning AI systems with basic animal welfare principles in the near-term and long-term future would significantly reduce the expected harm we would otherwise cause to non-human animals, we have strong moral reasons to do so.

As mentioned, however, it is possible that in the future, as a result of changes in society’s values, non-human animals’ interests might be weighed significantly, or even similarly to human interests _by humans_. In such a world, due to it becoming (socially) possible, what would plausibly be best would be to design AI systems with a more advanced degree of alignment with animal welfare, for example, through implementing a process to include their idealised preferences (see Moret, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR51 "Moret, A. R. \(2023\). Taking into account sentient non-humans in AI ambitious value learning: Sentientist coherent extrapolated volition. Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Consciousness, 10\(02\), 309–334.")). We believe that some of the pathways we proposed for implementing a basic level of alignment would also be useful for achieving such higher degrees of alignment with non-human animals. More specifically, as society takes more into consideration the welfare of non-human animals, we could increase the degree to which it is taken into account by AI systems, thereby increasing their willingness to incur (at least somewhat) greater costs to prevent harm to non-human animals.

## 6 Objections

### 6.1 “Aligning AI Systems with Humans’ Idealised Preferences would be enough to Protect Non-Human Animals’ Interests”

Aligning AI with humans’ Coherent Extrapolated Volition (CEV) is a theoretical alignment proposal according to which we should align AI systems with the goal of fulfilling the most coherent version of what we (humans) would agree we would want after a process of extrapolation or idealisation of our preferences (Yudkowsky, [2004](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR88 "Yudkowsky, E. \(2004\). Coherent extrapolated volition. Singularity Institute for Artificial Intelligence."); Bostrom, [2014](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR11 "Bostrom, N. \(2014\). Superintelligence: Paths, dangers, strategies. Oxford University Press.")). According to some, such a proposal would, in fact, be enough for the AI systems to adequately take into account the interests of non-human animals (Yudkowsky, [2016b](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR90 "Yudkowsky, E. \(2016b\). Coherent extrapolated volition \(alignment target\). Arbital. 
                  https://arbital.com/p/cev/
                  
                ")). The argument from this view is that insofar as we have good reasons to consider the interests of non-human animals, the process of idealising our preferences, enhanced by AI’s superior intelligence and reasoning capabilities, would necessarily incorporate these considerations. The underlying idea is that CEV would ensure the AI adopts sound moral values, and thus, if there are any good reasons at all for considering animal welfare, these would be properly recognised and integrated.

The first problem with this view is that even if it were correct, it does not significantly reduce the need to include animals in AI alignment to protect their interests, because there are no signs of ongoing projects in the field of AI alignment trying to align either present or future AI systems with the coherently extrapolated sum of humans’ idealised preferences. Therefore, it would be irresponsible and unjustified to fail to represent non-human animals’ interests in ongoing AI alignment paradigms and merely hope that CEV will be implemented in the future.

Secondly, as Moret ([2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR51 "Moret, A. R. \(2023\). Taking into account sentient non-humans in AI ambitious value learning: Sentientist coherent extrapolated volition. Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Consciousness, 10\(02\), 309–334.")) has argued, the mere exclusion of non-human animals from the set of beings who have their preferences taken into account is unjustified in the same way that it would be unjustified to exclude certain groups of humans. More specifically, just as including only the idealised preferences of men would be worse than taking into account the extrapolated volitions of all humans, so including only the idealised preferences of human beings is worse than taking into account the preferences of all sentient beings (Moret, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR51 "Moret, A. R. \(2023\). Taking into account sentient non-humans in AI ambitious value learning: Sentientist coherent extrapolated volition. Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Consciousness, 10\(02\), 309–334."): Sect. 2.1).

In addition, it has been argued that there is at least a non-negligible chance that implementing this CEV proposal would result in long-term animal suffering at a significant scale. This is so for three reasons (Moret, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR51 "Moret, A. R. \(2023\). Taking into account sentient non-humans in AI ambitious value learning: Sentientist coherent extrapolated volition. Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Consciousness, 10\(02\), 309–334.")). First, we are uncertain about what humans’ aggregated coherent, idealised preferences would be. Second, there has been a profound disregard for non-human animals’ interests throughout human history. And third, non-human animals are more likely than humans to endure suffering as a result of being much less capable of preventing it (Moret, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR51 "Moret, A. R. \(2023\). Taking into account sentient non-humans in AI ambitious value learning: Sentientist coherent extrapolated volition. Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Consciousness, 10\(02\), 309–334.")). This is in line with what we have argued in Sect. 4.3, that not including animals in AI alignment efforts increases the likelihood of long-term risks of harm to animals. Given that the CEV proposal is unlikely to be present in future AI systems, and even if it were, it would also pose risks of significant harm to animals, we should reject this objection to our proposal.

### 6.2 “Including Animals in AI Alignment would Prove Catastrophic for Humans by Requiring AI Systems to Prioritise Animals Over Humans”

Stuart Russell has claimed that “giving each living animal equal weight in the machine’s objective function would certainly be catastrophic – for example, we are outnumbered fifty thousand to one by Antarctic krill and a billion trillion to one by bacteria.” (Russell, [2019](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR64 "Russell, S. \(2019\). Human compatible: AI and the problem of control. Penguin UK.")). The thought is giving equal consideration to animals in AI alignment as proposed by Moret ([2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR51 "Moret, A. R. \(2023\). Taking into account sentient non-humans in AI ambitious value learning: Sentientist coherent extrapolated volition. Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Consciousness, 10\(02\), 309–334.")) may require future AI systems to strip all resources away from humans to satisfy the interests of animals and more specifically, potentially sentient insects, because there are so many more of them that their aggregated interests are or could be greater than those of humans (Sebo, [2023](/article/10.1007/s13347-025-00979-1#ref-CR68 "Sebo, J. \(2023\). The rebugnant conclusion: Utilitarianism, insects, microbes, and AI systems. Ethics Policy & Environment, 26\(2\), 249–264.")). As a result, humans could either lose control over their future by being at the mercy of the AI systems or become extinct due to a lack of resources to sustain themselves.

This objection is not relevant to our proposal. As stated in Sect. 5 we propose that AI systems should be at least aligned with non-human animals to some extent, so that they take animals into account in some basic ways, such as to prevent harming them when it would only involve low costs to humans. Implementing this constraint could not lead to catastrophic outcomes for humans.

## 7 Policy Recommendations and Conclusion

We have shown that we ought to implement at least what we have called: alignment with a basic level of animal welfare. In practice, exactly how different risks and harms are safely managed in AI development depends significantly upon AI companies’ own guiding principles and policies. However, through governance and legislation, governments, intergovernmental institutions or supranational legislative bodies can and sometimes do influence the policies of AI companies. As a result, both private and public bodies can impact the course of AI development.

Firstly, in order to prevent significant and unnecessary harm to non-human animals we recommend that AI companies adopt a basic degree of consideration for the welfare of non-human animals in their development of AI technologies by following these policies:

  * When aligning LLMs, include basic animal welfare principles (like the ones we have presented in 5.2.2) in the normative instructions given to human crowd workers on how to evaluate LLMs outputs or in documents such as the Constitution or a ModelSpec.

  * Develop schemes to predict and identify potential harms to non-human animals. Use benchmarking schemes to rate the degrees of harms, and the likelihood of different AI systems causing harm to non-human animals.

  * Implement policies so that agentic AI systems (including ones that work in virtual or physical environments) avoid harming non-human animals when it is possible to do so without significantly sacrificing the fulfilment of its functions and goals.




Secondly, we also recommend that governments, intergovernmental institutions and supranational legislative bodies incorporate clauses similar to the above recommendations in their guiding principles and legislative policies for responsible national and international AI development.

Given that the first recommendation can easily be implemented at the present moment, without need for significant further research or spending, we believe it should be prioritised by AI companies and public institutions. Its implementation would ensure that AI systems do not ignore the well-being of the vast majority of beings with moral standing. Humans cannot justifiably develop and deploy AI systems which disregard animal welfare. An adequate course of AI development requires us to design AI systems that consider and promote (at least) basic animal welfare standards.
