**Concerns about AI and power concentration can sometimes feel abstract or distant. How concerned are you about AI-enabled power concentration, and on what timeline do you expect this to become relevant?**

Power concentration from AI could come in different flavors and through different mechanisms.

In the commercial context, the low switching costs between different model providers and the relative lack of product differentiation between truly “general” systems makes it possible that, at some point in the near future, the leading AI company could pull ahead of the rest and absorb their competitors’ users. This could generate a revenue fly-wheel for attracting talent and financing more compute infrastructure, feeding into even better models. If that feedback loop is strong enough, the industry could rapidly consolidate around one, dominant company -- especially once you factor in recursive self-improvement. In terms of timing, Anthropic hinted at this possibility in 2023 in a [leaked pitch deck](https://techcrunch.com/2023/04/06/anthropics-5b-4-year-plan-to-take-on-openai/), which stated: “We believe that companies that train the best 2025/26 models will be too far ahead for anyone to catch up in subsequent cycles.”

Now, it’s noteworthy that Anthropic said “companies” rather than a single company, suggesting they were foreseeing (at least at the time -- I don’t know their current view) a more oligopolistic end-game rather than a true winner-take-all monopolist. This makes sense to me, too, as frontier AI companies need substantial compute infrastructure to compete, and it is hard to imagine literally all data center capacity getting rolled-up into a single company. Instead, we’re more likely to see the emergence of power blocks that correspond to the owners of the largest compute clusters. Given the amount of cross-ownership between the hyperscalers and leading AI companies, the post-AGI economy may be effectively controlled by a relatively small network of investors and chief executives. The sociologist [C. Wright Mills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Power_Elite) famously argued that something like this happened in the U.S. in the early 20th century, as the economies of scale from industrialization displaced the decentralized craft and agrarian economy with a few hundred mega-corporations. We thus emerged from World War II with a power structure centered around a relatively concentrated class of owners and managers; a new “power elite” small enough in number to tacitly coordinate through shared board seats, elite membership clubs and the like. AGI could recreate these dynamics on steroids. Insofar as AGI represents a true “everything” technology, whole cross-sections of work that are today distributed across many companies and occupations could become aggregated into a handful of companies, similar to how Google and Facebook’s ad markets hoovered-up revenues that used to flow to hundreds of local media outlets.

Power concentration in the political context is even scarier. As China is demonstrating, AI is an extremely powerful tool for censorship, surveillance and social control. In the limit, this points to a form of regime lock-in, in which resistance becomes futile. Indeed, political resistance may not even occur to you, as AI-enabled content moderation and recommender systems can be trained to subtly shape your beliefs and values. With true AGI in hand, authoritarian regimes could even eliminate internal resistance by simply automating the machinery of government itself.  


**What can we learn from previous periods of technological disruption about how institutions adapt or fail to adapt to transformative technology?**

A common technique used by superforecasters is to consider the reference class of the thing they’re forecasting. You may not be able to predict the future in detail, but you can at least extract some general lessons. The reference class for previous periods of transformative technological change -- the agricultural revolution, the printing revolution, the industrial revolutions -- all featured equally transformative institutional change: agriculture drove the transition from hunter-gather societies to early city states; the printing press drove the transition from feudalism to modernity; and industrialization gave rise to professionalized administrative states and welfare systems. So while I can only speculate about what our governments and institutions will look like post-AGI, I think we can be confident in predicting that they’ll look extremely different indeed.

When you dig into the mechanisms behind why disruptive technology leads to institutional change, another lesson is the central relevance of transaction costs. As the economist Ronald Coase helped elucidate in his theory of the firm, transaction costs are the costs associated with things like search, bargaining, coordination, monitoring and enforcement. Shocks to these core transaction costs create white space for new institutions to emerge and economize on transaction costs better than what came before. The internet and mobile revolutions created space for ride-sharing to displace publicly-regulated taxi commissions, for example -- an institutional regime change in miniature.

Obviously, AI and AGI have enormous implications for transaction costs. AI is already driving massive reductions in the cost of monitoring, enforcement, bargaining and so forth. But with true AGI, transaction and principal-agent costs have the potential to be driven to near zero; what some economists recently dubbed the “[Coasean Singularity](https://www.nber.org/books-and-chapters/economics-transformative-ai/coasean-singularity-demand-supply-and-market-design-ai-agents).” Séb Krier of Google DeepMind has an [excellent essay](https://www.aipolicyperspectives.com/p/coasean-bargaining-at-scale) exploring what this could mean for society, and I have [my own thoughts](https://www.secondbest.ca/p/ai-and-leviathan-part-i), though much more work needs to be done.

**[Your work](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2503.05710) discusses two failure modes: the “despotic Leviathan” (authoritarian control) and the “absent Leviathan” (state collapse). Which outcome seems more likely to you?**

My intuition is that it depends on the initial conditions of the country in question. China is already well underway to building a digital panopticon, suggesting a big shock to transaction costs will only deepen their vertical integration. In liberal democracies, in contrast, constitutional constraints and slower adoption of AI relative to the private sector could lead to the rapid displacement of government functions with private forms of governance, as with the ride-sharing example. The proliferation of powerful AI creates massive negative externalities, from biorisks to the ability to spy on your neighbor, that will need to be internalized somehow. In lieu of governments adapting quickly enough, we may thus see the rise of privatized walled gardens that provide peace, security and other AI-native public goods from the bottom-up.

**Where should policymakers start? What concrete policy interventions could be implemented now to address your primary concerns about AI and power concentration?**

There is a Red Queen Dynamic between public and private sector AI adoption, in which governments will need to run just to stay in place. I thus support reforms to accelerate public sector adoption of AI to increase the odds that our institutions can adapt and co-evolve with the technology. Public sector adoption also provides a means to incentivize investments in AI applications that are privacy and civil liberties preserving. If some amount of surveillance is inevitable, for example, it would be better to have tools that embed safeguards against abuse and misuse. Robust state capacity is a check against concentrations of private power, while engineering respect for privacy and civil liberties into the machinery of government itself protects against AI-enabled authoritarianism. I have in mind Estonia’s advanced e-government, with its layers of robust encryption and APIs for integrating with services developed by the private sector and civil society.
