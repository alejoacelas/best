## Abstract

All known life is homochiral. DNA and RNA are made from “righthanded” nucleotides, and proteins are made from “left-handed” amino acids. Driven by curiosity and plausible applications, some researchers had begun work toward creating lifeforms composed entirely of mirror-image biological molecules. Such mirror organisms would constitute a radical departure from known life, and their creation warrants careful consideration. The capability to create mirror life is likely at least a decade away and would require large investments and major technical advances; we thus have an opportunity to consider and preempt risks before they are realized. Here, we draw on an indepth analysis of current technical barriers, how they might be eroded by technological progress, and what we deem to be unprecedented and largely overlooked risks (_1_). We call for broader discussion among the global research community, policy-makers, research funders, industry, civil society, and the public to chart an appropriate path forward.

A recent analysis of the potential threat posed by mirror-image life forms (_1_) presented an important topic for the scientific community. Major concerns were raised by the authors, who argued that many aspects of the immune response to mirror bacteria could be deficient. However, there was limited consideration of the crucial roles of the third pillar of biomolecules, namely carbohydrates (comprising oligo- and polysaccharides, a.k.a. glycans), in considering the risks of mirror-image life. We believe that glycans, in addition to proteins and nucleic acids, which are discussed in more detail in the commentary and technical report (_2_) must be considered to reach an accurate assessment of our preparedness for the arrival of mirror-image organisms.

Our reply revisits the complexity of the cell surfaces of all microbial species, which feature diverse glycan landscapes. Glycan-dependent interactions play key roles in the innate and adaptive immune responses exhibited by human and other immune systems. These recognition systems might not have encountered the mirror-image of present-day proteins or nucleic acids. Still, they have co-evolved alongside mirror-image carbohydrates, which already exist on the cell surface of microbial species. We note that the deleterious impact of mirror-image life forms might be mitigated by these essential recognition processes, thereby acknowledging the critical contribution of glycans, even though it is currently unclear whether their recognition would be sufficient to compensate for other expected reductions in immune defenses. Therefore, analyzing the unique stereochemical complexity of the “glycan language” should be central when considering the harmful impacts of any future mirror-image life forms.

We acknowledge that the accompanying report (_2_) discussed catabolism of, and chemotaxis to, enantiomeric glycans by present-day bacteria. The report touches on glycan–lectin and glycan–antibody interactions in the present-day immune system and peptidoglycan-based microbe-associated molecular patterns (MAMPs). Nevertheless, it oversimplifies the stereochemical relationships between glycans and the plasticity of key molecular interactions between natural proteins and mirror-image glycans. For example, _“because interactions [...] are stereospecific, pattern-recognition receptors would not typically recognize mirror lipopolysaccharides, mirror peptidoglycan, or the other opposite-chirality macromolecules […]”_ (_2_). We hope to provide more nuance to the conversation.

**Glycan diversity across and within species:** Glycans coat the surfaces of every cell across all domains of life and are crucial for functions at both the cellular and organismal levels (_3_). Their prominent display on cell surfaces highlights their vital roles in communication between cells. For instance, glycan recognition is linked to self _versus_ non-self discrimination by immune systems. Gene expression data indicate that glycans are indispensable for life, and new glycan-dependent functions continue to be discovered. For example, it is well-known that a significant proportion of eukaryotic proteins are covalently modified with glycans, a property that profoundly affects glycoprotein function (_3_). Emerging analysis of protein glycosylation in Archaea uncovered a diversity unparalleled in both eukaryotic and bacterial systems (_3_). Furthermore, although cells can operate without nuclei, no living cell is known that functions without glycans on its surface (_4_). Among the biopolymers in terrestrial organisms, nucleic acids, proteins, and glycans are fundamental. Even meteorites contain sugar-related organic compounds in amounts comparable to amino acids (_5_). It is striking that, in contrast to nucleic acids and proteins, the degree of glycan chemical complexity (including stereochemical, topological and functional group diversity) within even a single bacterial species is often profound, in part, due to strong evolutionary pressure from bacteriophages (_6_). Crucially, the ‘alphabet’ of glycans—the monosaccharide building blocks—is species-specific, compared to the uniform alphabets of amino acids and nucleotides. The collective alphabet of glycans across all living things already includes mirror-image monosaccharides, although they are taxonomically gated. For example, while eukaryotes have D-mannose, many bacteria use derivatives of L-mannose (_7_). As a corollary, immune systems such as ours have co-evolved over millions of years encountering monosaccharides of the opposite chirality and have evolved to denote them as "foreign" and elicit a potent response. Mirror-image glycans might therefore be recognized by our immune systems if a mirror microorganism threat ever did emerge.

**Fundamental symmetry-related characteristics of biopolymer building blocks: Mirror-image isomers of glycans have a symmetry that enables their recognition by innate immune proteins.** This unique property of glycans is embedded in their stereochemical diversity, which contrasts with that of nucleic acids and proteins. For example, most encoded L-amino acids (excluding glycine, threonine and isoleucine) contain a single stereogenic center. The nucleosides and nucleotides employ D-ribose or D-deoxyribose sugar rings and their mirror-image isomers are all built from the same enantiomeric ribose (or deoxyribose) molecules (_8_). In contrast, in carbohydrates, if one stereogenic center is inverted, even in a simple six-carbon sugar such as D-glucose, it results in a different sugar with distinct chemical properties. For example, inversion of C-2 in D-glucose gives D-mannose, inversion at C-4 gives D-galactose. The mirror-image isomer of D-mannose is L-mannose, a monosaccharide similar to the common bacterial monosaccharide L-rhamnose (also known as 6-deoxy-L-mannose). A similar relationship is evident for L-galactose, an enantiomer of common D-galactose: L-galactose shares features with the common monosaccharide L-fucose (6-deoxy-L-galactose). L-Galactose is also a biochemical intermediate in plants (_9_). This structural plasticity is unique to glycans. Protein and nucleic acid biomolecules are diversified principally through functional group differentiation (_e.g._ , different amino-acid side chains or different nucleoside nucleobases), or post-translational modifications (PTM) and epigenetic modifications, respectively; these do not typically alter the chirality of the underlying amino acid and nucleotide building blocks. In contrast, glycans achieve diversity through extensive stereo-diversification of both carbohydrate building blocks and glycosidic linkages (_10_). This diversification, together with functional group variation, ring size, and carbohydrate carbon number, impacts any consideration of experiments to assess the implications of mirror-image glycans in biology and ultimately to assess any potential threat of organisms that might feature such biopolymers.

**A vast****stereo-diversification of carbohydrates** has already been documented for gram-negative and gram-positive bacteria (_11_), and archaea (_12_). Microbial monosaccharide diversity encompasses hundreds of building blocks, with fucose, rhamnose, xylose, and others found in both D-and L-configurations (_13, 14_). Bacteria can even assemble their glycans by using both D- and L-enantiomers of the same monosaccharide, further blurring the line between the sides of the mirror. For example, the lipopolysaccharide (LPS) of some _Pseudomonas_ species (_15_) or of a non-typable _Helicobacter pylori_ strain (_16_) contains both L- and D-rhamnose. In giant viruses, rhamnose is present in both its enantiomeric forms in a single saccharide chain (_17_). For the monosaccharide arabinose, both D- and L-enantiomers are widespread: D-arabinose is present in the cell wall of bacteria, most notably the bacterium _Mycobacterium tuberculosis_ that causes tuberculosis, while L-arabinose is a component of plant polysaccharides. The plant pathogen _Agrobacterium tumefaciens_ is a remarkable case of differential use of arabinose enantiomers: D-arabinose is part of the LPS produced by the strain B6 (_18_), while L-arabinose released by the wounds of plant cells/tissues triggers the virulence genes of the bacterium (_19_). Moreover, L- and D-arabinose can both be metabolized by bacteria—highlighting not only a lack of toxicity, but flexibility in metabolic pathways (_20_). Collectively, this shows that microbes already encode the proteins necessary to recognize, synthesize, process and degrade many mirror image sugars (_i.e.,_ L- and D-enantiomers).

**Protein-glycan interactions****are influenced by carbohydrate stereochemistry****.** Lectins are naturally occurring glycan-binding proteins with specificity for selected carbohydrates and small glycan epitopes. In many organisms, including humans, lectins are critical elements of innate and adaptive immunity. Analysis of glycan binding to lectins from diverse species provides insight into the impact of stereochemistry on protein**–** glycan interactions. Currently, we do not fully understand the extent to which the native lectins that exist in existing organisms can recognize mirror-image glycans, including the mirror-image glycans found in present-day organisms. Some lectins have been observed to bind not only canonical L-fucose but also the “fucose like” L-galactose (_21_). Several lectins that bind to natural L-fucose equally strongly bind to L-glucose (_22_). Other lectins have been documented to recognize the identical stereochemical motif of the three consecutive carbons in D-galactose, L-mannose, L-rhamnose and L-lyxose (_23_). The vast array of the monomers present in microbial glycans (_24_) is almost certainly currently underestimated (_13_), and the capacity of human lectins to recognize diverse glycans from the microbial world is only starting to be defined (_25_).

**Recognition of mirror-image sugars by the immune system:** Glycans function in innate and adaptive immunity, and these immune responses are unlikely to depend on which enantiomer is presented. With regard to adaptive immunity, carbohydrates alone are often considered non-immunogenic, but when linked to a T-cell-dependent antigen, they can elicit potent antibody responses (_26_). Data indicate that certain T cell receptors can recognize carbohydrates when presented by appropriate antigen-presenting molecules. Sugars are not known to bind to the molecules that present antigen to T cells unless these sugars are linked to lipids, peptides, or have zwitterionic motifs. These structural features bind to the appropriate site on the antigen-presenting molecule. Glycolipids play a role in many processes such as natural killer T-cells (NKT) activation in response to infection (_27_). Mirror (glyco)lipids may also be presented by CD1 (_28_), providing another potential avenue for adaptive immune responses against mirror bacteria through their glycans. Furthermore, zwitterionic polysaccharides (ZPS) are peptide and lipid-independent carbohydrates that are antigenic and bind to the MHCII cleft by ionic interactions, thereby inducing a T cell response (_29_). The ZPS, such as _S. pneumoniae_ type 1 polysaccharide (Sp1) and _B. fragilis_ polysaccharide A (PSA), are examples (_30_). Also, the terminal lipid A like lipid on the reducing end of PSA is required for T cell induction because this lipid activates the antigen-presenting cell through Toll-like receptors (TLRs) (_31_). Exploration of the role of chirality of lipid A on its functions and interactions with TLR4/MD2 and TRIF will be critical to understand the response to _bona fide_ mirror image glycolipids. The potential for B cells to recognize carbohydrate antigens is highlighted by a recent study that demonstrated that germline-encoded antibodies can recognize carbohydrates (_32_). Additionally, most humans have antibodies against L-rhamnose (_33_) and other microbial glycans (including five-membered ring variants of D-galactose and L-arabinose called ‘furanose forms’) that closely resemble the mirror-image of sugars in our bodies, demonstrating that we are poised to respond to mirror-image glycans. As with lectins, anti-carbohydrate antibodies display plasticity in their recognition: for example, some mouse and human monoclonal antibodies that bind to D-galactose terminal epitopes cross-react with L-rhamnose (_34_). These observations argue that mirror-image monosaccharides that correspond to naturally existing enantiomers (such as L-mannose) will likely elicit higher levels of immunogenicity on average, while only the decidedly less common case of non-natural mirror-image monosaccharides (_e.g.,_ L-keto-3-deoxy-octonic acid, (_35_)) could potentially escape immune detection.

Beyond adaptive immunity, there is also potential for innate immunity to respond to mirror-image carbohydrates. Immune cells (_e.g.,_ macrophages, dendritic cells, neutrophils, and B cells) can recognize abnormal cell surface glycosylation patterns to promote degradation and/or antigen presentation. Indeed, uptake of cells presenting non-host glycans can lead to their degradation by innate immune cells. Interestingly, macrophages can distinguish mirror-image particles (_36_). Innate immune cells express a suite of lectins that collectively assist in self/non-self discrimination. Lectins recognize foreign carbohydrates on microbial cells and may already be able to recognize different stereoisomers of existing glycans. For example, the human lectin intelectin-1 uses chelated calcium to recognize 1,2-diols (_37_), which are present in both enantiomers of a carbohydrate. C-type lectins and siglecs on myeloid and lymphoid cells are involved in recognizing bacterial MAMPs based on glycans (_38, 39_). Many immune C-type lectins recognize diequatorial diols in sugars, which, by symmetry, also survive mirror inversion. For example, the immune lectin DC-SIGN can recognize glycans displaying D-mannose and L-fucose (_37_) residues. This lectin also interacts with L-rhamnose with weak affinity (_40_) and D-rhamnose and L-galactose moieties, even with higher affinity.

In conclusions, glycans, as first point of contact between hosts and pathogens, have evolved and diversified more rapidly and extensively than other biomolecules. This could make them a unique point of interaction with the mirror image world, since our ability to recognize their ever changing nature is more ancient and sophisticated than MHC peptide/TCR recognition (easily thwarted by D peptides).

**Enantiomers of natural glycans have biological activity _in vivo_ :** It is reasonable to expect that D-glucose-binding receptors do not recognize L-glucose _in vivo_ : for example, CNS transporters do not take up 2-[18F]fluoro-2-deoxy-L-glucose (L-FDG), in contrast to clinically used FDG (_41_), but there are exceptions where L-glucose exhibits an active role in metabolism (_42_) and _in vivo_ imaging (_43_). A remarkable exception is the well-known equal sweetness of L-glucose and D-glucose (_44_), both enantiomers of sucrose (_45_), and equally sweet perception of seven enantiomeric pairs of common glycans (_44_). Similar sweetness has been attributed to the ability of sweet taste TAS1R2/TAS2R3 receptors to bind both enantiomers of sugars (_46_). In contrast, L-mannose-6-phosphate, an enantiomer of the commonly used D-mannose-6-phosphate (_47_), and N-acetyl-L-galactosamine (L-GalNAc), an enantiomer of the FDA-approved targeting agent D-GalNAc, do not engage their natural receptors (_48_). These carbohydrates have different biological activities associated with their enantiomers and are clearly biologically active. This behavior is likely due to their interactions with as yet uncharacterized receptors on the surface of cells. Overall, this heterogeneity in outcome, with some glycan interacting proteins exhibiting sharp enantio-preferences and others seemingly promiscuous, is bound to be more egregious with glycans than with proteins or nucleic acids, because of the greater natural abundance of mirror-image enantiomers and the potential for diastereoisomers that are pseudo enantiomers.

**Interrogating the consequences of mirror life forms** should include the accumulating knowledge of cross-chiral recognition in protein–glycan interactions. Similar investigations can be incorporated into contemporary research on microbe–host interactions, lectin–glycan binding, and serological analyses of glycan-specific antibodies. For instance, it is relatively straightforward to synthesize mono- and oligosaccharides containing some of the accessible mirror-image sugars (_e.g_., multivalent displays of L-mannose and D-fucose) and then evaluate recognition by the human polyclonal IgG/IgM repertoire. These mirror-image mono- and oligosaccharides could be incorporated into glycan arrays alongside traditional glycans to build the knowledge base for cross-chiral recognition in glycobiology. Enantiomers of simple glycopeptide antigens can be tested in animal models for their ability to be recognized or elicit an immune response. **Predicting cross-chiral recognition** between mirror-life glycans and present-day proteins, antibodies, and defense systems could be advanced using machine learning algorithms that aggregate the binding properties of stereoisomers (_22_) or just by direct docking studies into experimentally or AlphaFold based immune receptor structures. These are just a few examples of experiments that are feasible and would give rise to evidence-based exploration of the risks posed by mirror-image life forms decades before their potential emergence.

This letter focuses on mirror copies of known life forms, which would necessarily be coated with enantiomeric forms of the glycans. However, the synthesis of simple, natural forms of life offers an intriguing possibility to test the universal need for glycans on the surfaces of all known cells. “ _We can imagine building a minimal synthetic cell with very simple membranes and natural components, and without many, if any, glycan modifications, and we hope to see how few carbohydrates are needed for life_ ” (Kate Adamala and John Glass, personal communication).

**Conclusions:** Mirror isomers of natural glycans could plausibly bind to immune lectins or antibodies of the innate and adaptive immune system, respectively. These glycans are immunogenic as their stereochemical arrangements are similar to those present in “foreign” glycans for which immune responses already exist. Given that innate immunity lectins can promote the lysis and degradation of the cells to which they bind, this component of the immune response may remain functional against mirror-image organisms. Experiments can and should be conducted to provide an evidence-based evaluation of the breadth of immune recognition of mirror-image glycans and to identify potential blind spots of the immune system, particularly with respect to potential T-cell responses to mirror glycans. It would be helpful to interrogate the immunology (or even pathology) of existing microorganisms that already use D and L monosaccharides. Additional studies may be conducted to understand innate and adaptive immune responses towards glycoproteins, glycolipids, or conjugate vaccines made with D and L monosaccharides presented on peptide and glycerolipids of inverted stereochemistry. We are confident that these experiments will test the preparedness of the immune system to recognize the myriad “foreign” stereoisomers of common carbohydrates that will cover the surface of mirror-image life forms.

We welcome Perrin’s comments and appreciate his engagement with our work. Perrin notes many of the questions which gave us pause for thought, and which are investigated in some detail in the technical report referenced in our article [1]. Limitations on space meant that we could not elaborate on all of these topics in the _Science_ article.

Perrin states that “to fully create a self-amplifying system capable of using _natural_ building blocks, hundreds of additional _ent._ -enzymes will be needed for the synthesis of amino acids, vitamins, sugars, etc.” We agree that a set of such enzymes will be needed and would be a technical hurdle for creating a mirror bacterium, but we see no reason why this hurdle could not be cleared in our 10-30 year time estimate. In Section 2.2 of the technical report we outline one approach, arguing that “If scientists were to succeed at creating sufficiently efficient mirror transcription and translation systems from a handful of mirror-image RNAs and mirror proteins, and combine them with a mirror genome, they could synthesize directly from the mirror genome the thousands of proteins and RNAs required for life. The resulting mirror proteins could then synthesize mirror lipids, carbohydrates, a cell wall, and metabolites.” [1]

Perrin further notes that “[m]embrane chirality would pose problems” in the construction of a mirror cell using the “top-down” pathway (in the nomenclature of the report [1]). He argues that in this pathway, mirror membrane proteins for energy generation and transport would need to initially function within a natural-chirality membrane, and that their function might be disrupted during the conversion of a natural-chirality membrane to a mirror membrane. In our view, the available evidence suggests that such incompatibility is unlikely to pose an insurmountable barrier: for example, the membrane protein ATP synthase can function in artificial membranes composed of synthetic copolymers [2], and hence appears tolerant to diverse membrane environments. _E. coli_ with a mixed-chirality membrane containing 20-30% of archaeal lipids is viable with unchanged growth rates [3], further indicating that many membrane proteins are tolerant to altered membrane compositions. It therefore seems likely that many membrane proteins could remain functional, and protein engineering could be applied to fix those that cause problems. Concerns about membrane chirality also only apply to a top-down pathway; if a mirror bacterium were created using a bottom-up pathway, the mirror membrane containing mirror proteins could be synthesized directly, avoiding the problem.

Perrin also claims that mirror bacteria would face substantial immune resistance because D-peptides can be immunostimulatory. While we agree that adaptive immune receptors have the potential to bind D-proteins and peptides [4], binding to these receptors may be insufficient for a robust adaptive immune response, because the innate immune response (which is critical for an effective adaptive response) is likely to be severely impaired. For example, while B cells can mount antibody responses to diverse molecules, they only do so weakly in the absence of innate immune activation[[5](http://web.archive.org/web/20260530125331/https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0264410X16300688)]. The existing literature supports this, as substantial immune responses to D-peptides have only been observed when samples were administered with adjuvants or were contaminated with small amounts of L-peptides (see Section 4.3 of the technical report; [6]; [7]; [8]). D-peptides and D-proteins are weakly or non-immunogenic on their own [[9](http://web.archive.org/web/20260530125331/https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-018-24517-6), [10](http://web.archive.org/web/20260530125331/https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acschembio.5b01006)]. Antigen-presenting cells are unlikely to be able to cleave and process mirror proteins [[11](http://web.archive.org/web/20260530125331/https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0168365911009886), [12](http://web.archive.org/web/20260530125331/https://analyticalsciencejournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/ddr.430350105)], and so will generate few mirror peptides as candidates for binding to MHC molecules. Those D-peptides that are formed may bind MHC and T cell receptors more weakly than natural peptides [[13](http://web.archive.org/web/20260530125331/https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9188848/), [14](http://web.archive.org/web/20260530125331/https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF00126739)]. The claim that D-peptides themselves are robust adjuvants is not supported, as the relevant citation in Perrin’s eLetter [15] refers to a hydrogel adjuvant with D-peptide components, which is not comparable to mirror-bacterial derived D-proteins. We cover the existing literature on the immunogenicity of D-peptides and proteins in depth in section 4.3 of the technical report and the lack of innate immune activation in sections 4.1 and 4.2, including several of the citations provided in Perrin’s letter [6, 7].

Perrin states that “For an _ent_.-bacterium to be pathogenic, new _ent_.-virulence factors would be needed.” In Chapter 4 of the technical report, we explain in detail how, despite lacking functional virulence factors, mirror bacteria might still passively enter a host, proliferate in blood and internal compartments, and plausibly cause disease and death via sepsis-like immune dysregulation, nutrient depletion, changes to blood chemistry, and/or physical blockages.  


Perrin also states that mirror bacteria “would find the bloodstream to be nutrient-poor since ammonia, acetate, glycine, glycerol – ingredients typically found in minimal media – are not available in significant quantities.” However, in Chapters 1 and 4 of the technical report we argue that mirror bacteria would likely be able to utilize a wide range of achiral substrates (Table 1.1), some chiral nutrients (Tables 1.2 and 1.3), and trace metals, which are all present in quantities expected to be sufficient for growth in blood. For example, mirror bacteria could use a number of nutrients present in human serum as carbon sources, including glycerol, L-alanine, and L-lactate, which are all found at concentrations of a few hundred micromolar, and acetate, acetoacetate, pyruvate, and succinate, present in the tens of micromolar [16]. If mirror bacteria were created, it would be relatively straightforward to engineer them to catabolize ᴅ-glucose, providing them with a substantial additional source of nutrients (Section 3.1).

Finally, Perrin makes several related points on the potential to develop medical countermeasures for mirror bacteria, arguing that it would be straightforward to synthesize enantiomers of at least some antibiotics, that many such antibiotics could be effective at killing mirror bacteria while causing minimal damage to the host, that some achiral and racemic antibiotics are also available, and that mirror bacteria would present many promising drug targets. We essentially agree with all of these points and discuss them in Chapter 5 of the technical report. Despite these encouraging observations, that chapter also argues that it is unclear whether mirror antibiotics would substantially reduce the overall risks of mirror bacteria because: 

  1. To successfully treat infections, antibiotics typically require an accompanying effective immune response, and as discussed above, mirror bacteria would not necessarily trigger an effective immune response;
  2. Developing, scaling, and administering antibiotics to all, or even a substantial number of humans during a mirror-bacterial pandemic would be extremely logistically difficult;
  3. Other aspects of pandemic response (e.g. detection and sample handling) were outside of the scope of the technical report but would also need to be modified for mirror bacteria;
  4. Crucially, medical countermeasures could not plausibly prevent potential harms to non-human animals, plants, and ecosystems (see also Chapters 6, 7, and 8).



In closing, we thank Perrin for his comments, but we remain deeply concerned about the risks of mirror bacteria. We recognize and agree that existing and naturally-emerging pathogens are a critical health security issue. We also recognize the significant effort that would be required to create self-replicating mirror bacteria, but we believe that this goal is achievable. We continue to maintain that mirror bacteria would appear to be exceptionally dangerous because of their unprecedented potential for broad immune evasion and ecological spread. We look forward to further discussion in the coming months to determine the path ahead.

###### Antibiotics Through the Looking-Glass: Computational Approaches to Tackling the Threats of Mirror Life

Lorenzo Pedroni

  * Department of Food and Drug, University of Parma, Parma, Italy



Chiara Dall'Asta

  * Department of Food and Drug, University of Parma, Parma, Italy



Gianni Galaverna

  * Department of Food and Drug, University of Parma, Parma, Italy



Luca Dellafiora

  * Department of Food and Drug, University of Parma, Parma, Italy



_Would you follow the white rabbit?_ In the 1800s, Louis Pasteur debated around the so-called _mirror_ _life_ , today referring to enantiomeric systems – e.g. _left-handed_ carbohydrates and nucleotides, and _right-handed_ proteins (Wang et al., 2016. _Nature Chemistry_ doi:10.1038/nchem.2517; Lander et al., 2023. _Chembiochem,_ doi:10.1002/cbic.202200537). During the same period, in a milestone of nineteenth-century literature, Alice wondered if the milk through the looking-glass was good to drink. Since then, scientists and novelists have speculated and romanticized the topic. Although mirror organisms are theoretical, technological advancements may enable their creation before effective containment strategies are developed, leaving the biosphere vulnerable to their potential spread. Herein, Adamala _et al._ warned that mirror bacteria pose an unprecedented threat to life as they could evade immune systems and exhibit enhanced fitness and antibiotic resistance. These aspects cannot be deepened yet due to technical impairments like those limiting the production of D-proteins, but computational models may reliably overcome these limits. 

In a computational proof-of-concept open to cumulative research, we wondered whether the broadly used antibiotic amoxicillin could be effective _in wonderland_ , i.e. against the D-counterpart of its native target (Pedroni et al., 2024. _Zenodo,_ doi: 10.5281/zenodo.14672236). The theoretical evidence gathered thus far is compelling, yet it calls for further in-depth interdisciplinary analysis, suggesting the potential inadequacy of amoxicillin against its mirror target and aligning with current doubts about the antibiotics’ effectiveness in a mirror scenario.

_What can be found there?_ Regardless of the results obtained on amoxicillin, computational approaches have proved to offer a versatile and effective first-line framework for analysis, free from biohazards and technical limitations of reagents supply. Indeed, these methods might systematically map the mirror-image proteins associated with virulence, pathogenicity and pharmacological interventions that are likely to evade the currently available containment strategies. This would help identify weaknesses, enabling testing hypotheisis and the development of effective countermeasures in advance, as it is preferable to take preventive measures rather than face adverse consequences. In this context, computational space provides a secure environment to pre-test and design strategies or hypotheses to tackle this threat, or to determine if mirror life constitutes a risk so challenging to contain that it becomes unacceptable.

###### In response to "Confronting risks of mirror life".

David Perrin

  * Professor
  * UBC Chemistry Department



In the December 12th issue of _Science_ , Adamala _et al_. in “Confronting risks of mirror life” raise the specter of “mirror-life” organisms—bacteria whose molecular components are the enantiomers of those found in natural life—warning of the “unprecedented risks” such organisms might pose to human health. Yet a number of critical aspects were not fully discussed. These include the immune system’s capacity to respond, the complex nature of bacterial virulence factors, and the vast pharmacological arsenal capable of mitigating these risks. Without mention of these issues, readers are left with an inflated view of the danger posed by enantiomeric organisms (a more reasoned analysis is presented in the technical report - Ref. 1). In response to authors’ inviting community members to join this discussion, I, as a bioorganic chemist working on peptides and nucleic acids, wish to forward the following counterpoint, which I will restrict to the risks to human health.

The chemical syntheses of enantiomeric DNA (_ent_.-DNA) from L-deoxyribose and enantiomeric T7-RNA Polymerase (_ent_.-RNA Pol) from D-amino acids enabled the polymerization of enantiomeric RNA (_ent_.-RNAs) from L-ribonucleoside triphosphates (Xu and Zhu _Science_ 2022 378: 405-412). It reasons that in the presence of synthetic _ent_.-tRNA synthases, _ent._ -polymerases, L-ATP, L-nucleoside triphosphates, and D-amino acids, such a system would amplify life’s key complex molecules from simple building blocks thereby closing the loop on the central dogma. Nevertheless, to fully create a self-amplifying system capable of using _natural_ building blocks, hundreds of additional _ent_.-enzymes will be needed for the synthesis of amino acids, vitamins, sugars, etc. And still, such a system would only be life-like, but not living!

Two defining properties of life will be required: 1) the ability to generate ATP (energy), and 2) the synthesis of a membrane to concentrate substrates in cells using transporter proteins that exploit ion gradients. Missing is a discussion of the chiral nature of membranes (Martin, Podolsky _et al. ChemBioChem_ 2021 22: 3148–3157). Membrane chirality would pose problems for hijacking a living cell (Ref. 1) by injecting all required _ent_.-DNA/proteins/substrates to racemize first the cytosol and then the membrane, before emerging as _ent_.-life. It is difficult to imagine how host membrane proteins, which at the outset would be needed for energy synthesis and transport, will maintain their activity throughout membrane racemization before being displaced by their new _ent.-_ forms, the secretion of which would need to coincide with enantio-membranogenesis.

Notwithstanding the improbability of jump-starting _ent._ -life, a number of issues require critical analysis. First, is immunity. The editorial strongly suggests that _ent_.-bacteria would evade immune detection, thereby becoming virulent pathogens. This claim is poorly substantiated: references 4 and 5 do not critically address D-antigen immunogenicity (Ref. 1 does so in part). Others report the immune system can mount a response against D-peptide antigens (Van Regenmortel and Muller _Current Opinion in Biotechnology_ 1998 9: 377-382, Benkirane, Friede _et al. J. Biol. Chem._ 1993 268: 26279-26285; Scheerer, Kramer _et al._ _J. Mol. Recognit._ 2007 20: 263–274). Indeed, antigens, designed from D-peptides to increase serum stability, elicit high and sustained levels of neutralizing antibodies in animals (Briand, Benkirane _et al. Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. USA_ 1997 94:12545–12550). D-peptides are also highly efficient immunostimulatory adjuvants (Yan, Shi _et al. ACS Biomater. Sci. Eng._ 2018 4: 2000 2006).

Bacterial virulence factors are overlooked (although addressed in Ref. 1). The bacterial strains that scourged society and still stoke fear (e.g. cholera, diphtheria, bubonic plague, tetanus, anthrax, shigella, botulism, listeria, salmonella) are pathogenic because they secrete specific virulence factors. These modify endogenous cellular proteins, release calcium, inhibit nerve and other functions, or proteolyze key targets, causing cells to die releasing their contents to nourish the bacteria thereby enabling spread. There is no reason to believe that the enantiomers of known virulence factors would confer pathogenicity.

For an _ent_.-bacterium to be pathogenic, new _ent._ -virulence factors would be needed. Could these be designed or evolved? In theory, yes. In practice, such would be exceptionally difficult. Yet, it would be contradictory to argue that an _ent.-_ bacterium could secrete an active virulence factor while escaping our immune system. Notably, an _ent_.-bacterium would have little to gain from lysing the cell since many of its contents would not serve as nutrients. Unless an _ent_.-bacterium could degrade L-proteins and epimerize L-amino acids into D-amino acids (including at the beta-positions of Thr and Ile) and metabolize D-glucose, it would find the bloodstream to be nutrient-poor since ammonia, acetate, glycine, glycerol – ingredients typically found in minimal media – are not available in significant quantities.

Conceding, for the sake of argument, an _ent_.-bacterium with competent virulence factors and immune system stealth appeared, one might ponder the medical establishment’s ability to respond. The authors appreciate that certain antibiotics would need to be resynthesized as their enantiomers. However, achieving this is not particularly difficult for synthetic chemists who report enantioselective routes (Nicolaou, Pulukuri _et al. J. Am. Chem. Soc._ 2017 139: 15868–15877; Tatsuta, _The Journal of Antibiotics_ 2013 66: 107–129). Moreover, antibiotics such as ciprofloxacin and nitrofurantoin are achiral, and thus should be immediately effective against _ent_.-bacteria. Racemic drugs, e.g. ofloxacin, should also be equally effective. 

A wide range of known drugs and toxins, when used as their enantiomers, are likely to be _much more_ potent for killing an _ent_.-bacterium than an natural one owing to significantly lower human toxicity. Consider puromycin - too toxic for human use - yet its enantiomer is nontoxic to cells (Pestka, Vince _et al. Antimicrobial Agents and Chemother._ 1973 4: 37-43), and thus could become a powerful weapon against _ent_.-bacteria. Similarly, many other known drugs e.g. streptozotocin, D-cordycepin, D-arabinocytosine, and L-bortezomib, when resynthesized as their enantiomers, are likely to be more selective against _ent_.-pathogens. Arguably, _ent_.-penicillin and all enantiomers of a vast pharmacopeia of beta-lactam antibiotics would become go-to leads. This is not to say that all enantiomers are already known to be safe; indeed, their use would require trials. Yet in many cases, the safety profiles of each enantiomer, as well as the racemate are already known, owing to regulatory decisions, patent filings, and cost-of-goods analysis (Testa and Trager, _Chirality_ 1990 2: 129-133; Fassihi, _International Journal of Pharmaceutics_ 1993 92: 1-14; Hancu and Modroiu _Pharmaceuticals_ 2022 15: 240).

Importantly, _every_ enzyme, which would normally be excluded as a target owing to cross-reactivity with the human isoform, becomes a viable target. In addition, myriad natural products, with no known target, now become new leads. Since L-oligonucleotides won’t cross-react with the host genome, antisense and crispr technologies will provide many leads. Finally, vaccines could be developed using D-peptide antigens.

The medical community’s infrastructure and wellspring of drugs is not to be underestimated for effectively neutralizing a hypothetical _ent._ -bacterium. While it is thought provoking to consider the hypothetical risks of engineering _ent.-_ bacteria “through the looking glass” of a biotech lab, their development remains firmly in the distant realm of the hypothetical. To quote Lewis Carroll’s Through the Looking Glass: “ _Contrariwise, if it was so, it might be; and if it were so, it would be; but as it isn’t, it ain’t. That’s logic_.”

I submit that the editorial should have discussed these points so as to not scare the reader into overestimating the purported dangers, or thinking that the danger is imminent. The risks of mirror-life organisms are likely to be far less virulent than those posed by drug-resistant pathogens that can evolve or jump zoonotically here on earth.
