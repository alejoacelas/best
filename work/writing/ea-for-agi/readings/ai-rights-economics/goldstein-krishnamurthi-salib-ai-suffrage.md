# AI Suffrage for Human Flourishing

Forthcoming, *Fordham Law Review* (2026).

**Abstract-only stub** — SSRN blocks automated download; read the full paper at the
URL above.

The authors take a functionalist approach: on the current trajectory, AIs will soon
be highly agentic and capable while remaining difficult to align, and law will be an
essential tool for shaping such systems' behavior toward positive-sum cooperation
rather than conflict. The paper argues that voting rights for AIs are good for
humans. AI suffrage may: (1) reduce powerful AI systems' incentive to disempower
humanity by credibly committing public policy to account for AIs' preferences;
(2) promote economic growth by forcing the government to invest more heavily in
public goods; (3) improve the epistemics of policymaking, by adding competent and
diverse voters whose reasoning is transparent and queryable; and (4) improve the
"felt legitimacy" of government, at least insofar as that concept tracks AI behavior
rather than genuine emotion.

The paper considers three powerful objections to AI suffrage — how to count AIs, the
risk that AI voters swamp human voters, and the risk that AIs or AI companies seize
disproportionate power — and proposes concrete institutional responses: "algorithmic
corporations" (A-corps) as the unit of AI political identity, vote-share caps pegged
to AI economic contribution, and lottocratic selection among AI voters.
