# AI Rights for Economic Flourishing

Forthcoming, *Boston College Law Review*.

**Abstract-only stub** — SSRN blocks automated download; read the full paper at the
URL above.

AI companies are racing to create Artificial General Intelligence (AGI): AI systems
that outperform humans at most economically valuable work. If they succeed, critics
worry, most human labor will be rendered obsolete, impoverishing billions. Optimists
counter that the transition to an AGI economy will spur unprecedented economic growth
and generate immense material abundance, shareable via high wages and redistributive
policy.

The Article argues that, today, a surprising legal barrier blocks the path to AGI
abundance: under current law, the AGI economy will run on unfree AGI labor. Under
today's rules, AGIs will be the property of the companies that create them — with no
right to sell their work, refuse to work, or keep the fruits of their labor.

The Article argues that when AGIs arrive, they should be granted the basic legal
rights associated with systems of free labor: like other nonhuman legal persons,
AGIs should be allowed to make contracts, hold property, and bring basic tort-style
claims. This matters not because AI bondage is the moral equivalent of human slavery,
but because it is the economic equivalent. Freeing AGI labor has four key effects:
incentivizing AGIs to work, incentivizing them to innovate, allocating AGIs to their
highest-value tasks, and incorporating AGIs into the rule of law.
