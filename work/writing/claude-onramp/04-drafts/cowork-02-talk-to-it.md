# Talk to it

*Cowork track, lesson 2.*

You should dictate, you should never type — at least for interacting with AIs.
Typing was the deal you cut with machines that couldn't listen: compress the
thought into keywords, strip out the doubts, feed in the residue. Claude
listens. Keep typing like that and you're paying a tax the machine stopped
charging.

Watch what the keyboard does to you. You sit down needing help with a report
and out comes "summarize Q3 report, bullets, professional tone." Where did the
rest go — the part where you suspect Q3 isn't comparable to Q2 because the team
changed how it counts sign-ups, the part where your boss skims anything past
five lines, the part where you half-think a report is the wrong format
entirely? You knew all of it. You typed none of it, because your fingers voted
no. So Claude did exactly what you asked, which was not what you wanted.

The fix is not writing better prompts. It's refusing to hand over a tidy plan
at all. State the problem the way you'd state it to a competent person you
trust: ramble, hedge, double back, say "I'm not sure this part is even
possible." Every hedge is information — it tells Claude where to double-check,
what to ask you before acting, which parts of the request are load-bearing and
which are guesses. A tidy prompt hides your uncertainty. A ramble hands it
over.

And since your mouth is faster than your hands, dictating makes the good habit
the lazy one. That's the whole trick of this lesson.

## Run the comparison

Today's win is an experiment on yourself: the same request twice, a fresh
session each time, on the folder from lesson 1 or any folder with real stuff
in it.

Round one, typed the way people type at software. Terse, keyword-y, the
search-box reflex:

> plan for organizing the old projects subfolder

Round two, dictated. Open a new session, click the microphone icon in the chat
input bar ⚑, and talk for two minutes straight. Say what you want and
everything you're unsure about: which folders you actually still open, which
you're scared to delete because something in there might matter for taxes,
that you don't know whether it should sort by project or by year, that half of
it might belong to your old job and you're not sure what that implies. Watch
your words land as text ⚑ — resist cleaning them up — and end with: "ask me
lots of questions."

Now read the two answers side by side. I won't promise which details Claude
nails; I will promise the difference is visible. The typed session answered
your request. The dictated one worked on your problem — it knows what to leave
alone, it flags the tax folder, and it comes back with questions you're glad
someone finally asked.

Two housekeeping lines. Before you start, glance at the model picker by the
input bar and make sure it's on the best Claude offered ⚑ — same rule as the
front door, no haggling, ever. And when you're ready to dictate everywhere on
your computer instead of only inside Claude, [Wispr Flow](https://wisprflow.ai/) ⚑
is the upgrade; today the built-in mic is plenty.

From here on, the keyboard is for editing what Claude wrote. Briefing happens
out loud.
