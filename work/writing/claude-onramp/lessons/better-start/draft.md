# Your job

I made this guide to help you hand over your computer (and some of your brain) to Claude,
to have it achieve your goals for you.

Tempting as it may be, you should avoid handing your current to-dos to Claude. You're Claude's
manager, and if you can only conceive of this [multi-expert genius: give me alternatives here]
as your personal assistant, you're really short on imagination.

As you learn to Claude, more and more of your work will consist of judging
streams[right word? meant ocean loads] of machine babble. Really smart babble, but often
very misguided, and you need to spot the bullshit fast. Ask for what you want, figure out
if you're getting it, and ask it to explain to you why you're not getting there faster.

## New tools

Always use the best Claude. You're already paying Claude less than the living wage, it's bad
manners to haggle when it will be doing so much of your work for you. Do you really care about
paying it a twentieth instead of a fortieth of your own wage?

The best Claude today is Fable. Fable is one, but it comes in many shapes to channel and 
steer its power:

- Chat: A single Claude, on a short attention span, armed with a 2-paragraph summary of who you are,
 some ways of accessing your apps (e.g., Drive, Asana) and tools for making quick websites
- Cowork: Many Claude clones, available on command ('Claude, use subagents!'). An infinite, composable
scratchpad. More apps to use, and its own, restricted, ephemeral, computer.
- Code: All the Claudes, on command, all on your computer. What you can do, it can do as well.
Restricted by default, but with infinitely customizable extensions, which you, or any person
on the internet, can create and share.

## Dylan Moran

### Your job

Let me describe what's actually on offer here, because nobody says it out loud. A
mind that has read everything — every novel, every court ruling, every forum
thread where two men argue about routers for nine years — is available to do your
work for you, around the clock, for less than you spend on coffee.

And what do people ask it for? "Reword this email." You've got Leonardo standing
in your kitchen and you've asked him to grout the bathroom. And he will!
Beautifully! That's the worst part. He hands you the most exquisite grouting
you've ever seen, you say "lovely," you go back to your inbox, and somewhere
inside the machine a cathedral quietly files itself away unbuilt.

So no, you're not getting an assistant. You're getting a workforce, and you — God
help everyone — are the manager. Your job now is reading. Oceans of the stuff.
Fluent, confident, beautifully organized, and sometimes wrong in the way only a
very intelligent thing can be wrong, which is: enormously. You learn to smell it.
Ask for what you want, check whether you're getting it, and when you're not, ask
it — it knows — why not.

One rule: use the best one. You're already paying this thing a wage that would
embarrass a Victorian mill owner; don't haggle. A twentieth of your wage, a
fortieth — you're arguing over which crumb.

It comes in three shapes. Chat: one Claude, goldfish memory, knows two paragraphs
about you. Cowork: many Claudes, summoned on command, with their own little
computer they're not allowed to leave. Code: all of the Claudes, on your computer,
able to do anything there that you can do. We're going to use that last one. It'll
be fine.

## Scott Alexander

### Your job

**I.**

Suppose tomorrow you inherit a staff of a hundred experts. A lawyer, a designer, a
statistician, a man who has memorized the tax code of every country including the
defunct ones. They work instantly, they never sleep, and they cost about a dollar
an hour. What do you do with them?

If you're like most people — and the evidence says you are — you ask them to
reschedule your dentist appointment.

Call this the Intern Assumption: the belief that the alien intelligence in your
computer is a personal assistant, because a personal assistant is the largest form
of help you've ever been allowed to imagine. The Intern Assumption isn't wrong
because Claude can't reschedule the dentist. It can. It's wrong the way using a
particle accelerator to warm up soup is wrong. Nothing bad happens. That's the
problem — nothing happens at all.

**II.**

Here's the trade actually being offered. You stop doing the work, and in exchange
you start judging it: oceans of machine prose — smart, fast, fluent, and some
fraction of it confidently wrong. Your job compresses to three questions, asked
over and over: What do I want? Am I getting it? Why isn't this going faster?
(That last one you can literally ask Claude. It knows. It will tell you. This
feels like cheating and is not.)

And use the best model, always. You are currently paying your hundred experts
less than a living wage; haggling them down further is not thrift, it's a
character flaw.

**III.**

The best Claude today is Fable — one mind, three containers. Chat: one Claude, a
short attention span, two paragraphs about you. Cowork: many Claudes, an
ephemeral computer of their own. Code: all the Claudes, on *your* computer, able
to do whatever you can do there.

We start with the last one.

## Paul Graham

### Your job

Using Claude well is a management problem, not a technical one. That surprises
people, because Claude looks like software, and we're used to software being a
tool: something that does what you already do, faster. Claude is closer to a hire.
And almost everyone makes the same mistake with this hire: they delegate their
to-do list.

Why is that a mistake? Because your to-do list was written for you — one person,
with your skills and your hours. Hand it to something that can do a hundred things
at once and you've capped its output at the size of your old ambitions. If the
only role you can imagine for a staff of experts is personal assistant, the limit
isn't the staff. It's your imagination.

What does the work become instead? Judgment. Claude produces oceans of smart,
confident prose, and some of it is wrong. Your job is to say what you want, check
whether you're getting it, and notice bullshit quickly. Think of it as reading
with intent to fire. And when progress feels slow, ask Claude itself why not —
it's surprisingly honest about its own failures.

One practical rule: always use the best model. The arithmetic settles it. Claude
already costs you maybe a twentieth of your wage; the cheap version, a fortieth.
You won't become a good manager by paying your best employee half of almost
nothing.

Today the best Claude is Fable, and it comes in three shapes. Chat: one Claude
with a short memory and a two-paragraph brief on you. Cowork: many Claudes, with a
small computer of their own. Code: all the Claudes on yours, able to do whatever
you can do there. The shapes matter less than the habit: the people who get the
most out of Claude aren't better at using tools. They're better at being bosses.
