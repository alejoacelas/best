# Practice — Claude Cowork

The hands-on track for Cowork users: one exercise per session, all building the same personal website.

## Setup

Cowork is the Windows of Claude agents: you click and it works, because Anthropic manages it centrally. Fine. Start here.

1. You need a paid Claude plan — Pro, Max, Team, or Enterprise. Free won't do.
2. Download the Claude desktop app at [claude.com/download](https://claude.com/download) (macOS, Windows, or Linux beta — Cowork does not exist on web or mobile) and sign in.
3. Click the **Cowork** tab next to Chat. First run downloads a virtual machine, the padded room where Claude runs code. Let it finish.
   - If there's no Cowork tab or the setup hangs, see [Get started with Claude Cowork](https://support.claude.com/en/articles/13345190-get-started-with-claude-cowork).
4. Install [Wispr Flow](https://wisprflow.ai/) and do its two-minute onboarding. You will dictate every prompt in this course. Never type at an AI: your mouth carries the doubts and half-thoughts your fingers strip out.
   - If dictation lands in the wrong window, check Wispr's mic and accessibility permissions in [their help center](https://help.wisprflow.ai/) [?: current Wispr Flow help URL].
5. Make a folder on your computer called `website`. In Cowork, create a project from that folder. The folder is the fence: Claude sees what's inside it, not the rest of your disk.
6. First prompt, dictated: "This folder will hold my personal website. Create a page that says hello and states my name, and open it so I can see it." If a page opens in your browser, you're set.
   - App closed or laptop asleep means tasks stop. Keep it open while Claude works.

## think

1. Open a task in your website project. Dictate for three minutes straight: who the site is for, what you do, sites you envy, the joke you may want in the footer, everything — including what you're unsure about.
2. Do not organize the ramble first. Thinking is effortful and your brain will bargain for less of it; Claude is forgiving of mess, so hand the mess over.
3. End with: "Build a first version. Ask me questions where I was vague."
4. Let it run. Look at the result and lower your standards: a mediocre site that exists beats the perfect one you'd never start.

## understand

1. Find the part of the site that's wrong. There is one.
2. Do not re-roll. Ask instead: "Walk me through what you did there and why."
3. Read the explanation and check it against what you see on the page. You can delegate your thinking, not your understanding — if you don't know why it failed, you'll hit the same wall next week.
4. Only then: "What are some ways to fix it?" Pick one, test it.
5. If the failure came from something it can't actually do, write that down. Knowing what it lies about is the skill.

## ease

1. Notice what you re-explained in the last two sessions — your name, the tone you want, "open the result when done."
2. Put those lines in the project's Instructions. Memory is on per project, so what Claude learns here stays here.
3. Rule for the rest of your life: the second time an instruction leaves your mouth, promote it to Instructions.
4. Test it: new task, no briefing, "improve the About section." It should already know you.
5. Keep the project pinned. Starting must cost one click and one breath, or you'll stop starting.

## mingle

1. Find a personal site you admire. Ask a coursemate for theirs.
2. Give Claude the URL: "Steal what's good here — layout, structure, that thing they do with the header — and adapt it to my site." The world has figured things out for you; take them.
3. Send your site's screenshot to the person you stole from. Say what you took.
4. Take one trick from this exchange and add it to your Instructions.

## stop

1. Decide what Claude may never do to this project. Real constraints only — every constraint gets painful with an agent, so it should be worth it. Mine: never delete photos, never rewrite text I marked as final.
2. Dictate them into the project Instructions as "Never" lines.
3. Check your permission mode. "Ask before acting" pauses for approval; "Act without asking" is faster. Pick "Act without asking" for this project — Cowork still asks before permanently deleting files no matter what.
4. Worry less about security than your instincts say. The risk here is an over-enthusiastic friend, not a thief.

## ground

1. It will lie to you. "The site works" is not evidence; find the small test.
2. Dictate: "Check every link on the site and every image. Give me a table: item, works or broken, how you checked."
3. Open the site yourself and click two links at random. Compare against its table.
4. Send the site to a friend outside the course. Dictate their reactions, verbatim, into a new task: "Here's what a real person said. What do we change?"

## context

1. Run the same request three times, fresh tasks, different context each time: "Rewrite my About page." Once with nothing extra, once with your CV dropped in the folder, once with a three-minute ramble about what you actually care about.
2. Compare the three. Notice what it latched onto — the CV version will sound like a CV. Models grab the strongest pattern you feed them, not the most important one.
3. Keep the winner. Delete the context that hurt; withholding is a move too.
4. Feed it text. If something lives in a fancy format (XLSX, a design tool), export it as plain text or CSV before dropping it in the folder.

## ending

1. Publish. Cowork builds the files but doesn't host websites; you need one outside step.
2. Easiest: drag your website folder onto [Netlify Drop](https://app.netlify.com/drop). Free, no account needed to start, live URL in seconds. For a custom domain or automatic updates, that's where Claude Code and a hosting service come in — ask a Code-track coursemate for an hour.
3. Send the link to the course group and to three people who knew you before this course.
4. Last dictation: "List what we built and what I now know how to do that I didn't eight sessions ago." Read it. That list is the course.
