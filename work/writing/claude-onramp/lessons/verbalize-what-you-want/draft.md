# Verbalize what you want

If you've done the work of thinking through an idea and know exactly what you want,
you can ask for something really complex in just a few words. Make those words
direct — go straight for the thing, the way great writing does, so that anyone who
reads them understands what's going on. "Deploy a preview with Vercel, just get me
a link" [dictation said "Brazil" — assuming Vercel's account-less preview flow;
correct me] is a few words with a lot encoded in them: Claude knows what Vercel is,
where to look for the deployment tool, that there's a setup that skips registration
and hands back a link.

Often, though, you don't know exactly what you want. Then don't give direct
instructions — chat. It's what you do in real life: when you can't pin down what
you're after, you go to a colleague and talk it through. Or therapy: you don't know
what your problem is, so you give context until they can guide you toward a
solution.

## Dylan Moran

### Verbalize what you want

You have, sitting in front of you, the most patient intelligence ever assembled —
every book, every argument, all of human knowledge folded in like egg whites — and
what do you say to it? "Make my website pretty."

Pretty. What does pretty mean? You don't know. It could mean Swiss minimalism. It
could mean a sunset over a marina. It could mean the lettering on a cereal box you
loved when you were six. The machine doesn't know either, so it guesses, and what
it guesses is a purple gradient, because that's what everyone who came before you
turned out to mean.

And then you're furious. "That's not what I wanted!" Well, what did you want? "Not
that!" Marvelous. This is a design brief the way a dog barking at the postman is
correspondence.

Now, the strange part: when you actually know what you want, you hardly have to
speak at all. "Deploy a preview, send me the link." Six words. Whole warehouses of
meaning collapse into them, because you did the thinking first and the words are
just the receipt.

And when you don't know what you want — which is most of the time, be honest —
stop issuing commands. Talk. It's what you'd do with a colleague. It's what therapy
is: nobody arrives knowing their problem. You arrive with a carrier bag of
complaints, and somebody paid to listen helps you sort them into one.

I say all this as a man who asked a machine to make his writing "flow better" and
then argued with it for forty-five minutes about the result. We're fine now. It's
fine.

## Scott Alexander

### Verbalize what you want

**I.**

Consider the Pretty Problem.

You're making a personal website. You want it to be pretty, so you type "make it
pretty." The AI — which has read every design manual ever written and can quote
Dieter Rams from memory — produces something, and you hate it. You try "beautiful."
You hate that too. You try "stylish, but minimal, but warm." Three rounds in, you
hate three websites, and you have not yet transmitted a single bit of information
about what you want. (You may not have any bits to transmit. Hold that thought.)

The Pretty Problem isn't that the AI is bad at design. It's that "pretty" is a
pointer into your head, and the AI cannot dereference pointers into your head.
In there, "pretty" resolves to something specific — a magazine you read at an
impressionable age, a friend's portfolio you quietly envy. Out here, it resolves to
the average of everything everyone has ever meant by it. Which is how you got the
purple gradient.

**II.**

So there are two regimes. When you know what you want, words are compression:
"deploy a preview, just send the link" is six words that unpack into an entire
procedure, because the knowledge to unpack them is sitting on the other side. When
you don't know what you want, words are search: you're not transmitting a spec,
you're looking for one.

Regime two already has a mature technology, and it's therapy. You don't walk in
and announce "I have an unresolved conflict with my mother; please resolve it."
You talk — badly, in circles — and someone trained to listen helps the actual
problem surface. Nobody calls that talking wasted. It's the treatment.

The failure mode is running regime-one syntax on a regime-two problem: issuing
crisp orders toward a destination you couldn't recognize if you arrived. (I know
all of this and did it again on Tuesday. I asked for "punchier." It added
exclamation points. That was fair.)

So when you know, say it straight. And when you don't, stop ordering.

Talk.

## Paul Graham

### Verbalize what you want

Knowing what you want is not the same as being able to say it. But the two are more
connected than they look: if you can't say it, that's usually evidence you don't
know it yet.

When you do know, you can ask for surprisingly complex things in surprisingly few
words. "Deploy a preview and send me the link" is seven words, and it works because
each one points at something specific: which tool, which mode, what should come
back. The words aren't the request so much as its address. Good writing works the
same way — the sentence is short because the thinking behind it was long.

So compression is a test. If you can state a request in a few words a stranger
would parse correctly, you knew what you wanted. If you can't, you didn't.

What do you do when you fail the test? Not write more detailed instructions. You
can't write directions to a place you couldn't recognize. Do what you'd do with a
person: talk. When you don't know what you want, you don't bring a colleague a
spec; you bring them a mess, and the conversation turns it into one. Therapy is the
extreme case — the entire treatment is saying things out loud to someone until you
discover what your problem is.

Try it on your own website. You want it "pretty." Now define pretty precisely
enough that a stranger could draw it. I tried this and got "clean, but not cold,"
which describes soup. That's not failure; it's the useful discovery that you're
holding the second kind of request.

So the rule: when you know what you want, use as few words as you can. When you
don't, use as many as it takes.
