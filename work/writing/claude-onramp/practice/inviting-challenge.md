# Inviting challenge

How the practice sessions engage people beyond following instructions — a design
note to react to. The one-line version: **every session has a floor anyone can
finish rushed, and one invited ceiling for whoever wants to think.**

## The principle

Instructions produce completion; challenge produces expression. The course's real
skill isn't operating a tool — it's noticing what you actually mean when you ask
for something. "Make it pretty" is the canonical case: the person who can say
*what* pretty means for their site (three words, one example) has learned the
thing; the tool steps are just where that learning happens.

But challenge must be invited, never required. A rushed reader who skips every
challenge still ships a website and still absorbs the eight lessons from the
steps themselves. If the exercise breaks when the reader doesn't engage deeply,
the exercise is broken.

## What I added directly (the clear win)

One italic *Stretch:* line at the end of every session, in all three tracks.
Rules it follows:

- **One line, one move.** Not a second exercise — a reframe of the one they just
  did.
- **Skippable by construction.** It's at the end, it's italic, and no later step
  depends on it.
- **Specific over profound.** "Three specific words, not 'clean'" beats "reflect
  on your aesthetic values."

## The five mechanisms behind the Stretch lines

Each Stretch instantiates one of these; they're the reusable moves if you want to
write more or swap mine out:

1. **Articulation** — replace a vague word with its meaning before using it.
   *What's a pretty website? Three words and an example site.* (think, mingle)
   This is the "what am I actually expressing to the AI" muscle, most directly.
2. **Prediction before reveal** — commit to a guess, then look. The gap is the
   lesson, and it's self-grading. (understand: predict its explanation; context:
   predict each version; ground: predict which item fails)
3. **Adversarial play** — try to break your own rule and feel which layers hold.
   (stop: talk Claude into breaking a harmless Never-rule)
4. **Notice your own reaction** — treat your hesitation or annoyance as data.
   (ease: what exactly stopped you last time; Tag ease: why does the channel feel
   costly?)
5. **Outside calibration** — compare what you meant to express with what a human
   received. (ending: three people, one word each)

## Knobs to react to

- **The name.** "Stretch" is the current pick. Alternatives: "Push further,"
  "If you have ten minutes," "Harder:". Whatever it is, it should be the same
  word in all 24 places so readers learn it's optional once.
- **Placement.** End-of-session (current) keeps the floor clean. The alternative
  — inline at the step it modifies — engages more people but makes rushed reading
  slower. I'd keep end-of-session.
- **A session-zero articulation exercise.** Before the first build, have people
  dictate one sentence: "when [person] sees this site, I want them to think
  [what]." Cheap, and it seeds think/mingle/ending. Not added — it changes Setup,
  which you may want minimal.
- **Public stretches in Tag.** Tag's channel makes challenge social: stretches
  could ask people to *post* their prediction before Claude answers, making the
  gap visible to the team. Higher engagement, higher exposure; I kept them
  private-by-default.
- **A shared vocabulary payoff.** The articulation stretches could feed a running
  "banned vague words" list per cohort (pretty, clean, professional, pop) —
  each person contributes the word they caught themselves using. Fun, but needs a
  facilitator; noted, not built.

## What I deliberately didn't do

- No "reflection questions" blocks — they read as homework and get skipped with
  guilt rather than by choice.
- No second difficulty tier ("advanced track") — it splits the cohort and doubles
  maintenance.
- No stretch on Setup — day one is for the win, not for thinking about the win.
