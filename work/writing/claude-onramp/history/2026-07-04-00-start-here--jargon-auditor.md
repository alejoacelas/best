# Jargon audit — 00-start-here.md — 2026-07-04

Audience: smart, non-technical reader; page 00 precedes every definition the
guide gives (terminal → 02 §1, model → 01 §4, permissions → 01 §5, context →
01 §10).

## Changes

1. "No terminal, nothing to configure." → "No commands to type, nothing to
   configure."
   Why: "terminal" isn't defined until Part 2 §1, and its ordinary meaning
   (airport, endpoint) carries none of the technical one — the reassurance only
   lands if you already know the word. "No commands to type" states the actual
   thing being spared.

2. "granting permissions, and managing context —" → "granting permissions, and
   managing context (how much one conversation can hold) —"
   Why: the plain reading of "managing context" is "supplying good background
   info," which is not what Part 1 §10 teaches (conversations have finite
   capacity; start fresh). Three plain words pin the real mechanism without
   breaking the four-item list.

## Considered and kept

- **"Already fluent with agents in a folder, models, permissions, and context
  windows?"** — deliberate jargon: the sentence is a filter, and recognizing
  the terms *is* the test for skipping ahead. A reader who can't decode it is
  correctly routed to Part 1.
- **"choosing a model"** — plain "model" (a version of a product, as in a car
  model) maps well onto the technical meaning; Part 1 §4 then names the models.
- **"granting permissions"** — ordinary meaning is exactly the technical one.
- **"chatbot"** — common vocabulary by now; ordinary usage carries it.
- **"configure"** — ordinary meaning (set up, arrange) matches.
- **"works out of the box"** — idiom, widely understood, not field jargon.
- **"app," "sidebar," "screenshot," "tier"** — everyday computing words; plain
  meanings are the technical meanings.
- **"delegate/delegating"** — the guide's load-bearing plain word, not jargon.
