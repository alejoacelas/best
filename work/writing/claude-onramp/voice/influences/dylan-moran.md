# Dylan Moran — *Like, Totally* (2006)

Source: the full show transcript in [`sources/dylan-moran-like-totally-2006.txt`](sources/dylan-moran-like-totally-2006.txt)
(cleaned from YouTube auto-captions; quotes below are verbatim from it with
punctuation added and obvious caption errors fixed, since the captions have neither).
He's in the library for surprise: sentences where you don't know how the sentence is
going to end, usually landing on something out of the blue or unexpectedly intense.

## Pattern: The late swerve
**What it is:** The sentence builds in one register — grand, warm, reasonable — and the
last few words belong to a different, lower or darker one. The swerve always sits at the
end; everything before it is played straight.
**Verbatim:**
> "People from every corner of the Earth, every creed, every religion, every culture,
> come here — to vomit in minicabs."
>
> "You're not going to realize your dream somewhere else, you can do it all here: you
> can be trapped in traffic in Tufnell Park for 20 years, and you'll never have that
> experience anywhere else."
>
> "It's a disgusting place. It's always wet — even when it's dry."
**Formula:** Highly codifiable. Build a list or claim in an elevated register; make the
final concrete noun phrase the one thing that doesn't belong. Put the swerve in the last
3–6 words — if it lands mid-sentence the reader coasts through it. The escalating list
version ("every creed, every religion, every culture") is almost mechanical: three
parallel items raise the register, the payoff drops it.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — the single most transferable pattern here.
A lesson sentence can play its setup straight and end somewhere unexpected without
costing any meaning.

## Pattern: The long con
**What it is:** The late swerve stretched over a paragraph. He builds a genuinely warm,
lovingly detailed scene — no jokes, real sentiment, the audience settles into it — and
then detonates it with one intense image. The longer and warmer the setup, the harder
the detonation.
**Verbatim:**
> "Your mother would appear at your side, just this vision of Laura Ashley print dress,
> smelling of magnolias and biscuits, and put the jelly in front of you... and you would
> lift your little spoon up excitedly to press it in and winkle out that first divot of
> black jelly — and then the cage would come down. The cage with the Japanese fighting
> spiders inside."
>
> (Closing the whole show, after two straight minutes on meeting the person you love,
> candlelight, bare shoulders:) "You realize this is the person for you — and then the
> cage comes down, and your mother jumps from the wardrobe with a cigarillo pointing out
> of the corner of her mouth."
>
> "She came to the old stile and began to climb it, but it broke, you see... she could
> see the lights at home, and she began to walk towards home — and then a tank ate her.
> Good night."
**Formula:** Partly codifiable: sincere setup with sensory detail, zero irony, then one
short violent sentence. What resists codification is the nerve — the setup only works if
the writer commits to the sentiment for real, and the detonation image has to be intense
rather than merely random. Note he reuses the exact phrase "the cage comes down" an hour
apart as a callback: the detonation itself can become a motif.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Rarely — maybe once in a whole course. But one
well-placed instance buys a lot of goodwill.

## Pattern: Absurd precision
**What it is:** Where a generic quantity or object would do, he supplies an absurdly
specific one — a number, a brand, a full product description. The specificity is the
joke; it implies a whole world.
**Verbatim:**
> "People bring out 19 different kinds of potatoes, sheets of ham, waves of ham — you
> take a bite out of the middle, you save yourself the price of a poncho."
>
> "A ridiculous place, located three-quarters of a mile from the surface of the Sun."
>
> "You go and you buy stuff you didn't really want anyway — you know, the Ecuadorian
> women's choir doing the songs of Kenny Rogers."
>
> "These amazing-looking people... they look like they weigh as much as a photograph of
> themselves."
**Formula:** Highly codifiable. Scan a draft for generic placeholders ("a lot", "some
things", "a gadget") and replace with one over-specific instance. Two rules: the
specific must be slightly wrong in a *telling* direction (19 kinds of potatoes says
rural hospitality-as-aggression), and one per passage — two absurd specifics in a row
read as trying.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — examples in teaching text are placeholders
by default ("suppose you have a document"); this pattern is exactly the fix.

## Pattern: Broken third
**What it is:** A parallel list where the first items are real and the last one is
invented but delivered in the same register — or a list that quietly escalates from
reasonable to impossible with no change of tone.
**Verbatim:**
> "They all fling themselves into the sea, which is inhabited almost exclusively by
> things designed to kill you: sharks, jellyfish, swimming knives."
>
> "All a woman wants is what anybody wants — you know, friendship, and companionship,
> and respect, and a certain amount of leadership with submission, and cooperation at
> all times, and preemptive empathy, and, you know, general telepathy. It's no big
> deal, is it?"
**Formula:** Highly codifiable — it's a construction, not a sensibility. Keep the
syntactic frame identical across items; corrupt only the content; never signal the
corruption (no "and even..."). The deadpan tag after ("It's no big deal, is it?") is
part of the move.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes, sparingly — lists are everywhere in teaching
text, so one broken third goes a long way.

## Pattern: Alien's-eye redescription
**What it is:** He describes a familiar thing literally, as if to someone who has never
seen it — stripping the social wrapper off and reporting only what actually happens.
**Verbatim:**
> "You learn very very quickly that [driving] is mostly about swearing, actually.
> That's all you're doing: swearing in a box with wheels."
>
> "You say 'bedtime, bedtime' — that's not what the child hears. What the child hears
> is: lie down in the dark for hours, and don't move. I'm locking the door now."
>
> "You know how he got into that position? He got there by lifting things... He went
> right over to the heavy thing, and lifted it, put it back down, and didn't move it
> anywhere. And then he lifted it again, hundreds of times, and said to the people who
> had stopped to observe this aberrant behavior: look how good I am at lifting the
> heavy, in my underpants." (Schwarzenegger becoming governor)
>
> "You wouldn't buy a toaster when you're drunk — because that's too important. It's
> got to be crispy in just the right way, hasn't it?" (on how couples actually meet)
**Formula:** Highly codifiable, and the recipe is teachable: pick the familiar thing,
ban its usual vocabulary (no "bodybuilding", no "career"), and narrate only observable
actions from first principles. The comparison version (drunk marriage vs. sober toaster)
adds a second recipe: hold the familiar thing next to a trivial one we treat more
carefully.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — arguably the best fit of all. A course
about AI is full of familiar-but-unexamined things, and redescribing them from first
principles is simultaneously funny *and* the actual teaching.

## Pattern: Cross-domain simile, twice specified
**What it is:** He describes an intangible (a sound, a face, a national mood) with a
concrete image from a wildly unrelated domain — then, instead of stopping, adds one more
clause of specification that makes it weirder and more exact at once.
**Verbatim:**
> "You couldn't speak German, because it's a horrible sound. It sounds like typewriters
> eating tinfoil, being kicked down the stairs."
>
> "An Irish face always looks like it's been told two very important pieces of
> information at the same time: at one shoulder somebody's just run up and said 'you've
> just won a hundred million pounds and loads of stuff,' and at the other shoulder
> somebody's just whispered in their ear, 'but you only have three minutes to live.'"
>
> "That English smile — hello, hello — looks like you have a rotten oyster under your
> tongue."
**Formula:** Partly codifiable. The frame is mechanical: *X is like [concrete object] +
[concrete verb] + [one more clause]* — the extra clause is what separates it from a
stock simile (typewriters eating tinfoil would be good; *being kicked down the stairs*
is his). The distance of the domain jump is taste.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes, at low frequency — one per lesson, on the
concept that matters most, so the image does mnemonic work.

## Pattern: Play both parts
**What it is:** Instead of asserting a claim about people, he collapses it into a short
dramatized scene and acts out the dialogue — analysis becomes theater. The claim is
never stated; the scene carries it.
**Verbatim:**
> "If you're Catholic, you walk in the room, you see the plate of biscuits. There could
> be other things going on in the room — the room could be on fire, it could be full of
> naked clowns killing each other with crossbows — this doesn't matter to you, because
> all you see is the plate of biscuits: oh no, I'm going to eat them. I know I am...
> I've actually started to eat the biscuits. Help me. Oh, the shame, the shame — I
> can't tell which is nicer, the biscuit or the shame."
>
> "'Why did you put the wet towel on the bathroom floor?' 'Well, me and the guys were
> talking about it over a period of weeks, you know, in the bunker, and we figured that
> was the best place for it. It wouldn't work in the kitchen.'"
>
> "What did you do today? — I had some soup."
**Formula:** Partly codifiable: find the claim ("Catholic guilt is compulsive"), delete
it, write the 4–10 line scene that forces the reader to conclude it. The compression of
the scenes is the hard part — every line is doing double duty.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes, unusually well for this course: its natural
dialogue form is the human-and-Claude transcript, so claims about how people misuse AI
can be *staged* as tiny example exchanges instead of asserted.

## Pattern: Under-reaction
**What it is:** Immediately after the wildest image, a flat mundane coda one register
below it — the voice of someone who finds the horror unremarkable.
**Verbatim:**
> "...and then a tank ate her. Good night. Night night."
>
> "Say what you like about Nazi Germany — those hats! Everywhere those stormtroopers
> went: 'check him out' — before he killed us. That happened a lot."
>
> "'I would stab you to death, but I can't afford to take the two weeks off work.' ...
> Nothing that can't be sorted out by a nice cup of tea."
>
> "It sounds like typewriters eating tinfoil being kicked down the stairs... Now,
> that's not prejudice. That's just observation."
**Formula:** Highly codifiable: after the peak sentence, add one short flat sentence
that treats it as administrative. The coda must be shorter than the peak.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — it's cheap, safe, and works in a single
added sentence.

## Pattern: Turn the gun around
**What it is:** After mocking a group, he includes himself — or the audience — as a
member, one beat later, matter-of-factly.
**Verbatim:**
> "The kind of people who turn to Jesus tend to be the sort of people who haven't done
> that well with everybody else. Like the people who are here, for instance."
>
> "I thought for ages, you know, everybody was like this — I thought everybody woke up
> a couple of mornings a week in the shower with Marmite-clotted handcuffs. But
> apparently not."
>
> "Where would you go? People fling themselves all over the planet... Why would anybody
> want to go there? What is the point of that country? — I usually never leave the
> house, but we all went to Australia recently."
**Formula:** Highly codifiable: any time a draft says "people do X" with any edge, add
the beat where the writer (or reader) turns out to do X too. It converts superiority
into complicity, which is why the edge stays likable.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — a teaching voice that mocks bad AI habits
*needs* this pattern, or it reads as smug.

## What resists extraction
The persona underneath: a man being dragged unwillingly through his own act, forgetting
his own thread on purpose ("Now, I meant to talk about something else earlier on, and I
forgot what it was. I've remembered what it is again — but I've also forgotten, and
that's really what adulthood is like most of the time"). The patterns above are
portable as *tweaks*; the sense that one consciousness with one worldview is generating
all of them is not. Also unportable: rhythm and delivery — the transcript flattens
pauses that carry half the jokes — and the sheer density (a swerve every third sentence
works on stage; on the page it would exhaust). Steal locally, at maybe a tenth of his
frequency.
