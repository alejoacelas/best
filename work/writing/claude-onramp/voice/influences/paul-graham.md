# Paul Graham — 20 essays

Corpus: 20 essays from [paulgraham.com](https://www.paulgraham.com/articles.html), mixing his
long classics (How to Do Great Work, What You Can't Say, Why Nerds Are Unpopular, Hackers
and Painters, How to Make Wealth, What I Worked On, The Age of the Essay) with mediums
(How to Write Usefully, Do Things That Don't Scale, Cities and Ambition, How to Think for
Yourself, The Bus Ticket Theory of Genius, Putting Ideas into Words, Crazy New Ideas, Life
is Short) and shorts (Keep Your Identity Small, Write Like You Talk, Maker's Schedule,
The Top Idea in Your Mind, Founder Mode). Per-essay extraction notes with verified-verbatim
quotes: [`history/2026-07-05-corpus/pg/`](history/2026-07-05-corpus/pg/).

**What the voice is.** Plain spoken declaratives that a friend could say out loud — he
tests them exactly that way ("just don't let a sentence through unless it's the way you'd
say it to a friend"). Evaluation is never carried by adjectives: it's carried by rhythm
(the short sentence after the long ones), by one concrete simile, or by a number.
Confidence and doubt are managed like an instrument panel — the boldest claims stay bald,
and hedges sit exactly on the weak joints, which makes everything unhedged read as
certain. Evidence is mostly himself, told at his own expense. Structure is questions: he
asks the reader's next question out loud and walks through it. Anything that would break
the even tone — jokes, salt, caveats — is exiled to footnotes rather than cut. The tightness
isn't compression after the fact; it's that every sentence is either a step in the
argument or a verdict on one.

## Pattern: The snap
**Seen in:** all 20 essays — this is the base rhythm of the voice.
**What it is:** Paragraphs of medium-length reasoning end on one clipped declarative —
shorter than everything before it — that lands the point and stops. Variants: the
two-word rebuttal, the "Which…"/"Except…" fragment as afterthought, the one-word beat
("Huh."), and the display case — an aphorism given its own paragraph so white space does
the italicizing.
**Verbatim:**
> "Why don't smart kids make themselves popular? If they're so smart, why don't they
> figure out how popularity works and beat the system, just as they do for standardized
> tests? One argument says that this would be impossible, that the smart kids are
> unpopular because the other kids envy them for being smart, and nothing they could do
> could make them popular. I wish." (Why Nerds Are Unpopular)
>
> "One reason programmers dislike meetings so much is that they're on a different type of
> schedule from other people. Meetings cost them more." (Maker's Schedule — the essay's
> opening: the snap works as line two)
>
> "There are a lot of forces that will lead you astray when you're trying to figure out
> what to work on. Pretentiousness, fashion, fear, money, politics, other people's
> wishes, eminent frauds. But if you stick to what you find genuinely interesting,
> you'll be proof against all of them. If you're interested, you're not astray." (How to
> Do Great Work)
>
> "…partly because I was focused on my mother, whose cancer had returned. / She died on
> January 15, 2014. We knew this was coming, but it was still hard when it did." (What I
> Worked On — "/" marks the paragraph break; the landing is its own two-sentence
> paragraph)
>
> "And in my experience, the harder the subject, the more informally experts speak.
> Partly, I think, because they have less to prove, and partly because the harder the
> ideas you're talking about, the less you can afford to let language get in the way. /
> Informal language is the athletic clothing of ideas. / I'm not saying spoken language
> always works best." (Write Like You Talk — the display case: alone in its paragraph)
**Formula:** Highly codifiable, and the single most transferable PG move: after two or
more long sentences, one short flat sentence — no trailing qualifier, restating nothing,
flipping or compressing. The memoir shows it works for grief as well as jokes: the
heavier the content, the flatter the sentence. Frequency is taste; his is roughly one
snap per paragraph-cluster, one display case per couple of pages.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — most instructional drafts bury their landing
sentence mid-paragraph. Cheapest fix in this whole document.

## Pattern: The calibrated hedge
**Seen in:** at least 14 of 20 — and he theorizes it explicitly in How to Write Usefully.
**What it is:** Hedges are not politeness; they're an instrument panel. "I think," "I
suspect," "probably," "as far as I can tell" sit exactly where the claim outruns the
evidence — and nowhere else — so every unhedged sentence reads as certain. The hedge
often *licenses* audacity: the next clause escalates harder than an unhedged version
could. Sub-move: uncertainty as a beat — sections close on a flat "I don't know" that is
then treated as the interesting result.
**Verbatim:**
> "It may not be possible to prove that you have to work hard to do great things, but
> the empirical evidence is on the scale of the evidence for mortality." (How to Do
> Great Work)
>
> "If I remember correctly, the most popular kids don't persecute nerds; they don't need
> to stoop to such things." (Why Nerds Are Unpopular)
>
> "And it's true that 'I think x' is a weaker statement than simply 'x.' Which is
> exactly why you need 'I think.' You need it to express your degree of certainty."
> (How to Write Usefully)
>
> "Will it have any effect? I have no idea. But that uncertainty may be the most
> interesting point of all." (The Bus Ticket Theory of Genius)
**Formula:** Codifiable as an editing rule: for each hedge, ask what information it
carries — if it changes what the sentence claims, keep it and check it's pinned to the
weakest clause; if it's throat-clearing, cut it. Never hedge twice in one sentence.
Forecasts get soft hedges ("I wouldn't be surprised if"); present-tense claims get none.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — and it's already the house rule here (the
pg-compressor agent preserves hedges). The corpus adds the placement discipline: hedge
the joint, not the sentence.

## Pattern: Self-posed question, blunt answer
**Seen in:** at least 12 of 20; the structural spine of several.
**What it is:** He asks the reader's next question out loud, then answers it immediately
— often in a fragment that sounds like a ruling. The question does the transition work a
topic sentence would. Variants: the bare "Why?" as its own beat; the "Ok, so" checkpoint
that restates the settled point in spoken shorthand before asking the next question; the
twist answer that contains its own counterexample.
**Verbatim:**
> "Is our time any different? To anyone who has read any amount of history, the answer
> is almost certainly no." (What You Can't Say)
>
> "Ok, so life actually is short. Does it make any difference to know that? It has for
> me." (Life is Short)
>
> "What rational person would decide that the way to write great novels was to begin by
> spending several years creating an imaginary elvish language, like Tolkien, or
> visiting every household in southwestern Britain, like Trollope? No one, including
> Tolkien and Trollope." (The Bus Ticket Theory of Genius)
>
> "Why? Once again, anyone currently in school might think this a strange question to
> ask. How could things be any other way? But they could." (Why Nerds Are Unpopular)
**Formula:** Fully codifiable: at each section boundary, write the question the reader
would actually ask, then answer in under ten words before elaborating. One per section;
every paragraph turns it into a tic.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — lessons are question-shaped by nature, and
this makes the structure audible instead of implied.

## Pattern: One simile per abstraction
**Seen in:** at least 15 of 20.
**What it is:** Similes are rare, always physical, drawn from homely or mechanical
domains (clothes, sports, kitchens, engines), and strictly rationed: one image per
abstraction, then straight back to argument. The scarcity is the mechanic. Variant: the
worked analogy — instead of scattering five images, one analogy gets a full paragraph of
internal mechanics mapped one-to-one, and may return fifty lines later.
**Verbatim:**
> "We need a language that lets us scribble and smudge and smear, not a language where
> you have to sit with a teacup of types balanced on your knee and make polite
> conversation with a strict old aunt of a compiler." (Hackers and Painters)
>
> "They're like someone trying to play soccer while balancing a glass of water on his
> head." (Why Nerds Are Unpopular)
>
> "For someone on the maker's schedule, having a meeting is like throwing an exception.
> It doesn't merely cause you to switch from one task to another; it changes the mode in
> which you work." (Maker's Schedule)
>
> "A startup is like a mosquito. A bear can absorb a hit and a crab is armored against
> one, but a mosquito is designed for one thing: to score." (How to Make Wealth — worked
> analogy: the galley in the same essay runs for three paragraphs)
**Formula:** Codifiable as a budget: per abstract claim, at most one simile; concrete
vehicle from an unrelated everyday domain; stated once, not decorated. Picking a vehicle
that flatters or matches the reader's world (the exception metaphor, aimed at
programmers) is the taste part.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — ideal for the one concept per lesson that
must stick; the budget rule prevents the tutorial-cutesiness failure mode.

## Pattern: Coin it, then spend it
**Seen in:** at least 12 of 20.
**What it is:** An idea gets a concrete, faintly ridiculous name early ("the maker's
schedule," "the bus ticket theory," "the Morris technique," "the stranger"), then the
name is reused verbatim as load-bearing vocabulary — never paraphrased into synonyms.
By late in the essay the handle can be bent for a joke. Same move as Scott Alexander's
"coin it once, hammer it forever" — the strongest cross-writer convergence in this
library.
**Verbatim:**
> "There are two types of schedule, which I'll call the manager's schedule and the
> maker's schedule." (Maker's Schedule)
>
> "I encourage them to go preposterously, bus ticket collectorly, deep." (The Bus Ticket
> Theory of Genius — the handle inflected as an adverb, ~15 uses in)
>
> "New York tells you, above all: you should make more money." (Cities and Ambition —
> the city-message template, re-instantiated ~12 times as the essay's whole structure)
>
> "But the stranger is rational, so you always can, if you ask him what he needs. …
> Happy now?" (Putting Ideas into Words — the personified-reader variant: name the
> abstraction, then talk to it)
**Formula:** Fully codifiable: one coinage per piece, introduced with "which I'll call,"
reused word-for-word at every point of application, acknowledged or bent by the third
use. What resists codification is knowing which coinage can bear the weight.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — a course that coins its handles once and
spends them consistently reads as authored; synonym rotation is what makes tutorials
feel committee-written.

## Pattern: Definition by contrast
**Seen in:** at least 10 of 20.
**What it is:** A key term is defined by splitting it from its near-synonym or near-miss
reading — stated as a flat equation or negation, both halves parallel in syntax, no
elaboration. Often the essay's organizing device (section headings as equations).
**Verbatim:**
> "Wealth is not the same thing as money." (How to Make Wealth)
>
> "It's not mere skepticism, but an active delight in ideas that subvert the
> conventional wisdom, the more counterintuitive the better." (How to Think for
> Yourself)
>
> "It falls between what and how: architects decide what to do, and engineers figure out
> how to do it." (Hackers and Painters)
>
> "You don't want small in the sense of a village, but small in the sense of an all-star
> team." (How to Make Wealth)
**Formula:** Fully codifiable: "X is not Y" or "not X-in-sense-A but X-in-sense-B," both
halves syntactically parallel, delivered as one sentence. The definition is the
sentence; resist explaining it.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — teaching is mostly rescuing terms from their
near-misses ("an agent is not a chatbot"), and this is the tightest container for that.

## Pattern: Received wisdom, then the correction
**Seen in:** at least 11 of 20.
**What it is:** The common belief is voiced in its own most appealing words — sometimes
for a full sympathetic beat — then killed flat and replaced. Variants: proverb repair
(quote the saying, declare it "not quite right," issue the fix); propose-and-kill (raise
the plausible rival explanation, execute it with a two-word verdict and one concrete
counterexample); the hostile retranslation.
**Verbatim:**
> "So 'If at first you don't succeed, try, try again' isn't quite right. It should be:
> If at first you don't succeed, either try again, or backtrack and then try again."
> (How to Do Great Work)
>
> "Hire good people and give them room to do their jobs. Sounds great when it's
> described that way, doesn't it? Except in practice, judging from the report of founder
> after founder, what this often turns out to mean is: hire professional fakers and let
> them drive the company into the ground." (Founder Mode)
>
> "It might seem that the answer is simply that it's populated by adults, who are too
> mature to pick on one another. But I don't think this is true. Adults in prison
> certainly pick on one another." (Why Nerds Are Unpopular)
**Formula:** Codifiable: state the belief better than its believers would, pivot with
"Actually" / "But this isn't true" / "isn't quite right," and make the correction
shorter than the belief. The counterexample must be concrete — prison, Mongol nomads —
never "there are counterexamples."
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — every lesson fights a default habit, and
this is the honest way to fight it: steelman first, then flip.

## Pattern: Reader in the experiment
**Seen in:** at least 12 of 20.
**What it is:** "You" is not addressed, it's *deployed* — dropped inside a hypothetical
as the test subject, or given short imperative orders, sometimes with quoted inner
speech to perform. The argument becomes something that happens to the reader.
**Verbatim:**
> "Suppose in the future there is a movement to ban the color yellow. Proposals to paint
> anything yellow are denounced as 'yellowist', as is anyone suspected of liking the
> color." (What You Can't Say)
>
> "When you hear someone say something, stop and ask yourself 'Is that true?' Don't say
> it out loud." (How to Think for Yourself)
>
> "Tim Cook doesn't send you a hand-written note after you buy a laptop. He can't. But
> you can." (Do Things That Don't Scale)
>
> "You'd feel like an idiot using 'pen' instead of 'write' in a conversation with a
> friend." (Write Like You Talk)
**Formula:** Codifiable: convert "people tend to X" claims into "you" scenarios where
the reader does something concrete; make advice executable (quoted self-talk, a test
they can run today) rather than aspirational. The yellowist move — inventing an example
world and then reusing it as shared vocabulary — combines this with coin-it-then-spend-it.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes, maximally — a hands-on course is already in
second person; this upgrades "you can" narration into runnable experiments.

## Pattern: The counted claim
**Seen in:** at least 8 of 20.
**What it is:** Arithmetic where a lesser writer would use adjectives. Three shades: the
conversion (a vague quantity turned into a small integer you can picture), the
suspiciously exact figure (precision as deadpan wit and proof of memory), and the honest
back-of-envelope (run the math to an absurd figure, then hedge the number while standing
by the structure).
**Verbatim:**
> "You only get 52 weekends with your 2 year old. If Christmas-as-magic lasts from say
> ages 3 to 10, you only get to watch your child experience it 8 times." (Life is Short)
>
> "I'll often spend 2 weeks on an essay and reread drafts 50 times. If you did that in
> conversation it would seem evidence of some kind of mental disorder." (Putting Ideas
> into Words)
>
> "Combine all these multipliers, and I'm claiming you could be 36 times more productive
> than you're expected to be in a random corporate job. … I wouldn't try to defend the
> actual numbers. But I stand by the structure of the calculation." (How to Make Wealth)
>
> "I tried living in Florence when I was 25, thinking it would be an art center, but it
> turned out I was 450 years too late." (Cities and Ambition)
**Formula:** Highly codifiable: find the vague magnitude, compute the small number, hold
it against a physical object ("a handful of 8 peanuts"). For invented estimates, hedge
the figure, never the reasoning. Overuse kills the wit — a couple per piece.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — a course about AI is full of magnitudes
("200K tokens") begging for conversion into countable images.

## Pattern: "I'm not saying" pre-emption
**Seen in:** at least 9 of 20.
**What it is:** Right after a claim that could be read as more extreme than intended, an
explicit disclaimer names the overreading, then restates the narrower real claim — the
strong version survives because the strawman is dead. Variant: grant two or three
genuine exceptions in one breath, then close the door ("But for nearly everyone else…").
**Verbatim:**
> "I'm not saying that struggles are never about ideas, just that they will always be
> made to seem to be about ideas, whether they are or not." (What You Can't Say)
>
> "I'm not saying spoken language always works best. Poetry is as much music as text, so
> you can say things you wouldn't say in conversation. … But for nearly everyone else,
> spoken language is better." (Write Like You Talk)
>
> "Putting ideas into words is certainly no guarantee that they'll be right. Far from
> it. But though it's not a sufficient condition, it is a necessary one." (Putting Ideas
> into Words)
**Formula:** Fully codifiable: after the strong claim, one sentence naming what you're
not arguing, one sentence restating what you are. Keeps edge without armor-plating every
sentence with qualifiers.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — advice text constantly gets overread
("always use agents"); this is the surgical alternative to hedging everything.

## Pattern: Mirror aphorism
**Seen in:** at least 10 of 20; usually closing a section.
**What it is:** The quotable line is built from symmetry — two clauses with identical
syntax and swapped or crossed terms, so the sentence's shape enacts the reversal it
describes. He builds the argument first and mints the coin last.
**Verbatim:**
> "So hackers start original, and get good, and scientists start good, and get
> original." (Hackers and Painters)
>
> "Fashion is mistaken for good design; moral fashion is mistaken for good." (What You
> Can't Say)
>
> "The guys that guys envy, girls like." (Why Nerds Are Unpopular)
>
> "Curiosity is itself a kind of originality; it's roughly to questions what originality
> is to answers." (How to Do Great Work)
**Formula:** Codifiable as construction: write the claim plainly, then rebuild it as two
parallel clauses with the load-bearing terms swapped; hinge on a semicolon or comma;
place at the end of the section it summarizes. Whether the symmetry reveals or merely
decorates is the taste test — his always encode a real crossing.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Partly — one per lesson as the portable takeaway;
more and it sounds like a quote calendar.

## Pattern: Unflattering anecdote as proof
**Seen in:** at least 13 of 20.
**What it is:** The evidence for a general claim is a story where he looks obsessive,
mistaken, or ridiculous — mapped lunch tables, a failed city bet, an ill-timed Lisp
book. The embarrassment is what buys the credibility; the joke is never at the reader's
expense. The aphorism arrives *after* the story ("anecdote, then the rule"), welded on
with "Which means" or "But."
**Verbatim:**
> "So with my unerring nose for financial opportunity, I decided to write another book
> on Lisp." (What I Worked On)
>
> "At sixteen I was about as observant as a lump of rock." (The Age of the Essay)
>
> "I'm not claiming I write great software, but I know that when it comes to code I
> behave in a way that would make me eligible for prescription drugs if I approached
> everyday life the same way." (Hackers and Painters)
>
> "How you live affects how long you live. Most people could do better. Me among them."
> (Life is Short)
**Formula:** Codifiable: when a claim needs evidence, reach for the memory where you got
it wrong; tell it in two or three plain sentences with one concrete prop; generalize
only after. Same function as Scott's "turning the weapon on himself" and Moran's "turn
the gun around" — the third writer in the library to insure mockery with membership.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — "I make this exact mistake in my own
prompts" is worth a page of authority, provided the story is real.

## Pattern: Exposed-gears reasoning
**Seen in:** at least 7 of 20; dominant in the late short essays.
**What it is:** Deductions advance one clipped sentence at a time, each step opening
with a cheap connective — And yet, So, Which means, Ditto — so the logic is fully
visible and nothing hides in subordinate clauses. Variants: the staircase (restate the
claim two or three times, each tighter, end on the harshest version); the case ladder
(same claim instantiated across parallel cases, rungs shrinking, broken with a "But").
**Verbatim:**
> "If the person proposing the idea is reasonable, then they know how implausible it
> sounds. And yet they're proposing it anyway. That suggests they know something you
> don't." (Crazy New Ideas)
>
> "At best the plan is vague and incomplete. Very likely there's some undiscovered flaw
> that invalidates it completely. At best it's a plan for a plan." (Putting Ideas into
> Words)
>
> "The same is true for investors. … You see this pattern with startup founders too. …
> Ditto for essayists." (How to Think for Yourself)
**Formula:** Highly codifiable: one inference per sentence; sentence-initial connectives
allowed and encouraged; each rung shorter than the last. "Ditto" appearing signals the
pattern has earned compression.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — explanations of *why* a practice works
(process supervision, verification loops) want exactly this visible-chain form.

## Pattern: Thinking on stage
**Seen in:** at least 10 of 20.
**What it is:** The essay narrates its own derivation — "I finally realized today,"
"then it struck me," a mystery posed and solved in view — and sometimes narrates its own
rhetoric ("I need to burn my reputation with some group of people"). Conclusions arrive
as corrections of his earlier self, which licenses tentativeness and makes them feel
earned rather than defended.
**Verbatim:**
> "I finally realized today why politics and religion yield such uniquely useless
> discussions." (Keep Your Identity Small)
>
> "I knew it was a good time to have ideas. Now I'd go further: now I'd say it's hard to
> do a really good job on anything you don't think about in the shower." (The Top Idea
> in Your Mind)
>
> "The reason I've spent so long establishing this rather obvious point is that it leads
> to another that many people will find shocking." (Putting Ideas into Words)
>
> "At one point in this essay I found that after following a certain thread I ran out of
> ideas. I had to go back seven paragraphs and start over in another direction." (The
> Age of the Essay)
**Formula:** Partly codifiable: open on the moment of realization rather than the
settled thesis; mark upgrades of your own view explicitly ("I used to think… now I'd
say"); one meta-comment per piece announcing a risky move before making it. Resists
codification: it only reads as honest if the derivation actually happened that way.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — a course by someone visibly still figuring
things out is more trustworthy than one by an oracle, and it models the behavior the
course teaches.

## Pattern: Footnote as pressure valve
**Seen in:** at least 14 of 20 (the short pieces don't need it — "the pressure valve
isn't needed at this length").
**What it is:** Jokes, salt, pedantic corrections, and whole miniature essays are exiled
to footnotes so the body keeps its even tone. The notes aren't apparatus; several hold
the best lines in the essay, and one performs the diplomatic rephrasing of an insult the
body committed — the apology-as-footnote doubling the joke.
**Verbatim:**
> "You know what kind of business you should start if you like the outdoors? One to
> recover data from crashed hard disks." (How to Make Wealth, fn.)
>
> "When I ask myself what in my life is like high school, the answer is Twitter." (How
> to Think for Yourself, fn.)
>
> "The more diplomatic way of phrasing this statement would be to say that experienced
> C-level execs are often very skilled at managing up. And I don't think anyone with
> knowledge of this world would dispute that." (Founder Mode, fn. — softening "some of
> the most skillful liars in the world")
>
> "What you learn about programming in college is much like what you learn about books
> or clothes or dating: what bad taste you had in high school." (Hackers and Painters, fn.)
**Formula:** Fully codifiable: when a digression, joke, or caveat breaks the paragraph's
stride, exile it instead of cutting it. Requires a footnote-bearing medium; in one that
lacks them, asides and collapsible notes do the same job.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Partly — depends on the course's format supporting
footnotes or expandable asides; if it does, this solves the "funny but off-topic"
disposal problem cleanly.

## What resists extraction
Three things. First, the voice's evenness: any single pattern above is easy to fake; the
discipline of *nothing else* — no ornament between the moves, no adjective doing a
simile's job, no unearned emphasis — is the style, and it's a deletion habit, not an
insertion one. Second, the fact that the calibration is real: his hedges track his
actual uncertainty, built over years of being checked in public; borrowed hedges placed
for rhythm read instantly as mannerism. Third, the derivations are genuine — the essays
read as thinking because they are the thinking, drafts reread fifty times until the
reasoning and the prose are the same object. You can steal the moves; what made them
necessary was writing to find out, not to perform.
