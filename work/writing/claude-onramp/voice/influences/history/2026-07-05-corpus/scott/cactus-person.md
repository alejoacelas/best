# Universal Love, Said The Cactus Person (2015 · ~2800 words · fiction)
https://slatestarcodex.com/2015/04/21/universal-love-said-the-cactus-person/

## Candidate: the immovable refrain
Matches existing: new
What: One two-line exchange repeated verbatim (a dozen times) as the answer to every escalating argument; the thesis and the comedy are both in the non-response, and any break in the pattern (one speaker answering alone, the refrain interrupting mid-argument) reads as an event.
Quotes:
> “Universal love,” said the cactus person.
> “Transcendent joy,” said the big green bat.
Notes: Kin to coin it once, hammer it forever, but the unit is a whole dialogue beat and it never turns self-aware — the fixedness is the point. Near the end the cactus person's line lands alone after the shouting match, as a rebuke.

## Candidate: rhyme smuggled into prose
Matches existing: new
What: Scene-setting paragraphs are metrical, internally rhymed verse typeset as ordinary prose, so the dream-world hums under a straight paragraph surface.
Quotes:
> The sea was made of strontium; the beach was made of rye. Above my head, a watery sun shone in an oily sky.
> The sea turned hot and geysers shot up from the floor below. First one of wine, then one of brine, then one more yet of turpentine, and we three stared at the show.
> A meteorite of pure delight struck the sea without a sound.
Notes: Every interlude between dialogue rounds does this; the rhyme-words are pharmaceuticals (sertraline, quetiapine, clozapine), tying the vision to the narrator's medicine cabinet.

## Candidate: blog-voice in wonderland
Matches existing: new
What: The narrator argues inside the vision in the default essay idiom — citations, hedges, "like," analytic labels — and the register clash against the mystical setting is the running joke.
Quotes:
> You know, the usual Terence McKenna stuff.
> “I take it you’re not consequentialists,” I said.
> “Okay,” I said. “I’m going to hazard a guess as to what you’re talking about, and you tell me if I’m right. You’re saying that, like, all my Western logocentric stuff about factoring numbers in order to find out the objective truth about this realm is missing the point, and I should be trying to do some kind of spiritual thing involving radical acceptance and enlightenment and such. Is that kind of on the mark?”
Notes: The whole piece cites a real paper (Marko Rodriguez) mid-hallucination — the deadpan sourcing is part of the register clash.

## Candidate: coin it once, hammer it forever
Matches existing: coin it once, hammer it forever
What: The bat's sermon mints a capitalized coinage and repeats it verbatim about ten times, until the capitals themselves are the joke and the phrase works as a proper noun.
Quotes:
> A line of prophets who have come before you tell you that the secret to these forbidden mysteries is an ancient and terrible skill called GETTING OUT OF THE CAR, and you resolve to learn this skill.
> You try every button on the dashboard, but none of them is the button for GETTING OUT OF THE CAR.
> And he tells you that it’s not about pressing buttons on the dashboard and you just need to GET OUT OF THE CAR.
Notes: Same mechanics as the essays' coinages but compressed into one speech; the caps do the reuse-marking that "as I keep saying" would do in an essay.

## Candidate: parallel-frame pairs
Matches existing: parallel-frame pairs
What: The climactic duel is two identical sentence frames with swapped payloads, stripping words each round until only the frames are left shouting at each other.
Quotes:
> “I can’t get out of the car until you factor the number.”
> “I won’t factor the number until you get out of the car.”
> “FOR THE LOVE OF GOD JUST FACTOR THE FUCKING NUMBER!”
> “FOR THE LOVE OF GOD JUST GET OUT OF THE FUCKING CAR!”
Notes: The pair structure enacts the thesis (symmetrical incomprehension); compression across rounds is the escalation mechanism.

## Candidate: the stumbling correction
Matches existing: new
What: Speakers correct themselves mid-sentence and give up with a trailing "Whatever." — hesitation noise kept in the dialogue so the voice stays human inside the absurd.
Quotes:
> If you can factor that number, then it will convince people back in the real – back in my world that this place is for real and important.
> Likewise, if I understood the illusoriness…illusionality…whatever, of time, then I wouldn’t care that I only understood it for a single instant.
> You seem like a reasonable person. Bat. Freaky DMT entity. Whatever.
Notes: Four occurrences ("Your spine. Your wing. Whatever." is the fourth); the fragment-fragment-"Whatever." shape repeats exactly.

## Candidate: the flat landing
Matches existing: the flat landing
What: The apocalyptic vision cuts to one plain physical sentence, then bookkeeping details, deflating the sublime on contact.
Quotes:
> I vomited, hard, all over my bed.
> The clock on the wall read 11:55, meaning I’d been out about an hour and a half.
> “Yeah,” said the big green bat. “That’s what I got too.”
Notes: The last quote is the two-line twist ending — a flat landing doing punchline duty; single occurrence at that scale.

## Register note
In fiction the argument is fully dramatized: the essayist's steelman becomes a character, and the best argument in the piece (the bat's car sermon) is against the narrator. Prose rhythm does work the essays leave to logic — rhyme, refrain, and syntactic mirroring carry the meaning — and he permits sound-play the essays never would ("I saw the big green bat bat a green big eye," "a bat bigger and greener than which it was impossible to conceive").
