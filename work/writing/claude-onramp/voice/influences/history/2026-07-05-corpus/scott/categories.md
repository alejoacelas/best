# The Categories Were Made For Man, Not Man For The Categories (2014 · ~5,200 words · argument)

## Candidate: ventriloquism
Matches existing: ventriloquism
What: Opposing positions get full speeches in their own voice and idiom — King Solomon argues back across several paragraphs, a redneck gets phonetic dialect, the anti-trans position gets its whole worldview stated from inside.
Quotes:
> Solomon says he’s never heard of these gene things before, and that maybe genetics is involved in your weird foreign words “fish” and “mammal”, but dag are just finned creatures that swim in the sea, and behemah are just legged creatures that walk on the Earth.
> “Well,” Crazy Beard Guy tells you, “Ah reckon that might be what a fish is, but a feesh is some’in that swims in the orshun.”
> Our entire shtick is trying to believe what’s actually true, not on what we wish were true, or what our culture tells us is true, or what it’s popular to say is true.
Notes: The Solomon dialogue runs a dozen paragraphs — ventriloquism here scales up from speeches to whole scenes; "I take this argument seriously, because sticking to the truth really is important" always precedes the demolition.

## Candidate: the flat landing
Matches existing: the flat landing

Quotes:

## Candidate: parallel-frame pairs
Matches existing: parallel-frame pairs
What: Identical sentence frames with swapped nouns, so the analogy does the arguing — the reader completes the transfer from borders to categories before he states it.
Quotes:
> Statements like “the Zambezi River is full of angry hippos” are brute facts. Statements like “the Zambezi River is the territory of Namibia” are negotiable.
> In the same way, statements like “whales have little hairs” are brute facts. Statements like “whales are not a kind of fish” are negotiable.
> Imagine if we could ameliorate schizophrenia with one safe simple surgery, just snip snip you’re not schizophrenic anymore. Pretty sure that would win all of the Nobel prizes. Imagine that we could make a serious dent in bipolar disorder just by calling people different pronouns.
Notes: Also runs as escalating triples ("Imagine if… Imagine if… Imagine that…") where the third frame is the real, contested case.

## Candidate: the i-could ladder
Matches existing: new
What: An anaphoric run of arguments he declines to fully make — five consecutive paragraphs opening "I could…" — ending on a pivot to the one he actually wants, so the rejected arguments still land while looking waved-away.
Quotes:
> I could point out that trans-Napoleonism seem to be mysteriously less common than transgender.
> I could point out that if the extent of somebody’s trans-Napoleonness was wanting to wear a bicorne hat, and he was going to be suicidal his entire life if he couldn’t but pretty happy if I could, let him wear the damn hat.
> But I think what I actually want to say is that there was once a time somebody tried pretty much exactly this, silly hat and all.
Notes: Single sustained occurrence (five rungs) but clearly a template; transfers as a way to acknowledge a stack of counterarguments without owing each a section.

## Candidate: the hyphenated monster-word
Matches existing: new
What: Ad-hoc hyphenated compounds as comic category names — an entire failed definition or bureaucratic mood crammed into one ungainly noun.
Quotes:
> You try to explain that no, Solomon is wrong, dag are actually defined not by their swimming-in-sea-with-fins-ness, but by their genes.
> Then you divide the solar system into three types of objects: planets, planetoids, and dammit-our-categorization-scheme-wasn’t-as-good-as-we-thought.
> That’s very interesting, and I can see why we need a word to describe creatures closely related along the phylogenetic tree, but make up your own word, because behemah already means ‘four-legged-land-dweller’.
Notes: Three occurrences; the comedy is that an essay about how categories are man-made keeps manufacturing visibly hand-made ones. Transfer as a rare spice — one per piece.

## Candidate: coin it once, hammer it forever
Matches existing: coin it once, hammer it forever
What: A homely proper-noun coinage ("the Hair Dryer Incident") introduced with capital letters and full ceremony, then repeated verbatim as if it were an established scandal.
Quotes:
> And then I think of the Hair Dryer Incident.
> The Hair Dryer Incident was probably the biggest dispute I’ve seen in the mental hospital where I work. Most of the time all the psychiatrists get along and have pretty much the same opinion about important things, but people were at each other’s throats about the Hair Dryer Incident.
> And approximately half the psychiatrists at my hospital thought this was absolutely scandalous, and This Is Not How One Treats Obsessive Compulsive Disorder
Notes: Third quote is the sibling micro-move — Title Case to mimic the voice of officialdom for one clause.

## Register note
Argument-essay default, but the argument advances through staged fiction — the time-travel dialogues with Solomon and Crazy Beard Guy do the philosophical work that other essays do with citations. Then section IV breaks the game abruptly with a real patient ("trying to cut off various parts of his body"), and the playful register never fully returns.
