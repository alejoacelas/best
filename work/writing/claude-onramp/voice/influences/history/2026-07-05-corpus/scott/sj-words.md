# Social Justice And Words, Words, Words (2014 · ~3900 words · argument)
https://slatestarcodex.com/2014/07/07/social-justice-and-words-words-words/

## Candidate: the deliberately clunky handle
Matches existing: coin it once, hammer it forever
What: He names a source or concept with a deliberately awkward, over-literal handle, then repeats it verbatim — never shortened, never varied — until the repetition itself is the joke and the handle becomes the essay's anchor.
Quotes:
> I recently learned there is a term for the thing social justice does. But first, a png from racism school dot tumblr dot com.
> My suspicion about the gif from racism school dot tumblr dot com is that the statements on the top show the ways the majority of people will encounter “privilege” actually being used
> If racism school dot tumblr dot com and the rest of the social justice community are right, “racism” and “privilege” and all the others are innocent and totally non-insulting words
Notes: Five verbatim uses across the piece; "motte" and "bailey" and "a cute story about a dog and a lizard" get the same treatment. The refusal to abbreviate is the mechanic.

## Candidate: the flat landing
Matches existing: the flat landing
What: After a long evidence-heavy buildup, the conclusion is one plain short sentence set alone as its own paragraph — sometimes a single word.
Quotes:
> So.
> A sinking feeling tells me it probably isn’t that easy.
> This is the current state of my relationship with social justice.
Notes: "So." sits alone between the two "Everyone knows this" paragraphs and their collision; the last quote is the essay's final line, landing the whole water-supply parable in eleven words.

## Candidate: ventriloquism
Matches existing: ventriloquism
What: Opponents get full invented speeches in quotation marks, in their own idiom, often more fluent than a real opponent would manage — the rebuttal is staged as dialogue rather than described.
Quotes:
> then your interlocutor can just say “You’re deliberately only engaging with straw-man feminists who don’t represent the strongest part of the movement, you can’t hold me responsible for what they do”
> they say “But God just means there is order and beauty in the universe, surely you’re not objecting to that?”
> why not describe the state of affairs as it is: “The word racism can mean many things to many people, and I suppose a group of black people chasing a white kid down the street waving knives and yelling ‘KILL WHITEY’ qualifies by most people’s definition, but I prefer to idiosyncratically define it my own way, so just remember that when you’re reading stuff I write”?
Notes: Also used for speeches he wishes opponents would give (third quote) — ventriloquism runs both hostile and steelman directions.

## Candidate: parallel-frame pairs
Matches existing: parallel-frame pairs
What: Two consecutive paragraphs built on identical scaffolding — same opening formula, same evidence structure, same verbatim closer — so the contradiction between them needs no stating.
Quotes:
> It is no shame to be racist as long as you admit that you are racist and you try your best to resist your racism. Everyone knows this.
> He deserved it because he was racist. Everyone knows this.
> Statements like “God is just the order and love in the universe” and “No one perceives reality perfectly directly” and “Men should not interject into safe spaces for women” are the motte – extremely defensible, but useless.
Notes: The "Everyone is a little bit racist" / "Donald Sterling is racist" pair is the essay's engine; the motte/bailey paragraphs (third quote and its bailey twin) repeat the device with a three-item slot swap.

## Candidate: the machine-gun list with a self-aware item
Matches existing: the machine-gun list with a self-aware item
What: Evidence delivered as a strafing run of real titles or quotes, each a sentence fragment starting "Or", with one item comically longer than the rest and a trailing shrug.
Quotes:
> Maybe 18 Reasons Why The Concept Of Female Privilege Is Insane. Or An Open Letter To The Sexists Who Think Female Privilege Is A Thing. Or The Idea Of Female Privilege – It Isn’t Just Wrong, It’s Dangerous.
> ask anyone in social justice whether trans men (or trans women) have male privilege. You end up in places like STFU TRANSMISOGYNIST TRANS FOLKS or Cis Privilege Is Just A Tenet Of Male Privilege or On Trans People And The Male Privilege Accusation or the womyn-born-womyn movement or Against The Cisgender Privilege List or How Misogyny Hurts Trans Men: We Do Sometimes Have Male Privilege But There Are More Important Things To Talk About Here.
> Why Are Comments About White People Not Racist, Can Black People Be Racist Toward White People? (spoiler: no), Why You Can’t Be Racist To White People, et cetera et cetera.
Notes: Here the self-aware item is the absurdly long final title, not a confession — the length gradient does the same work. Note also "(spoiler: no)", a parenthetical-afterthought cameo inside a list item.

## Candidate: concede the diagnosis
Matches existing: new
What: A recurring one-line concession of how the author must sound ("paranoid"), placed at section hinges — he names the reader's likely dismissal of him, agrees with it, and uses the concession as the springboard into more evidence.
Quotes:
> Does that sound kind of paranoid? I freely admit I am paranoid in this area. But let me flesh it out with one more example.
> Sorry, there’s that paranoia again.
> I probably still sound paranoid. So let me point out something I think the standard theory fails to explain, but my theory explains pretty well.
Notes: Three beats, one per section — a refrain that structures the essay. Cousin of "turning the weapon on himself" but softer: he pre-empts the reader's verdict rather than his own accusation.

## Candidate: the deadpan parable
Matches existing: new
What: A miniature invented world ("Suppose the government...", the doctor counterfactual) narrated flatly with escalating concrete detail, then cashed out in a single application sentence — the analogy is never labored past that line.
Quotes:
> Suppose the government puts a certain drug in the water supply, saying it makes people kinder and more aware of other people’s problems and has no detrimental effects whatsoever.
> At some point you might start to wonder whether the government was being entirely honest with you.
> No doctor ever feels the need to declare that if we talk about antibacterial drugs we should call bacterial toxins “antihumanial drugs”.
Notes: Twice at full length (God-as-order believers in I, water supply in IV) plus the compressed doctor version; the parable always ends the section it appears in, feeding "the flat landing".

## Candidate: caps as hysteria
Matches existing: new
What: When rendering a voice as unhinged, he drops into unpunctuated all-caps mid-sentence — the typography does the mocking, no adverb or "he screamed" needed.
Quotes:
> the other doctors wouldn’t say YOU TAKE THAT BACK YOU PIECE OF GARBAGE ONLY HUMANS CAN HAVE DRUGS THIS IS A FALSE EQUIVALENCE BECAUSE BACTERIA HAVE INFECTED HUMANS FOR HUNDREDS OF YEARS BUT HUMANS CANNOT INFECT BACTERIA, they would just be mildly surprised at the nonstandard terminology and continue with their normal lives.
> he runs around shrieking like a banshee and occasionally yelling “AAAAAAH! MY FINGERS! MY PRECIOUS FINGERS!”
Notes: The unpunctuated run-on inside the caps is part of the effect. Contrast with "Answer to Job", where caps carry deadpan authority instead — same typography, opposite valence.

## Register note
This is his argument-essay default at full paranoid pitch: numbered sections that each re-derive the thesis from a new angle, and the comedy (caps, fake shrugs, "et cetera et cetera") is load-bearing evidence-delivery, not decoration. The recurring self-diagnosis refrain replaces the neutral transitions a standard essay would use.
