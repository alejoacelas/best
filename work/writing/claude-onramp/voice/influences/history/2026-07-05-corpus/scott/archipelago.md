# Archipelago and Atomic Communitarianism (2014 · ~6700 words · argument)
https://slatestarcodex.com/2014/06/07/archipelago-and-atomic-communitarianism/

## Candidate: ventriloquism
Matches existing: ventriloquism
What: Positions get quoted speeches in their own idiom — slogan-strings for liberalism, exclamation-mark panic for harm-arguments, sheepish surrender for the imagined oppressor. The speech does the argumentative work; he never paraphrases when he can impersonate.
Quotes:
> The one person says “Gay marriage will result in homosexuality becoming more accepted, leading to increased rates of STDs! That’s a harm! We must ban gay marriage!”
> “Your right to swing your fist ends where my nose begins” or “If you don’t like gay sex, don’t have any” or “If you don’t like this TV program, don’t watch it”
> “Yeah, guess we need someone to work at these factories now that all our workers have gone to the communally-owned factory down the road, we should probably at least let people unionize or something so they will tolerate us”
Notes: 6+ occurrences; the third quote shows the variant where a whole hypothetical concedes in character, run-on syntax mimicking reluctance.

## Candidate: coin it once, hammer it forever
Matches existing: coin it once, hammer it forever
What: A silly proper noun is minted with an explicit christening ("UniGov for short") and then used with a straight face in every subsequent sentence that needs it; the -topia suffix spawns a whole reusable family.
Quotes:
> He could just smite warmongers, but he thinks it’s more natural and organic to get all the communities into a united government (UniGov for short).
> Atheistopia is the enemy of Christiantopia, but only in the same way the Democratic Party is the enemy of the Republican Party
> imagine the lesbian separatist having to pay taxes to Christiantopia which oppressed her
Notes: UniGov appears ~10 times, Christiantopia 3, plus Lesbiantopia and Transtopia; the coinage lets later abstract points stay concrete for free.

## Candidate: capitals as air quotes
Matches existing: new
What: Mid-sentence phrases get Title Caps (or full caps) to mark them as somebody's cant rather than his own claim — mock institutions and mock values, no scare quotes needed.
Quotes:
> things that Weaken The Moral Fabric Of Society
> a bunch of communities built around the idea of Decent Small-Town God-Fearing People where everyone has white picket fences
> no one would have to deal with Those Durned Bureaucrats In Washington telling them what to do
Notes: Also the all-caps variant "to make EAT THIS DONUT NOW a less salient option"; 6+ occurrences. Transfers cheaply — one capitalized phrase per page, not more.

## Candidate: parallel-frame pairs
Matches existing: parallel-frame pairs
What: Consecutive paragraphs run on one identical syntactic frame with the ideology swapped in; the last item in the series deflates to something petty and personal.
Quotes:
> And so the equivalent of our paleoconservatives go out and found communities based on virtue, where all sexual deviancy is banned and only wholesome films can be shown and people who burn the flag are thrown out to be eaten by wolves.
> And the equivalent of our social justiciars go out and found communities where all movies have to have lots of strong minority characters in them, and all slurs are way beyond the pale, and nobody misgenders anybody.
> And some people who just really want to lose weight go out and found communities where you’re not allowed to place open boxes of donuts in the doctors’ lounge.
Notes: Same frame also does the meta-level lift: "I can be homosexual and you don’t have to" is rerun one level up as "I can live in the community I want and you can live in the community you want".

## Candidate: the callback example
Matches existing: new
What: A homely concrete example planted early (donuts in the doctors' lounge, battered women's shelters) is reprised near-verbatim sections later, so by the end one noun carries a whole prior argument.
Quotes:
> I cringed every time one my coworkers brought in a box of free donuts and placed wide-open in the doctors’ lounge; there was no way I wasn’t going to take one (or two, or three).
> And some people who just really want to lose weight go out and found communities where you’re not allowed to place open boxes of donuts in the doctors’ lounge.
> It seems like a great way to start searching for utopia, or at least getting some people away from their metaphorical abusive-husbands.
Notes: Two independent chains (donuts, abusive husbands) in one essay; the callback lands hardest when the surrounding prose has gone abstract.

## Candidate: cartoon enforcement in bureaucrat voice
Matches existing: new
What: Inside flat administrative prose about institutions and taxes, the enforcement clause is suddenly cartoon-violent — and the sentence never breaks stride to acknowledge it.
Quotes:
> people who burn the flag are thrown out to be eaten by wolves
> If anyone in Christiantopia tries to prevent her from reaching that embassy, or threatens her family if she leaves, or expresses the slightest amount of coercion to keep her around, UniGov burns their city and salts their field.
> transsexuals may not be able to go to a transsexual island somewhere and build Transtopia where anyone who misgenders anyone else gets thrown into a volcano
Notes: The comedy is entirely in the register clash; the surrounding sentences stay procedural ("The absolute minimum the united government can do is enforce freedom of movement").

## Candidate: steal the sneer
Matches existing: new
What: He adopts the opponents' dismissive epithet as if it were a technical term and repeats it while arguing for the people it insults — conceding the sneer to isolate the actual question.
Quotes:
> There were other arguments of about this caliber, but they all seemed to boil down to “conservative Christians are icky”.
> Yes, conservative Christians are icky. And they should be allowed to form completely voluntary communities of icky people that enforce icky cultural norms and an insular society promoting ickiness, just like everyone else.
> I think white separatists have exactly the right position about where the sort of white people who want to be white separatists should be relative to everyone else – separate.
Notes: "icky" runs four times in two sentences; the third quote is the cousin move where the group's own label is turned into the verdict.

## Candidate: the flat landing
Matches existing: the flat landing
What: After paragraphs of qualification, a clipped declarative — often its own paragraph — closes the door. Sometimes a two-beat concession ("And so it does. Fine.") instead of a rebuttal.
Quotes:
> Another person on the Facebook thread complained that this line of arguments leads to being okay with white separatists. And so it does. Fine.
> What if we held an exit rights party, and nobody came?
> If people want to go do their own thing in a way that harms no one else, you let them. That’s the Archipelagian way.
Notes: The middle quote is a one-line paragraph; the concession variant ("And so it does. Fine.") is the most transferable — accept the reductio, refuse the alarm.

## Register note
Argument-essay default, but Part III–IV is a conworlding fable: a wizard narrates policy positions as story events ("After a while the wizard decides to formalize and strengthen his system"), so institutional design gets stated in fairy-tale past tense and the whimsy carries the load that citations carry elsewhere. The essay openly re-enters argument register in Part V ("it doesn’t look like a practical solution for real problems").
