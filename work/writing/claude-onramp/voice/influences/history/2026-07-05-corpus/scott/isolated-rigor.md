# Beware Isolated Demands For Rigor (2014 · ~2600 words · argument)
https://slatestarcodex.com/2014/08/14/beware-isolated-demands-for-rigor/

## Candidate: coin it once, hammer it forever
Matches existing: coin it once, hammer it forever
What: He inherits the ox from the opening Perry quote, mutates it to "steal cows," and reuses the phrase verbatim as the verdict-word for every domain until it needs no gloss.
Quotes:
> Or you can use it to steal other people’s cows. This is what I accused Matt Yglesias of doing.
> Applied inconsistently , you’re just stealing cows again, coming up with a clever argument against the programs you don’t like while defending the ones you do.
> But when people never even begin to question the idea of different cultures but make exacting demands of anyone before they can talk about different races – even though the two ideas are statistically isomorphic – then I think they’re just out to steal cows.
Notes: Five-plus reuses; the coinage is borrowed and bent, not invented fresh — hammering someone else's image works the same.

## Candidate: the portable typology
Matches existing: new
What: A small typology (holy madman / worldly scholar / cow-stealer) is built once in the opening parable, then rerun as the verbatim skeleton of every subsequent domain — charity, taxes, statistics.
Quotes:
> Heraclitus could drown in his deeper understanding of personal identity and become a holy madman, eschewing material things and taking no care for the morrow because he does not believe there is any consistent self to experience it.
> Once you learn about utilitarianism and effective charity, you can become the holy madman, donating every cent you have beyond what is strictly necessary to survive and hold down a job to whatever the top rated charity is.
> Applied consistently, you become the holy madman demanding either total anarchy or some kind of weird system of tax earmarks which would actually be pretty fun to think about.
Notes: Parallel-frame pairs at section scale; by the third rerun the reader is running new cases through the template unprompted.

## Candidate: ventriloquism
Matches existing: ventriloquism
What: Opponents get full escalating speeches in their own idiom — the contrarian's rant arrives in first person with its own exclamation points, and the Science Czar's objections are each genuinely good, which is what makes the last one damning.
Quotes:
> “Well, maybe the medical community wasn’t investigating it the right way! Maybe the investigators were biased! Maybe they didn’t randomize right! Maybe they used a population unusually susceptible to cancer-getting! Ninety percent of medical studies are wrong! Those twenty experiments showing a lack of effect could be total bunk!”
> The Science Czar notes that their study was correlational only, and that correlational studies suck. We really can't show that minimum wages are any good without a randomized controlled trial.
Notes: The Science Czar sequence sustains it across a whole section — a ventriloquized character with a story arc, not just a speech.

## Candidate: credit the czar first
Matches existing: new
What: An on-the-record, unhedged concession to the adversary's best behavior placed immediately before the turn, so the knife stroke that follows reads as fair.
Quotes:
> I have a huge amount of sympathy with the Science Czar on this one, by the way.
> Everything the Science Czar has said so far makes perfect sense and he is to be commended for his rigor and commitment to the job.
> When Cosma Shalizi says he’s not sure about the factor analysis in IQ, I have no quarrel with him, because Cosma Shalizi’s response to everything in the world is to glare at it for not being sufficiently statistically rigorous.
Notes: Authorial voice, so distinct from ventriloquism; he also grants the consistent version dignity ("at least they would be fun to talk to at dinner parties").

## Candidate: the flat landing
Matches existing: the flat landing
What: After the ventriloquized rant, the rebuttal is two matched one-sentence paragraphs — the landing split in half, "Or maybe" doing all the work.
Quotes:
> Yes, maybe these things happened in each of the twenty studies that disagree with you.
> Or maybe they happened in the one contrarian study you are getting so excited about.
Notes: A paired variant of the pattern — the second short paragraph is the actual landing; the first is its setup.

## Candidate: the parenthetical afterthought
Matches existing: the parenthetical afterthought
What: Parens that escalate the bit or interrupt with mock-defensive asides — never caveats.
Quotes:
> The Science Czar (of course we have a science czar! We're not monsters!) notes that p < 0.05 is really a shoddy criterion that can prove anything and they should come back when they have p < 0.01.
> (actually, I can think of something even worse than that , which is a TV western based on this premise, where a roving band of pre-Socratic desperadoes terrorizes Texas. The climax is no doubt when the hero strides onto Main Street, revolver in hand, saying “There’s a new sheriff in town.” And Parmenides gruffly responds “No, I’m pretty sure that’s impossible.”)
Notes: The second parenthesis is a whole paragraph and secretly the essay's blueprint — see next candidate.

## Candidate: the gag becomes the finale
Matches existing: new
What: A joke planted as a throwaway parenthesis returns as the closing set piece — section V is the TV western the parenthesis pitched, played straight for two pages and re-arguing the essay in costume.
Quotes:
> The climax is no doubt when the hero strides onto Main Street, revolver in hand, saying “There’s a new sheriff in town.” And Parmenides gruffly responds “No, I’m pretty sure that’s impossible.”
> “No, I’m pretty sure that’s impossible,” says Parmenides. “There’s no such thing as change, only the appearance thereof.”
> “The only truly consistent people are the dead, Protagoras,” he said – and squeezed the trigger.
Notes: Single occurrence at this scale, but the plant-and-payoff mechanic is the transferable part; each philosopher's death restates one section of the argument.

## Candidate: the absurd concrete token
Matches existing: the absurd-scale restatement
What: Mock-precise numbers and one incongruous list item shrink an abstraction to laughable concrete size — the token of the existing pattern, without the full restatement frame.
Quotes:
> Apply it consistently, and conservatives shouldn’t have to pay for sex ed, liberals shouldn’t have to pay for wars, and libertarians shouldn’t have to pay for anything , except maybe a $9.99 tax bill yearly to support the police and a minimal court system.
> But now suppose that a team shows that, in a sample of six restaurants in Podunk Ohio, there was a nonsignificant trend towards the minimum wage making things a little worse.
> I see this a lot in medicine. Someone jumps on a new study showing the selenium or chromium or plutonium or whatever cures cancer.
Notes: "plutonium" is a broken third in an otherwise plausible list — same detonation logic as the price-tag $9.99; the first quote is also a parallel-frame triple whose third item breaks.

## Register note
Argument register that tips into fiction at both ends — a philosophical parable opens, a fully dramatized western closes — and in between the abstractions are always somebody (Heraclitus, the Science Czar, Yglesias), so the argument advances by casting and reusing characters rather than by definition.
