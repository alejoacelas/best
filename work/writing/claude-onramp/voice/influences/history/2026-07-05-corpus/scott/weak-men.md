# Weak Men Are Superweapons (2014 · ~2800 words · argument)
https://slatestarcodex.com/2014/05/12/weak-men-are-superweapons/

## Candidate: the bookend toy case
Matches existing: new
What: Opens with a minimal named-character micro-drama (Alice and Beth) posed as a puzzle, then closes by re-reading the same scene through the theory the essay built. The cast never grows or changes names.
Quotes:
> There was an argument on Tumblr which, like so many arguments on Tumblr, was terrible. I will rephrase it just a little to make a point.
> First, why did Beth take the bait?
> In the example we started with, Beth chose to stand up for the people who self-diagnosed autism without careful research.
Notes: The bookend gives the essay a plot; the opening puzzle ("why did each take the bait?") is answered only in the final section.

## Candidate: ventriloquism
Matches existing: ventriloquism
What: Hypothetical partisans get full speeches in their own idiom; in the late dialogue the ventriloquized religious people even speak the blog's own jargon back.
Quotes:
> “I am a proud atheist and I don’t like religion. Think of the terrible things done by religion, like the actions of the Westboro Baptist Church. They try to disturb the funerals of heroes because they think God hates everybody. But this is horrible. Religious people can’t justify why they do things like this. That’s why I’m proud to be an atheist.”
> And they say “Hey! I thought we told you to stop talking about them! You are unfairly discrediting us through the inoculation effect! That is epistemically unvirtuous!”
Notes: The second quote hands the essay's own terminology to a character mid-tantrum — the steelman speaks SSC, which is also the joke.

## Candidate: parallel-frame pairs
Matches existing: parallel-frame pairs
What: The same sentence frame is run with the tribes swapped, so the mechanism rather than a side reads as the culprit.
Quotes:
> Then suppose the atheist posts on Tumblr: “I hate religious people who are rabidly certain that the world was created in seven days or that all their enemies will burn in Hell, and try to justify it through ‘faith’.
> On the other side of the world, a religious person is writing “I hate atheists who think morality is relative, and that this gives them the right to murder however many people stand between them and a world where no one is allowed to believe in God”.
Notes: Runs at section scale too — the feminist NAMALT section is immediately mirrored by the men's-rights NAWALT section, introduced as "The gender-flipped version of feminism has the same thing."

## Candidate: the day-by-day ladder
Matches existing: new
What: A paragraph frame — plausible statement, reasons you can't object, near-identical refrain — iterated four times ("The next day…") until one summary sentence springs the trap.
Quotes:
> So again you just get uncomfortable.
> So again you stay uncomfortable.
> You have been boxed in by a serious of individually harmless but collectively dangerous statements.
Notes: "serious" [sic] in the original. The refrain's only variation is one verb (get → stay); the sameness is what makes the boxing-in palpable.

## Candidate: the parenthetical afterthought
Matches existing: the parenthetical afterthought
What: Parens that escalate or answer each other across paragraphs — a running bit conducted entirely in asides.
Quotes:
> From feminist website Bitchtopia (look, I don’t name these websites, I just link to them): Not All Men Are Like That :
> This is not just an idiosyncracy of Bitchtopia (look! I’m sorry! I swear I didn’t name that website!). There’s also an entire notallmenarelikethat dot tumblr dot com (of course there is) and it’s now a feminist meme abbreviated NAMALT.
> Then the next day you hear people complain about Israeli atrocities in Palestine (what, you thought this was past czarist Russia? This is future czarist Russia, after Putin finally gets the guts to crown himself).
Notes: The second parenthesis replies to the first — the aside has continuity; the third breaks a grim hypothetical for exactly one beat, then resumes.

## Candidate: the trap question
Matches existing: new
What: He states the opponent's comfortable inference as a question inviting agreement, ends the beat there, and lets the next example detonate it.
Quotes:
> Now there’s practically no implication that these people are typical. So that’s fine, right?
> Again, not a straw man. The Soviet Union contained several million of these people. But if you’re an atheist, would you just let this pass?
> What are the chances a black guy reads that and says “Well, good thing I’m not a thug who robs people, he’ll probably love me”?
Notes: Frequent — the whole of section II advances by these; the reader answers wrong on purpose and the essay corrects them.

## Candidate: extrapolate the jargon
Matches existing: new
What: He adopts a community's own acronym, then extends the coinage one absurd step past its home turf and treats the extension as established usage without re-explaining it.
Quotes:
> I don’t know where to find neo-Nazi blogs, but I’ll bet if there are some, they have places where they talk about how annoying it is when people try to distract from the real issues by using the old NAJALT.
> But I shouldn’t make fun of NAJALT.
Notes: Coin-it-hammer-it applied to a borrowed coinage; "the old NAJALT" — the "old" — sells the invented acronym as long-established.

## Candidate: the compound-epithet recap
Matches existing: new
What: Material built up over paragraphs is compressed into one improvised hyphenated modifier, the strained syntax showing the accumulation congealing.
Quotes:
> And everyone knows that Jews victimize Christians and are basically child-murdering Christ-killing economy-ruining atrocity-committing scum.
> I will be Worst Argument In The World -ed and swiftly dispatched.
Notes: Two occurrences; the first fuses the ladder's four separate slanders into a single word-mass — the superweapon assembled in one phrase.

## Register note
This is the argument-essay default the other pieces deviate from: numbered sections, one toy case opened and closed, and scrupulously symmetric examples across tribes (atheist/religious, feminist/MRA, plus his own liberal identification) so the mechanism, not any side, is the villain.
