# Who By Very Slow Decay (2013 · ~3950 words · personal)
https://slatestarcodex.com/2013/07/17/who-by-very-slow-decay/

## Candidate: the second-person prophecy
Matches existing: new
What: A long passage cast as future-tense second person — "you will" — so the reader becomes the case study; the clinical catalog reads as a prophecy addressed to them, not a description of patients.
Quotes:
> If you are like the patients I see dying, then here is how you will go.
> You will become bedridden, unable to walk or even to turn yourself over.
> And you will die, but not quickly.
Notes: Sustained across ~10 consecutive paragraphs of section I; transfers better as a 2–3 sentence burst than a full section.

## Candidate: triple with a broken third
Matches existing: new
What: Two parallel items establish a calm pattern; the third detonates it by dropping register or exploding in length.
Quotes:
> Let me puncture all your pleasant dreams. You’ll probably never become an astronaut. You’re not going to bang Emma Watson. And your last words will probably be something like “mmmrrrgggg graaaaaaaaaaaHAAACK!”
> Pseudomonas aeruginosa is supposed to smell fruity. Gardnerella is supposed to smell fishy. Clostridium is supposed to smell like the worst thing you can possibly imagine covered in feces and left to rot on a warm summer day.
Notes: The frame is kept word-for-word between items so the break lands entirely on the payload.

## Candidate: the machine-gun list with a self-aware item
Matches existing: the machine-gun list with a self-aware item
What: A concrete-token breakdown whose last item turns the camera on the author — here in a parenthesis, with percentages that knowingly sum past 100.
Quotes:
> The screams are coming about 33% from the confused demented old people I mentioned, 33% from people having minor procedures performed without anaesthetics for one or another good reason, and 33% from people who just have very painful diseases (plus 1% from me sitting in the break room looking up examples of hospital poetry for this post).
> There are wordless shrieks. There are some angry screams, like “$#%! YOU GET ME OUT OF HERE!”. There are a lot of people screaming “SOMEBODY HELP ME!” And there are some religious screams, like “OH GOD!” or “JESUS HELP ME!” or “CHRIST NO!”.
Notes: Second quote is the plain machine-gun list (no self-aware item) that immediately follows the first — he runs the taxonomy twice at two grain sizes.

## Candidate: ironic capitals
Matches existing: new
What: Institutional euphemism or cliché is set in capitals (or scare quotes) inside his own sentence, so the system's official voice indicts itself without commentary.
Quotes:
> If it’s especially bad they try to attack the staff, and although the extremely elderly are really bad at attacking people this is nevertheless Unacceptable Behavior and they have to be restrained ie tied down to the bed.
> but someone from 2000 miles away flies in at the last second and makes ostentatious demands that EVERYTHING POSSIBLE must be done for the patient.
> Every day, your doctors will meet with your family another time, and eventually, as your condition worsens and your family has more time to be hit on the head with a big club marked ‘REALITY’, they will start to relent.
Notes: Three distinct devices (Title Case, ALL CAPS, quoted label) doing one job; the flat "ie tied down to the bed" gloss is part of the move.

## Candidate: ventriloquism
Matches existing: ventriloquism
What: Side characters get whole speeches in their own idiom — quoted cafeteria one-upmanship, and indirect speech that absorbs the family's diction wholesale.
Quotes:
> “I have a blind 90 year old patient with stage 4 lung cancer with brain mets and no kidney function, and the family is demanding I enroll her in a clinical trial from Sri Lanka.” “Oh, that’s nothing. I have a patient who can’t walk or speak who’s breathing from a ventilator and has anoxic brain injury, and the family is insisting I try to get him a liver transplant.”
> Your family will start yelling at the doctors, asking how the hell these quacks were ever allowed to practice when for God’s sake they’re trying to kill off Grandma just so they can avoid doing a tiny bit of work.
Notes: In the sincere register the ventriloquism is sympathetic on all sides — the family's absurd demand is voiced from inside their fear.

## Candidate: the flat landing
Matches existing: the flat landing

Quotes:

## Candidate: the parenthetical afterthought
Matches existing: the parenthetical afterthought
What: Parens that escalate the joke or drop into backstage candor — never a caveat.
Quotes:
> Doctors and nurses eat this kind of thing up and put it on shiny plaques that go on the walls of the hospital. (I suggest a wall near the gastroenterology unit, to expedite care for people who start vomiting.)
> (this originally read “morphine”, but just today the palliative care doctor at my hospital gave an impassioned lecture about how people need to stop auto-associating morphine with euthanasia, because it makes it really hard for him to offer morphine painkillers to patients who need them without them freaking out. So potassium chloride it is.)
Notes: The second is a whole process-note paragraph in parens — the revision history left visible, ending on a flat "So potassium chloride it is."

## Register note
When he's sincere the jokes migrate to the edges (parentheses, ironic capitals) and the center moves to plain declaratives and repetition: anaphora ("We have enough life. We have life up the wazoo. We have more life than we know what to do with.") and parallel frames ("the same way that Newcastle needs to cultivate a culture of coal, the same way a man who is burning to death needs to cultivate a culture of fire") carry the anger. The irony always targets institutions, never the dying.
