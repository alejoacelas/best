# The Toxoplasma of Rage (2014 · ~6,100 words · argument)
https://slatestarcodex.com/2014/12/17/the-toxoplasma-of-rage/

## Candidate: parallel-frame pairs
Matches existing: parallel-frame pairs
What: The essay's central tradeoff gets restated once per section in near-identical two-clause syntax — responsible-but-ignored vs. outrageous-but-viral — with only the actors swapped (Vegan Outreach/PETA, feminists, police-brutality campaigners).
Quotes:
> Vegan Outreach can get everyone to agree in principle that factory-farming is bad, but no one will pay any attention to it.
> First, they can respond to rape in a restrained and responsible way, in which case everyone will be against it and nobody will talk about it.
> If campaigners against police brutality and racism were extremely responsible, and stuck to perfectly settled cases like Eric Garner, everybody would agree with them but nobody would talk about it.
Notes: Used as a refrain across sections, not just within a paragraph — the repetition itself argues the mechanism is general.

## Candidate: coin it once, hammer it forever
Matches existing: coin it once, hammer it forever
What: A vivid coinage introduced mid-argument, then reused verbatim as an established technical term; a second image ("shoot themselves in the foot") is coined in section I and echoed in later sections in ever-shorter forms.
Quotes:
> But aside from that, there’s the PETA Principle: the more controversial something is, the more it gets talked about.
> I propose that the Michael Brown case went viral – rather than the Eric Garner case or any of the hundreds of others – because of the PETA Principle.
> Like PETA, their incentive gradient dooms them to shoot themselves in the foot again and again.
Notes: By the third use the foot-shooting image is compressed to a two-word paragraph-ender ("More foot-shooting.") — the callback shrinks each time.

## Candidate: the flat landing
Matches existing: the flat landing
What: After a long causal buildup, a plain short sentence — often a fragment — stands as its own paragraph and closes the beat.
Quotes:
> But at least they’re paying attention!
> Once again, the tradeoff.
> But let’s talk about toxoplasma.
Notes: Also used as pivots ("And let's talk about Tumblr."), not just landings — the fragment paragraph both ends one movement and cranks the next.

## Candidate: turning the weapon on himself
Matches existing: turning the weapon on himself
What: A full section (IV) applies the essay's accusation to the author's own blog, with his own traffic data as the evidence, and no softening.
Quotes:
> The less useful, and more controversial, a post here is, the more likely it is to get me lots of page views.
> And that “regret” all the way on the right is my “things i will regret writing” tag, for posts that I know are going to start huge fights and probably get me in lots of trouble.

Notes: The self-indictment is quantified (2,000 vs. 16,000 hits), which makes it harsher than a gesture of humility.

## Candidate: the parenthetical afterthought
Matches existing: the parenthetical afterthought
What: Parentheses that escalate or address the reader directly rather than caveat — a deadpan aside, a preemptive apology to specific readers, a dare.
Quotes:
> (you thought I was joking about the hate for kitten picture bloggers. I never joke.)
> (no, well-intentioned and intelligent critics, I am not talking about you)
> (You think I’m exaggerating? Listen: “YOU KNOW WHAT NOBODY HATES EACH OTHER ABOUT YET? VIDEO GAMES.”)
Notes: The last one is the essay's final line — a parenthesis as the closing punch.

## Candidate: the demon gets a speaking part
Matches existing: new
What: Abstract forces (memes, Moloch) are given literal quoted dialogue, in all caps, stating their agenda with cartoon bluntness — personification pushed all the way to a script.
Quotes:
> It’s the first time outside of a chain letter that I have seen our memetic overlords throw off all pretense and just go around shouting “SPREAD ME OR YOU ARE GARBAGE AND EVERYONE WILL HATE YOU.”
> Finally he decides “YOU KNOW WHAT NOBODY HATES EACH OTHER ABOUT YET? BIRD-WATCHING. LET ME FIND SOME STORY THAT WILL MAKE PEOPLE HATE EACH OTHER OVER BIRD-WATCHING”.
Notes: Cousin of ventriloquism, but the voiced party is a caricatured abstraction, not a steelmanned opponent — the caps mark it as monster-speech, not argument.

## Candidate: anaphora that breaks on the last beat
Matches existing: new
What: A sentence-opener repeated three-plus times in consecutive sentences or paragraphs, with the final iteration varying the frame to land the point — the break is the payoff.
Quotes:
> It’s not a coincidence that the worst possible flagship case for fighting police brutality and racism is the flagship case that we in fact got.
> It’s not coincidence, it’s not even happenstance, it’s enemy action.
> Lost is the ability to agree on simple things like fighting factory farming or rape. Lost is the ability to even talk about the things we all want.
Notes: The "enemy action" break also pays off a saying planted four sections earlier ("once is happenstance, twice is coincidence, three times is enemy action") — anaphora plus long-range callback in one move.

## Candidate: the incredulous cross-examination
Matches existing: new
What: A run of second-person rhetorical questions fired at an implied opponent, each more specific and damning than the last; sometimes closed with a curt self-answer.
Quotes:
> You’re saying none of these shootings, hundreds each year, made as good a flagship case as Michael Brown? In all this gigantic pile of bodies, you couldn’t find one of them who hadn’t just robbed a convenience store? Not a single one who didn’t have ten eyewitnesses and the forensic evidence all saying he started it?
> Could even a deliberate PR campaign by the nation’s police forces have done better? I doubt it.
Notes: The escalation is in concreteness ("hundreds each year" → "convenience store" → "ten eyewitnesses"), not volume.

## Register note
This is Scott's argument-essay default at full scale: numbered roman sections, each a fresh case study of one mechanism, refrain restated per section. What's distinctive is the register climb — journalistic case-recap in I–III, self-audit in IV, then mythic personification (toxoplasma, Moloch, "powers and principalities") in V–VI, with the comedy turning darker as the abstraction level rises.
