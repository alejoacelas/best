# Arguments From My Opponent Believes Something (2013 · ~750 words · comedy)
https://slatestarcodex.com/2013/06/13/arguments-from-my-opponent-believes-something/

## Candidate: the kinda-like chain
Matches existing: new
What: Each fallacy is named by transcribing its inferential slide step by step in Title Case, the colloquial "Which Is Kinda Like" marking every illegitimate hop; an identical stem repeats ten times so all the variation lands in the tail.
Quotes:
> 3. Argument From My Opponent Believes Something, Which Is Kinda Like Believing It Really Strongly, Which Is Kinda Like Being A Fanatic
> 8. Argument From My Opponent Believes Something, Which Might Suggest A Course Of Action, But A Suggestion Is Kinda Like An Obligation, And She Has No Right To Order Me Around
> 9. Argument From My Opponent Believes Something, Which Might Suggest A Course Of Action, Which Could In Theory Be Implemented Through Violence, And Violence Is Wrong
Notes: Kin to "coin it once, hammer it forever," but the repetition is the joke's engine rather than a callback — and the chains lengthen as the list goes on, an escalation you can feel in the item widths.

## Candidate: ventriloquism
Matches existing: ventriloquism
What: Every list item is illustrated by an invented specimen quote in the target's own idiom — parody rather than steelman, but the same mechanic of letting a position speak in a full sentence of its native register.
Quotes:
> “Statists believe government can solve all our problems . They need to understand the world doesn’t work that way.”
> “People say smoking is dangerous and unhealthy, but this just serves to stigmatize smokers and make them feel unwelcome in society.”
> “The high priests of the economic orthodoxy take it on faith that anyone who doubts the market is a heretic who must be punished.”
Notes: Ten specimens in ten items; the piece contains almost no authorial prose outside them.

## Candidate: alternating-target examples
Matches existing: new
What: The specimen quotes alternate between left-coded and right-coded targets (market skeptics, statists, environmentalists, feminism, euthanasia, contraception, immigration restriction, public health), so the rhetorical pattern — not a tribe — reads as the villain.
Quotes:
> “Environmentalist extremists are fanatically obsessed over saving the planet, refusing to even consider any contradictory ideas.”
> “Some people want to liberalize immigration laws, but our country is under no obligation to let in any foreigner who asks.”
Notes: Example-selection mechanics rather than sentence mechanics, but load-bearing: one-sided specimens would collapse the piece into the fallacy it mocks.

## Candidate: the mundane-topic stress test
Matches existing: new
What: After cataloguing a rhetorical disease, close with one specimen paragraph exhibiting every symptom at once on the most trivial subject available — the gap between register and topic does all the arguing.
Quotes:
> For best effect, combine all ten as densely as possible:
> It is an unchallengeable orthodoxy that you should wear a coat if it is cold out.
> Or can we accept the unjacketed human body to be potentially just as beautiful as someone bundled beneath ten layers of coats?
Notes: Single occurrence, but it is the piece's climax and its proof — the fallacies demonstrably work on winter coats, so they prove nothing anywhere.

## Candidate: name the phrase doing the work
Matches existing: new
What: The appended clarification defuses the strongest objection by conceding it, then isolates the exact phrase that carries the argumentative load — a quoted-fragment diagnosis instead of a rebuttal.
Quotes:
> I’m not claiming these aren’t real problems, I’m claiming they’re things that they are fully general arguments – you can accuse anyone of them and no one can ever prove you’re wrong.
> Therefore, we should be extremely skeptical of arguments where “X is a religion” is doing the work.
Notes: Single occurrence here (an EDIT, blog convention), but "where [quoted phrase] is doing the work" is a transferable diagnostic sentence-shape.

## Register note
Pure comedy in listicle form: no sections, no argument prose, no first-person until the appended EDIT — the structure (repeated stem, lengthening chains, composite parody as finale) carries everything Scott's argument essays do with transitions and analysis. The one paragraph of straight argumentation is quarantined at the end as an edit.
