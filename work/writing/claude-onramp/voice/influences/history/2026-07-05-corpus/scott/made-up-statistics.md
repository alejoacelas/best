# If It's Worth Doing, It's Worth Doing With Made-Up Statistics (2013 · ~1,450 words · argument)
https://slatestarcodex.com/2013/05/02/if-its-worth-doing-its-worth-doing-with-made-up-statistics/

## Candidate: ventriloquism
Matches existing: ventriloquism
What: The objection the essay answers is staged as a full script-format dialogue, and the skeptic's position is later voiced again in first person inside a sentence — the opposing view always speaks in its own idiom before being answered.
Quotes:
> Student: I don’t know the future. Why do you want to me to pretend I do?
> Student: Exactly which sandwich are we talking about here?
> They’re not arguing that utilitarianism fails to outperform zero information, they’re arguing that it fails to outperform our natural intuitive ways of looking at things, the one where you just think “North Korea? Sounds awful. The people there deserve our sympathy.”
Notes: The student gets the best line in the piece ("Exactly which sandwich") — the ventriloquized voice is allowed to be funnier than the narrator.

## Candidate: the parenthetical afterthought
Matches existing: the parenthetical afterthought
What: Parentheses that drop register mid-argument — a caps-lock confession, a stage direction editorializing on a person, a deadpan gloss.
Quotes:
> But suppose some doctor’s internet is down (you have NO IDEA how much doctors secretly rely on the Internet) and she can’t remember the prevalence of breast cancer.
> Julia: (who is heroically nice and patient) Okay, let’s back up. Suppose you buy a sandwich. Is the sandwich probably poisoned, or probably not poisoned?
> And it informs a lot of the objections to utilitarianism – the problem of quantifying exactly how bad North Korea shares some of the pitfalls of quantifying exactly how likely your computer is to break (for example, “we are kind of making this number up” is a pitfall).
Notes: Also non-paren cousins in the same register: "some, er, rationality virgins".

## Candidate: parallel-frame pairs
Matches existing: parallel-frame pairs
What: Corrections and comparisons built as two sentences (or clauses) with identical scaffolding and swapped content — "not arguing X, arguing Y", "It's hard to A. It's hard to B."
Quotes:
> It’s not that I think it will work. It’s that I think it will fail to work in a different way than our naive opinions fail to work, and we might learn something from it.
> It’s hard to make a mistake in calculating the utility of living in North Korea that’s off by a factor of a hundred. It’s hard to come up with values that make a war suddenly become okay/abominable when the President changes parties.
Notes: The repeated frame does the arguing — the reader compares the swapped nouns and draws the conclusion himself.

## Candidate: the even-if ladder
Matches existing: new
What: Concessions stacked in ascending size — each "Even if" or "But it's probably not" grants a bigger hit to his own claim, then shows the claim still standing; the numbers bound the estimate from absurd extremes.
Quotes:
> Sure, the actual badness of North Korea may not be exactly 37%. But it’s probably not twice as good as living in the First World. Or even 90% as good. But it’s probably not two hundred times worse than death either.
> Even if we are wrong by a very large amount (let’s say we’re off by a factor of four and the real number is 20%), if the insight we encoded into the number is sane we’re still doing better than giving no information at all
> Even if your data is completely made up, the way the 5% chance of breaking your computer was made up, the fact that you can apply normal non-made-up arithmetic to these made-up numbers will mean that you will very often still be less wrong than if you had used your considered and thoughtful and phronetic opinion.
Notes: Three occurrences in 1,450 words — the essay's core move. Transfers well: pre-concede more than the critic would ask for.

## Candidate: the remember-that-experiment run
Matches existing: new
What: Evidence delivered as a chain of "Remember...?" / "And the one where...?" questions that presume the reader already shares the references — a machine-gun list of concrete studies in interrogative form.
Quotes:
> Remember the Bayes mammogram problem? The correct answer is 7.8%; most doctors (and others) intuitively feel like the answer should be about 80%.
> Remember that scope insensitivity experiment where people were willing to spend about the same amount of money to save 2,000 birds as 200,000 birds? And the experiment where people are willing to work harder to save one impoverished child than fifty impoverished children? And the one where judges give criminals several times more severe punishments on average just before they eat lunch than just after they eat lunch?
Notes: Cousin of the machine-gun list, but the question form flatters the reader as an insider and skips the citation apparatus entirely.

## Candidate: the canonized aphorism
Matches existing: new
What: A borrowed one-liner is elevated with mock-scriptural or attributed framing and made to carry a whole argumentative beat — including the essay's final word.
Quotes:
> Thus is it written: “It’s easy to lie with statistics, but it’s easier to lie without them.”
> I also really like ciphergoth’s quote: “Sometimes pulling numbers out of your arse and using them to make a decision is better than pulling a decision out of your arse.”
Notes: The second closes the essay — he lets someone else's cruder phrasing land the thesis rather than restating it himself.

## Candidate: the stutter-step escalation
Matches existing: new
What: A word repeated across sentence fragments, each repetition escalating until the last swaps the intensifier for a hard number.
Quotes:
> Some things work okay on System 1 reasoning. Other things work badly. Really really badly. Factor of a hundred badly, if you count the bird experiment.
Notes: Single occurrence, but the vague-to-number payoff is the whole essay in miniature and reads like a deliberate move.

## Register note
Argument register in miniature: no numbered sections, and the opening objection arrives as a comic playlet instead of prose exposition. Compared to the long-essay default it is chattier and more second-person ("Remember...?", "you have NO IDEA"), and it ends on a borrowed punchline rather than a built crescendo.
