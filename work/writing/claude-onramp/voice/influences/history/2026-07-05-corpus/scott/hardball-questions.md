# Hardball Questions For The Next Debate (2015 · ~2,700 words · comedy)
https://slatestarcodex.com/2015/11/16/hardball-questions-for-the-next-debate/

## Candidate: open on the true compliment
Matches existing: new
What: Every fake question begins with accurate, even generous biography — real achievements, correctly described — so the reader can't tell where fact ends and the trap begins.
Quotes:
> One of your most important achievements as a neurosurgeon was inventing the functional hemispherectomy
> You were on your college debate team, and you were good at it. Really good. You won the national championships and you were pretty widely believed to be the best debater in the country
> Certainly their five-hundred-plus year reign was marked by impressive military, political, and artistic achievements.
Notes: Five for five across the piece; the truer the opening, the longer the deadpan can hold.

## Candidate: playing the premise straight
Matches existing: new (same name as in niceness.md — one move across both pieces)
What: The absurd premise gets full scholarly apparatus — citations, hedged statistics, historiography, concessions to the other side — and the register never winks; the hedges themselves become the jokes.
Quotes:
> My calculations suggest about 90%, which is very high compared to what we know about similar traits – but actually not entirely outside the realm of plausibility.
> Far be it from me to impugn his business ethics, but I don’t remember Nazi refugees in Argentina becoming an unstoppable force aided by a weapon of legendary mystical power.
> even the Huffington Post, not normally a source for magic-sword-related journalism, wrote about it
Notes: The core engine of the piece. Real trivia is load-bearing — the Chiang Kai-shek history and GHWB's tennis-court "unleash Chiang" habit are true, which is what lets the Holy Lance conspiracy feel researched rather than asserted.

## Candidate: the refrain slot
Matches existing: new
What: Every section funnels into the identical verbatim frame "So my question for you is" — the fixed formula teaches the reader the shape, so the joke becomes whatever fills the slot; the final section breaks the frame's composure entirely.
Quotes:
> So my question for you is – what do you think happens to that person who is in an empty hemisphere, locked out of all sensory input and motor control?
> So my question for you is: do you think Barbara Bush is an unrecognized political super-genius, or are there probably hundreds of thousands of Americans who would make a better president than you would?
> My question for you is: WHY DIDN’T YOU CALL IT THE TRUMP CARD?!?!!!!111111111asdfdf
Notes: Kin to "coin it once, hammer it forever" but structural — a reused frame rather than a coined image. The last iteration is the only place in the piece the deadpan mask drops, and it's positioned as the final line.

## Candidate: the bait-and-switch question
Matches existing: new
What: The most earnest setup — a real policy idea he half-believes, argued at length — resolves into a question that ignores every word of it.
Quotes:
> As an admirer of the Ottoman Empire, you’d be in a uniquely good position to import some of the advantages of the millet system into the modern Western world.
> So my question for you is – weren’t you a pretty crappy CEO?
Notes: Single occurrence, but the piece's most structural joke: the setup's length is the fuse, and it only detonates because the other four questions trained the reader to expect the question to follow from the setup.

## Candidate: the escalating question run
Matches existing: new
What: A volley of rhetorical questions, each raising the stakes of the last, ending on the most extreme; the escalation does the work a punchline would.
Quotes:
> Do you think they’re conscious? Do you think they’re wondering what happened? Do you think they’re happy that the other half of them is living a happy normal life? Do they sit rapt in unconditioned contemplation of their own consciousness like an Aristotelian god? Or do they go mad with boredom, constantly desiring their own death but unable to effect it?
> That the best debater in the country would also be the best choice for President? Or that he would be really really really good at making us think that he would be?
Notes: The Carson run climbs mundane → cosmic in five questions; the section then just ends, no answer offered.

Matches existing: the machine-gun list with a self-aware item
What: A drumbeat inventory of concrete proper nouns; here there's no confessional item — the accumulation of near-identical names is itself the gag.
Quotes:

## Candidate: the cross-question callback
Matches existing: new
What: A joke established in one section returns in a later one cited as settled fact, with a straight face and the original's exact vocabulary.
Quotes:
> I need not add that according to the the laws of genetics, the chance of this happening by coincidence is hundreds-of-thousands to one even assuming implausibly high heritability of the fitness-to-be-president trait.
Notes: Single occurrence — the Rubio section invoking the Bush section's regression math as established science ("the the" is in the original). Turns a list of standalone bits into one build.

## Register note
Pure comedy, but built from the argument-essay's own machinery: citations, hedges, regression equations, and steelman concessions are all retained intact and aimed at absurd premises. Almost no sentence-level jokes — the humor lives entirely in the never-breaking moderator deadpan, which cracks exactly once, on the last line.
