# Burdens (2014 · ~1,500 words · personal)
https://slatestarcodex.com/2014/08/16/burdens/

## Candidate: the flat landing
Matches existing: the flat landing
What: A buildup paragraph ends on a colon or a held breath, and the payoff is one plain short sentence isolated as its own paragraph — used for both the essay's opening reveal and its thesis.
Quotes:
> “I feel like I’m a burden”.
> And that is: humans don’t owe society anything. We were here first.
> Now he attempts suicide, says he feels like a burden on everyone around him. Well, what am I going to say?
Notes: The thesis sentence gets its own one-line paragraph after a full paragraph of wind-up ("There is something else I've never said…"); the case vignette lands on a helpless rhetorical question instead.

## Candidate: turning the weapon on himself
Matches existing: turning the weapon on himself
What: The essay's category — being a burden — gets applied to the author, unsoftened; and his own therapeutic technique is prosecuted in his own sarcastic voice before anyone else can.
Quotes:
> “Sure, you’re a burden if you live, but if you kill yourself, that would make you an even bigger burden!” A++ best psychiatrist.
> By the time I am a burden – it’s possible that I am already, just because I can convince the system to give me money doesn’t mean the system is right to do so, but I expect I certainly will be one before I die –
Notes: "A++ best psychiatrist" is the register-drop version; the second quote is the earnest version — same move at two temperatures in one short essay.

## Candidate: the parenthetical afterthought
Matches existing: the parenthetical afterthought
What: Whole-paragraph parentheticals that swat an anticipated objector mid-argument — the paren marks it as an aside to a different reader, letting the main line continue unbroken.
Quotes:
> (if you’re one of those people who sees red every time someone mentions evolution or cavemen, imagine him as a dockworker a hundred years ago, or a peasant farmer a thousand)
> (people talk as if the only possible use of information about the determinants of intelligence is to tell low-IQ people they are bad. Maybe they’ve never felt the desperate need to reassure someone “No, it is not your fault that everything is going wrong for you, everything was rigged against you from the beginning.”)
Notes: Both instances are paragraph-length and objection-shaped — a defensive variant of the pattern rather than the comic one.

## Candidate: the case-file clip
Matches existing: new
What: Patient vignettes drop into telegraphic fragments and comma-spliced clauses — subjectless sentences, chart-note rhythm — which reads as clinical restraint and makes the eventual full sentences hit harder.
Quotes:
> Another patient. 25 year old kid. Had some brain damage a few years ago, now has cognitive problems and poor emotional control. Can’t do a job. Got denied for disability a few times, in accordance with the ancient bureaucratic tradition.
> I had one patient who worked for GM, very smart guy, invented a lot of safety features for cars.
Notes: Fully realized once, echoed in the looser comma-splice of the GM vignette — mark as near-single occurrence, but distinctive enough to steal.

## Candidate: ventriloquism
Matches existing: ventriloquism
What: Speech that was never actually spoken gets quotation marks — the ugly subtext of his own therapy, the reassurance he wishes he could give — so the unsayable is voiced rather than described.
Quotes:
> “Sure, you’re a burden if you live, but if you kill yourself, that would make you an even bigger burden!”
> Maybe they’ve never felt the desperate need to reassure someone “No, it is not your fault that everything is going wrong for you, everything was rigged against you from the beginning.”
Notes: Inflected here: the voiced speaker is himself (subtext, hypothetical), not a steelmanned opponent — the quoting mechanics are the same.

## Candidate: the anaphoric maybe-run
Matches existing: parallel-frame pairs
What: Consecutive paragraphs or clauses open on the same frame ("Maybe his X…", "It might be…", "he wouldn't…") and walk through a hypothetical case by concession, each rung granting a real difficulty before dismissing it.
Quotes:
> Maybe his cognitive problems would make him a slightly less proficient hunter than someone else, but whatever, he could always gather.
> Maybe his emotional control problems would give him a little bit of a handicap in tribal politics, but he wouldn’t get arrested for making a scene, he wouldn’t get fired for not sucking up to his boss enough, he wouldn’t be forced to live in a tiny apartment with people he didn’t necessarily like who were constantly getting on his nerves.
> It might be based around helping others in less tangible ways, like providing company and cheerfulness and love. It might be a virtue ethics celebrating people unusually good at cultivating traits we value. Or it might be a sort of philosophically-informed hedonism along the lines of Epicurus, where we try to enjoy ourselves in the ways that make us most human.
Notes: Three separate runs in 1,500 words; the frames concede rather than assert, which fits the essay's gentle register.

## Register note
Personal-clinical rather than argumentative: first-person psychiatrist anecdotes carry the weight, the jokes nearly vanish (one sarcastic aside, no coinages, no absurd-scale images), and sentences shorten around the patients. The signature machinery — flat landings, parentheticals, ventriloquism — survives, but tuned way down and pointed inward.
