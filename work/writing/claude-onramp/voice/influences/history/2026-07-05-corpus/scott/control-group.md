# The Control Group Is Out Of Control (2014 · ~4700 words · argument)
https://slatestarcodex.com/2014/04/28/the-control-group-is-out-of-control/

## Candidate: the profane refrain as section punctuation
Matches existing: coin it once, hammer it forever
What: A shock phrase closes section I, then returns verbatim to close section II, with "once again" flagging its own reuse — the repetition, not the profanity, is the joke.
Quotes:
> Well, what now, motherfuckers?
> So – once again – what now, motherfuckers?
Notes: Here the reused coinage is a refrain doing structural work (marking each time the argument's floor gives way), not a concept name.

## Candidate: parallel-frame pairs
Matches existing: parallel-frame pairs
What: Two paragraphs (or two sentences) with identical syntax and swapped nouns, so the reader feels the analogy before it's stated. Used both for the essay's core object-level/meta-level analogy and for symmetrical accusations.
Quotes:
> That doesn’t tell you much until you give some other people a placebo drug you know doesn’t work – but which they themselves believe in – and see how many of them recover.
> That doesn’t tell you much until you take some other researchers who are studying a phenomenon you know doesn’t exist – but which they themselves believe in – and see how many of them get positive findings.
> Bem, you are abusing Bayes factor. […] Wagenmakers, you are overconfident.
Notes: The opening pair is near word-for-word across two full paragraphs — the strongest instance of this pattern in the three pieces.

## Candidate: build the list, then knock it down item by item
Matches existing: new
What: State a numbered reform list earnestly, then re-walk it later as one-line paragraphs, each pairing the item's noun with a deflating verb ("will just", "only assures", "just tells you"). The demolition inherits the list's structure, so it reads as inevitable.
Quotes:
> Replicating it will just reproduce the confounded results again.
> Pre-registration only assures that your study will not get any worse than it was the first time you thought of it, which may be very bad indeed.
> Heterogeneity just tells you whether all of the studies were confounded about the same amount.
Notes: Six matched one-liners in a row in section III; distinct from the machine-gun list — the list here is a trap the essay sets for itself.

## Candidate: ventriloquism
Matches existing: ventriloquism
What: Hypothetical figures — a stunned scientist, God, Galileo — get invented direct speech in their own idiom, turning abstract epistemology into staged dialogue.
Quotes:
> “Wow, of the three hundred different terrible ways to mistake noise for signal, Bem has proven beyond a shadow of a doubt he hasn’t done any of them.”
> Suppose God came down from Heaven and said in a booming voice “EVERY SINGLE STUDY IN THIS META-ANALYSIS WAS CONDUCTED PERFECTLY WITHOUT FLAWS OR BIAS, AS WAS THE META-ANALYSIS ITSELF.”
> And then Galileo said “Wait a second, instead of debating exactly how objects fall, let’s just drop objects off of something really tall and see what happens”, and after that, Science.
Notes: Wider cast than the outgroup essay's steelmanned opponents — here the voices are thought-experiment characters, not adversaries.

## Candidate: turning the weapon on himself
Matches existing: turning the weapon on himself
What: The essay's accusation is applied to the author and to the essay itself, unsoftened — including a regress chain that interrogates his own judgment until it eats its tail.
Quotes:
> I am both extremely pleased at this victory for good science, and ashamed that my own field is apparently behind parapsychology in the “scientific rigor” department
> And the result is a lot of essays like this one, where people who think they’re smart take one side of a scientific “controversy” and say which studies you should believe.
> How do I know it’s crap? Well, I use my personal judgment. How do I know my personal judgment is right? Well, a smart well-credentialed person like James Coyne agrees with me. How do I know James Coyne is smart?
Notes: The self-application is the essay's actual conclusion, not a gesture — the Ouroboros passage is the thesis.

## Candidate: the flat landing
Matches existing: the flat landing
What: A long buildup ends on one plain short sentence, often as its own paragraph, that refuses to match the buildup's energy.
Quotes:

Notes: The last one lands the essay's scariest claim (no study survives real scrutiny) in ten flat words.

## Register note
This is Scott's argument-essay default at full stretch: numbered roman-numeral sections, each ending on a punchline or flat landing. What's distinctive versus his median argument piece is the register range — the same essay contains Bayes-factor arithmetic, "motherfuckers" as a refrain, and an all-caps Star Wars parody as the closing paragraph.
