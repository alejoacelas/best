# Meditations on Moloch (2014 · ~14,300 words · argument)
https://slatestarcodex.com/2014/07/30/meditations-on-moloch/

## Candidate: the god's-eye refrain
Matches existing: parallel-frame pairs
What: A two-sentence template — "From a god's-eye-view, X. From within the system, Y." — repeated after nearly every one of fourteen examples, identical syntax with swapped content. The pair-frame scales up from a local sentence trick to an essay-length refrain that does the arguing by accumulation.
Quotes:
> From a god’s-eye-view, we can agree that cooperate-cooperate is a better outcome than defect-defect, but neither prisoner within the system can make it happen.
> From a god’s-eye-view, every Congressperson ought to think only of the good of the nation. From within the system, you do what gets you elected.
> It is glorious that we can create something like this.
> It is shameful that we did.
Notes: ~14 occurrences of the god's-eye/within-system pair; the Vegas pair shows the same frame compressed to two four-word paragraphs.

## Candidate: the borrowed chorus
Matches existing: new
What: Lines from a quoted source (the Ginsberg poem that opens the essay) are re-injected as parenthetical interjections after his own examples, so the borrowed text becomes a recurring chorus that annotates the argument instead of decorating it.
Quotes:
> (Ashcans and unobtainable dollars!)
> (Moloch the vast stone of war! Moloch whose fingers are ten armies!)
> (Moloch whose eyes are a thousand blind windows!)
Notes: 8+ occurrences, each matched to the example it follows; the essay also ends by replaying four full stanzas. Transfers to any essay built on a strong epigraph.

## Candidate: coin it once, hammer it forever
Matches existing: coin it once, hammer it forever
What: "Gotcha!" is derived once (from the acronym of "Gods of the Copybook Headings") and then reused verbatim as a recurring rebuttal-stamp; "throw X under the bus" gets the same treatment as the essay's standing idiom for value-sacrifice.
Quotes:
> “If you don’t work, you die.” Gotcha! If you do work, you also die! Everyone dies, unpredictably, at a time not of their own choosing, and all the virtue in the world does not save you.
> Gotcha! You die anyway!
> Submit to Gnon? Gotcha! As the Antarans put it, “you may not surrender, you can not win, your only option is to die.”
Notes: "Gotcha!" appears ~6 times, "under the bus" ~8; by the later uses each carries the whole earlier argument in one word.

## Candidate: the flat landing
Matches existing: the flat landing
What: A long, careful buildup — often a full page of mechanism — ends on one plain, slangy short sentence standing as its own paragraph.
Quotes:
> Well, we have about a zillion think tanks researching new and better forms of propaganda. And we have constitutionally protected freedom of speech. And we have the Internet. So we’re kind of screwed.
> Yeah, you’re kind of screwed.
> Saudi Arabia uses a different tactic. They just put one guy in charge of everything.
Notes: The Saudi line lands flat right after a sentence-long list of America's constitutional machinery — the deadpan contrast is the joke.

## Candidate: ventriloquism
Matches existing: ventriloquism
What: Invented speakers get full speeches in quotation marks — a naive reformer, a rational central planner, the people inside the system — each in their own idiom, so positions argue in voice rather than in summary.
Quotes:
> And sometimes someone will say something like “I can’t believe people are too dumb to fix Science. All we would have to do is require early registration of studies to avoid publication bias, turn this new and powerful statistical technique into the new standard, and accord higher status to scientists who do replication experiments. It would be really simple and it would vastly increase scientific progress. I must just be smarter than all existing scientists, since I’m able to think of this and they aren’t.”
> People within the system, following the incentives created by these facts, think: “Let’s build a forty-story-high indoor replica of ancient Rome full of albino tigers in the middle of the desert, and so become slightly richer than people who didn’t!”
Notes: The planner/insider pair is ventriloquism nested inside a parallel-frame pair — two invented speeches with matched syntax.

## Candidate: the absurd-scale restatement
Matches existing: the absurd-scale restatement
What: A precise figure or percentage is immediately cashed out as one concrete, faintly ridiculous physical image, often with an ironic minimizer attached.
Quotes:
> Only 9% of Americans like it, suggesting a lower approval rating than cockroaches, head lice, or traffic jams.
> Remember: Moloch can’t agree even to this 99.99999% victory. Rats racing to populate an island don’t leave a little aside as a preserve where the few rats who live there can live happy lives producing artwork.
> Competition and optimization are blind idiotic processes and they fully intend to deny us even one lousy galaxy.
Notes: "one lousy galaxy" is the ironic minimizer at literal cosmic scale — the pattern's ceiling.

## Candidate: the parenthetical afterthought
Matches existing: the parenthetical afterthought
What: Parentheses that drop register or escalate the horror rather than hedge — a self-mocking aside, a joke pushed one step too far, a grim implication left half-said.
Quotes:
> (Slate Star Codex: Your source for macabre whale metaphors since June 2014)
> (or just invent a robot that doesn’t need food or sleep at all. What happens to the slaves after that is better left unsaid)
> (there are some scenarios in which a few capitalists who own the robots may benefit here, but in either case the vast majority are out of luck)
Notes: Very frequent; the darkest lines in the essay are almost all inside parentheses, which is what keeps the main text's register stable.

## Candidate: the ladder with a broken last rung
Matches existing: new
What: An anaphoric series of three-plus identically framed sentences that escalates steadily, then breaks the template on the final rung to deliver the point — the swerve, not the repetition, carries the argument.
Quotes:
> To expect God to care about you or your personal values or the values of your civilization, that’s hubris.
> To expect to be able to remove God from the picture entirely…well, at least it’s an actionable strategy.
> Once a robot can do everything an IQ 180 human can do, only better and cheaper, there will be no reason to employ humans at all, in the unlikely scenario that there are any left by that point.
Notes: The IQ ladder runs 80 → 120 → 180 with identical syntax before the last clause breaks pattern. Close cousin of parallel-frame pairs but the payoff is the deviation.

## Register note
Still an argument essay, but it borrows a liturgical register: a quoted poem serves as chorus, section VIII shifts into sermon cadence ("There are many gods, but this one is ours"), and it closes on a battle cry instead of a conclusion. The game-theory middle stays in his default explainer voice; the frame around it is prophecy.
