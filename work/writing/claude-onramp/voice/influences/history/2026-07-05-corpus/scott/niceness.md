## Candidate: ventriloquism
Matches existing: ventriloquism
What: Objections arrive as full speeches in the objector's own idiom — an imagined heckler gets whole paragraphs of first-person dialogue that state the counterargument better than the real opponent did.
Quotes:
> “Wait a second, no!” someone yells. “I see where you’re going with this. You’re going to say that agreeing not to spread malicious lies about each other would also be a civilized and beneficial system.
> “The FBI has determined that no false rape claims have ever been submitted, my source is an obscure report they published, when your local library doesn’t have it you will just accept that libraries can’t have all books, and suspect nothing.”
Notes: The yelling interlocutor runs two full paragraphs before Scott resumes; the second quote is him voicing the liar he's debunking.

## Candidate: the critic cast as extra
Matches existing: new
What: The named opponent is written into a historical or fictional scene as a speaking bit character, given cartoon dialogue that dramatizes his position at its most futile.
Quotes:
> During World War I, the English and Germans spontaneously got out of their trenches and celebrated Christmas together with each other, and on the sidelines Andrew was shouting “No! Stop celebrating Christmas! Quick, shoot them before they shoot you!” but they didn’t listen.
> Andrew is the poor shmuck who is sitting there saying “Ha ha, a god who doesn’t even control any hell-monsters or command his worshippers to become killing machines. What a weakling! This is going to be so easy!”
Notes: Ventriloquism's satirical cousin — the mouth-words mechanic without the steelman; the speech is deliberately a cartoon.

## Candidate: coin it once, hammer it forever
Matches existing: coin it once, hammer it forever

## Register note
This is the argument-essay default at full intensity, with one structural difference: it's a rebuttal, so the opponent's actual words are load-bearing — long block quotes recur as raw material, and the comedy moves (premise played straight, critic cast as extra) all serve prosecution rather than decoration.
