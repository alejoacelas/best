# All Debates Are Bravery Debates (2013 · ~1900 words · argument)
https://slatestarcodex.com/2013/06/09/all-debates-are-bravery-debates/

## Candidate: the vignette stack before the thesis
Matches existing: new
What: Sections I–III are self-contained anecdotes with no stated connection; the thesis arrives in section IV and re-lists the vignettes as curt fragments, cashing in the setup all at once.
Quotes:
> Suppose there are two sides to an issue. Be more or less selfish. Post more or less offensive atheist memes. Be more or less willing to blame and criticize yourself.
> But I think I underestimated an important reason why some debates have to be bravery debates.
Notes: The three-fragment recap sentence is the mechanic to steal — it proves the vignettes were parallel without ever saying "these three stories share a structure."

## Candidate: parallel-frame pairs
Matches existing: parallel-frame pairs
What: Opposed positions get sentences of identical syntax with the polarity flipped, so the symmetry — not any argument — carries the claim that both are right about different populations.
Quotes:
> Landmark claims its members are biased against ever thinking ill of themselves, even when they deserve it. The therapy book claims that patients are biased towards always thinking ill of themselves, even when they don’t deserve it.
> Suppose an Objectivist argues “Our culture has become too self-sacrificing! Everyone is told their entire life that the only purpose of living is to work for other people.
> But suppose Peter Singer argues “We live in a culture of selfishness! Everyone is always told to look out for number one, and the poor are completely neglected!”
Notes: The Landmark/therapy pair is the essay's hinge; the Objectivist/Singer pair extends it into full mirrored paragraphs.

## Candidate: ventriloquism
Matches existing: ventriloquism
What: Both sides of a dispute get invented direct speech — and the register contrast between the two voices (all-caps zealot vs. mild reasonable-guy) encodes the essay's claim that they come from different worlds.
Quotes:
> One guy is like “WE NEED TO DESTROY RELIGION IT CORRUPTS EVERYTHING IT TOUCHES ANYONE WHO MAKES ANY COMPROMISES WITH IT IS A TRAITOR KILL KILL KILL.” And the other guy is like “Hello? Religion may not be literally true, but it usually just makes people feel more comfortable and inspires them to do nice things and we don’t want to look like huge jerks here.”
> They’re like “Guys need to be more concerned about women’s boundaries, and women need to be willing to shame and embarrass guys who hit on them inappropriately.”
Notes: The twist over the base pattern: the typography of each speech is evidence for the argument.

## Candidate: the logistics reductio
Matches existing: new
What: An ideal solution is killed by spelling out its literal implementation steps in concrete nouns, letting the absurdity surface on its own — no "obviously this is impossible" needed.
Quotes:
> You can’t go around giving everyone surveys to see how selfish they are, and give half of them Atlas Shrugged and half of them the collected works of Peter Singer .
> You can’t even write really complicated books on how to tell whether you need more or less selfishness in your life – they’re not going to be as buyable, as readable, or as memorable as Atlas Shrugged .
Notes: Also in "The Control Group Is Out Of Control" ("You’d have to find a phenomenon that definitely doesn’t exist, somehow convince a whole community of scientists across the world that it does…") — a stable cross-piece move.

## Candidate: the flat landing
Matches existing: the flat landing
What: A section's long buildup — a whole anecdote about terrible restaurant owners, or about who deconversion memes work on — resolves in one plain short sentence.
Quotes:

## Candidate: turning the weapon on himself
Matches existing: turning the weapon on himself
What: The essay's framework is applied to the author's own blog in the final section, ending on his own unresolved case rather than a conclusion.
Quotes:
> I think most of my friends are too leftist but society as a whole is too rightist – should I spread leftist or rightist memes among my friends?
> I feel pretty okay about both being sort of a libertarian and writing an essay arguing against libertarianism , because the world generally isn’t libertarian enough but the sorts of people who read long online political essays generally are way more libertarian than can possibly be healthy.
Notes: The final sentence of the piece is a self-application, not a summary.

## Candidate: the parenthetical afterthought
Matches existing: the parenthetical afterthought
What: Parens carrying self-deprecation, sourcing hedges played as comedy, or a wink at the reader — mid-sentence register drops rather than caveats.
Quotes:
> After all, if there’s one thing the world doesn’t need (I thought) it’s more selfishness.
> while still keeping the basic human instinct of helping others when he wanted to and he felt they deserved it (as, indeed, do Rand characters).
> And they taught (again, according to this one person) that the solution was to treat everything that happens in your life as your responsibility
Notes: "(I thought)" retro-tags the whole sentence as a past self's error — two words doing a paragraph of framing.

## Register note
Argument built on personal register: the evidence is friends, Reddit threads, and his own high school, and the essay never leaves first person. Versus the argument-essay default there are no demolitions and no shouting at named opponents — the charity is structural, and the piece ends on an open question about himself instead of a verdict.
