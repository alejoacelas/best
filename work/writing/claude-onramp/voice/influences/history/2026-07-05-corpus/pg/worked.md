# What I Worked On (2021 · ~11,500 words · long)
https://www.paulgraham.com/worked.html

## Candidate: flat landing sentence
What: A paragraph of buildup ends on a short declarative sentence with no ornament — the emotional or comic weight rides on the plainness. Often the heavier the content, the flatter the sentence.
Quotes:
> My stories were awful. They had hardly any plot, just characters with strong feelings, which I imagined made them deep.
> Only Harvard accepted me, so that was where I went.
> She died on January 15, 2014. We knew this was coming, but it was still hard when it did.
Notes: Constant throughout; the death sentence shows it works for grief as well as jokes. Highly transferable as a tweak: end one paragraph per page flat.

## Candidate: self-deprecating hindsight
What: Narrates a past decision in the confident register the younger self felt, then undercuts it with present-day irony — often via a mock-grand epithet or a self-posed question answered against himself.
Quotes:
> So with my unerring nose for financial opportunity, I decided to write another book on Lisp.
> I can't honestly say, after reading so many Y Combinator applications, that this was the worst startup idea ever, but it was up there.
> (Had I diligently set aside the proper proportion of the money I'd made consulting for Interleaf? No, I had not.)
Notes: His main humor engine in this piece; the joke is always at his own expense, never the reader's.

## Candidate: one-word beat
What: A one- or two-word sentence — sometimes an interjection — dropped as its own beat to register a reaction, then the narrative moves on without elaborating.
Quotes:
> Aided by occasional visits to actual Cambridge, I gradually realized there wasn't one. Huh.
> Hmph. Well, I'd do it myself then.
> You mean like Microsoft Word? Exactly.
Notes: Rare enough to stay surprising (three or four times in 11,500 words). Simulates spoken timing on the page.

## Candidate: cross-domain simile
What: Similes are scarce but when they come they import a whole concrete world from an unrelated domain — architecture, comics, typography — in one clause, then stop.
Quotes:
> It was like a mini Bond villain's lair down there, with all these alien-looking machines — CPU, disk drives, printer, card reader — sitting up on a raised floor under bright fluorescent lights.
> If you could see inside Rtm's brain it would look like a colonial New England church, and if you could see inside Trevor's it would look like the worst excesses of Austrian Rococo.
> One morning as I was lying on this mattress I had an idea that made me sit up like a capital L.
Notes: Also "sealed up like a tomb of my old life." Never extended past one sentence; the scarcity is what makes each one land.

## Candidate: footnote as pressure valve
What: Digressions, pedantic corrections, and bonus jokes are exiled to numbered footnotes so the main narrative line stays clean; the footnotes themselves keep full essay voice.
Quotes:
> Technically the apartment wasn't rent-controlled but rent-stabilized, but this is a refinement only New Yorkers would know or care about.
> She reports that they were all different shapes and sizes, because there was a run on air conditioners and she had to get whatever she could, but that they were all heavier than she could carry now.
> So if you string together a lot of abstract concepts with a few simple verbs, you can make a little Italian go a long way.
Notes: 19 footnotes; several are pure charm (the air conditioners), others hold a whole secondary thesis (note 12 on customs outliving constraints).

## Candidate: anecdote, then the rule
What: A concrete story closes with one compressed aphoristic generalization, often introduced by "Which means" / "Which in turn means" — the abstraction always arrives after the evidence, never before.
Quotes:
> Which in turn means that prestige is a danger sign.
> But there's nothing like writing a book about something to help you learn it.
> If anything is going to lead you astray, it will be the desire to impress people.
Notes: Also "But attention is a zero sum game." The aphorism is usually a single sentence and often starts a coordinating conjunction ("But", "Which") to keep it welded to the story.

## Candidate: question to self, answered
What: The narrative advances by posing a question in his own head — sometimes verbatim inner speech — and answering it in the next sentence, so decisions read as live thought rather than summary.
Quotes:
> I had always liked looking at paintings. Could I make them? I had no idea.
> Now that I didn't need money anymore, why was I doing this? If this vision had to be realized as a company, then screw the vision.
> How should I choose what to do? Well, how had I chosen what to work on in the past?
Notes: Also "What on earth had happened? The referring urls showed that someone had posted it on Slashdot." Turns exposition into scene.

## Candidate: suspiciously exact number
What: Where a normal writer would round, he gives the precise figure or date — the exactness is itself the joke, or the proof of how vividly he remembers.
Quotes:
> there was about a 60% chance it had to do with HN, and a 40% chance it had do with everything else combined.
> It took 4 years, from March 26, 2015 to October 12, 2019.
> In Florence, after paying my part of the rent, my budget for everything else had been $7 a day.
Notes: The $7/day figure gets reused for contrast ("more than 4 times that every hour"). Precision as credibility device.

## Register note
Memoir, not argument — his longest essay, all first person and chronology, with humor much denser than his how-to pieces. The same short-declarative machinery carries it, but "you" nearly disappears except when a lesson surfaces; closes with a self-referential trick ("this is the last sentence of it").
