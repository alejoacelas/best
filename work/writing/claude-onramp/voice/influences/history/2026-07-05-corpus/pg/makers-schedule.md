# Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule (2009 · ~1,050 words · short)
https://www.paulgraham.com/makersschedule.html

## Candidate: coin the pair, then lean on it
What: Coins two contrasting terms in the second paragraph ("which I'll call..."), then uses them as load-bearing vocabulary in nearly every subsequent paragraph — the essay's argument is carried by repeating the coinage, never by synonyms.
Quotes:
> There are two types of schedule, which I'll call the manager's schedule and the maker's schedule.
> I never thought of it in these terms, but in effect I had two workdays each day, one on the manager's schedule and one on the maker's.
> All we ask from those on the manager's schedule is that they understand the cost.
Notes: The phrase "maker's schedule" or "manager's schedule" appears in most paragraphs of a 1,000-word essay. Repetition-as-glossary: he trusts the reader to enjoy the new handle, not tire of it.

## Candidate: borrowed technical metaphor
What: Explains a human or social experience with one metaphor lifted from the maker's own technical domain — programming, physics — stated in a single sentence and then unpacked in plain terms.
Quotes:
> For someone on the maker's schedule, having a meeting is like throwing an exception. It doesn't merely cause you to switch from one task to another; it changes the mode in which you work.
> Since most powerful people operate on the manager's schedule, they're in a position to make everyone resonate at their frequency if they want to.
Notes: The exception metaphor flatters the audience it describes — the metaphor's home domain is the reader's.

## Candidate: clipped second sentence
What: A normal-length sentence is followed by a very short one that states the consequence or verdict — the paragraph's point compressed to under six words.
Quotes:
> One reason programmers dislike meetings so much is that they're on a different type of schedule from other people. Meetings cost them more.
> Each type of schedule works fine by itself. Problems arise when they meet.
> You can't write or program well in units of an hour. That's barely enough time to get started.
Notes: The essay opens this way; the long-short rhythm recurs every few paragraphs. Probably the single most transferable PG tweak.

## Candidate: objection pre-empted
What: Names the reader's likely objection before they can raise it ("I know this may sound..."), then answers it — sometimes by turning the question directly on the reader, sometimes in a parenthesis that concedes the edge case.
Quotes:
> I know this may sound oversensitive, but if you're a maker, think of your own case. Don't your spirits rise at the thought of having an entire day free to work, with no appointments at all?
> (Unless their working day ends at the same time as mine, the meeting presumably interrupts theirs, but since they made the appointment it must be worth it to them.)
Notes: The first quote also shows his direct-address test: he doesn't assert the reader feels something, he asks.

## Candidate: hedged forecast
What: Predictions come wrapped in explicit soft hedges — "I wouldn't be surprised," "I suspect," "may," "perhaps," "Maybe eventually" — which lets him speculate about the future without staking his authority on it.
Quotes:
> I wouldn't be surprised if there start to be more companies like us. I suspect founders may increasingly be able to resist, or at least postpone, turning into managers, just as a few decades ago they started to be able to resist switching from jeans to suits.
> Maybe eventually, if the conflict between the manager's schedule and the maker's schedule starts to be more widely understood, it will become less of a problem.
Notes: The hedges cluster only around forecasts; present-tense claims about how schedules work get none. The confidence gradient is the pattern.

## Candidate: the essay names itself as the fix
What: Near the end, the essay presents its own existence as the solution to the problem it describes, then closes on a modest, concrete ask instead of a flourish.
Quotes:
> But now that I've realized what's going on, perhaps there's a third option: to write something explaining the two types of schedule.
> All we ask from those on the manager's schedule is that they understand the cost.
Notes: Single occurrence (it is the ending), but a distinctive close: the deflationary last line — a small request, no crescendo — is the flat-landing move applied to a whole essay.

## Register note
His shortest common form: a single distinction stretched exactly as far as it goes and no further, opened with a borrowed epigraph (Dickens) — rare for him — and no footnotes at all; the pressure valve isn't needed at this length.
