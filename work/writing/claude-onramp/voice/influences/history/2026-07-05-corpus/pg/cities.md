# Cities and Ambition (2008 · ~3300 words · medium)
https://www.paulgraham.com/cities.html

## Candidate: refrain template as spine
What: One sentence formula — city + "message" + colon + a second-person clause — is coined early and then re-instantiated a dozen times, so each new city slots into a groove the reader already knows. The essay's structure is the template's reuse.
Quotes:
> New York tells you, above all: you should make more money.
> The message Berkeley sends is: you should live better.
> The message Paris sends now is: do things with style.
Notes: ~12 instances; transfers as "coin a sentence-shape once, then let repetition do the organizing."

## Candidate: self-posed question, blunt answer
What: He asks the question a skeptical reader would ask, then answers it in a word or two — often a bare "I don't" or "No" — before elaborating.
Quotes:
> If even someone with the same natural ability as Leonardo couldn't beat the force of environment, do you suppose you can?
> I don't. I'm fairly stubborn, but I wouldn't try to fight this force.
> How much does it matter what message a city sends? Empirically, the answer seems to be: a lot.
Notes: Also "Does anyone who wants to do great work have to live in a great city? No; all great cities inspire some sort of ambition, but they aren't the only places that do."

## Candidate: bold claim, then name its audacity
What: States a superlative, immediately concedes it sounds absurd, then defends it with a twist rather than retreating. The concession buys the claim credibility.
Quotes:
> As of this writing, Cambridge seems to be the intellectual capital of the world. I realize that seems a preposterous claim. What makes it true is that it's more preposterous to claim about anywhere else.
> I wouldn't bet on it, but I wouldn't bet against it either.
Notes: Second quote is from footnote 4; the symmetric double-hedge is its own micro-move.

## Candidate: lived-it credential
What: Evidence for a city's message is that he personally tried living there; the anecdote is one or two sentences, always ends in a verdict, often a failed prediction told at his own expense.
Quotes:
> I'd always imagined Berkeley would be the ideal place — that it would basically be Cambridge with good weather. But when I finally tried living there a couple years ago, it turned out not to be.
> Often your information will be wrong: I tried living in Florence when I was 25, thinking it would be an art center, but it turned out I was 450 years too late.
> I understand the messages of New York, Cambridge, and Silicon Valley because I've lived for several years in each of them.
Notes: The self-deprecating failed-prediction version is the most transferable form.

## Candidate: image pair as argument
What: A contrast is carried by two matched concrete images — one sentence each, no adjectives of judgment — and the images do the arguing.
Quotes:
> When you walk through Palo Alto in the evening, you see nothing but the blue glow of TVs. In Cambridge you see shelves full of promising-looking books.
> Visually, Paris has the best eavesdropping I know.
Notes: The "eavesdropping" metaphor is set up in the body and paid off across sections.

## Candidate: hyper-specific number as wit
What: Drops a needlessly precise figure where a vague one would do; the precision reads as both authority and deadpan joke.
Quotes:
> It's just 178 square miles at one end of it.
> It turned out it was way, way uptown: an hour uptown by air.
> the ratio of New York to California residents in the Forbes 400 has decreased from 1.45 (81:56) when the list was first published in 1982 to .83 (73:88) in 2007.
Notes: Also "450 years too late." Cheap to steal; overuse kills it.

## Candidate: footnote as pressure valve
What: Footnotes hold what the body's register can't — rudeness, over-specific data, and reporting he did just for the joke. The body stays composed; the footnotes get to be salty.
Quotes:
> If you did that in New York, people would treat you like shit.
> (Zagat's lists the Ritz Carlton Dining Room in SF as requiring jackets but I couldn't believe it, so I called to check and in fact they don't.
> their houses are transformed by developers into McMansions and sold to VPs of Bus Dev.
Notes: All from notes 2–3; "I called to check" performs epistemic diligence as comedy.

## Candidate: inverted-expectation closer
What: The last sentence flips the reader's assumed causal order into an aphorism, looping back to the essay's terms.
Quotes:
> You'll probably have to find the city where you feel at home to know what sort of ambition you have.
Notes: Single occurrence, but the shape (end on a reversal of the obvious ordering) recurs across PG endings.

## Register note
More travelogue and first-person than the standard PG how-to essay: sections are separated by rules and organized by place, and the argument advances by accumulated personal observation rather than by a chain of reasoning. Hedges ("seems", "probably") are unusually dense because every claim is an empirical read on a city.
