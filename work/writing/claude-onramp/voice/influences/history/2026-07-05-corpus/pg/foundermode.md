# Founder Mode (2024 · ~1,100 words · short)
https://www.paulgraham.com/foundermode.html

## Candidate: cold open on a scene
What: Opens inside a specific recent event with named people, then spends one sentence on a single telling detail before pivoting to the question the essay will chase.
Quotes:
> At a YC event last week Brian Chesky gave a talk that everyone who was there will remember.
> Ron Conway, for the first time in his life, forgot to take notes.
Notes: The detail (Conway's notes) carries the entire "this talk mattered" claim; no adjectives needed.

## Candidate: quote the advice, then gut it
What: States the conventional wisdom in its own most appealing words — even lets it sound good for a beat, with a direct-address aside — then restates it in hostile translation.
Quotes:
> Hire good people and give them room to do their jobs. Sounds great when it's described that way, doesn't it?
> Except in practice, judging from the report of founder after founder, what this often turns out to mean is: hire professional fakers and let them drive the company into the ground.
Notes: The "doesn't it?" tag is the setup for the knife; the flip lands as a colon-introduced retranslation.

## Candidate: mystery narration
What: Poses the puzzle explicitly, marks it as a mystery to him personally, then narrates the moment of solution — the essay's spine is a detective beat, not a thesis defense.
Quotes:
> Why was everyone telling these founders the wrong thing? That was the big mystery to me.
> And after mulling it over for a bit I figured out the answer: what they were being told was how to run a company you hadn't founded — how to run a company if you're merely a professional manager.

## Candidate: ignorance as evidence
What: Repeatedly and flatly states how little is known about his own subject; the admitted blank spots become the argument that something real is undiscovered.
Quotes:
> There are as far as I know no books specifically about founder mode. Business schools don't know it exists.
> So is it a good idea, or a bad one? We still don't know. That's how little we know about founder mode.
Notes: Note the self-answered question ending in a snap close — same close mechanic as the long essays.

## Candidate: footnote for the undiplomatic version
What: The sharpest insult stays in the main text, and the footnote performs the diplomatic rephrasing — the apology-as-footnote doubles the joke instead of softening it.
Quotes:
> C-level execs, as a class, include some of the most skillful liars in the world.
> The more diplomatic way of phrasing this statement would be to say that experienced C-level execs are often very skilled at managing up. And I don't think anyone with knowledge of this world would dispute that.
Notes: Footnote 3 does the mirror job: the caveat to his own optimism is exiled there.

## Candidate: imagine-prompts and the exemplar close
What: Direct commands to the reader's imagination ("Can you imagine", "imagine how", "Look at", "Imagine what"), ending the essay on a two-proper-noun contrast that compresses the whole thesis into names.
Quotes:
> Can you imagine the force of will it would take to do this at the average company? And yet imagine how useful such a thing could be.
> Imagine what they'll do once we can tell them how to run their companies like Steve Jobs instead of John Sculley.
Notes: Jobs/Sculley closer is single occurrence, but the imagine-imperative repeats; mark the closer as the payoff of the pattern.

## Register note
Reportage register, unusual for PG: named living people, an event last week, and open combat with identifiable groups (VCs, C-level execs). Also his most deliberately unfinished essay — hedges here ("as far as I know", "will probably vary", "we can already guess") mark a forecast in progress rather than settled advice.
