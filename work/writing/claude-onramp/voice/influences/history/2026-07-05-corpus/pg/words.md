# Putting Ideas into Words (2022 · ~1200 words · short)
https://www.paulgraham.com/words.html

## Candidate: the personified reader
What: An abstraction (the imagined neutral reader) becomes a recurring character — "the stranger" — who gets pronouns, demands, and even a line of dialogue addressed to him.
Quotes:
> You have to pretend to be a neutral reader who knows nothing of what's in your head, only what you wrote.
> It takes me many cycles before I can get an essay past the stranger. But the stranger is rational, so you always can, if you ask him what he needs.
> If he's not satisfied because you failed to mention x or didn't qualify some sentence sufficiently, then you mention x or add more qualifications. Happy now?
Notes: One extended device rather than scattered instances, but it recurs across the paragraph and "Happy now?" — spoken to the character — is the sharpest move in the essay. Transfer: name the abstraction once, then treat it as a person.

## Candidate: staircase restatement
What: A claim is restated two or three times in a row, each version tighter or more extreme than the last, ending on the harshest formulation.
Quotes:
> At best the plan is vague and incomplete. Very likely there's some undiscovered flaw that invalidates it completely. At best it's a plan for a plan.
> If writing down your ideas always makes them more precise and more complete, then no one who hasn't written about a topic has fully formed ideas about it. And someone who never writes has no fully formed ideas about anything nontrivial.
> So if you never subject your ideas to that test, you'll not only never have fully formed ideas, but also never realize it.
Notes: The escalation is logical, not rhetorical — each restatement follows from the previous one, so the extreme final version feels proved rather than shouted.

## Candidate: not-saying / saying pivot
What: He pre-empts the overreading with an explicit "I'm not saying X" and immediately supplies the exact claim he is making — the concession and the claim in adjacent sentences.
Quotes:
> I'm not saying that writing is the best way to explore all ideas. If you have ideas about architecture, presumably the best way to explore them is to build actual buildings. What I'm saying is that however much you learn from exploring ideas in other ways, you'll still learn new things from writing about them.
> Putting ideas into words is certainly no guarantee that they'll be right. Far from it. But though it's not a sufficient condition, it is a necessary one.
> This much, I assume, won't be that controversial. I think it will accord with the experience of anyone who has tried to write about anything nontrivial.
Notes: The final quote's sufficient/necessary line is also the essay's last sentence — the concession pivot doubles as the close.

## Candidate: numbers on his own habits
What: Personal-process anecdotes come with specific quantities — weeks, reread counts, subject counts — and the number itself makes the point.
Quotes:
> I'll often spend 2 weeks on an essay and reread drafts 50 times. If you did that in conversation it would seem evidence of some kind of mental disorder.
> I've written about at least two subjects I know well — Lisp hacking and startups — and in both cases I learned a lot from writing about them.
> I'll sometimes think of a paragraph while walking or lying in bed that survives nearly unchanged in the final version.
Notes: "2 weeks... 50 times" is the essay's most-quoted line, and the joke lands because the number precedes it.

## Candidate: announced escalation
What: He tells the reader explicitly that the stakes are about to rise — naming the previous point as obvious and the next as shocking — before delivering it.
Quotes:
> The reason I've spent so long establishing this rather obvious point is that it leads to another that many people will find shocking.
Notes: Single occurrence, but it's the essay's hinge and a signature PG structural move: the meta-sentence buys attention for the strongest claim.

## Candidate: footnote that enacts the claim
What: A footnote demonstrates the sentence it annotates — the essay about writing in your head footnotes the moment the sentence was written in his head.
Quotes:
> I thought of this sentence as I was walking down the street in Palo Alto.
Notes: Single occurrence; a wink, not an apparatus. Same valve pattern as elsewhere — the other notes absorb qualifications ("Machinery and circuits are formal languages.").

## Register note
Late-period PG: very short, no section breaks, one claim driven to its logical extreme rather than a tour. Almost no similes and only one joke — the compression itself is the style; nearly every paragraph ends on a snap line.
