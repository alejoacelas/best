# How to Think for Yourself (2020 · ~3200 words · medium)
https://www.paulgraham.com/think.html

## Candidate: case ladder with cheap connectives
What: Opens with the same claim instantiated across parallel cases in successive paragraphs, joined by deliberately low-effort connectives ("The same is true for", "You see this pattern with", "Ditto for") — then breaks the ladder with a "But".
Quotes:
> The same is true for investors.
> You see this pattern with startup founders too.
> Ditto for essayists. An essay that told people things they already knew would be boring.
Notes: The break line is "But this pattern isn't universal." Four rungs, each shorter than the last — "Ditto" signals the pattern has earned compression.

## Candidate: hedge then snap
What: A claim arrives wrapped in "I suspect / seems / may" — then the next sentence is a short flat assertion that lands the point harder than confidence would have. The hedge marks calibration; the snap carries conviction.
Quotes:
> I suspect most people's unconscious mind will answer that question before their conscious mind has a chance to. I know mine does.
> It's a good way to make conversation when you meet strangers, but I don't do it to make conversation. I really want to know.
> But strong convictions are not a sign of independent-mindedness. Rather the opposite.
Notes: Third quote is footnote 5. The snap is usually 3–6 words and first person or a fragment.

## Candidate: not-X-but-Y definition
What: Defines a quality by first striking down the near-miss reading, then supplying the sharper positive one — contrast pairs doing the work of definition.
Quotes:
> It's not mere skepticism, but an active delight in ideas that subvert the conventional wisdom, the more counterintuitive the better.
> The end goal is not to find flaws in the things you're told, but to find the new ideas that had been concealed by the broken ones.
> Fastidiousness about truth means more than just not believing things that are false. It means being careful about degree of belief.
Notes: Also "an exciting quest for novelty, not a boring protocol for intellectual hygiene." Very high frequency in this piece; his main aphorism engine here.

## Candidate: reader in the second person, given orders
What: Advice is delivered as direct imperatives to "you", often with quoted inner speech the reader is told to perform, and concrete escape rules.
Quotes:
> When you hear someone say something, stop and ask yourself "Is that true?" Don't say it out loud.
> If you later find yourself in a situation that makes you think "this is like high school," you know you should get out.
> Treat it as a puzzle.
Notes: The quoted self-talk ("Is that true?") makes the advice executable, not aspirational.

## Candidate: grotesque simile for an abstraction
What: A rare but deliberately physical, slightly repulsive or homely simile makes an abstract distinction felt in the body. One per section at most.
Quotes:
> just as it would seem to someone fastidious about food to take a bite of a submarine sandwich filled with a large variety of ingredients of indeterminate age and provenance.
> Whereas the independent-minded are the gluttons of curiosity, who keep eating even after they're full.
> much as control rods slow chain reactions, so that the conventional-minded aren't as dangerous.
Notes: Third is footnote 3. He extends one metaphor structurally too ("the individual muscles we need to exercise, as it were" → components "can substitute for one another in much the same way muscles can").

## Candidate: name the parts, then work the schema
What: Decomposes the topic into an explicitly numbered anatomy, then gets extra mileage by running operations over the parts — ordering them by innateness, showing how they substitute and combine.
Quotes:
> It seems to me that it has three components: fastidiousness about truth, resistance to being told what to think, and curiosity.
> The three components of independent-mindedness work in concert: fastidiousness about truth and resistance to being told what to think leave space in your brain, and curiosity finds new ideas to fill it.
> Interestingly, the three components can substitute for one another in much the same way muscles can.
Notes: The one-sentence recombination summary ("work in concert...") is the transferable move: a mechanism stated as a single flowing sentence.

## Candidate: motto-swap closer
What: Ends by rewriting a familiar slogan into his version, double-hedged with "Perhaps" — the essay's whole argument compressed into an edit of a cliché.
Quotes:
> So perhaps curiosity is the compass here. Perhaps, if your goal is to discover novel ideas, your motto should not be "do what you love" so much as "do what you're curious about."
Notes: Single occurrence, but a signature PG ending shape: last line is the portable takeaway, hedged rather than trumpeted.

## Candidate: footnote as pressure valve
What: The body stays measured; footnotes take the personal shots and the jokes — naming Twitter, mocking extremists — plus one absurdist image.
Quotes:
> When I ask myself what in my life is like high school, the answer is Twitter.
> but subject to violent storms of conventional-mindedness that remind me of descriptions of Jupiter.
> They think themselves nonconformists, but actually they're niche conformists.
Notes: Notes 2 and 7. "Niche conformists" is an aphorism he parked in a footnote rather than let it heat up the body.

## Register note
The most systematic of the three pieces — a taxonomy essay with numbered components and explicit section breaks, closer to his how-to mode than to anecdote. Almost no autobiography beyond one line about his father; "you" density is very high throughout.
