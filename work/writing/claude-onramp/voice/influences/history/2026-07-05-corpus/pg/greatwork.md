# How to Do Great Work (2023 · ~11,000 words · long)
https://www.paulgraham.com/greatwork.html

## Candidate: the snap close
What: Paragraphs of medium-length reasoning end on one clipped declarative — shorter than everything before it — that lands the point and stops.
Quotes:
> If you're interested, you're not astray.
> And once you go down that route, you're lost.
> So just do the work and your identity will take care of itself.
Notes: Very high frequency; the rhythm of the whole essay. Transfers as: after the argument, one short sentence, no trailing qualifier.

## Candidate: one-sentence paragraph as display case
What: An aphorism gets its own paragraph, set apart with white space so it reads as the takeaway rather than a step in the argument.
Quotes:
> The following recipe assumes you're very ambitious.
> When an idea seems simultaneously novel and obvious, it's probably a good one.
> If you express your ideas in the most general form, they'll be truer than you intended.
Notes: Used sparingly relative to essay length, which is what preserves the effect.

## Candidate: proverb repair
What: Quotes a familiar saying, declares it "not quite right," and issues the corrected version — definition by contrast against received wisdom rather than against a strawman.
Quotes:
> So "If at first you don't succeed, try, try again" isn't quite right. It should be: If at first you don't succeed, either try again, or backtrack and then try again.
> "Never give up" is also not quite right. Obviously there are times when it's the right choice to eject.
> Originality is the presence of new ideas, not the absence of old ones.
Notes: Third quote is the adjacent move — correcting a folk definition without quoting it. Easy tweak-level transfer.

## Candidate: mirror aphorism
What: Aphorisms built from two parallel halves — same syntax, swapped terms — often hinged on a semicolon, so the symmetry does the persuading.
Quotes:
> Curiosity is itself a kind of originality; it's roughly to questions what originality is to answers.
> Good pain is a sign of effort; bad pain is a sign of damage.
> Style is doing things in a distinctive way without trying to. Trying to is affectation.
Notes: The third is the compressed variant: second sentence reuses the first's tail as its head.

## Candidate: simile from the workshop
What: Similes are rare, always physical, and drawn from mechanics or physics — one concrete image per abstraction, never decorative chains.
Quotes:
> Original thinkers throw off new ideas about whatever they focus on, like an angle grinder throwing off sparks.
> In schools they tend to exist only briefly before being answered, like unstable particles.
> One of the biggest mistakes ambitious people make is to allow setbacks to destroy their morale all at once, like a balloon bursting.
Notes: Also borrows technical terms straight ("Work has a sort of activation energy"). Scarcity is part of the mechanic.

## Candidate: question-then-answer pivot
What: Poses the reader's next question out loud, then answers it in a fragment or short sentence — the question does the transition work between sections.
Quotes:
> What are you excessively curious about — curious to a degree that would bore most other people? That's what you're looking for.
> How do you get from starting small to doing something great? By making successive versions.
> How do you find these? By being self-indulgent — by letting your curiosity have its way, and tuning out, at least temporarily, the little voice in your head that says you should only be working on "important" problems.

## Candidate: hedge then double down
What: A hedge ("probably", "may not be possible to prove") concedes the epistemics, then the very next clause escalates the claim harder than an unhedged version could.
Quotes:
> It may not be possible to prove that you have to work hard to do great things, but the empirical evidence is on the scale of the evidence for mortality.
> Out of all those different types of work, the one you're most suited for is probably a pretty close match. Probably a comically close match.
> But I doubt it would be possible to do great work without being earnest. It's so hard to do even if you are.
Notes: The hedge buys license for the sweep; it never softens the conclusion.

## Candidate: footnote as pressure valve
What: Jokes, self-deprecation, and tangents that would break the essay's even tone get exiled to footnotes, where the register loosens noticeably.
Quotes:
> Some say psychoactive drugs have a similar effect. I'm skeptical, but also almost totally ignorant of their effects.
> Which may help explain why people's reaction to a new idea is often the first half of laughing: Ha!
> Attacking a project as a "toy" is similar to attacking a statement as "inappropriate." It means that no more substantial criticism can be made to stick.
Notes: 29 footnotes in this essay; the main text stays almost humorless so the footnotes can't be cut without losing the wit.

## Register note
Atypically long (his longest essay) and atypically impersonal: sustained second-person imperative handbook, sections separated by white space with no headers, almost no anecdote per thousand words. The meta turn at the end ("Believe it or not, I tried to make this essay as short as I could... So I'm going to pull a sneaky trick on you.") is the one place the narrator steps on stage.
