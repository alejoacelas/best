# The Top Idea in Your Mind (2010 · ~1,100 words · short)
https://www.paulgraham.com/top.html

## Candidate: opening by self-revision
What: Opens by stating his previous belief and then upgrading it in explicit steps — "I knew X. Now I'd go further" — so the thesis arrives as a correction of his own earlier self.
Quotes:
> I realized recently that what one thinks about in the shower in the morning is more important than I'd thought.
> I knew it was a good time to have ideas. Now I'd go further: now I'd say it's hard to do a really good job on anything you don't think about in the shower.
Notes: Recurs mid-essay too ("I'd noticed startups got way less done... but it was not till we ourselves raised money that I understood why").

## Candidate: shower as refrain
What: One homely concrete image is planted in the first sentence, then repeated verbatim at every abstraction point and again as the closing test — the image is the essay's spine and its instrument.
Quotes:
> That becomes what you think about when you take a shower in the morning.
> But it's easy to figure this out: just take a shower. What topic do your thoughts keep returning to?
Notes: Appears five times. Transfer: pick one mundane anchor image and make the conclusion literally use it.

## Candidate: anecdote as proof
What: The load-bearing evidence is first-person biography — specific companies, specific stretches of years — told flatly and dated, not dramatized.
Quotes:
> I'd hated raising money when I was running Viaweb, but I'd forgotten why I hated it so much. When we raised money for Y Combinator, I remembered.
> The reason this struck me so forcibly is that for most of the preceding 10 years I'd been able to think about what I wanted. So the contrast when I couldn't was sharp.

## Candidate: staccato hammer
What: A point gets driven in with a run of consecutive short sentences of near-identical shape, each adding one increment — rhythm as emphasis instead of intensifiers.
Quotes:
> The reason is that they have to be. It's hard to get money. It's not the sort of thing that happens by default. It's not going to happen unless you let it become the thing you think about in the shower.
> You can't directly control where your thoughts drift. If you're controlling them, they're not drifting.
Notes: Second quote is the compressed variant — a two-sentence logical snap.

## Candidate: first-person calibration hedges
What: "I suspect", "I think", "I've found" prefix the claims, and he volunteers his own fallibility as part of the evidence — the hedge marks how he knows, not how sure he is.
Quotes:
> I suspect a lot of people aren't sure what's the top idea in their mind at any given time. I'm often mistaken about it.
> I've found I can to some extent avoid thinking about nasty things people have done to me by telling myself: this doesn't deserve space in my head.
Notes: "I'm often mistaken about it" — confessing the failure mode strengthens the advice that follows.

## Candidate: odd concrete simile
What: Two similes in one paragraph, both physically specific and slightly strange (an invasive fish, velcro) — the strangeness makes the abstraction stick.
Quotes:
> I've found there are two types of thoughts especially worth avoiding — thoughts like the Nile Perch in the way they push out more interesting ideas.
> they have the same velcro-like shape as genuinely interesting ideas, but without the substance.
Notes: Same scarcity discipline as elsewhere: these are nearly the only similes in the piece.

## Candidate: confessed selfish motive
What: Humor by deflating his own apparent virtue — a wise-sounding claim gets punctured with an admission that his motives are self-interested, often via a bystander's misreading.
Quotes:
> Turning the other cheek turns out to have selfish advantages.
> My wife thinks I'm more forgiving than she is, but my motives are purely selfish.
Notes: The parenthetical jab at professors ("professional fundraisers who do a little research on the side") is the same deflation move aimed outward.

## Register note
The most first-person of the four: personal history is the evidence, and the reader's "you" only takes over in the final third. Also the only one quoting a primary source at length — Newton's letter and his biographer — then siding against the scholar ("I'm more sympathetic to Newton"), a rare scholarly-apparatus moment in PG.
