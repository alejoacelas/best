# Life is Short (2016 · ~1,700 words · medium)
https://www.paulgraham.com/vb.html

## Candidate: convert the abstraction into a countable number
What: To make a vague quantity felt, he converts it into a small integer and then holds the integer up against physical objects. The argument is carried by arithmetic, not adjectives.
Quotes:
> You only get 52 weekends with your 2 year old.
> If Christmas-as-magic lasts from say ages 3 to 10, you only get to watch your child experience it 8 times.
> If you had a handful of 8 peanuts, or a shelf of 8 books to choose from, the quantity would definitely seem limited, no matter what your lifespan was.
Notes: The peanuts/books move — testing a number against objects you can picture — is the transferable tweak.

## Candidate: "Ok, so" checkpoint
What: Mid-essay he pauses, restates what's been established in spoken-register shorthand ("Ok, so..."), then asks the next question. It marks a settled sub-conclusion and resets the reader before the next step.
Quotes:
> Ok, so life actually is short. Does it make any difference to know that?
> It has for me.
Notes: Single occurrence here but the identical move appears in "What You Can't Say" ("Ok, so written and spoken..." pattern in Talk too) — a corpus-level pattern.

## Candidate: personal anecdote as the load-bearing evidence
What: The pivotal claims are settled by something that happened to him, told in two or three plain sentences with no scene-setting. The anecdote is the proof, and the most emotionally exposed lines are the shortest.
Quotes:
> Since there didn't seem any way to answer this question, I stopped wondering about it. Then I had kids. That gave me a way to answer the question, and the answer is that life actually is short.
> After my mother died, I wished I'd spent more time with her. I lived as if she'd always be there. And in her typical quiet way she encouraged that illusion. But an illusion it was.
> We had the best time a daddy and a 3 year old ever had.
Notes: Note the inversion "But an illusion it was." — archaic word order used exactly once, at the emotional peak.

## Candidate: junk-food metaphor — one vivid image, then drop it
What: Abstract nouns get a single sensory metaphor, often bodily or domestic, stated flatly as a definition and not elaborated.
Quotes:
> It's the junk food of experience.
> The area under the curve is small, but its shape jabs into your consciousness like a pin.
> The "flow" that imaginative people love so much has a darker cousin that prevents you from pausing to savor life amid the daily slurry of errands and alarms.
Notes: "jabs... like a pin" fuses a math image (area under the curve) with a physical one — mixed registers on purpose.

## Candidate: imperative landing — "Just don't wait."
What: A paragraph of reasoning ends in a bare imperative of two to four words. The essay's final paragraph compresses the whole argument into one imperative sentence plus a flat closing line.
Quotes:
> You don't need to be constantly reminding yourself why you shouldn't wait. Just don't wait.
> Relentlessly prune bullshit, don't wait to do things that matter, and savor the time you have. That's what you do when life is short.
Notes: Closing formula: three-verb summary sentence, then a plain "that's what/that's the" declarative. Highly transferable ending shape.

## Candidate: turning the advice on himself
What: When prescribing, he immediately includes himself among the guilty — a clipped self-implicating fragment or a footnote confession. It converts preaching into company.
Quotes:
> How you live affects how long you live. Most people could do better. Me among them.
> I chose this example deliberately as a note to self. I get attacked a lot online. People tell the craziest lies about me.
> And I have so far done a pretty mediocre job of suppressing the natural human inclination to say "Hey, that's not true!"
Notes: The second and third are from footnote [2] — the footnote is where the confession lives, keeping the main text clean.

## Register note
His most personal register: mother's death, his son turning 7, first-person throughout. Sections are unlabeled; the essay moves by "one more thing" enumeration ("I can think of two more things one does when one doesn't have much of something"). Same mechanics as the argumentative essays, aimed inward.
