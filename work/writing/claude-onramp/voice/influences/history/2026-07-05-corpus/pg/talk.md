# Write Like You Talk (2015 · ~700 words · short)
https://www.paulgraham.com/talk.html

## Candidate: hand over the deliverable in sentence one
What: The essay opens by giving away the entire payload — trick named, benefit stated — in a single colon-hinged sentence. Everything after is demonstration and edge cases.
Quotes:
> Here's a simple trick for getting more people to read what you write: write in spoken language.
Notes: Single occurrence per essay by nature, but it's the standard opening of his short how-to pieces; the close then restates it ("just don't let a sentence through unless it's the way you'd say it to a friend").

## Candidate: the friend test
What: The recurring yardstick is a concrete social scene — saying the sentence to a friend — and he re-invokes it at every judgment point. "You'd feel like an idiot" makes the standard visceral rather than stylistic.
Quotes:
> You'd feel like an idiot using "pen" instead of "write" in a conversation with a friend.
> But just imagine calling Picasso "the mercurial Spaniard" when talking to a friend.
> And it's so easy to do: just don't let a sentence through unless it's the way you'd say it to a friend.
Notes: Same test phrase ("to a friend") repeated five times across 700 words — repetition as the essay's spine, not a flaw.

## Candidate: one-sentence-paragraph aphorism
What: A definitional metaphor gets a paragraph to itself — no lead-in, no follow-up. The white space around it does the italicizing.
Quotes:
> Informal language is the athletic clothing of ideas.
Notes: Single occurrence, but so structurally deliberate it suggests a pattern: coin the X-is-the-Y-of-Z line, isolate it.

## Candidate: ok-so question hinge
What: Mid-essay pivot: concede the established point in spoken shorthand, pose the next question, then answer it in a conditional one-liner that names the reader's goal.
Quotes:
> Ok, so written and spoken language are different. Does that make written language worse?
> If you want people to read and understand what you write, yes.
Notes: Identical machinery to "Ok, so life actually is short. Does it make any difference to know that?" in Life is Short.

## Candidate: exceptions listed, then dismissed
What: Before the prescription, he pre-empts overreach with "I'm not saying...", grants two or three genuine exceptions in one breath, and closes the door with a "but for nearly everyone" clause.
Quotes:
> I'm not saying spoken language always works best. Poetry is as much music as text, so you can say things you wouldn't say in conversation.
> But for nearly everyone else, spoken language is better.
Notes: The exception list itself carries a jab ("or at the more bogus end of the humanities") — concessions double as humor slots.

## Candidate: hear the prose — sound metaphors for editing
What: Writing quality is described in auditory-physical terms: sentences clank, raise eyebrows, get read out loud. The editing advice is staged as listening, which enacts the essay's thesis.
Quotes:
> When you write something you wouldn't say, you'll hear the clank as it hits the page.
> Even one sentence of this would raise eyebrows in conversation. And yet people write whole books of it.
> Before I publish a new essay, I read it out loud and fix everything that doesn't sound like conversation.
Notes: "And yet people write whole books of it." — the short incredulous follow-on sentence is its own micro-move.

## Register note
A 700-word how-to whose prose is its own proof: the essay about writing like you talk is written like talk, and he points at the gap himself ("People often tell me how much my essays sound like me talking"). No sections, no footnotes — the shortest, most instructional register in the set.
