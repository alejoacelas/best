# Hackers and Painters (2003 · ~5600 words · long)
https://www.paulgraham.com/hp.html

## Candidate: simile as verdict
What: The judgment on something is delivered by comparison rather than adjective — one vivid, often domestic or historical image that carries the whole evaluation. The insulting ones are the funniest.
Quotes:
> Computer science is a grab bag of tenuously related areas thrown together by an accident of history, like Yugoslavia.
> We need a language that lets us scribble and smudge and smear, not a language where you have to sit with a teacup of types balanced on your knee and make polite conversation with a strict old aunt of a compiler.
> There is always a big time lag in prestige. It's like light from a distant star.
Notes: Also "It's as relaxing as painting a wall." and "like a thousand barely audible voices all singing in tune." Highest simile density of the three pieces; each simile replaces a paragraph of argument.

## Candidate: reversal aphorism
What: A quotable line built on symmetry — two clauses with mirrored or crossed terms, or a phrase folded back on itself. The syntax does the wit.
Quotes:
> So hackers start original, and get good, and scientists start good, and get original.
> Big companies win by sucking less than other big companies.
> This sounds like a paradox, but a great painting has to be better than it has to be.
Notes: Also "It should be a pencil, not a pen." These close sections; he builds the argument first and mints the coin last.

## Candidate: unflattering self-anecdote as proof
What: The evidence for a claim is a story where he looks obsessive, mistaken, or miserable — the embarrassment is what makes it credible.
Quotes:
> I remember sitting back in the dentist's chair, waiting for the drill, and feeling like I was on vacation.
> I'm not claiming I write great software, but I know that when it comes to code I behave in a way that would make me eligible for prescription drugs if I approached everyday life the same way.
> For a long time I felt bad about this, just as I once felt bad that I didn't hold my pencil the way they taught me to in elementary school.
Notes: Also the abrupt confession pivot: "Boy, was I wrong." Every major turn in the essay is anchored to a first-person memory (Yahoo, Viaweb, grad school).

## Candidate: fragment punch
What: A grammatical fragment or two-word sentence lands right after a longer setup — the rhythm break is the emphasis.
Quotes:
> Not Leonardo. How hard he worked on part of a painting didn't depend at all on how closely he expected anyone to look at it.
> He was like Michael Jordan. Relentless.
> It doesn't necessarily mean being self-sacrificing. Far from it.
Notes: Also "Ditto for hacking." Maybe 5 occurrences in 5600 words — a spice, not a staple.

## Candidate: definition by contrast pair
What: A fuzzy concept gets defined by splitting it into two opposed roles or halves in one balanced sentence — what/how, money/love, scientist/engineer.
Quotes:
> It falls between what and how: architects decide what to do, and engineers figure out how to do it.
> Universities and research labs force hackers to be scientists, and companies force them to be engineers.
> More generally, it means that you have one kind of work you do for money, and another for love.
Notes: The pair is always parallel in syntax; the definition is the sentence, no elaboration needed.

## Candidate: hedge on a sweeping claim
What: The boldest generalizations carry a one-word epistemic tag — "probably", "I suspect", "I think", "As far as I know" — placed so the claim stays sweeping while the confidence is calibrated.
Quotes:
> Microsoft themselves did this at the start. So did Apple. And Hewlett-Packard. I suspect almost every successful startup has.
> Empathy is probably the single most important difference between a good hacker and a great one.
> As far as I know, when painters worked together on a painting, they never worked on the same parts.
Notes: The hedge licenses the audacity: he can claim more because he marks it as suspicion, not proof.

## Candidate: footnote as second punchline
What: Footnotes here aren't just citations — several are freestanding jokes or extra jabs that would have broken the paragraph's stride.
Quotes:
> What you learn about programming in college is much like what you learn about books or clothes or dating: what bad taste you had in high school.
> Except text editors and compilers. Hackers don't need empathy to design these, because they are themselves typical users.
> Well, almost. They overshot the available RAM somewhat, causing much inconvenient disk swapping, but this could be fixed within a few months by buying an additional disk drive.
Notes: A reader who skips the notes misses some of the best lines — the footnote is a reward, not an apparatus.

## Register note
Derived from a lecture, and it shows: loosely linked sections with no headers, more jokes per page than the standard PG essay, and a mock-dialogue bit ("High-level language? Compiler? Object code?") that reads as a stage routine.
