# Do Things That Don't Scale (2013 · ~3,200 words · medium)
https://www.paulgraham.com/ds.html

## Candidate: state the belief, then "Actually"
What: Opens a point by voicing the common (wrong) belief in the reader's own frame, then pivots to the correction — often with a bare "Actually" or a not-X-but-Y recast of the question itself.
Quotes:
> Actually startups take off because the founders make them take off.
> The question to ask about an early stage startup is not "is this company taking over the world?" but "how big could this company get if the founders did the right things?"
> It's not the product that should be insanely great, but the experience of being your user.
Notes: The whole essay opens this way (mousetrap belief → "Actually"). The pivot sentence is always shorter than the belief it corrects.

## Candidate: you, imperative
What: Advice is delivered as second-person commands in very short sentences — two or three in a row — rather than as recommendations about founders in the third person.
Quotes:
> You can't wait for users to come to you. You have to go out and get them.
> That would be a great problem to have. See if you can make it happen.
> Tim Cook doesn't send you a hand-written note after you buy a laptop. He can't. But you can.
Notes: The Tim Cook run is the extreme case: three sentences of 12, 2, and 3 words. The clipped length is doing as much work as the "you."

## Candidate: mechanical metaphor
What: Abstractions about startups are grounded in physical machinery or physics — cranks, fires, projectiles — introduced plainly ("A good metaphor would be", "It's like") and dropped after a sentence or two.
Quotes:
> A good metaphor would be the cranks that car engines had before they got electric starters.
> It's like keeping a fire contained at first to get it really hot before adding more logs.
> I occasionally meet founders who seem to believe startups are projectiles rather than powered aircraft, and that they'll make it big if and only if they're launched with sufficient initial velocity.
Notes: Also "They're like someone looking at a newborn baby..." and the pop-culture variant "You can be ornery when you're Scotty, but not when you're Kirk." One metaphor per concept, never mixed.

## Candidate: count the reasons, name the vices
What: Diagnoses why people get something wrong by explicitly enumerating causes ("Three reasons, I think") — and the diagnosis is often a blunt pair of vices stated like a chemical formula.
Quotes:
> Why do we have to teach startups this? Why is it counterintuitive for founders? Three reasons, I think.
> There are two reasons founders resist going out and recruiting users individually. One is a combination of shyness and laziness.
> So why do founders think launches matter? A combination of solipsism and laziness.
Notes: "A combination of X and laziness" appears twice — the reused frame is itself a move. The count promises structure the following paragraphs then visibly deliver ("One is... Another reason... But perhaps the biggest thing...").

## Candidate: one vivid prop per anecdote
What: Each named-company anecdote is compressed to a few sentences and hung on a single concrete physical detail or line of dialogue — a laptop, rolly bags, screws — which carries the whole memory.
Quotes:
> When anyone agreed to try Stripe they'd say "Right then, give me your laptop" and set them up on the spot.
> When I remember the Airbnbs during YC, I picture them with rolly bags, because when they showed up for tuesday dinners they'd always just flown back from somewhere.
> Eric Migicovsky of Pebble said one of the things he learned was "how valuable it was to source good screws." Who knew?
Notes: The "Who knew?" tag after the screws quote is the one-word-beat pattern from his memoir showing up in a how-to essay.

## Candidate: the sworn-testimony sentence
What: A one-sentence paragraph phrased as personal testimony ("I have never once seen...") stands alone as a verdict — the isolation and the witness framing give it the force of data.
Quotes:
> I have never once seen a startup lured down a blind alley by trying too hard to make their initial users happy.
Notes: Single occurrence as a standalone paragraph, but so structurally deliberate it suggests a pattern: when he wants maximum weight, he gives a claim its own paragraph and stakes his experience on it.

## Candidate: hedge inside the sweep
What: Sweeping claims carry a small embedded hedge — "usually," "probably," "I think," "nearly" — that concedes the exception in passing without slowing the sentence, which makes the generalization feel measured rather than weaker.
Quotes:
> There may be a handful that just grew by themselves, but usually it takes some sort of push to get them going.
> The need to do something unscalably laborious to get started is so nearly universal that it might be a good idea to stop thinking of startup ideas as scalars.
> If they hadn't gone through that phase, they probably wouldn't have sold $10 million worth of watches when they did go on Kickstarter.
Notes: The hedge is never a separate sentence or clause of throat-clearing; it's one word lodged mid-claim.

## Candidate: parallel conditional close
What: The essay ends on a run of parallel "If you have to X... you'll Y" sentences, the last flagged "And most importantly" — an accelerating cadence instead of a summary paragraph.
Quotes:
> If you have to be aggressive about user acquisition when you're small, you'll probably still be aggressive when you're big.
> And most importantly, if you have to work hard to delight users when you only have a handful of them, you'll keep doing it when you have a lot.
Notes: Single occurrence (it's the ending), but a recognizable PG close: parallelism supplies the sense of conclusion so no "in conclusion" is needed.

## Register note
The canonical PG how-to essay: one-word section headers (Recruit, Fragile, Delight, Fire...), heavy second person, and the "we at YC" authority stance ("we advise founders," "startups we've funded") grounding every claim in observed cases rather than theory.
