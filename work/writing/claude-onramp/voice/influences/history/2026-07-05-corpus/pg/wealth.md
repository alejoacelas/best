# How to Make Wealth (2004 · ~8,700 words · long)
https://www.paulgraham.com/wealth.html

## Candidate: fake-precise math, then hedge the number, not the structure
What: He runs a back-of-the-envelope calculation to an absurdly specific figure, then explicitly disowns the figure while standing by the reasoning — precision as rhetoric, honesty as the safety net.
Quotes:
> Combine all these multipliers, and I'm claiming you could be 36 times more productive than you're expected to be in a random corporate job.
> Like all back-of-the-envelope calculations, this one has a lot of wiggle room. I wouldn't try to defend the actual numbers. But I stand by the structure of the calculation.
> I'm not claiming the multiplier is precisely 36, but it is certainly more than 10, and probably rarely as high as 100.
Notes: Recurs later: "If the mean is 30x, the median is probably zero." The bracketing hedge ("more than 10... rarely as high as 100") is itself quantified.

## Candidate: definition by contrast
What: Key terms get defined by splitting them from their near-synonym, stated as a flat equation or negation, then re-stated with the terms reversed a few paragraphs later so the distinction sticks.
Quotes:
> Wealth is not the same thing as money.
> Wealth is what you want, not money. But if wealth is the important thing, why does everyone talk about making money?
> What is technology? It's technique. It's the way we all do things.
> You don't want small in the sense of a village, but small in the sense of an all-star team.
Notes: The section headings do it too — "Smallness = Measurement," "Technology = Leverage." Definitions-as-equations organize the whole essay.

## Candidate: the worked analogy
What: A simile isn't dropped and abandoned — it gets a full paragraph of internal mechanics, with the parts of the image mapped one-to-one onto the argument, sometimes returning later in the essay.
Quotes:
> A big company is like a giant galley driven by a thousand rowers. Two things keep the speed of the galley down. One is that individual rowers don't see any result from working harder.
> A startup is like a mosquito. A bear can absorb a hit and a crab is armored against one, but a mosquito is designed for one thing: to score. No energy is wasted on defense.
> Suppose you are a little, nimble guy being chased by a big, fat, bully. You open a door and find yourself in a staircase. Do you go up or down? I say up.
Notes: The galley returns for two more paragraphs (ten rowers in a boat, then the ten best rowers); the guerilla image returns fifty lines after its first use. Transfer: pick one analogy and mine it, don't scatter five.

## Candidate: second-person hypothetical
What: Abstract economics is taught by putting "you" inside a small scenario — you own a car, you make violins, you're a hacker in your mid twenties. The essay opens this way and keeps returning to it.
Quotes:
> If you wanted to get rich, how would you do it? I think your best bet would be to start or join a startup.
> Suppose you own a beat-up old car. Instead of sitting on your butt next summer, you could spend the time restoring your car to pristine condition.
> If you make violins, and none of the local farmers wants one, how will you eat?
Notes: The "you" is always doing something concrete, never "consider the case where you..."

## Candidate: the kicker close
What: Sections and the essay itself end on a short punchline sentence — an aphorism, a joke, or both — after longer explanatory sentences have done the work. The rhythm is long, long, snap.
Quotes:
> If starting a startup were easy, everyone would do it.
> A McDonald's franchise is controlled by rules so precise that it is practically a piece of software. Write once, run everywhere.
> Let the nerds keep their lunch money, and you rule the world.
Notes: The last one closes the essay by cashing in an image planted 80 lines earlier — kicker and callback at once.

## Candidate: footnotes as pressure valve
What: The footnotes hold what the tight main text won't: the best jokes, provocations, and mini-essays. Digressions get exiled rather than cut, so the body stays lean without losing the material.
Quotes:
> You know what kind of business you should start if you like the outdoors? One to recover data from crashed hard disks.
> This is why hackers give you such a baleful stare as they turn from their screen to answer your question. Inside their heads a giant house of cards is tottering.
> Internally most companies are run like Communist states. If you believe in free markets, why not turn your company into one?
Notes: Eleven footnotes; several are funnier and more quotable than the body text they hang off. Footnote 9 is a whole life-advice essay in miniature.

## Candidate: self-deprecating founder anecdote
What: Viaweb stories are told against himself — the mistakes plain, the adverbs comic — so the war stories certify the advice without bragging.
Quotes:
> We had a chance to do this, and stupidly, as we then thought, let it slip by. After that we became comically eager to sell.
> When VCs asked us how long it would take another startup to duplicate our software, we used to reply that they probably wouldn't be able to at all. I think this made us seem naive, or liars.
> Viaweb came close to tanking several times. Our trajectory was like a sine wave.
Notes: Same credibility mechanism as the D-table admission in "Nerds": confess the low point first, then generalize.

## Candidate: planted phrase, repeated verbatim
What: A concrete image or sentence is introduced once, then re-invoked word-for-word later ("Remember that...?") to bind distant parts of the argument together — the repetition is exact, not paraphrased.
Quotes:
> Remember that magic machine that could make you cars and cook you dinner and so on? It would not be so useful if it delivered your dinner to a random location in central Asia.
> Starting or joining a startup is thus as close as most people can get to saying to one's boss, I want to work ten times as hard, so please pay me ten times as much.
> They are like the corporate boss that you can't go to and say, I want to work ten times as hard, so please pay me ten times a much.
Notes: The "ten times as hard" sentence appears three times across the essay; "the nerds' lunch money" appears twice and supplies the final line. Callbacks are his large-scale glue.

## Register note
A book chapter (from Hackers & Painters), so unusually it has named section headings ("The Pie Fallacy," "Millions, not Billions") and a footnote apparatus — the most lecture-like of the three, sustained second person throughout, economics explained from first principles with jokes as rest stops.
