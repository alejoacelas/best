# Keep Your Identity Small (2009 · ~800 words · short)
https://www.paulgraham.com/identity.html

## Candidate: discovery in real time
What: Narrates the reasoning as it happened — "realized today", "then it struck me" — so the essay reads as a live derivation the reader watches, not a finished position defended.
Quotes:
> I finally realized today why politics and religion yield such uniquely useless discussions.
> Then it struck me: this is the problem with politics too.
Notes: The timestamps ("today") are doing work: they license tentativeness and make the conclusion feel earned.

## Candidate: propose and kill
What: Raises the obvious rival explanation in full, in its most plausible form, then executes it with a blunt two-word verdict and one counterexample.
Quotes:
> One possible explanation is that they deal with questions that have no definite answers, so there's no back pressure on people's opinions.
> But this isn't true. There are certainly some political questions that have definite answers, like how much a new government policy will cost.
Notes: The kill sentence is always short; the counterexample always concrete.

## Candidate: mundane pairing for deflation
What: Deflates grand topics by pairing them with deliberately trivial ones in the same list — the bathos is the argument that topic isn't what matters.
Quotes:
> Why does this happen with religion and not with Javascript or baking or other topics people talk about on forums?
> And there are other topics that might seem harmless, like the relative merits of Ford and Chevy pickup trucks, that you couldn't safely talk about with others .
Notes: This is most of the essay's humor; no jokes are told, the juxtapositions do it.

## Candidate: mid-claim hedge
What: A hedge dropped inside the sentence, mid-flight ("if it's right", "probably"), which lets him keep building on the theory without pausing to defend it.
Quotes:
> The most intriguing thing about this theory, if it's right, is that it explains not merely which kinds of discussions to avoid, but how to have better ideas.
> For example, a discussion about a battle that included citizens of one or more of the countries involved would probably degenerate into a political argument. But a discussion today about a battle that took place in the Bronze Age probably wouldn't.
Notes: The "if it's right" appears exactly where a reader would object; it buys passage, not modesty.

## Candidate: the sting close
What: Sentences and the essay itself end on a compressed sting — a short, quotable verdict with no wind-down after it.
Quotes:
> But on religion everyone's an expert.
> By definition they're partisan.
> The more labels you have for yourself, the dumber they make you.
Notes: The final one closes the essay cold — no summary paragraph after the maxim.

## Candidate: homely analogy quarantined in a footnote
What: The most vivid, most extended analogies live in the footnotes, built from household objects and bureaucratic forms, keeping the main text abstract and fast.
Quotes:
> But arguably that is more of a placeholder than an actual label—like putting NMI on a form that asks for your middle initial—because it doesn't commit you to believing anything in particular.
> Considering yourself a scientist is equivalent to putting a sign in a cupboard saying "this cupboard must be kept empty." Yes, strictly speaking, you're putting something in the cupboard, but not in the ordinary sense.
Notes: Both from footnote 2; the other footnote hides a simile too ("like a core going critical").

## Register note
Early, compact PG: a single argument derived start to finish with no section breaks, no imperatives until the final paragraph, and the "you" mostly generic rather than direct address. Closest of the four to a proof written as prose.
