# Crazy New Ideas (2021 · ~1,350 words · medium)
https://www.paulgraham.com/newideas.html

## Candidate: chain of short inferences — "And yet... That suggests..."
What: Deductions advance one clipped sentence at a time, each starting with a plain connective (And yet, That suggests, So, Which means). The logic is fully exposed; no step hides inside a subordinate clause.
Quotes:
> If the person proposing the idea is reasonable, then they know how implausible it sounds. And yet they're proposing it anyway. That suggests they know something you don't.
> Even the people who have the new ideas undervalue them initially. Which means that before they reach the stage of proposing them publicly, they've already subjected them to an excessively strict filter.
Notes: Sentence-initial "And"/"Which means" fragments are doing paragraph-level argument work — the most imitable single tweak here.

## Candidate: open on a first-person fear
What: The essay begins with his own emotional stance toward the topic — what he'd be afraid to say — before any general claim. The "I" is a datapoint the argument then explains.
Quotes:
> There's one kind of opinion I'd be very afraid to express publicly.
> If someone I knew to be both a domain expert and a reasonable person proposed an idea that sounded preposterous, I'd be very reluctant to say "That will never work."
> And yet there are clearly a lot of people who don't share my fear of dismissing new ideas.
Notes: He re-invokes "my fear" at the pivot — the personal opening becomes the hinge for the second half's question (why don't others share it?).

## Candidate: hatchling vs. eagle — paired concrete images
What: Abstractions get a single vivid, physical comparison, sometimes as a matched pair (weak thing vs. strong thing). Images come from animals, toys, aviation — kid-simple domains for grown-up claims.
Quotes:
> It's a mere hatchling. Received wisdom is a full-grown eagle by comparison.
> It doesn't just affect the way we think; it is the Lego blocks we build thoughts out of.
> And even they usually have to suppress their intuitions at first, like a pilot flying through cloud who has to trust his instruments over his sense of balance.
Notes: One image per abstraction, then straight back to argument — same discipline as the similes in What You Can't Say.

## Candidate: interrogate the opposition — "Are they mistaken, or are you?"
What: Objections and puzzles are staged as strings of direct questions, often forcing a binary, then answered by enumeration ("One reason... Another reason..."). The questions are aimed at the reader's own behavior.
Quotes:
> Why has this smart and reasonable person proposed an idea that seems so wrong? Are they mistaken, or are you? One of you has to be.
> Why do they do it? Why risk looking like a jerk now and a fool later, instead of just reserving judgement?
Notes: "One of you has to be." — three-beat verdict fragment right after the binary question; the rhythm is the point.

## Candidate: footnote as scope valve
What: The main text states the principle cleanly; the footnote carries the qualification or the spicier aside that would clog or inflame the argument if inline.
Quotes:
> I'm not claiming this principle extends much beyond math, engineering, and the hard sciences. In politics, for example, crazy-sounding ideas generally are as bad as they sound.
> This is one reason people with a touch of Asperger's may have an advantage in discovering new ideas. They're always flying on instruments.
Notes: Both from the notes ([2], [4]). Note [4] even reuses the body's pilot metaphor as a punchline — footnotes get jokes too.

## Candidate: ranked put-downs — "the lowest form of all"
What: He escalates a parallel structure across consecutive sentences, repeating the frame and tightening it, so the last item lands as a verdict.
Quotes:
> The lowest form of dismissal is mere factionalism: to automatically dismiss any idea associated with the opposing faction. The lowest form of all is to dismiss an idea because of who proposed it.
Notes: Single occurrence — but the shape (repeat the superlative frame, out-do it) is a recognizable aphorism-builder worth naming.

## Candidate: end by recruiting the reader
What: The close shifts from analysis to instruction in second person, then adds a final one-line reward for compliance. Also mid-close: a short fragment of pathos before the ask.
Quotes:
> Just look for a reasonable domain expert proposing something that sounds wrong.
> Having new ideas is a lonely business. Only those who've tried it know how lonely.
> And if you help them, you'll probably learn something in the process.
Notes: "Only those who've tried it know how lonely." — verbless echo fragment; the repetition of "lonely" replaces any adjective pile-up.

## Register note
Late-period PG: shorter paragraphs, heavier hedging ("probably", "I think", "something like"), five footnotes on 1,350 words. Warmer close than the early essays — it ends on kindness ("If you're nice, as well as wise") rather than a challenge.
