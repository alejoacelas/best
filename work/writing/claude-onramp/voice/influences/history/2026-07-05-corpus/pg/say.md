# What You Can't Say (2004 · ~5,500 words · long)
https://www.paulgraham.com/say.html

## Candidate: ask the question, answer it in a fragment
What: He stages a dialogue with himself — poses a short question, then answers it immediately, often in a clipped fragment or verdict. The question does the transition work between paragraphs; the terse answer sounds like a ruling.
Quotes:
> Is our time any different? To anyone who has read any amount of history, the answer is almost certainly no.
> Do we have no Galileos? Not likely.
> Just start listing ideas at random? Yes, because they won't really be random.
Notes: Very high frequency — a dozen-plus instances; also "Why do they do this? Because they don't think it's fitting that kids should use the whole language."

## Candidate: aphorism by mirrored inversion
What: He builds quotable lines by repeating a structure with one term flipped or doubled back on itself — parallel clauses where the second inverts or escalates the first. These land at paragraph ends.
Quotes:
> Fashion is mistaken for good design; moral fashion is mistaken for good.
> Almost certainly, there is something wrong with you if you don't think things you don't dare say out loud.
> Argue with idiots, and you become an idiot.
Notes: Also the closer "How can you see the wave, when you're the water?" — the mirror is the whole joke.

## Candidate: one absurdly concrete simile per abstraction
What: Each abstract claim gets exactly one short, visual, slightly comic simile — never a run of them. The image is homely or physical (clothes, sports, terrain), which makes the abstraction feel checked against the world.
Quotes:
> The age of consent fluctuates like hemlines.
> They're as unhappy on the territory of humor as a mounted knight on a skating rink.
> Like skirmishers in an ancient army, you want to avoid directly engaging the main body of the enemy's troops.
Notes: Transfers well as a tweak: after a hard abstract paragraph, spend one sentence on a single concrete image, then move on.

## Candidate: graded hedges — "I suspect" / "probably" / "odds are"
What: Hedges are not throat-clearing; they mark exactly how confident he is, and the boldest claims get the hedge so he can say them at all. "Odds are" states near-certainty casually; "I suspect" flags his most heretical guesses.
Quotes:
> I suspect the statements that make people maddest are those they worry might be true.
> Odds are you just think what you're told.
> I suspect the biggest source of moral taboos will turn out to be power struggles in which one side only barely has the upper hand.
Notes: The hedge licenses the edgy content — an unhedged version of these sentences would read as arrogant, a hedged mild claim as mush.

## Candidate: pre-empt the misreading with "I'm not saying"
What: Right after a claim that could be read as more extreme than intended, he inserts an explicit disclaimer sentence stating what he is not arguing, then restates the narrower claim. Keeps the strong claim while cutting off the strawman.
Quotes:
> I'm not arguing for or against this idea here.
> I'm not saying that struggles are never about ideas, just that they will always be made to seem to be about ideas, whether they are or not.
> I admit it seems cowardly to keep quiet.
Notes: The third is the confessional variant — conceding the objection before the reader raises it.

## Candidate: second-person thought experiment
What: He hands the reader a hypothetical and runs them through it in "you" — a time machine, an invented taboo, a swap of upbringings. The reader is the test subject, not an observer, which makes the argument feel like something that happened to them.
Quotes:
> Suppose in the future there is a movement to ban the color yellow. Proposals to paint anything yellow are denounced as "yellowist", as is anyone suspected of liking the color.
> If you could travel back in a time machine, one thing would be true no matter where you went: you'd have to watch what you said.
> Take a label — "sexist", for example — and try to think of some ideas that would be called that. Then for each ask, might this be true?
Notes: The yellowist world is sustained across two later sections — an invented example reused as shared vocabulary.

## Candidate: staccato verdict after a long run
What: After several long explanatory sentences, he drops to a two-to-five-word sentence that passes judgment. The rhythm break is the emphasis; no intensifiers needed.
Quotes:
> We did. And we had no idea how silly we looked.
> They always do. By now they're mostly used ironically.
> Was it right or wrong? Ideally, no one got far enough to ask that.
Notes: The essay's last paragraph is almost all staccato: "Always be questioning. That's the only defence. What can't you say? And why?" — he closes on questions, not a summary.

## Register note
The long-essay register: named sections, 17 footnote markers, a systematic "here are five methods" structure underneath the conversational surface. More scholarly apparatus (Galileo, Milton, Ludendorff) than his short pieces, but sentence mechanics stay identical.
