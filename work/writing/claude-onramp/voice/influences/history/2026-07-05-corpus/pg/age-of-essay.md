# The Age of the Essay (2004 · ~4500 words · long)
https://www.paulgraham.com/essay.html

## Candidate: question, then walk through the door
What: A short rhetorical question opens a paragraph or section and the very next sentence starts answering it. The question does the transition work a topic sentence would; the answer often begins with "Well," or "To answer that".
Quotes:
> How did things get this way? To answer that we have to go back almost a thousand years.
> If all you want to do is figure things out, why do you need to write anything, though? Why not just sit and think? Well, there precisely is Montaigne's great discovery.
> So what's interesting? For me, interesting means surprise.
Notes: 8+ occurrences; the essay's whole structure is question-driven. Transfers as a paragraph-opener move, one per section, not every paragraph.

## Candidate: flat snap close
What: Paragraphs end on a very short declarative sentence (3–7 words) that lands the point and stops dead — often after longer, winding sentences.
Quotes:
> Most of what ends up in my essays I only thought of when I sat down to write them. That's why I write them.
> Kids are the ones who have it all figured out. They're just mistaken.
> Who are you to write about x? You are whatever you wrote.
Notes: The snap is earned by the buildup; the short sentence restates nothing, it flips or compresses.

## Candidate: domestic simile for an abstraction
What: An abstract claim gets pinned to one homely, physical comparison — apartments, food, drawing — stated as "just as X, Y" or "like X". Rare enough per essay that each one lands.
Quotes:
> Just as inviting people over forces you to clean up your apartment, writing something that other people will read forces you to think well.
> They're like a food that's not merely healthy, but counteracts the unhealthy effects of things you've already eaten.
> The effect was rather as if we were visited by beings from another solar system.
Notes: Also "like an illustrator inking over a pencil drawing". One simile per abstract claim, always concrete, never mixed.

## Candidate: self as specimen
What: He uses his own writing process and past failures as the evidence for the claim — including the essay currently being written, examined mid-flight.
Quotes:
> At one point in this essay I found that after following a certain thread I ran out of ideas. I had to go back seven paragraphs and start over in another direction.
> At sixteen I was about as observant as a lump of rock.
> I write down things that surprise me in notebooks. I never actually get around to reading them and using what I've written, but I do tend to reproduce the same thoughts later.
Notes: The anecdotes are unflattering by default; the self-deprecation buys the reader's trust for the general claim that follows.

## Candidate: slang drop
What: Mid-argument, in an otherwise bookish register, a sudden colloquial deflation — an interjection, a stumble, or a crude word — that mimics live speech.
Quotes:
> Oy. So I'm going to try to give the other side of the story: what an essay really is, and how you write one.
> the conclusion-- uh, what is the conclusion? I was never sure about that in high school.
> But none of us had the balls at the time to hypothesize that it was, in fact, all a mistake.
Notes: Also "(how hard can it be?)". Works because it's scarce — a few drops per essay against a formal baseline.

## Candidate: footnote as pressure valve
What: Scholarly caveats, source citations, and admissions of simplification are shunted into numbered notes so the main text keeps its conversational stride.
Quotes:
> I'm compressing the story a bit.
> Trevor Blackwell points out that this isn't strictly true, because the outside edges of curves erode faster.
> I'm thinking of Oresme (c. 1323-82). But it's hard to pick a date, because there was a sudden drop-off in scholarship just as Europeans finished assimilating classical science.
Notes: The body text makes bold historical claims cleanly; the footnote absorbs the "actually it's more complicated". Directly transferable to any hedge-heavy draft.

## Candidate: labeled guess
What: Speculative claims get an explicit epistemic label — "My guess", "I have a hunch", "seems to have been", "may be" — so the reader can tell live conjecture from settled fact without the sentence going soft.
Quotes:
> My guess is that it doesn't matter-- that anything can be interesting if you get deeply enough into it.
> I have a hunch you want to pay attention not just to things that seem wrong, but things that seem wrong in a humorous way.
> What tipped the scales, at least in the US, seems to have been the idea that professors should do research as well as teach.
Notes: The hedge sits on the frame ("my guess is"), never inside the claim itself — the claim stays sharp.

## Register note
Self-referential piece — an essay about essays that demonstrates its own advice, down to the closing line ("And that's certainly not something I realized when I started writing this."). Sectioned with one-word headers (Mods, Trying, Surprise); more historical narrative than the standard PG essay.
