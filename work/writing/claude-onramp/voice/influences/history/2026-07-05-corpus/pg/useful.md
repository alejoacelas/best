# How to Write Usefully (2020 · ~2,800 words · medium)
https://www.paulgraham.com/useful.html

## Candidate: rule, then tiny example
What: Every abstract rule is immediately followed by a miniature concrete instance — often self-referential, pointing at the essay's own sentences as the demonstration.
Quotes:
> For example, it's more useful to say that Pike's Peak is near the middle of Colorado than merely somewhere in Colorado. But if I say it's in the exact middle of Colorado, I've now gone too far, because it's a bit east of the middle.
> Something you're sure of, you can state baldly with no qualification at all, as I did the four components of useful writing.
> For example, I added condition (c) in "A Way to Detect Bias" after readers pointed out that I'd omitted it.
Notes: The example is never a case study — one sentence, then back to the argument. The self-referential ones ("as I did...") make the essay its own evidence.

## Candidate: equation aphorism
What: The most compressed claims take the form "X is Y" or "X and Y are the same thing" — a definition posing as a discovery, usually landing at a paragraph's end.
Quotes:
> Useful writing is bold, but true.
> Brevity is the diction of command.
> I think we just have to face the fact that elegance and curtness are two names for the same thing.
Notes: Also "publication bias is the way to go" — taking a term with a fixed valence and flipping its sign is a sub-move of this.

## Candidate: the hedge as content
What: Qualifications aren't apologies; they carry information about degree and scope, and he theorizes this explicitly while doing it — "I think," "probably," "sort of true" placed with visible deliberateness.
Quotes:
> Which I realize is almost awkwardly reductive, but nonetheless true.
> And it's true that "I think x" is a weaker statement than simply "x." Which is exactly why you need "I think." You need it to express your degree of certainty.
> I'm not going to try to explore the structure of qualification here. It's probably more complex than the whole topic of writing usefully.
Notes: The essay is partly a defense of hedging, written in hedges. Directly transferable: a hedge should change what the sentence claims, or be cut.

## Candidate: domestic simile for mental process
What: Invisible processes of thinking and revising get physical, homely vehicles — briars, vegetables, a manual transmission — each mapped precisely, several given a twist in a trailing fragment.
Quotes:
> They become like briars that catch your sleeve as you walk past.
> You're like a parent saying to a child "we can sit here all night till you eat your vegetables." Except you're the child too.
> These two counterbalance each other, like the accelerator and clutch in a car with a manual transmission.
Notes: Also "It seems inelegant, like a program that's too long." The "Except you're the child too" twist — simile, then a correction to the simile — is the memorable variant.

## Candidate: the "Which" fragment
What: A sentence fragment opening with "Which" (or "Except," "Or") attaches an afterthought to the previous sentence as its own beat — the written equivalent of a raised eyebrow after the claim.
Quotes:
> Which is exactly why you need "I think."
> Which means of course that it's not a rectangle, but a sort of ragged comb, like a Riemann sum.
> Except you're the child too.
Notes: Frequent here; the fragment always modifies or complicates what preceded it, never adds new subject matter.

## Candidate: coin it, then use the coinage
What: A trick gets attributed to a named real person, compressed into a capitalized name ("the Morris technique"), and then the name is used later as established vocabulary — the essay builds its own jargon and immediately spends it.
Quotes:
> I learned it from my friend Robert Morris, who has a horror of saying anything dumb. His trick is not to say anything unless he's sure it's worth hearing.
> And here, as with correctness and importance, you can use the Morris technique to ensure that you will.
> Plus if you've used the Morris technique, your writing will seem quite confident.
Notes: Same move as "the Pie Fallacy" in How to Make Wealth. The named-friend attribution doubles as evidence and as warmth.

## Candidate: concede, then flip
What: An objection is granted in a short flat sentence ("That's sort of true") before the turn — the concession is real but measured, and the flip lands harder for it.
Quotes:
> That's sort of true. It will be invulnerable to valid attacks. But in practice that's little consolation.
> Some say that qualifications weaken writing. For example, that you should never begin a sentence in an essay with "I think," because if you're saying it, then of course you think it.
> That may seem strange now that the trend is to publish every random thought, but it worked for me.
Notes: Also "In fact you constantly admit you're wrong. You just do it before publishing instead of after." — the flip often relocates the objection rather than denying it.

## Candidate: question open, vista close
What: The essay opens by asking the question it will answer, in plain words, and closes not with a summary but by widening the frame — the last paragraphs promise territory rather than restate conclusions.
Quotes:
> What should an essay be? Many people would say persuasive. That's what a lot of us were taught essays should be. But I think we can aim for something more ambitious: that an essay should be useful.
> How many essays are there left to write though? The answer to that question is probably the most exciting thing I've learned about essay writing. Nearly all of them are left to write.
> If most essays are still unwritten, most such ideas are still undiscovered.
Notes: The final sentence is a mirrored-pair aphorism doing double duty as the vista. One essay's evidence for the shape, but both other pieces open on a question or hypothetical too.

## Register note
Late-period and meta: an essay about his essay method that performs each rule as it states it (bald statements where he's sure, "perhapses" where he isn't). Sections are separated by bare rules, not headings; only two footnotes, one of them a single-sentence image ("Put railings on the balconies, but don't put bars on the windows.").
