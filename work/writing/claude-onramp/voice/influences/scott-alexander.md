# Scott Alexander — 20 Slate Star Codex posts

Corpus: 20 posts spanning his registers — the four "most popular" (Meditations on
Moloch, The Control Group Is Out Of Control, The Toxoplasma of Rage, I Can Tolerate
Anything Except The Outgroup), the argument classics (The Categories Were Made For Man,
In Favor Of Niceness Community and Civilization, Archipelago, Social Justice and Words
Words Words, Right Is The New Left, Weak Men Are Superweapons, Beware Isolated Demands
For Rigor), the famous shorts (Cardiologists and Chinese Robbers, All Debates Are
Bravery Debates, Arguments From My Opponent Believes Something, If It's Worth Doing
It's Worth Doing With Made-Up Statistics, Burdens), plus register outliers: Who By Very
Slow Decay (sincere/clinical), Hardball Questions For The Next Debate (pure comedy),
Universal Love Said The Cactus Person and Answer to Job (fiction). Per-post extraction
notes with verified-verbatim quotes:
[`history/2026-07-05-corpus/scott/`](history/2026-07-05-corpus/scott/).

All eight patterns from the original single-essay analysis (Outgroup) recurred across
the corpus; six new ones emerged that a single essay couldn't show.

**What the voice is.** An explainer's plain register that keeps a full theatrical
company on call. The argument itself is stated cleanly — numbered sections, mechanisms,
evidence — but every position gets to *speak* (invented speeches, cartoon dialogue,
God in all caps), every key concept gets a silly proper name that is then used with a
straight face, and the register around the argument keeps detonating on schedule: a
flat three-word paragraph after a page of buildup, a parenthesis meaner than its host
sentence, a ladder of identical sentences whose last rung snaps. The humor is
load-bearing — it delivers evidence and marks structure — and the blade is always
tested on himself before anyone else. In his sincere pieces the same machinery survives
but migrates to the edges; in his fiction it becomes the plot.

## Pattern: The flat landing
**Seen in:** at least 15 of 20 — the most universal move in the corpus.
**What it is:** A long, careful buildup — a page of mechanism, a case history, a
rhetorical crescendo — resolves into one plain short sentence standing as its own
paragraph. Variants: the one-word paragraph ("So."), the paired landing where two
matched one-liners split the blow, the fragment that both ends one movement and cranks
the next ("But let's talk about toxoplasma."), and in fiction the punchline reply —
the shorter God's answer, the bigger the horror it casually contains.
**Verbatim:**
> "She'd seen countless psychiatrists, psychologists, and counselors, she'd done all
> sorts of therapy, she'd taken every medication in the book, and none of them had
> helped. / So she came to my hospital and was seen by a colleague of mine, who told her
> 'Hey, have you thought about just bringing the hair dryer with you?' / And it worked."
> (Categories — "/" marks paragraph breaks; three words as their own paragraph)
>
> "'Well, maybe the medical community wasn't investigating it the right way! Maybe the
> investigators were biased! Maybe they didn't randomize right! Maybe they used a
> population unusually susceptible to cancer-getting! Ninety percent of medical studies
> are wrong! Those twenty experiments showing a lack of effect could be total bunk!' /
> Yes, maybe these things happened in each of the twenty studies that disagree with
> you. / Or maybe they happened in the one contrarian study you are getting so excited
> about." (Isolated Rigor — the paired landing after the ventriloquized rant)
>
> "Am I calling hospitals hellish? Sure am. It has nothing to do with the decor, which
> has actually gotten much nicer in your newer hospitals until it's hard to tell them
> apart from a stylish office building. It's nothing to do with the staff, either –
> most doctors and some nurses seem pretty happy and trade banter around the water
> coolers like everyone else. It's mostly the screams." (Who By Very Slow Decay)
>
> "'Yes,' said Job, 'but all else being equal, I'd rather be in the perfectly just and
> happy universe.' / 'OH, DON'T WORRY,' said God. 'THERE'S A VERSION OF YOU IN THAT
> UNIVERSE TOO. HE SAYS HI.'" (Answer to Job)
**Formula:** Highly codifiable: after the buildup, one short declarative in its own
paragraph, refusing to match the buildup's energy; strip every qualifier from it. Same
core mechanic as Paul Graham's snap and Moran's under-reaction — the one pattern all
three writers share.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — the cheapest, safest tweak in the library.

## Pattern: Coin it, hammer it
**Seen in:** at least 15 of 20.
**What it is:** A vivid or deliberately silly name is minted once — with full ceremony —
and then reused verbatim, never abbreviated, never paraphrased, until it works as a
technical term and the repetition itself is a running joke. Variants: the proper-noun
coinage ("the PETA Principle," "UniGov," "Mrs. Grundy" — inflected to "grundier by the
day" once it's fully paid off), the profane refrain as structure ("Well, what now,
motherfuckers?" closing successive sections), the borrowed coinage bent to his use
("steal cows," inherited from a quote and made the verdict-word for every domain), the
clunky handle whose refusal to shrink is the joke ("racism school dot tumblr dot com,"
five verbatim uses), and the long-range callback — a homely image planted early
(donuts in the doctors' lounge) reprised sections later so one noun carries a whole
argument.
**Verbatim:**
> "And then I think of the Hair Dryer Incident." (Categories)
>
> "You see Mrs. Grundy, chattering to her grundy friends about how scandalous it is
> that some people read books about sex" (Right Is The New Left)
>
> "But aside from that, there's the PETA Principle: the more controversial something
> is, the more it gets talked about." (Toxoplasma)
>
> "Smart people, kind people! I even said so in my article. Boll weevils in our
> beautiful walled garden!" (Niceness — three prior coinages compressed into one cry)
**Formula:** Fully codifiable: one coinage per piece; christen it explicitly; repeat
word-for-word at every application; let the third-or-later use acknowledge itself.
Converges exactly with PG's "coin it, then spend it" — the strongest cross-writer
pattern in the library. What resists codification: picking a handle strong enough to
bear ten repetitions.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — course concepts *are* coinages; the
discipline of never rotating synonyms is what makes them stick.

## Pattern: Parallel-frame pairs
**Seen in:** at least 14 of 20, at every scale from clause to essay.
**What it is:** Two sentences — or two paragraphs, or two whole sections — built on
identical syntax with the nouns swapped, so the reader computes the equivalence before
it's stated. At essay scale it becomes a refrain template ("From a god's-eye-view, X.
From within the system, Y." — ~14 instances structuring all of Moloch). The symmetry
is often the entire argument: mirrored partisan speeches indict the mechanism, not a
side.
**Verbatim:**
> "Statements like 'the Zambezi River is full of angry hippos' are brute facts.
> Statements like 'the Zambezi River is the territory of Namibia' are negotiable."
> (Categories)
>
> "He is not a heathen, he is a heretic. He is not a foreigner, he is a traitor."
> (Niceness)
>
> "Landmark claims its members are biased against ever thinking ill of themselves, even
> when they deserve it. The therapy book claims that patients are biased towards always
> thinking ill of themselves, even when they don't deserve it." (Bravery Debates)
>
> "'I can't get out of the car until you factor the number.' / 'I won't factor the
> number until you get out of the car.'" (Cactus Person — the frames stripped down each
> round until they're just shouting at each other)
**Formula:** Fully codifiable: write frame A, copy it exactly for B, swap only the
load-bearing terms, resist all variation. Identical to PG's mirror aphorism at sentence
scale; Scott's contribution is running it at paragraph and section scale.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — the natural form for before/after and
wrong-way/right-way teaching contrasts.

## Pattern: The parenthetical afterthought
**Seen in:** at least 14 of 20.
**What it is:** Parentheses that never hold caveats — they escalate, confess, or drop
register, and the aside is routinely funnier or darker than its host sentence, which is
what keeps the main text's register stable. Variants: the whole-paragraph parenthetical
(a pre-emptive strike at one specific reader), parens that reply to each other across
paragraphs (a running bit conducted entirely in asides), the closing parenthesis as the
essay's final punch, and the bare "(!)".
**Verbatim:**
> "Eventually more animals discover the carcass, the faster-breeding animals in the
> carcass multiply, the whale is gradually consumed, and everyone sighs and goes back to
> living in a Malthusian death-trap. / (Slate Star Codex: Your source for macabre whale
> metaphors since June 2014)" (Moloch)
>
> "So this is the second reason why this particular objection to the Bible is silly. If
> God wants to call a whale a big fish, stop telling God what to do. / (also, bats)"
> (Categories — the parenthetical is a whole paragraph: the fish argument's payoff in
> two words)
>
> "(you thought I was joking about the hate for kitten picture bloggers. I never
> joke.)" (Toxoplasma)
>
> "From feminist website Bitchtopia (look, I don't name these websites, I just link to
> them)… This is not just an idiosyncracy of Bitchtopia (look! I'm sorry! I swear I
> didn't name that website!)." (Weak Men — parens with continuity)
**Formula:** Highly codifiable: after a sentence lands, one aside a register below it —
double down with a joke or drop into direct address. One per page, not per paragraph.
The PG equivalent is the "Which…" fragment; Scott's version is louder and can carry a
running gag.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — the easiest way to let a teaching text have
a second, warmer voice without breaking its main register.

## Pattern: Ventriloquism
**Seen in:** at least 16 of 20 — he never paraphrases when he can impersonate.
**What it is:** Positions argue in voiced speech, in their own idiom — orthography and
syntax do the characterization (all-caps zealot, seminar hedger, phonetic dialect).
The cast is wide: steelmanned opponents given better speeches than they'd write
themselves, parody specimens, thought-experiment characters (God, Galileo, a stunned
scientist), abstract forces given monster dialogue ("SPREAD ME OR YOU ARE GARBAGE AND
EVERYONE WILL HATE YOU"), speech that was never spoken (the ugly subtext of his own
therapy), and — in the best trick — characters who speak the blog's own jargon back at
it mid-tantrum.
**Verbatim:**
> "Solomon says he's never heard of these gene things before, and that maybe genetics
> is involved in your weird foreign words 'fish' and 'mammal', but dag are just finned
> creatures that swim in the sea, and behemah are just legged creatures that walk on
> the Earth." (Categories — a dozen paragraphs of it)
>
> "One guy is like 'WE NEED TO DESTROY RELIGION IT CORRUPTS EVERYTHING IT TOUCHES…
> KILL KILL KILL.' And the other guy is like 'Hello? Religion may not be literally
> true, but it usually just makes people feel more comfortable…'" (Bravery Debates —
> the typography of each speech is itself evidence for the essay's claim)
>
> "And they say 'Hey! I thought we told you to stop talking about them! You are
> unfairly discrediting us through the inoculation effect! That is epistemically
> unvirtuous!'" (Weak Men)
>
> "Student: Exactly which sandwich are we talking about here?" (Made-Up Statistics —
> the ventriloquized voice is allowed to be funnier than the narrator)
**Formula:** Partly codifiable: find the claim about what a group thinks, delete it,
write them a first-person speech in their register — steelman if they're to be
answered, parody if they're the specimen. The mimicry itself (catching the idiom) is
talent. Corresponds to Moran's "play both parts."
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes, unusually well here: the course's native
dialogue form is the human-and-Claude transcript, so positions about how people use AI
can be staged rather than asserted.

## Pattern: Concede first, strike second
**Seen in:** at least 6 of 20. *New — invisible in the single-essay analysis.*
**What it is:** Before the turn, an on-the-record, unhedged concession: credit the
adversary's best behavior, agree with the reader's likely dismissal of him, or adopt
the opponent's sneer as if it were a technical term. The concession is real, which is
what makes the strike read as fair. Variant: accept the reductio outright and refuse
the alarm.
**Verbatim:**
> "Everything the Science Czar has said so far makes perfect sense and he is to be
> commended for his rigor and commitment to the job." (Isolated Rigor)
>
> "Does that sound kind of paranoid? I freely admit I am paranoid in this area. But let
> me flesh it out with one more example." (Social Justice and Words³)
>
> "Yes, conservative Christians are icky. And they should be allowed to form completely
> voluntary communities of icky people that enforce icky cultural norms and an insular
> society promoting ickiness, just like everyone else." (Archipelago)
>
> "Another person on the Facebook thread complained that this line of arguments leads
> to being okay with white separatists. And so it does. Fine." (Archipelago)
**Formula:** Highly codifiable: name the strongest objection or the reader's worst read
of you; grant it in one plain sentence with no "but" inside it; then turn. The PG
cousins are "I'm not saying" and the steelman-then-reversal; Scott's version concedes
more than the critic would dare ask for.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — advice that concedes its own limits first
("this will feel slower at the start — it is") earns the right to insist.

## Pattern: The ladder that breaks
**Seen in:** at least 8 of 20. *New — this is Moran's "broken third" scaled up to
paragraphs.*
**What it is:** An anaphoric run — three-plus sentences or paragraphs on one repeated
frame, escalating steadily — whose final rung snaps the template, and the snap carries
the point. Variants: the day-by-day ladder whose refrain varies by one verb until a
summary sentence springs the trap; the even-if ladder of ascending concessions; the
"I could…" ladder of arguments waved away that still land; the hypothesis parade where
each paragraph opens with a candidate and closes with a one-line execution ("So
scratch that.").
**Verbatim:**
> "It's not a coincidence that the worst possible flagship case for fighting police
> brutality and racism is the flagship case that we in fact got. … It's not
> coincidence, it's not even happenstance, it's enemy action." (Toxoplasma — the break
> also cashes a saying planted four sections earlier)
>
> "You'll probably never become an astronaut. You're not going to bang Emma Watson. And
> your last words will probably be something like 'mmmrrrgggg graaaaaaaaaaaHAAACK!'"
> (Who By Very Slow Decay)
>
> "So again you just get uncomfortable. … So again you stay uncomfortable. … You have
> been boxed in by a serious of individually harmless but collectively dangerous
> statements." (Weak Men — the refrain's only variation is one verb; the sameness is
> what makes the boxing-in palpable)
>
> "Sure, the actual badness of North Korea may not be exactly 37%. But it's probably
> not twice as good as living in the First World. Or even 90% as good. But it's
> probably not two hundred times worse than death either." (Made-Up Statistics)
**Formula:** Highly codifiable: keep the frame word-for-word between rungs so the break
lands entirely on the payload; the escalation should be in content (concreteness,
stakes), not volume; the broken rung is the thesis.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — for any "people try A, then B, then C"
teaching sequence, this shape makes the failure pattern felt rather than described.

## Pattern: Turning the weapon on himself
**Seen in:** at least 9 of 20.
**What it is:** The essay's accusation is applied to the author, unsoftened — and at
his best, *quantified* or performed live rather than confessed. Toxoplasma indicts his
own blog with his own traffic data; Cardiologists corrects itself mid-sentence ("at
random – well, not quite random"); Control Group's regress chain interrogates his own
judgment until it eats its tail, and that Ouroboros is the essay's actual conclusion.
**Verbatim:**
> "The less useful, and more controversial, a post here is, the more likely it is to
> get me lots of page views." (Toxoplasma — followed by the 2,000-vs-16,000 hit counts)
>
> "How do I know it's crap? Well, I use my personal judgment. How do I know my personal
> judgment is right? Well, a smart well-credentialed person like James Coyne agrees
> with me. How do I know James Coyne is smart?" (Control Group)
>
> "I chose to pick on them at random – well, not quite random, one of them yelled at me
> the other day…" (Cardiologists)
>
> "'Sure, you're a burden if you live, but if you kill yourself, that would make you an
> even bigger burden!' A++ best psychiatrist." (Burdens)
**Formula:** Partly codifiable: after any accusation aimed outward, ask whether it
applies to you; if yes, state it in the theory's least flattering vocabulary, with
evidence, and no softening clause after it. All three library writers carry a version
(PG's unflattering anecdote, Moran's turn-the-gun-around) — the shared conclusion:
mockery must be insured with membership.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — indispensable for a course that mocks bad
AI habits.

## Pattern: The machine-gun list with a self-aware item
**Seen in:** at least 7 of 20.
**What it is:** Evidence strafed as short parallel concrete tokens — real titles, real
cases, proper nouns — with one item that breaks pattern: a confession, an absurdly long
final entry, or a wink at the list's own absurdity. Compression variants: "same"
replacing the repeated predicate; the question form ("Remember…? And the one where…?")
that flatters the reader as an insider and skips citations entirely.
**Verbatim:**
> "Philadelphia cardiologist, same. North Carolina cardiologist, same. 11 Kentucky
> cardiologists, same. … Etc, etc, etc." (Cardiologists)
>
> "The screams are coming about 33% from the confused demented old people I mentioned,
> 33% from people having minor procedures performed without anaesthetics for one or
> another good reason, and 33% from people who just have very painful diseases (plus 1%
> from me sitting in the break room looking up examples of hospital poetry for this
> post)." (Who By Very Slow Decay)
>
> "Also skilled at missing the obvious: Martin Luther King. Desmond Tutu. Aung San Suu
> Kyi. Nelson Mandela was smart and effective at the beginning of his career, but fell
> into a pattern of missing the obvious when he was older. Maybe it was Alzheimers."
> (Niceness)
**Formula:** Codifiable: every item a specific noun or observable case; keep items
short and parallel; plant exactly one pattern-breaker about two-thirds through. Which
tokens ring true is cultural ear.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — swap abstract category lists for
named-tool, named-behavior lists, and let one item be funny.

## Pattern: Play it straight
**Seen in:** at least 7 of 20. *New.*
**What it is:** He commits to an absurd or opposing premise with full procedural
seriousness — planning steps, weighing logistics, citing sources — and never winks; the
premise detonates itself. Variants: the logistics reductio (kill an ideal by spelling
out its literal implementation in concrete nouns); the deadpan parable (a miniature
invented world narrated flatly, cashed out in one application sentence); and at essay
scale the sting reveal — a whole section performed in the register the essay is about
to indict, then unmasked as a demonstration performed on the reader.
**Verbatim:**
> "Suppose I am a radical Catholic who believes all Protestants deserve to die, and
> therefore go around killing Protestants. So far, so good." (Niceness)
>
> "My calculations suggest about 90%, which is very high compared to what we know about
> similar traits – but actually not entirely outside the realm of plausibility."
> (Hardball Questions — regression math performed on a magic-sword conspiracy)
>
> "You can't go around giving everyone surveys to see how selfish they are, and give
> half of them Atlas Shrugged and half of them the collected works of Peter Singer."
> (Bravery Debates — the logistics reductio)
>
> "If you read Part I of this post and found yourself nodding along… consider it
> evidence that a sufficiently motivated individual – especially a journalist! – can
> make you feel that way about any group." (Cardiologists — the sting unmasked)
**Formula:** Partly codifiable: adopt the premise in first person, keep the register
scholarly (hedges and citations *increase* the deadpan), and let concreteness do the
demolition — "no 'obviously this is absurd' allowed." The sting variant is
architecture, not a tweak: open in the register you intend to indict. Cousin of Moran's
alien's-eye redescription; the comic engine of Hardball Questions end to end.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Partly — the logistics reductio transfers
directly (kill a bad workflow by narrating its real steps); the sting is a once-per-
course weapon.

## Pattern: The question barrage
**Seen in:** at least 5 of 20. *New.*
**What it is:** A volley of second-person questions fired at the reader or an implied
opponent, escalating in *specificity* rather than volume — fragment questions
multiplying a number, or a trap question that invites the comfortable answer so the
next example can detonate it.
**Verbatim:**
> "You're saying none of these shootings, hundreds each year, made as good a flagship
> case as Michael Brown? In all this gigantic pile of bodies, you couldn't find one of
> them who hadn't just robbed a convenience store? Not a single one who didn't have ten
> eyewitnesses and the forensic evidence all saying he started it?" (Toxoplasma)
>
> "What if I told you the real number was a million cops? Five million cops? Ten
> million?" (Cardiologists — full sentences would kill the vertigo)
>
> "Now there's practically no implication that these people are typical. So that's
> fine, right?" (Weak Men — the trap question; the reader answers wrong on purpose and
> the essay corrects them)
**Formula:** Codifiable: each question more concrete than the last; use fragments once
the frame is established; if it's a trap, end the beat on the question and let the next
example spring it.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes, sparingly — one barrage at the moment the
learner is most likely to be overconfident.

## Pattern: The absurd-scale restatement
**Seen in:** at least 5 of 20.
**What it is:** A precise figure is cashed out as one concrete, faintly ridiculous
physical image, capped with an ironic minimizer — mundane arithmetic performed on lurid
material, dash, deflation.
**Verbatim:**
> "Only 9% of Americans like it, suggesting a lower approval rating than cockroaches,
> head lice, or traffic jams." (Moloch)
>
> "If you spend twelve hours a day on the task and can describe one crime every ten
> seconds, you can spend four months doing nothing but providing examples of burglarous
> Chinese – and still have absolutely no point." (Cardiologists)
>
> "Competition and optimization are blind idiotic processes and they fully intend to
> deny us even one lousy galaxy." (Moloch — the minimizer at literal cosmic scale)
>
> "'NOT AS MANY AS YOU THINK.' said God. 'IN THE END IT TURNED OUT TO BE ONLY ABOUT
> 10^(10^(10^(10^(10^984)))).'" (Answer to Job)
**Formula:** Highly codifiable: state the number, translate it into one picturable
scenario, attach the minimizer ("a mere," "only," "still no point"). PG's counted claim
is the sober sibling; Scott adds the deflating irony.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — any time the course cites a magnitude.

## Pattern: Capitals as casting
**Seen in:** at least 7 of 20. *New.*
**What it is:** Typography assigns words to a voice that isn't his. Title Caps mark a
phrase as somebody's cant (mock institutions, official euphemism); all caps cast a
speaker — the hysteric, the monster, the outburst at peak exasperation, or (inverted,
in fiction) the deadpan omnipotent calm of God. The flat gloss after the capitals is
part of the move.
**Verbatim:**
> "things that Weaken The Moral Fabric Of Society" (Archipelago)
>
> "this is nevertheless Unacceptable Behavior and they have to be restrained ie tied
> down to the bed" (Who By Very Slow Decay)
>
> "Science! YOU WERE THE CHOSEN ONE! It was said that you would destroy reliance on
> biased experts, not join them!" (Control Group — the essay ends here, no return to
> argument register)
>
> "Then the Lord spoke to Job out of the whirlwind, saying 'WHAT KIND OF UNIVERSE WOULD
> YOU PREFER ME TO HAVE CREATED?'" (Answer to Job — same typography, opposite valence)
**Formula:** Codifiable in the small: one Title-Caps phrase per page marks borrowed
cant, no scare quotes needed. The all-caps outburst is high-risk, dosage-everything —
he spends it about once per essay, at the emotional summit or as the final line.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Partly — Title-Caps-as-air-quotes transfers
cheaply ("Best Practices"); the outburst almost never belongs in teaching text.

## How the machinery transposes across registers
Worth knowing because the lessons will shift registers too:
- **Sincere** (Who By Very Slow Decay, Burdens): the jokes don't vanish — they migrate
  to the edges (parentheses, ironic capitals) while the center goes plain; sentences
  shorten around the patients; the irony targets institutions, never the dying. The
  signature moves survive "tuned way down and pointed inward."
- **Comedy** (Hardball Questions, Opponent Believes): the argument-essay's own
  apparatus — citations, hedges, regression math, steelman concessions — is retained
  intact and aimed at absurd premises; the deadpan never breaks except once, positioned
  as the final line. Every fake question opens with *true, generous* biography: the
  truer the opening, the longer the deadpan can hold.
- **Fiction** (Cactus Person, Answer to Job): the essay machinery becomes plot
  mechanics — the flat landing becomes the punchline reply, ventriloquism becomes a
  sustained two-voice register collision, the coinage gets capitals instead of "as I
  keep saying" (GETTING OUT OF THE CAR), and endings work by register shift rather than
  stated conclusion (the story snaps back into straight biblical paraphrase and the
  canon text reads as the payoff).

## What resists extraction
The single-essay analysis said it and the corpus confirms it: his deepest effects are
architectural. Cardiologists spends half its length performing the fallacy on you
before naming it; Isolated Rigor plants a throwaway parenthesis that returns as a
fully dramatized western re-arguing the whole essay in costume; Weak Men opens and
closes on the same two-character toy case. These are plant-and-payoff structures that
require designing the piece around them. Also unportable: the fairness itself — the
scrupulously symmetric examples (every mechanism demonstrated on both tribes), the
ventriloquized opponents given the best lines — which reads as style but is actually
ethics; and the sheer stamina of commitment, sustaining a deadpan or a refrain long
past the point a cautious writer would break it, which is what turns a trick into a
world. Steal the moves at tweak scale; the trust he's spending was earned essay by
essay.
