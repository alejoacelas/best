# Scott Alexander — I Can Tolerate Anything Except The Outgroup

Source: [I Can Tolerate Anything Except The Outgroup](https://slatestarcodex.com/2014/09/30/i-can-tolerate-anything-except-the-outgroup/) (Slate Star Codex, 2014). In the library because he makes dense expository argument feel alive — statistics turn into images, objections get full speeches, and the essay keeps surprising you at the sentence level without ever leaving argument mode. Chosen over "Meditations on Moloch": both sit in Scott's own "my most popular posts" list on the top-posts page, but Outgroup is the most-linked SSC post and its effects are pure Scott prose, where Moloch leans on Ginsberg's scaffolding.

## Pattern: The absurd-scale restatement
**What it is:** A statistic gets stated precisely, then immediately restated as a concrete physical image or comparison whose absurdity does the persuading. Often garnished with an ironic minimizer ("a mere").
**Verbatim:**
> Odds of this happening by chance? 1/2^150 = 1/10^45 = approximately the chance of picking a particular atom if you are randomly selecting among all the atoms on Earth.

> This is less astronomically unlikely; the odds are a mere one to one hundred quintillion against.

> Conservatives are all around me, yet I am about as likely to have a serious encounter with one as I am a Tibetan lama.
**Formula:** Highly codifiable: state the number exactly, then translate it into one physical image the reader can picture (atoms, lamas), then treat the huge number deadpan-casually ("a mere"). The only taste part is picking an image that's concrete but not cute.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — any time a course cites a number ("Claude reads 200K tokens"), restate it as an image ("every email you've sent in five years, at once").

## Pattern: Coin it once, hammer it forever
**What it is:** A vivid coinage (dark matter, 10^45, the shamrock song) is introduced early, then re-invoked verbatim as compressed shorthand — and by the second or third use he winks at his own repetition.
**Verbatim:**
> So in the same way, political tribes self-segregate to an impressive extent – a 1/10^45 extent, I will never tire of hammering in – based on their implicit tribal characteristics.

> It’s the many, many differences between these tribes that explain the strength of the filter bubble – which have I mentioned segregates people at a strength of 1/10^45?

> Once the Blue Tribe was able to enlist the blacks and gays and Muslims in their ranks, they became allies of convenience who deserve to be rehabilitated with mildly condescending paeans to their virtue. “There never was a coward where the shamrock grows.”
**Formula:** Codifiable: coin one image or number per piece, reuse it verbatim (not paraphrased) at each place it applies, and by the third use acknowledge the repetition out loud. What resists codification is knowing which coinage can bear the weight — reusing a weak one reads as a tic.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — running examples and callback phrases are exactly what makes a lesson feel authored instead of assembled.

## Pattern: The flat landing
**What it is:** An elaborate, sometimes whimsical buildup ends in a one-sentence paragraph, stated flat. The shortness *is* the punchline; the same brake also appears as a rhetorical question answered by a fragment.
**Verbatim:**
> …maybe a few meters away from me a dark matter blogger is writing on his dark matter computer about how weird it would be if there was a light matter person he couldn’t see right next to him.
>
> This is sort of how I feel about conservatives.

> It soon became clear that nobody there was actually against gay marriage.

> And that was when something clicked for me.
**Formula:** Codifiable as a rhythm rule: after two or more long paragraphs of buildup, land on one plain short sentence and give it its own paragraph; strip every qualifier from it. Timing — how long to let the buildup run — is taste.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — the single cheapest tweak here; most instructional drafts bury their landing sentence mid-paragraph.

## Pattern: Ventriloquism
**What it is:** Instead of summarizing an objection or an opponent, he writes them a whole speech in their own idiom — exaggerated enough to be funny, steelmanned enough to be fair — then answers the speech.
**Verbatim:**
> “But racism and sexism and cissexism and anti-Semitism are these giant all-encompassing social factors that verge upon being human universals! Surely you’re not arguing that mere political differences could ever come close to them!”

> “It’s not that we’re, like, against America per se. It’s just that…well, did you know Europe has much better health care than we do? And much lower crime rates? I mean, come on, how did they get so awesome? […] Vote for me!”

> And I would rather personally behead every single person in the world than give a smug bigot like you a single microgram more stupid self-satisfaction than you’ve already got.
**Formula:** The structure is codifiable: open a section with the reader's objection in first person and quotation marks, let it run a full paragraph, answer it. The voice itself — catching the "like," the trailing ellipsis, the tell-tale pet phrases — is mimicry talent and doesn't reduce to a recipe.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Partly — voicing the learner's objection ("okay, but won't Claude just make things up?") works great; full satirical monologues usually don't belong.

## Pattern: The machine-gun list with a self-aware item
**What it is:** Long lists built from brand-level concrete tokens (products, behaviors, verbatim shouts) rather than adjectives — with one item that runs long and turns confessional or absurd, breaking the rhythm on purpose.
**Verbatim:**
> …eating arugula, drinking fancy bottled water, driving Priuses, reading lots of books, being highly educated, mocking American football, feeling vaguely like they should like soccer but never really being able to get into it…

> …owning guns, eating steak, drinking Coca-Cola, driving SUVs, watching lots of TV, enjoying American football, getting conspicuously upset about terrorists and commies, marrying early, divorcing early, shouting “USA IS NUMBER ONE!!!”…

> …eating paleo, drinking Soylent, calling in rides on Uber, reading lots of blogs, calling American football “sportsball”, getting conspicuously upset about the War on Drugs and the NSA, and listening to filk…
**Formula:** Codifiable: every item a specific noun or observable behavior (Priuses, not "eco-consciousness"); keep items short and parallel; plant exactly one long, over-honest item about two-thirds through. Which tokens ring true for a given group is pure cultural ear.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — swap abstract category lists for named-tool, named-behavior lists, and let one item be funny.

## Pattern: The parenthetical afterthought
**What it is:** Parentheses used not for caveats but for a second beat after the sentence lands — escalating the claim, confessing, or apologizing to the people he just skewered.
**Verbatim:**
> (Less likely, actually. One time a Tibetan lama came to my college and gave a really nice presentation, but if a conservative tried that, people would protest and it would be canceled.)

> (sorry, I make fun of you because I love you)

> (except on the blog Stuff White People Like, where it obviously serves as a code word for the Blue tribe. I don’t know, guys. I didn’t do it.)
**Formula:** Codifiable: after a strong claim or joke, add a parenthetical that either doubles down with one more piece of evidence ("Less likely, actually…") or drops register into direct address ("I don't know, guys."). Resists codification: frequency control — he spends these maybe once per section.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — a lower-register aside after a formal point is one of the easiest ways to make a lesson sound like a person.

## Pattern: Turning the weapon on himself
**What it is:** The essay's own accusation gets applied, unsoftened, to the author — repeatedly and at full strength, in the same plain declarative voice used for everything else.
**Verbatim:**
> This essay is bad and I should feel bad.

> Yeah. Once I’ve written a ten thousand word essay savagely attacking the Blue Tribe, either I’m a very special person or they’re my outgroup. And I’m not that special.

> The worst thing that could happen to this post is to have it be used as convenient feces to fling at the Blue Tribe whenever feces are necessary. Which, given what has happened to my last couple of posts along these lines and the obvious biases of my own subconscious, I already expect it will be.
**Formula:** Partly codifiable: ask "does my own argument indict me?" and if yes, say so in the bluntest available sentence, with no defensive hedge attached. What resists codification is that it only works when true — performed self-criticism is precisely what the essay is about.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Partly — "I make this exact mistake in my own prompts" builds trust fast, but it needs a real example behind it or it reads as false modesty.

## Pattern: Parallel-frame pairs
**What it is:** Two sentences (or two whole scenarios) built on identical syntax with the nouns swapped, so the reader computes the equivalence themselves before he states it.
**Verbatim:**
> Every election cycle like clockwork, conservatives accuse liberals of not being sufficiently pro-America. And every election cycle like clockwork, liberals give extremely unconvincing denials of this.

> You wouldn’t celebrate Osama’s death, only Thatcher’s. And you wouldn’t call ISIS savages, only Fox News. Fox is the outgroup, ISIS is just some random people off in a desert.

> If you mix together Podunk, Texas and Mosul, Iraq, you can prove that Muslims are scary and very powerful people who are executing Christians all the time […] And if you mix together the open-source tech industry and the parallel universe where you can’t wear a FreeBSD t-shirt without risking someone trying to exorcise you, you can prove that Christians are scary and very powerful people who are persecuting everyone else all the time…
**Formula:** Fully codifiable: write sentence A, copy its exact frame for sentence B, swap only the load-bearing nouns, and start B with "And". The persuasive force comes from the syntax match, so resist the urge to vary the wording.
**Transfers to instructional prose?** Yes — perfect for before/after and wrong-way/right-way contrasts ("Ask Claude X and you get Y. Ask Claude X′ and you get Y′.").

## What resists extraction
The essay's deepest effect is structural, not sentence-level: it spends ten sections arming a weapon and then, in section XI, fires it at the author — the confession isn't an ornament, it's the thesis demonstrated live. No local tweak reproduces that; it requires designing the whole piece as a trap you're willing to be caught in. Related and equally untransferable: the humor never comes at the expense of the argument's fairness — he gives his targets the best lines (hypothetical-Brand's defense is genuinely good) — and the persona of a numbers-obsessed person visibly upset by what the numbers show is a worldview, not a technique. You can borrow the moves above; the reason they land is that the reader believes he means it.
