# Guide goals

What the guide must do to the reader. Drafted by Claude from `samples/`, the
old plan, and Alejo's instructions; provisional until Alejo edits.

## The transformation
The reader hands over their computer, and some of their brain, to Claude, and
has it achieve their goals for them (better-start's own words). Not "offload my
current to-dos": the reader becomes Claude's manager. Their new job is judging
ocean-loads of smart but often misguided machine output — ask for what you
want, figure out if you're getting it, ask why you're not getting there faster.
They finish able to hand off work they wouldn't otherwise have done at all, and
to decompose it into pieces they verify, trust, or don't care about.

## What the writing must do
1. **Aggressively prioritize.** Few messages, chosen hard. The reader's
   attention is spent on what we most want them to keep.
2. **Match flair to importance.** The most important lessons get the most
   provocative, most worked phrasing; skim the guide and what sticks is
   exactly what should stick.
3. **Order for absorption.** Each lesson lands on ground the previous one
   prepared; dependencies (installs, accounts, likely failures) mapped, not
   discovered by the reader.
4. **Carry Alejo's voice.** Challenging, slightly poetic, provocative-but-true
   (see `style-criteria.md`). The guide should feel like being coached by him,
   not like documentation.
5. **Engage rising.** The reader should want the next lesson more, not less,
   as they go.

## What it is not
- Not comprehensive documentation; the inventory (`../01-inventory/`) is where
  comprehensiveness lives, so the guide doesn't have to.
- Not neutral. It takes sides (best model always, dictate always, worry less
  about security) and says why.
