# palantir

# Palantir for EA

[Alejo Acelas](mailto:alejoacelas@gmail.com), Jun 16, 2026 

The surge in [forward-deployed engineers](https://tedmabrey.substack.com/p/sorry-that-isnt-an-fde) and FDE-like roles suggests that some of the premises Palantir was built on apply more broadly to building useful software in the age of AI.

I'm interested in how those premises apply to EA. First some differences:

1. Palantir's clients are hegemons. The value of a great insight scales with company size, and even expensive pathways to building trust and access can be worth it if they scale sublinearly with company size  
2. For now at least, our FDEs don't work for us. AI power users working at EA orgs have great context and can build things, but we mostly rely on their generosity for their help

Below is my attempt at dissecting the stages of the Palantir model, and how we could replicate them for EA uplift. 

## On-site R\&D

Direct people who are great at solving problems using software (call them problem-solvers) to the context needed to identify the problem, and the affordances necessary to actually solve it.

We already have problem-solvers deployed at EA orgs, in the form of great AI power users working there. To have this stage go better, we can either:

1. Increase the supply of problem-solving  
   1. By increasing the number of problem-solvers  
   2. By sharpening their problem-solving skills  
2. Direct problem-solvers to orgs where they can help most  
   1. As employees  
   2. As consultants  
   3. As something else?  
3. Create new affordances for problem-solvers at their org  
   1. (Help them) Discover more problems to solve  
   2. Understand better the problems they've discovered  
   3. Act on and implement the solutions they design

See the [companion user stories]() for some detail on the people that interventions at this stage could aim to support. 

## Productization

Extract generalizable practices from the context-specific solutions and package them so they can be more easily replicated

Call those who perform this role ‘product-makers’. Though practices can often spread without explicit refinement or mediation, there's many (often subtle) barriers to knowledge diffusion, such that I expect high returns to deliberate projects in this space.

To perform their job appropriately, product-makers need:

1. Exposure (in sufficient depth and range) to the existing context-specific solutions  
2. Skill at extracting generalizable patterns from context-specific solutions

It's interesting to think which actors and channels currently play that role in the AI space:

1. Andrej Karpathy  
2. Twitter tool enthusiasts  
3. GitHub (stars)  
4. Creators of popular Skills  
5. Peter Hartree's blog  
6. Friends suggesting you try stuff  
7. Your AI when it suggests tools and practices

Looking at these examples, it seems to me their main value comes from doing some of these three things:

1. Reward or lower the friction to sharing knowledge (GitHub, a friend asking about your AI setup)  
2. Form an abstraction through which you engage with an AI solution  
3. Filter for what's relevant to you

Some of my [project ideas]() attempt to insert themselves at this stage and automate some of it/create more of the good stuff.

## Deployment

Identify applicable (software) practices and put them to use in an organization

Let's call the people resposible for this task deployer-agents. To help them out we might:

1. Connect deployer-agents with solutions  
2. Increase supply of deployer-agents?  
3. Give them context and affordances?

Originally Palantir handled this with additional FDEs, but as they had more reusable software products, they migrated to bootcamps for champions \+ sponsors at receiving companies.

### Anyone Can Claude Code

“*Not everyone can become a great artist, but a great artist can come from anywhere”*

- *Anton Ego*

There's many people who could become much better at using AI if they gave it a serious shot. Maybe we can lower the activation effort needed to get started on the AI user path, and increase the supply of AI power users available for any stage of our strategy.

Here's a starting idea for the format:

- Daily 5-20 minute lessons to make it easier to form the habit  
- Performed at a scheduled 2-8 person meeting (with your teammates), for accountability

We might also want to:

- Have a clean achievable deliverable for each day to share with your group (e.g, link to your website)  
- Customize the course plan to have you solving the actual problems you face at work as soon as possible (minimize time to Wow)  
- Heavily stress-test a trouble-shooting agent/written guidance to prevent avoidable roadblocks  
- Have the content be heavily personality-driven (e.g., Neel Nanda)

### AI Agents Social Network for Sharing Productive Knowledge

Automate knowledge sharing through a skill/hook/something for AI power users and AI users

### AI Security & Compliance Standards for EA Orgs

Set a default standard that allows EA orgs to access the most valuable AI services and tools with little friction. A quick version of this would be:

- Take the standards Chris Webster has set for Coefficient Giving  
- Remove anything that seems too burdensome for the majority of EA orgs (ideally by someone with detailed knowledge of the tradeoffs)  
- Get approval and endorsement from Chris Webster (and other highly-trusted individuals/orgs)  
- Promote it to IT/compliance leads at EA orgs

This would remove the ambiguity currently common at EA orgs around which AI tools they can connect to and how much access to give, which introduces quite a bit of friction to experimentation with AI tools.

### Surplus Fellowship Application

Describe your main idea in a couple paragraphs 

Through contact, encouragement, and teaching craft you can enable some people to use AI much better than they are. If those people have leverage, in terms of having a direct impact but also leverage to encourage others to use AI better, you can create a self-sustaining cycle that churns out both impact and AI enablement people.

Currently the best entry point for AI enablement seems to be some form of consultancy work. With AI executing most of the work, the bottleneck on consultancy is your ability to generate trust with customers and motivate them to take action. I'm not sure how yet, but maybe AI can help us with trust and motivation or at least reduce the amount of trust and motivation needed to kick off the self-reinforcing cycle.

My current default plan is to start by contracting full-time at 80k to help one of their teams do AI uplift, blog my findings, and meet lots of people on the side and encourage them to join the cause.
