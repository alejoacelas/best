# How the blog defines the principles

Excerpts from **myea.blog**, with discussion of how the blog already gestures at
each of the five principles — and where it sharpens, complicates, or unifies
them. The full text these quotes are drawn from is kept, dated, in
[`myea-blog-2026-06-27.md`](./myea-blog-2026-06-27.md) (snapshot of
`https://myea.blog/all.txt`, downloaded 2026-06-27). The seed statement of the
principles is in [`prompt.md`](./prompt.md).

A useful thing the blog does that the seed prompt doesn't: it keeps naming the
*generating idea* underneath the five principles. From "Will kindness prevail":

> I tend to see all humans as having a shared goal (same utility function, for
> the economists in the crowd). If that's the case at some level, then all the
> remaining problems seem to be some form of information asymmetry … Knowledge
> is a great shared value.

That single move — *assume a shared goal, then most of what's left is an
information problem* — is the root the five principles branch from. **Open**
attacks the information asymmetry directly. **Kind** is the faith that the shared
goal is really there. **Strong** and **Fast** are the capacity and the velocity
to act once knowledge is shared. **Cooperative** is the constraint that keeps a
strong, fast agent trustworthy enough for the shared-goal assumption to hold.

---

## 1. Open

> **Seed:** honesty + giving as much information as you can; shared goals make
> knowledge into capacity; plus modularity, discoverability, gradual disclosure
> so people can choose and explore you.

**"Be so candid they ask you to stop"** gives the principle its operational rule
and its stopping criterion:

> The only reason you might consider [radical honesty] is because your own
> criteria to judge when to say something truthful is so fucked up that you'd be
> better off completely disregarding it in favor of a simple straightforward
> rule. … one that I found actually helpful in practice is: just keep going
> until people literally start asking you to stop.

**"Please forgive the errors in my ways"** states the openness *thesis* in its
strongest form — and immediately flags its failure mode:

> Maybe I have a delusional account of the world, where every extra piece of
> information can only help you make better decisions. What better way to make
> all the best decisions than to have all the best information, which you
> acquire through the perfect honesty of everybody else?

The "maybe … delusional" framing is the blog doing work the seed prompt leaves
implicit: openness is justified *by the shared-goal model*, and it over-fires
exactly when that model is wrong (when goals diverge, or when the cost of a
truth falls entirely on the receiver).

The seed's second half — **modularity, discoverability, gradual disclosure** —
is the whole thesis of **"Lay yourself bare on the web"**:

> Personally, my main aims in blogging are: 1. Discovering (at least one?) great
> long-term partner 2. Discovering great friends and collaborators 3. Having
> better conversations about the topics I care about most.

A blog *is* the modular, discoverable, gradually-disclosed self: posts are
chunks others can choose among and explore at their own pace. The blog is the
principle instantiated, not just described.

**Tension the blog adds (worth keeping for the Fable conversation):** the
fragment **"emotions important?"** is labeled by the author himself as the
"anti-thesis to my defense of extreme candidness":

> people's reactions and feelings are important … one of the main drivers of
> great human endeavours is the feelings people have about them. Narrative plays
> a big role here and precision and thickness of the information flow may be less
> important, at least in some domains.

So Open is not "maximize information flow." It's "default hard toward disclosure
*because your private censor is miscalibrated*, and let other people's stated
limits — not your own comfort — be the brake."

## 2. Kind

> **Seed:** believing in people; seeing the best of them; being creative and
> resourceful in finding ways to delight others.

"Believing in people" has a name and a defense in **"Faith in humanity"**:

> having a certain kind of faith in people's decency, which I call faith in
> humanity, is a centrally important moral virtue

— with the author's own amendment that "the faith I cling to is more about
*people's capacity to do good* than on people's decency." That distinction
matters for the principle: kindness here is less "everyone is nice" and more
"everyone is a latent collaborator," which ties Kind directly back to the
shared-goal root.

**"Will kindness prevail"** turns kindness from a feeling into a *strategy you
can always execute*:

> I can just latch on to kindness. Whatever may come, I can be consistently kind
> throughout. And that's something I can control … as we near our final,
> passing breath, I will have been kind to those who tried.

The "creative and resourceful in finding ways of delighting others" clause is
where the blog is richest, and where it suggests the seed is understating
something. **"The giving tree"**:

> I love the idea of giving your all to those you love … giving away everything
> until just a stump remains … I feel like I want to father children of all
> ages.

And **"Sad kindness"** reframes the whole principle through Katherine Rundell:

> attention might be so closely bound up with love as to be almost
> indistinguishable, and I think it might be the thing we owe the world most …
> one of the finest gifts that we can offer.

**Suggested refinement:** the seed says "delighting others"; the blog keeps
reaching for something heavier — *attention, devotion, faith in their capacity*.
Delight is the surface; the engine underneath is taking someone seriously. Worth
deciding with Fable whether Kind = "delight" or "attention/devotion that often
shows up as delight."

## 3. Strong

> **Seed:** capacity for action; expanding the range of things you can do;
> physical strength as the clearest case (carry more, last longer, more
> one-on-ones at EAG).

The seed's EAG example is in the blog almost verbatim — **"Compounding
yourself"**:

> Last EAG, the day I had most one-on-ones, I had like 16 or something … Most
> people I meet cannot do 16 one-on-ones in a day. That's like eight hours …
> And that required physical endurance, and I was there for it … I just run hard
> and I run fast, because physical endurance feels like something great.

The same post supplies the *general* definition behind the physical example —
strength as accumulated, compounding capacity:

> if you do things well in your career, many of your five-minute, many of your
> one-hour, many of your eight-hour tasks are things that took you years of
> expertise to develop.

**"True ambition has barely been tried"** pushes Strong from "capacity" to
"self-reshaping," which is a stronger claim than the seed makes:

> true ambition … looks less like a career and more like sinking your own hand
> into your living flesh and reworking what's inside you to serve the good
> purpose.

And **"You can't compound on your own"** adds the crucial qualifier the seed
omits — that most strength is *positional*, not internal:

> most of your productivity increase will come from locating yourself in
> networks with better affordances … your main productivity boost will come from
> creating your own personal country, connecting to the infrastructure that's
> built in there.

**Suggested refinement:** the seed frames strength as a property of the
individual ("the range of things *you* can do"). The blog insists a large share
of it is the network you're embedded in and the self you're willing to remake.
Strong ≈ capacity = (trained ability) × (position/affordances) × (willingness to
reshape yourself). "Have you tried violent passion?" supplies the fuel for that
reshaping.

## 4. Fast

> **Seed:** if it can be done now, do it now; if faster, do it faster; less
> friction is better.

**"There is rarely ever a good reason to do things later"** is the principle in
full, including the test to apply:

> if someone sends an email, you can probably reply now. Why not? What would it
> take? What special power will you acquire in the future that will make your
> reply better than the reply you'd write now? … Leaving things for later just
> feels like being inconsiderate with your future self.

It also already contains the seed's "less friction" clause as an AI move:

> one of the best uses you can make of AI is turning more tasks into "now and
> done" tasks.

**"Stop feeling dread. Start feeling regret"** supplies the emotional mechanism
that makes Fast sustainable rather than frantic:

> Dread is undirected. It prevents action … You can learn from acting on regret,
> you can't learn from acting on dread.

**"Act on all your great ideas"** extends Fast from execution speed to a stance
on *taste*: don't wait until you're sure an idea is good.

> each and every one of our non-boring ideas is great … everything that has even
> an ineffable sense that it could be good, that's great already. And you should
> do it.

**Suggested refinement:** Fast in the blog is not "rush." It's "collapse the
delay between intention and a started action, because (a) people value it, (b)
you stay in-context, and (c) acting is how you learn which ideas were real." The
limiter is explicit and worth importing: don't take on *background* commitments
freely — "you shouldn't let people impose on you some background process in your
mind."

## 5. Cooperative

> **Seed:** integrity / law-abiding / predictability; if you're strong you need
> principles that hold you back; the Kantian "would I will everyone to behave as
> I do?"; we are biased and self-serving, so tie yourself to the mast to act
> rightly even under undetected bias.

The Kantian clause is in **"Should we let everyone die to stand by virtue?"**
almost word for word:

> He doesn't call for "bombing datacenters" — if everyone who thought bombing
> datacenters was a good idea did it, the world would be much worse.

But the principle's real home is **"My pattern of disregarding people's
immediate preferences"**, which is a case study in *exactly* the failure mode the
seed names (a strong, fast, utilitarian-leaning agent overriding someone "with
only a minute and a half of thought"). Its conclusions are the principle in
operational form:

> if you're going to [aim high], we need safeguards to hold our worst human
> tendencies for doing self-justifying naked power-grabbing.

> I should default to following people's immediate preferences and only depart
> from that in cases where I had thought in detail and at length about the
> benefits and costs of not adhering to that rule.

> I'm very interested in finding other clear rules such that I can immediately
> recognize cases of wrongdoing in myself, and find ways of committing publicly
> to those rules and letting others quickly know if I'm deviating from them.

That last line is the "tie yourself to the mast" image made concrete:
*pre-commit publicly, and build tripwires so others can catch you* — because
your own bias is, by assumption, invisible to you in the moment.

**"You literally cannot die"** explains *why* Cooperative is what unlocks the
other four rather than just restraining them:

> stay clear of the things that kill you, and do literally everything fucking
> else … most of them are related to upholding virtues. So I think if you're
> honest and respect others and are well-intentioned and candid … you can for
> the most part avoid the things that could lastingly harm you.

So the guardrail is not a tax on Strong/Fast — it's the precondition for
spending them recklessly. A small set of inviolable virtues fences off the
catastrophic outcomes, which is precisely what makes "do everything else" safe.

**Suggested name (the seed flagged it was unsure):** candidates the blog
supports — *Bound*, *Lawful*, *Trustworthy*, *Predictable*. Given the mechanism
is "pre-commit + let others verify," **Trustworthy** or **Bound** may fit better
than "Cooperative," which undersells the self-binding.

## Cross-cutting tensions to resolve with Fable

- **Open vs. Kind/Cooperative.** The blog's biggest live conflict: candor whose
  cost lands on the receiver ("emotions important?", "Please forgive the errors
  in my ways", "My pattern of disregarding people's immediate preferences"). The
  five principles can actively pull against each other; the system needs an
  ordering or arbitration rule, not just five virtues in parallel.
- **Strong/Fast vs. Cooperative.** "True ambition" and "violent passion" point
  one way; "choose virtue over power" (the Superman reading in "My pattern …")
  points the other. The blog's resolution is *asymmetry*: Cooperative is a hard
  constraint, Strong/Fast optimize within it.
- **The unifying root.** If "all the remaining problems [are] some form of
  information asymmetry" given a shared goal, then Open is doing most of the
  work and the others are downstream. Worth pressure-testing whether the five
  are really one principle (reduce information asymmetry between aligned agents)
  plus its enabling conditions.
- **Is Kind "delight" or "attention"?** (see §2). And is Strong individual or
  positional? (see §3). Both are places the seed phrasing and the blog phrasing
  disagree.
