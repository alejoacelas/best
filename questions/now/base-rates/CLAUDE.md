# base-rates
<!--/ai-->

<!--ai-->
For many important decisions the honest question isn't "what's my clever
argument?" but "what's the base rate?" Pick the reference class you'd actually
want to belong to, then look at how often some behavior, choice, or condition
holds within it. Let that frequency be the prior.
<!--/ai-->

<!--ai-->
Two ways to use it:
- As an **intuition pump** — the rate tells you where the weight of the class
  sits, and you reason from there.
- As a **decision rule** — if the thing is common enough among the people you'd
  most want to resemble, that can be reason enough to just do it, and the burden
  shifts onto the case for deviating.
<!--/ai-->

<!--ai-->
Method:
- **Name the class deliberately** — who you'd want to be, not who's merely
  nearby. Choosing the reference class right is most of the work.
- **Read the rate, not the anecdote.** One vivid exemplar is a story; the
  frequency across the class is the evidence.
- **Discount the biases, but weigh the payoff — not just the odds.**
  Survivorship, selection, and secular trends inflate any rate read off the
  successful; it's what they did, not proof it was optimal. Yet a low rate isn't
  a veto. If the class fails 90% of the time but the surviving 10% capture impact
  a thousand times larger, the bet is still worth taking. Read the frequency
  against the size and sign of the payoff, never on its own.
- **Then decide** — adopt what's common enough; make any deviation earn its keep.
<!--/ai-->

<!--ai-->
## Layout
- One folder per question (`children/`, `bipolar-ii/`) — each holds the current
  base-rate pass for that question. A pass is: the decision, the reference class
  chosen for it, the rate that class shows, and what it implies.
- `reference-classes.md` — the reusable family of classes (the compounding-career
  spine, and the sign / era / type axes we cut along). Passes cite a cut from here
  instead of re-deriving one; the interesting move is often comparing cuts.
