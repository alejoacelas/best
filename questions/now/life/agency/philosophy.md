# The philosophy of trying

Analytic philosophy of action, on the disposition to actually try to solve one's problems
rather than passively accept them. Every quotation below was verified against the source;
the few confirmed only via a secondary source, or not at all, are flagged.

The organizing point: action theory has fixated on **one** break in the chain from judgment
to action — *akrasia*, knowing the better and doing the worse. But the "helplessness" this
question is about is usually a **different** break: **never forming the intention at all** —
the option never enters the choice set. Sections 1–2 map the breaks; section 3 supplies the
scale (grades of agency) that makes "more or less an agent" a rigorous idea rather than a
metaphor.

---

## 1. Akrasia / weakness of will

The precise puzzle is **synchronic**: at the moment of action the agent does *b* intentionally
and freely, while judging that, all things considered, *a* would be better. This is distinct from
the failure the akrasia framing hides: **never forming or sustaining the effective intention** —
a motivational/inertial break, not a cognitive one. The break can fall anywhere along
evaluation → judgment → intention-formation → action.

### Aristotle — the classical puzzle (*Nicomachean Ethics* VII)
Socrates denied akrasia is even possible: knowledge can't be "dragged about" by appetite.
Aristotle resolves it by distinguishing *having* knowledge from *using* it — the *akratēs* holds
the universal premise but, under passion, fails to actualize the *particular* one, possessing
knowledge only as "a man asleep, mad, or drunk" does.
- "it would be strange—so Socrates thought—if when knowledge was in a man something else could master it and drag it about like a slave."
- "the possession of knowledge in a sense and yet not having it, as in the instance of a man asleep, mad, or drunk."
- [Ross translation](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.7.vii.html) (verified verbatim)

### Davidson — ["How Is Weakness of the Will Possible?"](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/weakness-will/) (1970)
Recasts akrasia as a problem in action theory. He distinguishes **conditional / all-things-considered
judgments** ("relative to all considerations *C*, *a* is better than *b*") from **unconditional,
"all-out" judgments** (which he equates with forming an intention). The akratic's better judgment is
only the *relational* one, which can't logically contradict the all-out judgment he acts on — so
incontinence is possible without contradiction, but violates the **principle of continence** (act on
the judgment supported by *all* your reasons).
- Incontinent action: "In doing *b* an agent acts incontinently if and only if: (a) the agent does *b* intentionally; (b) the agent believes there is an alternative action *a* open to him; and (c) the agent judges that, all things considered, it would be better to do *a* than to do *b*."
- Principle of continence: "perform the action judged best on the basis of all available relevant reasons." (continence sentence verified via secondary transcription + SEP; primary: [*Essays on Actions and Events*](https://academic.oup.com/book/3354/chapter/144430774))

### Holton — [weakness of will is *not* akrasia](http://web.mit.edu/holton/www/pubs/Weakness.pdf) (1999)
Breaks with the whole tradition. Ordinary speakers call the weak-willed person *irresolute* —
abandoning a plan he set himself — not acting against a judgment of the good. Building on Bratman,
weakness of will is the *skill-failure* of revising intentions too readily, and can occur even when
no judgment of the best enters. **This is the break closest to the "helplessness" question.**
- "the central cases of weakness of will are best characterized not as cases in which people act against their better judgement, but as cases in which they fail to act on their intentions."
- "Weakness of will arises, I shall suggest, when agents are too ready to reconsider their intentions."
- "Let us grant the term 'akrasia' to refer to action voluntarily undertaken against one's best judgement… Then my contention will be that weakness of will is not akrasia."

## 2. The theory of action / trying

### O'Shaughnessy — trying as the "mental pineal gland"
A *volitionalist* theory: trying (willing) is the psychologically primitive, non-composite element
present in *every* action, including successful bodily action — the point where mind meets body.
Trying is not reserved for failure; it is the **common factor** across success and the thwarted
attempt, differing only in downstream effect. *The Will: A Dual Aspect Theory* (1980).
- Shared O'Shaughnessy/Hornsby thesis, per SEP: "the agent's moving his leg is triggered and sustained by the agent's *trying* to move his leg in just that way, and that the efficacious trying is itself an action." ([SEP, Action](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/action/))
- (His own definition of trying couldn't be confirmed from the paywalled 1973 paper — omitted rather than quoted.)

### Hornsby — [trying as the fundamental unit of action](https://www.newdualism.org/papers-Jul2020/Hornsby-14648.pdf) (*Actions*, 1980)
An action just *is* a trying: the trying is identical with the action, not a mental precursor, and
is the "common denominator" between success and failure — which is why the theory handles the
paralyzed attempt without a queer category of "mere tryings."
- "(T) ensures that every action is a person's trying to do something."
- "'try to ——' appears as a sort of common denominator, which is present both in intentional doings and in unsuccessful tryings."
- "I say that a person's trying to do something *is* an action."

### Frankfurt — [identification / second-order desires](https://www.phil.uu.nl/~joel/3027/Frankfurt-FreedomWillConceptPerson.pdf) (1971)
First-order desires (to do X) vs. *second-order desires* (about which first-order desires to have).
The key case is the *second-order volition* — wanting a given desire to be one's *will*, the desire
that actually moves one. This capacity is constitutive of **personhood**. A *wanton* has first-order
desires but no second-order volitions — "does not care about his will."
- "they are able to form what I shall call 'second-order desires.'"
- "when he wants a certain desire to be his will… I shall call his second-order desires 'second-order volitions.'"
- "it is having second-order volitions… that I regard as essential to being a person."
- "The essential characteristic of a wanton is that he does not care about his will."

### Bratman — planning agency and self-governance (*Intention, Plans, and Practical Reason*, 1987)
Intentions aren't belief–desire pairs but elements of *partial, hierarchical plans* filled in over
time. Plans impose consistency and means–end coherence, resist reconsideration (stability), and
coordinate action across time — the structure a resource-bounded, temporally extended agent needs.
Later work grounds *self-governance* in higher-order **self-governing policies** about which desires
count as reasons.
- Self-governing policies have "the authority to speak for the agent." (SEP, quoting Bratman 2000)
- Full-blown agency: "the *agent* is the source of, determines, directs, governs the action, and is not merely the locus of … causal pushes and pulls." ([SEP, Agency](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/agency/))

## 3. Grades of agency — the rigorous core under "agent thickness"

There *is* a philosophical scale, though the SEP terms are **"full-blown agency"** (Bratman) and
**"human action *par excellence*"** (Velleman) — and whether it's a true *continuum* is itself
contested.

**Minimal → standard → full-blown** ([SEP, Agency](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/agency/),
Schlosser). Bottom: "minimal agency" — goal-directed self-maintenance in very simple organisms, no
mental representation. Middle: the "standard theory" of *intentional agency*. Top: several hold the
middle captures only *deficient* instances.
- Velleman: "the standard theory captures only deficient instances of agency, in which the agent's participation is 'unwitting' or 'halfhearted'… It fails to capture 'human action par excellence'."
- Schlosser's caution: the scale "creates a rather implausible dichotomy between a kind of agency in which the agent does participate and a kind of agency in which the agent does not participate."

**Autonomy / self-governance as the thicker grade** (Frankfurt, Bratman). One governs oneself by
identifying with a desire via a higher-order attitude; [SEP *Personal Autonomy*](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/personal-autonomy/)
grounds this authority "in the simple fact that she alone can initiate her actions," and treats
weakness of will as governing oneself less "thoroughly" — a matter of degree.

**Korsgaard — action as self-constitution** (*Sources of Normativity*, 1996; *Self-Constitution*,
2009). Constitutivism grounds the whole scale in the *reflective structure of human consciousness*:
because we can turn attention on our own desires, no desire is automatically a reason — we must
endorse one, and in endorsing it we self-legislate, which *is* constituting oneself as an agent. The
self is action's product, not its presupposition.
- "The reflective mind cannot settle for perception and desire, not just as such. It needs a reason."
- "When you deliberate, it is as if there were something over and above all of your desires, something which is you, and which chooses which desire to act on."
- Practical identity: a "description under which you value yourself and find your life worth living and your actions to be worth undertaking." ([reflection PDF](https://phil971-2020.colinmclear.net/materials/readings/korsgaard-reflection.pdf); *Self-Constitution* page cites via [review](https://www.uvm.edu/~rharp/Academic/CKreview.pdf))

**The clearest marker of "thin" agency: the wanton** (Frankfurt) — acts on first-order desires,
forms no second-order volition. Korsgaard argues a fully reflective creature *cannot* be a genuine
wanton, "because of the reflective structure of human consciousness." SEP casts the lower reaches as
"deficient" or, per Mele, the "shrinking agent."

## The take-away for this question
The philosophy and the blogosphere ([blogosphere.md](blogosphere.md)) address **two different breaks
in the same causal chain.** Classical action theory (Aristotle, Davidson) fixes on the break *between
all-things-considered judgment and action* — akrasia. But Holton's relocation of "weakness of will"
to *over-ready revision of an already-formed intention*, and the phenomenon of *never forming the
intention at all*, are precisely what the folk concept of "agency" targets: not "I judged X best but
did Y," but "I never planted the flag." The scaffolding for *why that is a deficiency rather than a
quirk* is the grades-of-agency literature — full-blown/self-governed agency (Bratman), the *wanton*
(Frankfurt), self-constitution (Korsgaard). One caution to carry: whether that is a true continuum or
"an implausible dichotomy" is live (Schlosser).
