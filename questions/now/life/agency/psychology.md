# The psychology of not-trying

What the research literature says about why people leave *solvable* problems unsolved —
under-attempt, quit early, or never start. Ordered from the finding that most directly
reframes the question (learned helplessness) to the sturdiest behavioral effects.

A pedigree warning runs through all of it: these literatures were mostly built before the
2011–2015 replication reckoning. The older animal/clinical work on helplessness has been
*biologically overturned*; the mechanism-light trait constructs (self-efficacy, hope, locus
of control) survive as **correlational predictors** whose **causal** status is genuinely
disputed. Each section flags where it sits.

---

## 1. Learned helplessness — and its 2016 reversal

**The original.** Dogs given inescapable shock later failed to escape shock even when escape
became trivially available. The classic reading: they *learned* their responses were
independent of outcomes ("learned helplessness"), and this became a model of depression.
- Overmier & Seligman (1967), [*J. Comp. Physiol. Psychol.*](https://doi.org/10.1037/h0024166); Seligman & Maier (1967), [*J. Exp. Psychol.*](https://doi.org/10.1037/h0024514)
- Human extension: Hiroto & Seligman (1975), [*JPSP*](https://doi.org/10.1037/h0076270) — students given unsolvable problems then under-attempted solvable anagrams.
- Attributional reformulation: Abramson, Seligman & Teasdale (1978), [*J. Abnormal Psych.*](https://doi.org/10.1037/0021-843X.87.1.49) — *why* you attribute the failure (internal-stable-global vs. external-unstable-specific) governs how far the helplessness spreads. This "pessimistic explanatory style" line is the empirically healthier descendant.

**The reversal — the load-bearing item.** Maier & Seligman (2016), "Learned Helplessness at
Fifty: Insights from Neuroscience," [*Psychological Review*](https://doi.org/10.1037/rev0000033)
([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27337390/)). The originators inverted their own theory:
- **Passivity is not learned; it is the default.** Prolonged aversive stimulation *automatically* produces passivity via serotonergic neurons in the **dorsal raphe nucleus** — the animal never needs to learn that responding is futile. Helplessness is the unlearned, wired-in mammalian reaction to sustained adversity.
- **What is actually learned is CONTROL.** The **ventromedial prefrontal cortex** detects when a stressor is controllable and, when it does, inhibits the passivity circuit. Presence of control, not absence of it, is the thing the brain computes and learns.
- **So the 1967 name is a misnomer** (the authors' own word). Organisms don't learn helplessness from uncontrollability; they *fail to engage the control-detection circuit*. This flips the intervention logic from "unlearn the helpless association" to **"install the experience of control"** — and fits why *early experience of control* immunizes against later uncontrollable stress.

**Status.** The behavioral phenomenon replicates broadly. The *mechanism* is what changed —
authored by the originators, grounded in three decades of rodent optogenetics, so about as
authoritative a within-field reversal as exists. The human experiments and the
depression-as-helplessness model are the softer part; the attributional reformulation is
where most surviving human evidence sits.

## 2. Self-efficacy (Bandura)

**Finding.** Belief in one's capability to execute what a situation requires predicts whether
people *attempt* a task, how much *effort* they invest, and how long they *persist* — over
and above actual skill. Bandura (1977), [*Psychological Review*](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.84.2.191).

- Domain-specific, not global self-esteem. Built from four sources in descending potency: **mastery experiences** (actually doing it — most powerful), vicarious experience, verbal persuasion, physiological/emotional arousal.
- Distinguishes *efficacy expectations* (can I do the behavior?) from *outcome expectations* (will the behavior produce the result?).

**Status — predictor solid, lever disputed.** The self-efficacy→performance correlation is one
of the most replicated in applied psychology (meta-analytic ρ ≈ 0.38; [Stajkovic & Luthans
1998](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.124.2.240)). But Vancouver showed self-efficacy can be
*negatively* related to later performance within-person (overconfidence breeds complacency),
and Sitzmann & Yeo (2013), [*Personnel Psychology*](https://doi.org/10.1111/peps.12035), found
the relation largely *reflects past performance* rather than driving future performance.
Robust as a **marker**, disputed as a **cause**.

## 3. Problem-solving mechanics — and "does just listing options unstick you?"

**Means–ends analysis / problem-space search.** Newell & Simon (1972), *Human Problem Solving*.
Humans search a **problem space** (initial state, goal state, operators) using heuristics —
chiefly finding the largest difference between current and goal state, selecting an operator to
reduce it, recursing via subgoals. Foundational framework, not a falsifiable effect.

**Functional fixedness.** Duncker (1945), [*Psychological Monographs*](https://doi.org/10.1037/h0093599).
The candle problem: people fail to mount a candle because they see the tack *box* as a container,
not a mountable platform — prior function blocks novel use. Robust, repeatedly replicated.

**Einstellung (mechanization of thought).** Luchins (1942), [*Psychological Monographs*](https://doi.org/10.1037/h0093502).
After a run of problems solvable by one formula, people keep applying it even when a far simpler
solution exists. Strongly re-demonstrated by Bilalić, McLeod & Gobet (2008),
[eye-tracking chess experts](https://doi.org/10.1080/17470210701822621): the first idea literally
*directs gaze away* from better moves. **This is the mechanistic heart of "stuck":** a prior
solution captures attention and suppresses search.

**Does just listing/generating options dissolve a felt-stuck problem?** No single clean theory
names that exact operation, but three convergent strands explain it:
- **Fixation-release.** Stuckness is often *premature closure on one path*. Forcing enumeration breaks the attentional capture — the value of "list five ways" is not the fifth idea but that generating it dislodges the first. Incubation works the same way ([Sio & Ormerod 2009 meta-analysis](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0014212)).
- **Brainstorming, with a caveat.** Osborn's "generate many options" is only partly supported: **nominal groups (individuals listing separately) reliably out-produce interacting groups** ([Diehl & Stroebe 1987](https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.53.3.497)) due to production blocking and evaluation apprehension. The replicated active ingredient is *deferring judgment and separating generation from selection*, not quantity.
- **Pathways thinking** (§5) names the felt-stuck experience as low *pathways* cognition; generating routes is exactly the pathways operation.

Cleanest available statement: **listing dissolves stuckness when the block is fixation/premature
closure rather than genuine problem-space size** — and helps most when it's individual and
evaluation-deferred. No unified model predicts the exact threshold.

## 4. Procrastination / akrasia — the intention→action gap and its fix

**Meta-analysis.** Steel (2007), [*Psychological Bulletin*](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.133.1.65)
([PDF](https://studypedia.au.dk/fileadmin/www.studiemetro.au.dk/Procrastination_2.pdf)). Across
691 correlations, procrastination is predicted by **task aversiveness, delay to reward, low
self-efficacy/expectancy, and impulsiveness** — and only weakly by neuroticism or rebelliousness.

**Temporal Motivation Theory.** Motivation = (Expectancy × Value) / (1 + Impulsiveness × Delay).
Fuses expectancy theory with **hyperbolic discounting**: a distant deadline shrinks a task's
present value, so a more immediately rewarding activity wins; as the deadline nears, effort finally
appears. This is *why intention doesn't convert to action* — intentions form in a cool, far-off
frame; action must be produced in a hot, near frame where impulsiveness dominates. The gap is
quantified: a medium-to-large change in intention yields only a small change in behavior, d ≈ 0.36
([Webb & Sheeran 2006](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.132.2.249)).

**The fix — implementation intentions.** Gollwitzer (1999), [*American Psychologist*](https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.54.7.493).
Pre-commit an **if–then plan** ("If situation X arises, then I will do Y"), delegating action
initiation to the environmental cue and bypassing the moment-of-choice where impulsiveness wins.
Meta-analysis d = 0.65 across 94 tests / >8,000 participants ([Gollwitzer & Sheeran 2006](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0065-2601(06)38002-1)).

**Status.** TMT and its predictors are robust. Implementation intentions replicate but d = 0.65 is
likely inflated by publication bias; large field studies show *smaller but real* effects, strongest
for simple "get-started" behaviors. **Casualty to avoid:** the broader "willpower as depletable
resource" (**ego depletion**) failed its 2016 multi-lab replication
([Hagger et al.](https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691616652873), near-zero effect). Steel's account
doesn't depend on it; treat depletion-based explanations as unreliable.

## 5. Hope theory (Snyder)

**Finding.** Hope is a *cognitive* set with two separable, mutually reinforcing components —
**agency thoughts** ("I can get going and keep going") and **pathways thoughts** ("I can find
routes to the goal"). Higher hope predicts goal attainment and coping. Snyder et al. (1991),
[*JPSP*](https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.60.4.570) ([PDF](https://ou.edu/content/dam/Tulsa/research/The-Will-and-the-Ways_Validation-of-Hope_Snyder.pdf)).

Why it belongs: it decomposes "giving up" into two failures that map onto the other threads — an
*agency* deficit (looks like low self-efficacy / helplessness) and a *pathways* deficit (looks like
being problem-space-stuck, §3). Someone can under-attempt because they can't picture any route, or
because they see routes but feel no drive.

**Status.** Psychometrics solid, two-factor structure generally holds. But the evidence is
**overwhelmingly cross-sectional and self-report**, so it shares self-efficacy's causal-direction
ambiguity. A well-validated *measure* with weaker *causal* backing.

## 6. Locus of control (Rotter) and relatives

**Finding.** People hold a generalized expectancy about whether outcomes follow from their own
behavior (**internal**) or from luck/fate/powerful others (**external**); internals attempt,
persist, and gather information more, because they expect effort to pay off. Rotter (1966),
[*Psychological Monographs*](https://doi.org/10.1037/h0092976).

**Relatives.** *Explanatory/attributional style* (§1) is the closest cousin. **Core self-evaluations**
(Judge et al.) statistically *merges* locus of control, self-efficacy, self-esteem, and (low)
neuroticism into one higher-order trait — a caution that these constructs are **less independent
than their separate literatures imply**. Present them as facets of one agency dimension, not four
findings.

**Status.** Durable and predictive but with **modest effect sizes**; the original scale is dated
and much variance overlaps with neuroticism/self-efficacy.

## 7. Believed-possible vs. actually-possible — the help-seeking case

**Finding.** People asking strangers for help **underestimate the compliance rate by roughly
half**. In the canonical study, participants predicted they'd need to ask ~7 people to get one
"yes" when the true number was far lower. Flynn & Bohns (2008),
[*JPSP*](https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.95.1.128) ([Cornell copy](https://ecommons.cornell.edu/items/9c0adbfb-985c-4f7d-aae3-96959822bc4c)).

**Mechanism.** An *asymmetry of perspective* — the asker fixates on the instrumental cost of the
favor; the potential helper feels the interpersonal cost of *saying no*. The reluctance to ask
rests on a **systematic misprediction**, not a correct read of others' unwillingness. Consolidated
in Bohns (2016), [*Current Directions*](https://doi.org/10.1177/0963721415628011): across >14,000
strangers, requesters underestimate compliance by ~48%. Attenuates but persists for friends.

**Status.** Well-replicated field-experimental effect across request types, channels, and cultures —
one of the sturdier findings here precisely because it rests on real behavior, not self-report.

**Adjacent, one line each:** people underestimate how much others *like* them after conversations
(**the liking gap**, [Boothby et al. 2018](https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797618783714)) and how
positively others react to gratitude / reaching out (Kumar & Epley). The pattern: people
systematically underestimate the social upside of acting, which suppresses socially-mediated
attempts — the interpersonal analogue of the intrapersonal under-attempt in §1–§6.

## What to build load-bearing claims on

- **Sturdiest (behavioral, not self-report):** functional fixedness, Einstellung (modern chess replication), and the underestimation-of-compliance effect.
- **Reframe to lead with:** the flagship account of not-trying — learned helplessness — was reversed by its own authors: passivity is the wired-in default, and *agency is the thing that must be learned and can be taught*.
- **Don't lean on:** ego depletion / willpower-as-fuel (failed replication); and treat the *causal* efficacy of self-efficacy and hope as unproven even though their predictive correlations hold.
- **Construct overlap:** self-efficacy, locus of control, hope-agency, and optimistic explanatory style are highly intercorrelated and partly collapse into "core self-evaluations" — facets of one dimension, not independent evidence.
