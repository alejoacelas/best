# Cate Hall on agency

The writer this question started from. Former #1-ranked female poker player, ex–Supreme
Court litigator, startup/foundation operator; got sober and rebuilt her life around age 37.
Writes [Useful Fictions](https://usefulfictions.substack.com); book *You Can Just Do Things*
(with her husband Sasha) forthcoming 2026. Her one-line definition of the subject:

> agency is "the entirely learnable capacity to both see and act on all of the degrees of
> freedom that life really offers."

**Quote fidelity:** the lines below were returned as verbatim from the page text; the two
most-quoted ("if you're only asking…" and "No one is born with it…") are cross-corroborated
across independent sources. The rest are near-verbatim — accurate in substance and almost
certainly in wording. The one label I couldn't confirm ("Melvin") is flagged.

---

## 1. [How to be more agentic](https://usefulfictions.substack.com/p/how-to-be-more-agentic) — the flagship
Also on the [EA Forum](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/WBgzTbyFG4YXnDPXs/how-to-be-more-agentic).

**Claim.** Agency — "manifest determination to make things happen" — is a *learnable skill*, not
a fixed trait. The route to it isn't outworking people; it's finding **"real edges": things you
are willing to do that others aren't, often because they're annoying or unpleasant.** Her
template is poker: in an intensely competitive game, physical reads were a massive edge almost
everyone ignored.

**Her strategies for building agency:**
1. **Court rejection** — deliberately apply for things you expect to be rejected from, to decouple "no" from disappointment.
2. **Seek real feedback** through anonymous channels (she keeps public feedback forms).
3. **Increase your surface area for luck** — the most useful collaborations are unpredictable.
4. **Assume everything is learnable** — confidence, charisma, warmth are trainable, not innate.
5. **Love the moat of low status** — tolerate being visibly bad at a new thing; most people won't, so it's an edge.
6. **Protect against burnout**, "the ultimate agency-killer."

**Sharpest lines:**
- **"If you're only asking for things you get, you're not aiming high enough."**
- **"No one is born with it, everyone can learn it, and it's never too late."** (closing line)
- On luck: "The more confident I am that a conversation is relevant, the less likely I am to discover something exciting."
- On low status: the willingness to endure "a period of time where you are actually bad at the thing or fail to know things that are obvious."
- On burnout: "Burnout is the ultimate agency-killer" — when burned out, "I start looking for ways to rule out ideas and actions."

**Tactics:** apply above your level as rejection practice; keep an anonymous feedback form public;
set hard boundaries (offline by 6 p.m., a Sunday sabbath); discount work-ethic advice from people
who've never hit burnout.

## 2. [Maybe you're not Actually Trying](https://usefulfictions.substack.com/p/maybe-youre-not-actually-trying) — the core post for this question
See also her [Q&A on it at Commoncog](https://commoncog.com/cate-hall-q-a-on-agency/).

**Claim.** Agency isn't global; **"people are selectively agentic."** Someone relentlessly
resourceful at work applies almost no resourcefulness to a stuck relationship or their health —
often because an early failed attempt froze their orientation and they never revisited it. **The
feeling of straining hard is not evidence you're actually trying.**

**Framework:**
- **Three theaters** where selective agency shows up: work; relationships with others; relationship to self (health, emotions, introspection). People are often high-agency in one and near-zero in another.
- **"Faulty sensory appreciation"** (from the Alexander Technique): when stuck in a bad pattern, "the right way feels like the wrong way" — strain feels like effort, so you mistake willpower for progress.

**Sharpest lines:**
- **"People are not just high-agency or low-agency in a global sense, across their entire lives. Instead, people are selectively agentic."**
- **"The feeling of effort doesn't mean that you're Actually Trying."**
- **"The continuous need for willpower may be the sign of a badly-engineered life."**

**Her tests for whether you're Actually Trying:**
- "Have I done my best to come up with a set of potential solutions, using all the resources I have?"
- **The friend test:** "Am I doing as well by myself as I would by any friend who came to me with the same problem?"
- Ask directly: "How do I know I'm Actually Trying?" — and notice if you've simply never generated a real list of options.

## 3. [I used to be a high-performing robot](https://usefulfictions.substack.com/p/i-used-to-be-a-high-performing-robot) — agency is not innate

**Claim.** Read this to be disabused of the idea that agency is innate or that it's too late. Hall
narrates her arc from a dissociative, compulsively ambitious "high-performing robot" — achieving
constantly without genuine choice — through alcoholism and an abusive relationship into sobriety.
Her frame: **addiction is the *opposite* of agency, because it is automaticity.**

**Sharpest lines:**
- **"Agency is not the same as success, and it's not innate. It's something you can learn."**
- **"I was 30 the first time I woke up. Not to any grand revelation. Just to the dumb, obvious fact that I didn't want to live the life I had, and that I didn't have to."**
- "I consider the state of addiction to be the opposite of agency. Agency is the ability to recognize all of the degrees of freedom one has."
- Addiction and agency as "two sets of labels on the same dial… Addiction is automaticity. Agency is its opposite."

**Idea to keep:** distinguish *ambition-as-compulsion* (achievement you can't not do — a survival
strategy) from *ambition-as-agency* (choosing your aims). High output can coexist with near-zero agency.

## 4. [Find a way](https://usefulfictions.substack.com/p/find-a-way) — don't let reality reject you

**Claim.** When you hit an obstacle, treat it as a puzzle to solve rather than a verdict to accept.
Assume a path exists and hunt for it; reality is more malleable than the first "no" implies.

**Sharpest lines:**
- **"Flipping the script from 'that won't work' to 'how do I make this work?' can help you discover degrees of agency you didn't realize you had."**
- **"Rebel against what you see as the limits of reality and you'll find that reality tends to be quite malleable."**

**Tactics:** reframe obstacles as solvable puzzles; assume a solution exists if you want the thing
badly enough; accept that real solutions are often *non-normal* / unconventional; build stamina to
keep attempting past the first failure. (She references avoiding a low-effort, blame-free attempt
that only *looks* like trying — the "Melvin" label is unconfirmed.)

## 5. [Agency is for sociopaths](https://usefulfictions.substack.com/p/agency-is-for-sociopaths) — the moral objection

**Claim.** Answers the objection that agency is selfish (many ruthless people have it; tech culture
lionizes it). Resolution: agency is a **morally neutral, general-purpose tool** — the problem isn't
that good people have too much, it's that they have too little conviction to act on values they
already hold.

**Sharpest lines:**
- **"It's more accurate to think of agency as a general-purpose, morally neutral tool, like physical strength or intelligence."**
- "It would be a mistake… to draw from this a conclusion that agency itself is the problem."
- Her book is dedicated to **"the best who lack conviction"** (inverting Yeats — the best usually "lack all conviction").

**Idea to keep:** the scarce resource among good people isn't capability, it's the *courage to act
on existing values* against social inertia.

## The through-line
One argument in different keys: **agency is learnable, selective, and mostly bottlenecked by
willingness rather than ability.** People under-attempt solvable problems because (a) they're only
agentic in some theaters and never notice the frozen ones, (b) they mistake the feeling of strain
for actually trying, (c) they accept the first "no" instead of treating it as a puzzle, and (d)
they won't pay the social costs — rejection, low status, looking cringe — that constitute the real
edges. Her prescriptions are behavioral and uncomfortable by design: court rejection, aim past what
you'll get, tolerate being visibly bad, and ask whether you'd accept this level of effort from a friend.
