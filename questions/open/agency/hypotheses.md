# Hypotheses

Competing and complementary explanations of the question, each stated with enough precision to
be argued with or tested. This is the one file that synthesizes; the angle files
([psychology](psychology.md), [cate-hall](cate-hall.md), [philosophy](philosophy.md),
[blogosphere](blogosphere.md)) hold the raw sourced material each draws on.

## The explanandum, stated precisely

Let a problem *X* be **P-solvable** iff there exists a sequence of actions available to person *P*,
at a total cost *P* would on reflection accept, that resolves *X*. The phenomenon to explain —
call it **under-attempting** — is:

> *P leaves a P-solvable problem X unsolved, and P would, on reflection, prefer X solved at its
> actual cost.*

This deliberately excludes **rational non-attempt** (cost genuinely exceeds value) and **genuine
inability** (*X* is not P-solvable). What's left is the residual the folk word "helpless" points at:
attempts that *would* pass a cost–benefit test *P themselves* endorses, not made.

The hypotheses locate the break at different points in the chain a solved problem must pass through:

**perceive a problem → generate options → evaluate them → form an intention → execute → persist**

Most of the interesting action is *upstream* of where classical philosophy looks (akrasia sits at
form-intention → execute). The competition among hypotheses is largely: *which stage is the usual
bottleneck?*

---

## Stage 1 — Perceiving the problem

### H1 · Non-representation
**The condition is never encoded as a problem-with-candidate-solutions; it is experienced as a fixed
feature of the world, like weather.** The scarce cognitive act is the *re-categorization* of a
standing condition into a target. On this hypothesis, brainstorming "works" not by finding the answer
but by performing that re-categorization — the felt relief of listing options is the condition
becoming a problem.
- **Predicts:** prompting "list five things that would change X" dissolves stuckness *even when no listed option is adopted*; people report surprise that X was "solvable at all," not that a *particular* route existed.
- **Distinguishes from H3 (efficacy):** the person isn't estimating P(success) low — they're not estimating at all, because X isn't in the problem-space.
- **Sources:** Salamon's "[learned blankness](blogosphere.md)" (a domain "viewed as inaccessible"); Newell & Simon's problem-space ([psychology §3](psychology.md)); Cate Hall's friend test surfaces exactly the un-generated option set ([cate-hall §2](cate-hall.md)).

### H2 · Fixation (Einstellung)
**The first solution that comes to mind captures attention and actively suppresses search for
better or any ones; "stuck" is premature closure, not problem-space size.** This is the best-evidenced
mechanical account of feeling blocked.
- **Predicts:** interventions that *break the first frame* (incubation, forced enumeration, an outside view) outperform interventions that add effort within it. Eye-tracking shows the first idea directs gaze *away* from better options (Bilalić et al.).
- **Effect status:** sturdy, behavioral, replicated in expert chess ([psychology §3](psychology.md)).
- **Relation:** the mechanistic under-layer for Cate Hall's "an early failed attempt froze their orientation" — H2 is why the freeze persists.

## Stage 2 — Generating options

### H3 · Option-generation failure ≠ akrasia
**The dominant failure is that the effective option never enters the choice set — not that P judged
it best and failed to act (akrasia).** Action theory has over-studied the intention→execution break
and under-studied the perceive→generate break, because it presupposes the option is already before
the agent.
- **Precise contrast:** akrasia is "I judged *a* best, did *b*" (Davidson). This hypothesis is "*a* was never a candidate." Holton's relocation of *weakness of will* to over-ready revision of an already-formed intention is a third, distinct break — none reduces to the others ([philosophy §1](philosophy.md)).
- **Predicts:** most under-attempted problems, on debrief, reveal the person *never generated* the action that would have worked — not that they weighed and rejected it. The corrective is a generation prompt, not a willpower prompt.
- **Why it matters:** if true, the entire "discipline / motivation" framing of self-help is aimed at the wrong stage.

## Stage 3 — Evaluating options

### H4 · Efficacy-gating with asymmetric error
**Whether P attempts is gated by P's subjective P(success | effort), and this estimate is biased
*low* by an asymmetry: a failed attempt is legible and costly to self-image, a non-attempt is
deniable.** Under-attempting is then the equilibrium of a self-image-protecting agent.
- **Predicts:** attempts rise when failure is made non-diagnostic of ability (framed as experiment, made private, or reattributed) — the mechanism behind Cate Hall's "court rejection" ([cate-hall §1](cate-hall.md)).
- **Evidence & caveat:** self-efficacy robustly *predicts* attempting and persisting (Bandura), but its *causal* status is disputed (Vancouver's negative within-person effects; Sitzmann & Yeo) — so treat efficacy as a marker whose manipulation may or may not move behavior ([psychology §2](psychology.md)).
- **Overlaps:** locus of control, hope-agency, and optimistic explanatory style are facets of the same dimension, not independent support ([psychology §6](psychology.md)).

### H5 · Social-cost mispricing
**The cheap first move — asking, cold-emailing, requesting help — is systematically *overpriced* by
P, who overestimates the probability and cost of rejection.** The best-quantified instance of
under-attempting.
- **Magnitude:** requesters underestimate stranger compliance by ~48%; the asker fixates on the favor's instrumental cost while the helper feels the interpersonal cost of *refusing* (Flynn & Bohns; Bohns 2016) ([psychology §7](psychology.md)). People also underestimate how much others like them (liking gap) and welcome reconnection.
- **Predicts:** interventions that correct the *estimate* (showing true compliance rates) raise ask-rates; this is the empirical core of "you can just do things."
- **Bridges to AI (H9):** if the marginal action is already mispriced as expensive, collapsing its true cost toward zero should have outsized effect.

## Stage 4–5 — Forming the intention, executing, persisting

### H6 · Temporal discounting (the intention→action gap)
**Intentions form in a cool, distant frame; action must be produced in a hot, near frame where
impulsiveness dominates — so intention converts to behavior at a steep discount.** This is akrasia's
empirical shadow.
- **Magnitude:** a medium-large change in intention yields only a small change in behavior (d ≈ 0.36); motivation = (Expectancy × Value)/(1 + Impulsiveness × Delay) (Steel's TMT).
- **The fix that works:** implementation intentions ("if X, then Y") delegate initiation to a cue, bypassing the moment of choice (Gollwitzer; d smaller in field studies but real). **Do not** invoke ego depletion — it failed replication ([psychology §4](psychology.md)).

### H7 · Over-ready revision (Holton) / low persistence
**Even when an intention is formed, it is abandoned at the first friction — the *skill-failure* of
revising a resolution too readily.** Distinct from never-forming (H3) and from discounting (H6).
- **Predicts:** the gap between people isn't in starting but in *continuing past the first "no"* — Cate Hall's "[find a way](cate-hall.md)," PG's "never abandon the root node," Salvatier's "bouncing off" the detail ([blogosphere](blogosphere.md)).
- **Philosophical home:** Holton's account of weakness of will as over-ready reconsideration ([philosophy §1](philosophy.md)).

## Trait- and environment-level (cut across all stages)

### H8 · Agency is learned, domain-local control — not a global trait or a default
**Passivity is the wired-in default; what is learned, and learned *per domain*, is the expectation
of control.** So "helpless" people aren't globally low-agency — they never installed control in *this*
domain, which is why agency looks so selective.
- **The strongest single reframe here.** Maier & Seligman (2016) inverted their own theory: prolonged adversity produces passivity *automatically* (dorsal raphe); the prefrontal cortex learns *controllability* and switches it off. Intervention logic flips from "unlearn helplessness" to "**install an experience of control**" ([psychology §1](psychology.md)).
- **Converges with Cate Hall's "[selectively agentic](cate-hall.md)"** from the opposite direction — she observes the domain-locality; the neuroscience explains why it's the expected shape.
- **Predicts:** a single vivid experience of control in a domain bootstraps attempting there; agency transfers *poorly* across domains absent that experience. Early control immunizes.
- **Philosophical dignity:** the grades-of-agency literature (full-blown agency, the *wanton*, self-constitution) makes "more or less an agent" rigorous rather than metaphorical — though whether it's a true continuum is contested (Schlosser) ([philosophy §3](philosophy.md)).

### H9 · Environmental training / rational generalization
**Most systems people navigate — school, bureaucracy, employment — reward compliance and punish
off-menu initiative, so the base rate of unsanctioned trying being rewarded is genuinely low, and
people *rationally* generalize to not-trying.** On this hypothesis helplessness is partly *correct
learning about a low-agency environment*, not a bias.
- **Predicts:** under-attempting concentrates in exactly the domains where autonomy is historically punished, and lifts fast when people move into higher-agency environments (founder culture, the tweets in [blogosphere](blogosphere.md) are largely people *reporting the environment change*).
- **Tension with H4/H5:** those say the estimate is *biased*; H9 says it may be *accurate for the training distribution* and merely wrong for the new one. Distinguishable by whether correcting the estimate, or changing the environment, moves behavior more.

### H10 · AI as constraint-shift (why this is sharper now)
**AI collapses the cost of the *first attempt* — drafting the email, generating the option set, doing
the research — toward zero, so the binding constraint moves almost entirely from capability to the
disposition to initiate.** The helpless are increasingly helpless *by disposition alone*, which both
raises the return to agency and makes its absence starker.
- **Follows from the stack:** if H1–H3 are real (the bottleneck is upstream — representing the problem and generating options), AI is a near-perfect prosthesis for *exactly those stages*. It cannot form your intention (H6) or persist for you (H7), so it sharpens the residual down to the genuinely dispositional core.
- **Predicts:** the variance in outcomes explained by "did they even try with the tool" rises over time; the marginal return to H5-style corrected asking (now near-free to draft) rises.
- **Status:** a forward claim, not an established finding — the one hypothesis here to hold most tentatively and revisit.

## How the hypotheses relate

- **Not mutually exclusive.** The honest model is a *chain with multiple leaky stages*; the empirical question is which stage dominates for a given person/domain. A clean research move: for a sample of under-attempted problems, classify each break by stage (never-represented / fixated / never-generated / rejected-on-bad-estimate / intended-but-discounted / abandoned-early) and see where the mass sits.
- **The two best-evidenced, load-bearing claims:** H8 (the Maier–Seligman inversion — agency is learned control, passivity the default) and H5 (social-cost mispricing — the ~48% underestimate). Build on these first; they rest on behavioral data, not self-report.
- **The most decision-relevant if true:** H3 (option-generation, not willpower, is the usual bottleneck) — because it says the entire motivational framing of self-improvement aims at the wrong stage.
- **The one to hold loosely:** H10 (AI constraint-shift) — plausible and consequential, but forward-looking.
