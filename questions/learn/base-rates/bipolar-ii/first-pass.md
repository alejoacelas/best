## The question, sharpened

You're optimizing the *integral* of output over a long life, and treating suicide as
a loss only through foregone impact. That rules out the usual clinical answer
("stay stable") as question-begging: stability is only instrumental. The live
question is whether the elevated states are the **engine** of impact (so damping
costs you) or its **toxic overdose** (so damping preserves you). The reference class
and the evidence answer from two directions and mostly agree.

## What the reference class shows

**The pattern is "keep the engine, damp the tail," not "ride vs damp."** Across the
strong matches, the consistent productive core is the sub-psychotic engine — low
sleep-need on a hyperthymic baseline. Wherever the **psychotic tail** shows up
(SUBPSY fails), it reads as pure cost: Schumann's asylum death at 46, van Gogh's
suicide at 37, Kanye's ruinous public collapse, Lowell's ~16 hospitalizations. The
BP-II profile is, in effect, the sweet spot — enough activation to power a
compounding career, not enough to shatter it.

**Damped-and-compounded vs. rode-and-spiked.** The two members with the broadest
*utilitarian* impact are both damped: **Kay Redfield Jamison** built the modern
science of the illness, and **Ted Turner** built CNN and gave away ~$1B — and both
credit stabilization with letting them keep going for decades. The pure *riders*
cluster at the extremes: the highest single peaks (Byron, Schumann, Balzac) *and*
the most ruin and the shortest lives. Riding looks like a **higher-variance bet** —
bigger peak, fatter "impact left on the table" tail.

**The template case is a hybrid.** Turner *rode the founding and damped the build* —
his boldest bets (the superstation, CNN's 1980 launch) predate his 1985 lithium;
stabilization then let him steward and compound them. That's the roster's implicit
prescription for a builder: the disinhibited boldness is most valuable at the
high-variance founding moments, least valuable for the 30-year compounding that
follows.

**The big confound, stated honestly.** Every damped case is modern, because lithium
only arrived ~1949–70; every historical case rode because it *had* to. So
"damped people compound longer" is partly just "modern people live longer and get
medical care." Survivorship bites twice — we only have the modern damped cases
because they survived to disclose, and the historical riders we remember are the
ones mania didn't kill first. And there's a **domain** split: builders/leaders
(Roosevelt, Turner, Jamison) seem to benefit from stability — an institution needs
someone who shows up for decades — while artists (Byron, Schumann, Lowell) show
tighter mood-output coupling, and in the one within-person natural experiment,
Lowell *felt* lithium cost the work even as it saved the life. Treat the damp>ride
signal as a hypothesis the class is *consistent with*, not one it proves.

**One absence is itself data:** there's no cleanly self-disclosed bipolar case among
top tech/science founders. Musk has only speculated about himself. That's almost
certainly stigma/selection (in fields where the label is a liability, nobody
discloses), not a real base rate — so the living/disclosed set skews to
entertainers and clinicians who could afford to speak. A confound for anyone in a
building/analytical domain trying to see themselves in it.

## What the evidence shows

The reference class is suggestive; the population evidence is where the weight
actually is, and it leans the same way — *damp, but tune* — for a reason that
reframes the whole thing.

**The strong side (cohorts, registries):**
- **In BP-II the thing you'd suppress is almost all depression.** Long-term
  weekly-rating cohorts: BP-II patients are symptomatically depressed ~50% of weeks
  vs. hypomanic ~1.3% — a ~39:1 ratio (Judd 2003). Depression, not hypomania, is
  what demonstrably eats workdays. You are not choosing to keep a productive high;
  you're choosing whether to keep the *cycle* whose other pole devours half your
  weeks.
- **The achievement engine is a stable, heritable trait — not the episodes.** The
  creativity/achievement signal is carried by *mild-trait* carriers and even by
  *unaffected relatives* (Kyaga); top school performance at 16 *precedes* and
  predicts later bipolar risk (MacCabe); output follows an inverted-U that *degrades*
  in full episodes; reward/goal drive is stable across mood states. If the trait does
  the work and the episodes do the damage, damping the episodes while keeping the
  trait is the output-maximizing move. This is the spine of the answer.

**The ride side is weaker than its reputation:**
- Its one hard fact is real — mood-stabilizer **blunting exists**, but it's small,
  dose-dependent, and drug-specific (lithium's creativity effect ~0.33; ~50% report
  cognitive side-effects at standard doses) — and **lamotrigine largely spares it**
  (well-suited to BP-II's depression-predominant course). That's an argument about
  *how* to damp, not *whether*.
- The rest reverses on inspection: even Schou's famous 24-artist study runs 2:1
  *toward* improved productivity on lithium; hypomania inflates *self-rated*
  originality more than measured originality (a "creativity mirage"); and the
  stopped-meds-to-create memoirs (Forney, others) mostly conclude stability served
  the work better.

**Two "damp" pillars to *not* over-lean on:** kindling (episode sensitization is
real, but cycle-acceleration is likely a statistical artifact, and per-episode
"brain damage" is overstated) and lithium's anti-suicide effect (large
observational signal, statistically fragile in the cleanest RCT meta-analyses).
Direction consistent, magnitude oversold.

**Jamison is the keystone natural experiment**, because she ran it both ways and is
also the expert. She stopped lithium repeatedly to keep the highs — "difficult to
give up the high flights of mind and mood" — and it nearly killed her (a near-fatal
depression, a suicide attempt). But the *naive* guardrail over-damped exactly as
riders fear: on early high-dose lithium, an avid reader, she "could barely focus
long enough to get through a single paragraph." The resolution wasn't abandonment,
it was **calibration** — when the dose was lowered, the blunting "greatly
diminished without removing its effectiveness." Her verdict that she'd still choose
the illness for its intensity is *explicitly conditional*: "If lithium were not
available to me, or didn't work for me, the answer would be a simple no." Don't
ride; don't over-damp; calibrate.

## The synthesis — it's not ride vs damp, it's how to damp

Put the two together and the romantic framing inverts. The engine is the
**temperament** (which treatment leaves largely intact); the episodes are its
**toxic overdose**; and in BP-II the **depressions — not the lost highs — are what
shorten the productive life you're trying to integrate.** So the counterfactual that
maximizes lifetime impact isn't "ride the highs" and isn't "flatten everything":

> Suppress the destroyers (depression, full episodes, suicide risk) with the most
> cognitively benign tool at the minimum effective dose, and let the stable,
> trait-level drive that treatment leaves intact do the productive work over decades.

Your own plan — lamotrigine, minimum effective dose, protect sleep — *is* this, and
the evidence supports it. But it reframes *why*: not "damp because downside is
scary," but "the engine is your temperament and survives; the depressive pole is the
tax on lifetime output; the highs you'd be protecting are the rare, borrowed-against
front edge of the cycle that delivers those depressions."

**The one thing the class genuinely can't resolve** is the frontier your instinct is
circling: whether *sub-hypomanic* elevation — real activation that never tips into a
full episode — can be deliberately preserved as fuel. No study tests it. That's the
question your own daily tracking (sleep, mood, activation, output) is actually built
to answer, on an n of you. The reference class sets the priors; the experiment is
yours to run.

## The high-leverage decisions (the tuned-damp toolkit)

Ranked by leverage, and reframed around impact rather than safety:

1. **Sleep is both the fuel and the trip-wire — and that tension is the whole game.**
   Decreased sleep-*need* is your engine, but sleep *loss* is the on-ramp to the
   destroying tail. Protecting a floor on sleep trades a little peak height for
   staying on the productive side of the line. Highest leverage, strongest evidence
   (IPSRT; sleep is the most common manic prodrome).
2. **Drug choice is where the ride-side's one real cost gets neutralized.** Lamotrigine
   (low blunting, depression-focused) at minimum effective dose; if lithium, keep
   serum low (≤0.8) — Jamison's calibration lesson. Never dose-cut unilaterally.
3. **Prospective tracking = your private RCT.** The daily one-line log (sleep, mood,
   activation, one behavior) is the only instrument that can answer the sub-hypomanic-
   fuel question for you; it doubles as the early-warning signature.
4. **Irreversibility brake.** Cooling-off + a second signature above a threshold —
   the disinhibited-spending / big-decision guard, most valuable exactly when you
   feel most certain.
5. **Defend the depressive pole hardest** — it, not the highs, is where the lifetime
   output actually leaks; pre-set the crash protocol (named, clinician on call,
   commitments dropped) while well.
6. **A human monitoring layer** — 2–3 people licensed in advance to flag the tip and
   trigger the plan.

## Caveats that bite

- **The damp>ride finding is confounded ~1:1 with era, lifespan, survivorship, and
  domain** — and the decisive RCT (real-world output on vs. off treatment) does not
  exist. Everything is cohorts, registries, tasks, and memoirs.
- **Domain skew:** the class is artist/clinician-heavy; for a builder/analytical life
  the stability case is *stronger* (institutions reward showing up for 30 years) —
  but that same skew, plus disclosure stigma in those fields, is itself a confound.
- **The individual minority** who really is mania-dependent for output (Schou's lower
  quartile) is real — but even they pay the depressive tax and the suicide risk.
