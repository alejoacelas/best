A short, honest shelf: how presence and charisma actually work, and one long look
at Paul McCartney — the concert that made me want to understand what "endearing"
is built from. Each line says what the thing gives, whether it's worth it, and how
much to trust it. Free material I could pull sits in [`sources/`](sources/), listed
at the bottom.

## Charisma / style / presence

- **Olivia Fox Cabane, *The Charisma Myth*** — the practical starter. Charisma as
  presence + warmth + power, trained with concrete drills. Self-help register, thin
  evidence, but the framework sticks and the exercises are doable. Worth it as a
  first pass. Buy/borrow.
- **Vanessa Van Edwards, *Cues*** — warmth vs. competence signals, catalogued down
  to specific gestures and words. Pop-science, over-confident about its studies, but
  the most concrete inventory of what reads as warm or capable. Skim for the cues.
  Buy/borrow.
- **John Antonakis, Marika Fenley & Sue Liechti, "Learning Charisma," *HBR* (2012)**
  — the rigorous one. Twelve trainable tactics (nine verbal,
  three nonverbal); executives who practiced them scored ~60% higher on leadership.
  Start here for proof charisma is learned, not born. Paywalled ~$12 —
  [hbr.org](https://hbr.org/2012/06/learning-charisma-2); abstract saved below.
- **Max Weber, on charismatic authority (*Economy and Society*, 1922)** — the
  foundational idea: charisma as a basis of authority, resting on followers' devotion
  to an exceptional person, and inherently unstable until "routinized." Dense, but the
  source everything else stands on. Free excerpt below; full text at the
  [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/stream/MaxWeberEconomyAndSociety/MaxWeberEconomyAndSociety_djvu.txt).
- **Amy Cuddy, *Presence*** — readable on bringing your whole self to high-stakes
  moments. Caveat: its headline "power pose" result failed to replicate, and a
  co-author publicly disavowed it. Read the psychology of self-presentation, discount
  the postures. Borrow.
- **Robert Greene, *The Laws of Human Nature* / *The 48 Laws of Power*** — sharp on
  status, envy and reading people; cynical, cherry-picked from history, mixed as
  advice. Useful as a map of the dark side, not a manual. Borrow, read against the
  grain.
- **Nancy Duarte, *Resonate*** — for the delivery side: how great talks are shaped
  (the "what is / what could be" oscillation). More rigorous than most speaking books,
  built from analyzing real speeches. Worth it if presence-on-stage is the goal.
  Buy/borrow.

**Where to start:** the Antonakis HBR piece (30 minutes, evidence-backed, gives you a
checklist), then Cabane for the drills. Weber when you want the why underneath.

## Paul McCartney

Ranked as a path, not a pile.

1. **Ian Leslie, *John & Paul: A Love Story in Songs* (2025)** — start here for the
   "endearing" question. Traces the Lennon-McCartney bond through 43 songs; a #1
   Sunday Times bestseller and NYT Notable Book, praised for taking the emotional
   relationship seriously without going soft. Closest to *why* the partnership, and
   the man, move people. Buy.
2. **Paul McCartney, *The Lyrics: 1956 to the Present* (ed. Paul Muldoon, 2021)** —
   McCartney narrating 154 of his own songs. The closest thing to his voice on the
   page; where the endearing quality comes from him directly. Two volumes, dip in.
   Buy/borrow.
3. **Barry Miles, *Paul McCartney: Many Years From Now* (1997)** — the authorized
   biography, built on years of McCartney interviews. His own account of the Beatles
   years, so warm and partial by design; read for his perspective, not the referee's.
   Borrow.
4. **Philip Norman, *Paul McCartney: The Life* (2016)** — the thorough independent
   biography. Norman started as a Lennon partisan and came around; the balancing read
   against Miles. Long but the definitive single-volume life. Borrow.
5. **Tom Doyle, *Man on the Run* (2013)** — the messy, human 1970s: post-Beatles
   drift, drug busts, Wings, rebuilding. Where McCartney is most fallible and most
   likeable. Buy/borrow.

**Watch:**
- ***The Beatles: Get Back*** (Peter Jackson, 2021, Disney+) — 8 hours in the room as
  they write and bicker. The single best window into McCartney's working charm and
  how the partnership actually ran. Start here.
- ***McCartney 3,2,1*** (Rick Rubin, 2021, Hulu) — Rubin and McCartney at a mixing
  board, pulling songs apart. Intimate, black-and-white, endlessly endearing; the
  concert-feeling in conversation form.
- ***Man on the Run*** (Morgan Neville, 2025, Prime Video) — the 1970s/Wings years on
  film; same era and title as Doyle's book, different work. Rave reviews.
