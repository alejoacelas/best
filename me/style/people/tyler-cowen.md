# Tyler Cowen

**Thesis:** Tyler treats every subject, person, and stray fact as worth being curious about right now, and asks the one question that assumes you are too. His presence is radical attention delivered at speed.

## The moves

**Radical curiosity as a stance.** Tyler's default is that anything can be interesting if you build the context to see it. This is the practical form of his favorite phrase, "[context is that which is scarce](https://marginalrevolution.com/marginalrevolution/2025/07/context-is-that-which-is-scarce-3.html)": the ideas are cheap, the frame that makes them matter is the scarce thing. So he collects context relentlessly and hands it out. The stance is generous. It flatters no one but assumes everyone is worth a real question.

**The question only he would ask.** On [Conversations with Tyler](https://conversationswithtyler.com/) he reads every book and prior interview a guest has produced, then asks what nobody else thought to. He asks the Portuguese economist about the food in Djibouti, the novelist about French versus Russian ballet. Henry Oliver describes the format as [a chess game](https://www.commonreader.co.uk/p/understanding-conversations-with): the guest's opening moves set expectations, and Tyler "tries to evade and evolve" them with specific, selective, challenging questions. Guests are often told *not* to explain themselves, so the talk starts *in medias res* at high density.

**Asking for himself, not the audience.** His stated rule: start on the question you actually want answered and trust listeners to keep up. He aims "to develop, not defeat" the guest. The questions are never hostile but hard, conceptual, and fast, designed to push someone into a serious mode within seconds.

**The recurring bit.** Tyler runs signature devices that compound over years. He closes interviews on the guest's ["production function"](https://perell.com/podcast/tyler-cowen-production-function/) — how do you get so much done? On the blog he runs the overrated/underrated game, "[solve for the equilibrium](https://marginalrevolution.com/marginalrevolution/2019/04/solve-for-the-equilibrium-10.html)" (figure out where this ends up), "markets in everything," and "there is no great stagnation." Repeated tags become a shared language his readers think in.

**Straussian reading.** Tyler reads for the hidden layer and writes with one. "[So much of the world is Straussian](https://crookedtimber.org/2024/09/18/a-modest-interpretation-of-straussianism-in-action/)," he says — you are sent codes and have to decode them, which he calls a central message of Marginal Revolution from the start. It rewards close readers and lets him say more than the surface text does.

**Deadpan brevity and relentless production.** He blogs multiple times a day, every day, for [over twenty years](https://marginalrevolution.com/), in short flat declarative posts. The affect is dry and unshowy; the volume is the statement. Consistency over decades is itself the style.

## Where it comes from

- **Reading as an athletic practice.** Tyler claims to start [five or ten books a day](https://www.econtalk.org/tyler-cowen-on-reading/) and finish few, quitting instantly when bored. He reads in clusters around a question to do "cross-sectional mental econometrics," and reads fiction to understand people. His one real trick: "read lots, and start a long time ago."
- **Quake books.** A few books shake you and reorganize how you think; most just add a fact. He seeks the [quakes](https://ryanholiday.net/quake-reading/) but reads enormous volume for the small pieces.
- **Preparation as respect.** The CWT questions look effortless because the reading behind them is total. The curiosity is real, but it is also cultivated by a system: read everything, note what's unexplored, ask that.

This is the "cultivable" claim in action. The curiosity is genuinely his, and it is also a machine he built and runs daily.

## What Alejo could take

- **Build context on purpose, then give it away.** Treat the frame as the scarce good. When something bores you, assume you lack context and go get it before dismissing it.
- **Ask the question only you would ask.** Prepare enough that you can skip the obvious opener and start where it gets interesting. Specific beats broad.
- **Own a few recurring moves.** Repeated devices with your name on them become how people think alongside you. Consistency compounds.
- **Quit fast, read wide.** Abandon the boring thing without guilt to make room for volume, and cluster your reading around live questions.
