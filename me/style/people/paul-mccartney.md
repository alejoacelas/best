# Paul McCartney

**Thesis:** McCartney is endearing because he pairs the highest musical competence alive with a boyish, un-guarded warmth and lets you see him still delighted by his own gift — mastery that never stopped being a fan. High competence would earn respect; the warmth and the visible delight turn respect into affection, and affection is what a stadium of strangers feels.

## The moves

**1. The thumbs-up as default setting.** The persona is real before it's a brand: chummy, cheery, thumbs perpetually aloft. *Smash Hits* mocked it as "[Fab Macca Wacky Thumbs Aloft](https://freeasahub.wordpress.com/2022/10/18/back-in-the-sunshine-again-the-rehabilitation-of-pauls-public-image/)," and the gesture has been parodied for forty years. It reads as warmth because it's an unconditional signal — *glad to be here, glad you're here* — offered before you've done anything to earn it. Warmth given first is the thing that lowers a stranger's guard.

**2. Melody itself as warmth.** The gift people call melodic is, socially, a warmth signal: a McCartney tune resolves the way a reassuring sentence does. His bass carries this — he plays a countermelody, "[another voice in the song](https://blog.discmakers.com/2022/04/genius-of-paul-mccartneys-basslines/)," not a metronome. On *Something* he "[spends very little time on the root of each chord](https://blog.discmakers.com/2022/04/genius-of-paul-mccartneys-basslines/)," singing under George's vocal. Lennon: "[Paul is one of the most innovative bass players... half the stuff that's going on now is directly ripped off from his Beatles period.](https://www.openculture.com/2016/12/the-genius-of-paul-mccartneys-bass-playing-in-7-isolated-tracks.html)" The left-handed [Höfner violin bass](https://www.woodbrass.com/blog/en/legend/paul-mccartney-and-the-violin-bass-at-the-heart-of-the-beatles-sound/) matters here — its hollow body "sings" where a Fender growls, so the instrument that anchors the band also sounds friendly.

**3. Treating a stadium as individuals.** He reads the handmade signs out loud. In Las Vegas he stopped to note a fan's [122nd show](https://consequence.net/2025/10/paul-mccartney-got-back-tour-vegas-review/); to a sign about Shea Stadium in 1965 he shot back "[So was I!](https://consequence.net/2025/10/paul-mccartney-got-back-tour-vegas-review/)"; to one request he grinned "[No... that's pushing it.](https://consequence.net/2025/10/paul-mccartney-got-back-tour-vegas-review/)" Naming one person in a crowd of 50,000 makes every other person feel nameable. This is the mechanical core of "making a stadium feel intimate" — reviewers keep reaching for the word, calling the [UK's biggest arena "oddly intimate"](https://louderthanwar.com/17-thoughts-on-paul-mccartney-gig/).

**4. Between-song storytelling that hands over the past.** He doesn't just play the songs; he tells you where they came from. Before *Blackbird* he explains it was written for the civil-rights struggle, so it "[might just help them a little](http://www.meetthebeatlesforreal.com/2016/05/why-did-paul-write-blackbird.html)." He introduces new songs self-deprecatingly — "[Relatively new. It's new enough.](https://consequence.net/2025/10/paul-mccartney-got-back-tour-vegas-review/)" The stories convert spectators into confidants; you leave feeling told-to, not performed-at.

**5. Grief performed in the open.** He plays *Here Today*, written for Lennon, alone on acoustic guitar and regularly gets choked up: "[there's a lot of people crying](https://www.songfacts.com/facts/paul-mccartney/here-today)." The song is built on things unsaid — "[And if I say / I really loved you](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Here_Today_(Paul_McCartney_song))," which he admits he "would never have said to him." On the *I've Got a Feeling* duet with restored Lennon footage: "[I like that song because I get to sing with John again.](https://consequence.net/2025/10/paul-mccartney-got-back-tour-vegas-review/)" A man of unmatched status letting a crowd watch him grieve is the vulnerability that collapses the distance.

**6. Mastery worn as fandom.** In [*McCartney 3,2,1*](https://www.rollingstone.com/tv-movies/tv-movie-reviews/mccartney-3-2-1-tv-review-beatles-rick-rubin-hulu-1196447/) he leans over Rubin's mixing desk and marvels at his own basslines: "[I've grown to be a fan of the Beatles... 'What's that bassline?'](https://www.rollingstone.com/tv-movies/tv-movie-reviews/mccartney-3-2-1-tv-review-beatles-rick-rubin-hulu-1196447/)" He doesn't claim to know the secret. *The Beatles: Get Back* shows the flip side of the same trait — he [invents *Get Back* from vowel-sound gibberish](https://www.moviemaker.com/watch-paul-mccartney-improvise-get-back-in-peter-jacksons-get-back/) in two minutes at Twickenham while the others watch. Genius on display, but curious and playful about it rather than proprietary. The mastery is total; the ego around it is light.

**7. Liverpool groundedness.** He still tells people the Höfner cost him "[about £30](https://www.paulmccartney.com/news/the-lost-bass-how-the-1961-hoefner-bass-found-its-way-home)." The accent, the corny jokes, the ordinariness survived the fame. It's the counterweight that keeps the warmth from reading as a celebrity's condescension — he seems like someone from your town who got very good at one thing.

## Anatomy of the endearing

The cleanest frame is [Fiske's warmth × competence model](../charisma.md): we sort people on two axes, and the combination that produces *admiration and affection* (not mere respect, not mere liking) is **high warmth + high competence**. McCartney maxes both. Competence is not in question — sixty years, the bass Lennon says everyone ripped off, songs the culture treats as folk objects. Warmth is the thumbs-up, the melody, the sign-reading, the corniness.

But high-high alone produces admiration, which keeps its distance. The extra ingredient that turns admiration into *endearment* is **accessible vulnerability that lowers the status gap**: the visible grief in *Here Today*, the fan-of-his-own-work humility, the age. Someone that accomplished letting you see him choke up, or geek out, tells you he isn't guarding status against you. That's the disarming move — competence earns the height, vulnerability gives it back.

**Why it works on strangers.** All the moves are one-directional warmth offered *first*, before the crowd has done anything. You don't have to know him for the thumbs-up, the sign-reading, or the confession to land; they're designed to reach a person he'll never meet. This is the strongest evidence for Alejo's theory that style is the *relationship* — McCartney's presence is almost entirely relational technique, a set of ways of relating to people who are strangers to him.

**Why age deepens it.** Endearment feeds on the vulnerability, and age is vulnerability made unfakeable. The "[beloved elder performer](../charisma.md)" effect: a frail 80-something still delivering competence reads as generosity and mortality at once, and the gap between the fragile body and the intact gift is itself moving. Reviewers now default to gratitude — fans hold up signs that just say "[THANKS PAUL](https://www.mojo4music.com/articles/stories/paul-mccartney-live-in-palm-desert-reviewed-an-almost-endless-cavalcade-of-classics/)." What might have read as lightweight at 40 reads as grace at 83.

## The critique

The charge is that the warmth is the *whole* thing — that pleasantness without edge is lightweight. It starts inside the band: Lennon sneered at Paul's "[granny music](https://freeasahub.wordpress.com/2022/10/18/back-in-the-sunshine-again-the-rehabilitation-of-pauls-public-image/)," calling *Ob-La-Di, Ob-La-Da* "granny shit." The 1980s hardened it into consensus: the perceived crime was "[not being John Lennon](https://freeasahub.wordpress.com/2022/10/18/back-in-the-sunshine-again-the-rehabilitation-of-pauls-public-image/)," the thumbs-aloft persona proof that his gift had "shrivelled" into cosy establishment safeness.

The critique is worth taking seriously because it names the real tradeoff. The same warmth that endears reads, to a certain temperament, as evasion — a refusal to be difficult, dark, or cool. Lennon's danger made him legible as an artist in a way Paul's affability actively worked against. The style optimizes for being *loved*, and being loved and being *respected as deep* pull apart. McCartney chose loved. [*Man on the Run*](https://variety.com/2025/film/reviews/man-on-the-run-paul-mccartney-film-review-wings-documentary-1236505070/) is the corrective — it shows the 1970s cost behind the smile: the post-Beatles depression, the public failures with Wings, a confidence rebuilt "[through graft, humour and an almost religious faith in pop](https://www.loudersound.com/bands-artists/documentary/paul-mccartney-wings-man-on-the-run-documentary-review)." The affability isn't the absence of darkness; it's a decision made on top of it. That's the honest version — not that the critics are wrong about the tradeoff, but that the sunny default was chosen, not given.

## What Alejo could take

- **Warmth as default setting, given first.** The thumbs-up is unconditional and pre-emptive. Offering warmth before someone earns it is the fastest way to lower a stranger's guard, and it costs nothing.
- **Wear mastery as fandom, not property.** The most disarming version of being very good at something is staying visibly delighted by it and curious about how it works. Marvel at the thing rather than guard credit for it.
- **Treat the audience as individuals.** Reading one sign by name makes the whole room feel seen. In any group, naming and remembering one specific person does more than addressing the crowd.
- **Let the competence show the vulnerability.** Height earned by mastery is given back by letting people see you moved, uncertain, or grieving. That combination — not either alone — is what turns respect into affection.
- **Stay from your town.** The groundedness is what keeps warmth from reading as condescension. Keep the plain, the corny, the local; it's the counterweight that makes everything else believable.
