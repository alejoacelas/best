# Paul Graham

**Thesis:** PG writes like a smart friend thinking out loud at your kitchen table — plain words, no throat-clearing, and just enough visible doubt that when he does make a bold claim you believe he earned it.

## The moves

**1. Write like you talk.** The whole style rests on one rule: use spoken language. "[Here's a simple trick for getting more people to read what you write: write in spoken language.](https://paulgraham.com/talk.html)" The test is mechanical: "[Just don't let a sentence through unless it's the way you'd say it to a friend.](https://paulgraham.com/talk.html)" This is why he reads as a person and not an institution.

**2. Short Anglo-Saxon words.** He picks the plain Germanic word over the Latinate one on purpose — "[use simple, germanic words](https://paulgraham.com/writing44.html)." Fewer syllables per idea means the reader spends attention on the thought, not the decoding. "[No one uses "pen" as a verb in spoken English.](https://paulgraham.com/talk.html)"

**3. Concrete first, abstraction after.** He opens with a thing you can see, then earns the generalization. "[Great cities attract ambitious people. You can sense it when you walk around one.](https://paulgraham.com/cities.html)" The claim about ambition arrives only once you're already standing on the street with him.

**4. The earned contrarian turn.** He tells you to distrust received opinion, but frames it as method, not pose: "[Don't do as you're told. Don't believe what you're supposed to.](https://paulgraham.com/essay.html)" Because he's shown his reasoning first, the contrarian line reads as a conclusion you reached together, not a provocation.

**5. Admit uncertainty to gain trust.** He concedes the limits of his own claims, which is exactly what makes the claims credible. "[Putting ideas into words is certainly no guarantee that they'll be right. Far from it. But though it's not a sufficient condition, it is a necessary one.](https://paulgraham.com/words.html)" A writer who marks what he doesn't know has told you he'll also mark what he does.

**6. The aphorism as landing.** He compresses a whole argument into one portable line you can carry off: "[Informal language is the athletic clothing of ideas.](https://paulgraham.com/talk.html)" These are the sentences people quote, and they do the persuading after you've closed the tab.

## Where it comes from / how it's cultivated

PG treats style as a byproduct of a method, not a gift. His stated process:

- **Rewrite relentlessly.** "[Write a bad version 1 as fast as you can; rewrite it over and over; cut out everything unnecessary.](https://paulgraham.com/writing44.html)" The plainness is manufactured in revision, not born. He logged 44 of 67 minutes on "Writing, Briefly" as pure editing.
- **Writing is thinking, not transcription.** "[An essay is something you write to try to figure something out.](https://paulgraham.com/essay.html)" He expects "[80% of the ideas in an essay to happen after you start writing it](https://paulgraham.com/writing44.html)." The essayist's persona — genuinely working it out on the page — is real, because he is.
- **Words are a test of the idea.** "[Writing about something, even something you know well, usually shows you that you didn't know it as well as you thought.](https://paulgraham.com/words.html)" The visible doubt in move 5 comes from here: he's felt his own ideas fail the test, so he doesn't oversell.
- **Aim for surprise.** "[Essays should aim for maximum surprise.](https://paulgraham.com/essay.html)" The contrarian turns aren't contrarianism for its own sake; surprise is the target and disagreement is often where it lives.

This is the direct answer to your "cultivable" intuition. PG's style is almost entirely a set of learnable rules — spoken cadence, cut the unnecessary, concrete before abstract, show your doubt — applied by hand in revision. The personality shows through not by being performed but by removing everything that would hide it.

## What Alejo could take

- **The friend test, literally.** Before a sentence ships, ask: would I say this to a friend? It kills jargon and status-signaling in one move, and it's the fastest route to your own "write things I want to read" rule.
- **Manufacture plainness in the edit.** Draft fast and ugly; make it plain on the second pass by cutting, not by polishing. Density is a subtraction, matching your own PG density test.
- **Concede first, claim second.** Marking uncertainty in `[brackets]` — which you already do — is the same trust move as move 5. Naming the limit before someone else does buys you the strong claim.
- **End on a portable line.** Give each piece one sentence a reader could repeat from memory. That's the unit that travels and does the persuading later.
