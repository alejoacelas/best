# What personal charisma might be

Charisma is the effect you have on other people: they attend to you, warm to you, and let you move them. It is not a substance you carry but a response you produce, so it exists only in the room, between you and someone else. Your own theory already says this — style is "how you relate to other people: how you can inspire them or change them, and how other people relate to you." The three claims folded into that sentence are each right, and each has a literature behind it. Below I name them, connect each to research, then hand you a working model to hang observations on. The four profiles in this folder are four different solutions to the same problem, not four instances of one trait.

## Three claims worth keeping

**1. Charisma is relational, not a possession.** It lives in the effect on others. The founding statement is [Max Weber](https://www.britannica.com/topic/charismatic-authority)'s: *charismatic authority* rests on followers' belief that a person has extraordinary qualities. Weber located it in the relationship, not the character — the gift is *attributed*, and it lasts only while the followers keep attributing it. When the leader dies or the belief cools, charisma has to be "routinized" into offices and rules or it evaporates. The modern restatement is [charisma as a signal](https://www.professormarkvanvugt.com/images/LQ2017charisma.pdf) (Grabo, Spisak & van Vugt, 2017): charismatic behavior evolved as a hard-to-fake advertisement that you can coordinate a group toward urgent collective action, and the audience reads the signal. Both framings put charisma in the receiver. This is why you can't decide you're charismatic; you can only do things others read that way.

**2. It can be cultivated, yet must read as natural.** This is the central tension, and both halves are true. The teachable half: [John Antonakis](https://hbr.org/2012/06/learning-charisma-2) and colleagues isolated twelve **Charismatic Leadership Tactics** (nine verbal — metaphor, story, contrast, rhetorical question, moral conviction, voicing the group's sentiment, three-part lists, high goals, confidence they're reachable; three nonverbal — animated voice, expression, gesture). In their training studies, coaching people on these tactics raised observers' leadership ratings by roughly 60% ([HBR, "Learning Charisma," 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22741422/)). So charisma is a skill, not a birthright — which matches your intuition exactly. The natural half: the tactics only work when the effort is invisible. Visible trying reads as neediness or performance and kills the effect. So the skill you're cultivating is partly the skill of not being seen to cultivate it. [How much of the 60% survives outside the lab, at longer horizons, in people the audience already knows, is genuinely unsettled.]

**3. It's about inspiring and changing people.** The research name for "changing people" is *transformational leadership* — [James MacGregor Burns (1978)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformational_leadership) and Bernard Bass, whose model makes charisma ("idealized influence") plus "inspirational motivation" the core of leaders who shift what followers want, not just what they do. The mechanism underneath is partly *emotional contagion*: [Hatfield, Cacioppo & Rapson (1993)](http://www.elainehatfield.com/uploads/3/2/2/5/3225640/50._hatfield_cacioppo__rapson_1993.pdf) showed people automatically mimic and sync others' expressions, voices, and postures, and so catch their feelings. A charismatic person is, in part, someone whose emotional state is unusually catching. This is the concrete content of "inspire": your state propagates.

## A working model

Use two lenses together.

**Cabane's spine: charisma ≈ presence + warmth + power.** From [*The Charisma Myth*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Charisma_Myth) (Olivia Fox Cabane, 2012):
- **Presence** — full, undivided attention. The other person feels you are actually here, listening, not rehearsing your reply. Cabane treats this as the foundation the other two ride on.
- **Warmth** — perceived goodwill. Will you use your power in my interest? Warmth is read off signals of caring about *me*.
- **Power** — perceived ability to affect the world (status, competence, resources, will). Warmth without power is pleasant and forgettable; power without warmth is impressive and cold.

This is a practitioner framework, not a validated instrument — treat it as a good checklist, not a law.

**Cross it with warmth × competence.** [Susan Fiske's stereotype-content model](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0963721417738825) (Fiske et al., 2002) says we judge people on two axes, warmth and competence, and the combination determines the emotion we feel: high–high is *admired*, high competence/low warmth is *envied*, high warmth/low competence is *pitied*, low–low is held in *contempt*. Charisma that lasts sits in the admired quadrant: warm and competent at once. Cabane's "power" is roughly Fiske's competence; her "warmth" is his warmth. Presence is the delivery mechanism for both.

**The endearing correction.** Pure high–high can read as untouchable. The *endearing* version needs a third ingredient: a bit of accessible vulnerability that lowers the status distance without denting competence. The evidence is the [pratfall effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pratfall_effect) (Aronson, Willerman & Floyd, 1966): a highly competent person who commits a small blunder becomes *more* likable, while an average person committing the same blunder becomes less so. Competence buys you the room; a visible human flaw lets people close the distance. This is likely why you find McCartney endearing rather than merely impressive — mastery plus an unguarded ordinariness that invites you in. ## Types — there is no single charisma

Different people run different mixes of the same components:
- **Charismatic / visionary** (Thiel) — high power and conviction; charisma as a signal of a future others can't yet see. Runs on Antonakis's moral-conviction, contrast, and high-goal tactics. Warmth optional, sometimes deliberately withheld.
- **Warm / endearing** (McCartney) — high warmth plus real competence plus the pratfall softening. Emotional contagion of delight. Lowest status distance of the four.
- **Credibility / clarity** (Graham) — charisma that survives in text, where presence and voice are gone. Here it collapses into competence read as trustworthiness: plain language, visible reasoning, admitted uncertainty. The signal is "this person is honest and knows things."
- **Curiosity / attention** (Cowen) — charisma as the quality of attention *given*. Cabane's presence run as the primary engine: intense, generous interest in the other person and the world makes people feel more interesting around him.

The point of the folder: profile each person as a *mix*, not a copy of one template.

## The dark side

Charisma is content-neutral. It's a delivery system, and it delivers whatever you put in it. The same tactics that move people toward a good vision move them toward a bad one; demagogues score high on every measure here. Weber [saw this](https://www.britannica.com/topic/charismatic-authority) — charismatic authority is inherently unstable and unaccountable, answering to a person rather than to rules, which is exactly its danger. Emotional contagion has no ethics; it spreads panic and cruelty as readily as courage. So charisma is not a virtue and admiring it is not endorsing its owner. When you study these four, separate "how does the effect work" from "is the effect aimed at something good."

## How to cultivate it

Ordered roughly by leverage.

1. **Train presence first — it's the most learnable and highest-leverage piece.** Give one person your whole attention: no rehearsing your reply, no glancing at your phone, eyes and body turned in. This is the input [Cuddy's *Presence* (2015)](https://www.amycuddy.com/books) is named for, and it needs no confidence you don't have yet. *Caution:* Cuddy's famous "power pose" claim did **not** replicate — a [larger 2015 study (Ranehill et al.)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_posing) found no hormonal or behavioral effect, and her own co-author Dana Carney later [disavowed it](https://faculty.haas.berkeley.edu/dana_carney/pdf_my%20position%20on%20power%20poses.pdf). Take the "give full attention" advice; drop the "strike a pose to change your hormones" advice.
2. **Learn a few Antonakis tactics and drill them until they disappear.** Start with metaphor, story, and the three-part list. Rehearse until they're automatic, because the effect dies the moment the audience sees the effort. The [HBR piece](https://hbr.org/2012/06/learning-charisma-2) is the manual.
3. **Raise warmth signals deliberately.** Warmth is inferred, so make it inferable: use the other person's name, reflect their sentiment back ("you're worried this won't scale"), remember and reference what they told you last time.
4. **Show competence, then one honest flaw.** Establish that you know the thing, then let a real limitation or self-deprecation show. That's the pratfall move, and it converts "impressive" into "endearing" without costing you the room.
5. **Manage your own state, because it's contagious.** If you want a room energized or calm, be energized or calm first; contagion does the rest. This is more reliable than any verbal tactic.
6. **Aim it at something worth aiming it at.** Since charisma is content-neutral, the whole ethical weight is in what you point it toward. Decide that before you get good at the delivery.
