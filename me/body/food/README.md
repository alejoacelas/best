What should I eat — and how much of it can a machine decide for me?

The food pillar of `body/`. Two diet decisions, each with a short verdict here and a
deep-dive file for the methodology and sources behind it. Long-term effects were
weighted most heavily; the short-term is mostly a transition cost.

Read the verdicts with their evidence tier attached — [how I want evidence
presented](evidence.md). The short version: almost none of this is settled by trials
on the outcomes I care about (lifespan, energy). Most of it is confounded observation
or inference, and the docs now say so up front.

## Verdicts

- **Meal replacements — up to ~25-50% of daily calories long-term.**
  [`meal-replacements.md`](meal-replacements.md). One to two eating occasions from a
  complete formula (soy-isolate or a proper pea+rice blend), keeping a whole-food base
  indefinitely for chewing, fiber diversity, and phytonutrients. That partial
  replacement works *for weight loss* is `gold` (23 RCTs); that 25-50% is the right
  long-term *health* ceiling is `patchwork` — no trial has compared long-term
  fractions on anything I care about. Near-total only as a ≤12-week phase.

- **Veganism — probably longevity-neutral to mildly favorable, energy-neutral, *if* I supplement.**
  [`veganism.md`](veganism.md). All `cohort` or `patchwork`: **no RCT has ever tested
  whether a vegan diet changes lifespan or energy**, and healthy-user bias could
  produce the whole signal. On that evidence the mortality benefit tracks plant-food
  quality, not the vegan label, never reaches significance for vegans specifically (US
  Adventist cohort shows benefit, UK EPIC-Oxford shows none), and comes with real
  countervailing harms in stroke and fracture. The clearest-measured part is
  supplementation: [~52% of unsupplemented vegans are B12-deficient vs ~1% of
  omnivores](veganism.md) — B12, iodine, D, and algal DHA are cheap fixes, and a
  junk-food or unsupplemented vegan diet is the one clear loser.
