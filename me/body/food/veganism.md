# Veganism: long-term longevity, energy, and productivity

## Verdict

A well-run vegan diet is roughly longevity-neutral to mildly favorable, and energy/productivity-neutral, in healthy adults. Two things must be held apart:

- **Diet composition.** The mortality signal tracks plant-food *quality*, not the vegan label. Best current estimate for a healthful plant-based pattern is a ~10-15% lower all-cause mortality; strictly-vegan subgroups never reach significance in any single cohort, and in the UK's EPIC-Oxford the vegan point estimate sits slightly *above* 1. Real countervailing harms exist: higher stroke and higher fracture risk.
- **Supplementation discipline.** This is where veganism is won or lost. B12, iodine, vitamin D, and DHA/EPA are reliably low on plant-only diets. Unsupplemented, they cause fatigue, neurological and cognitive symptoms, and raise stroke/fracture risk. Supplemented, they are cheap and largely fixed. B12 is non-negotiable: [~52% of unsupplemented vegans are deficient vs ~1% of omnivores](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11124153/).

Net: for someone who supplements correctly and eats whole foods, the diet neither shortens life nor dulls the mind on the current evidence. A junk-food vegan diet forfeits the upside. An unsupplemented one is a genuine health risk.

## Longevity

**The cohort evidence is real but modest, mixed across cohorts, and never significant for vegans specifically.**

- **Adventist Health Study 2 (AHS-2), US.** [Orlich 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23836264/): all vegetarians combined all-cause mortality HR **0.88 (0.80-0.97)** vs nonvegetarians. By subtype: vegan **0.85 (0.73-1.01)** — crosses 1; pesco-vegetarian **0.81 (0.69-0.94)** the *only* significant subtype. The most reliable mortality benefit belongs to the fish-eaters, not the vegans.
- **Benefit vanishes with age.** [Extended AHS-2 follow-up to 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39098708/): age-varying all-cause HR **0.89 (0.83-0.95) at age 65**, attenuating to **0.98 (0.91-1.04) by age 85**.
- **EPIC-Oxford, UK.** [Key et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7613518/): over ~18 years, vegetarians all-cause HR **1.00 (0.93-1.08)** and vegans **1.14 (0.97-1.35)** vs regular meat-eaters. No mortality advantage; the vegan estimate points the wrong way. This is the strongest single-cohort counterweight to "veganism extends life."
- **Why the two cohorts disagree.** [Segovia-Siapco & Sabaté 2019](https://r.jordan.im/download/nutrition/segovia-siapco2019.pdf) attribute it to reference-group health and diet quality, not a real contradiction. EPIC-Oxford's meat-eaters were recruited from the same health-conscious milieu (whole-cohort standardized mortality ~40% of the UK average), compressing any contrast; and its vegans ate far less fiber (~27 g/d) than AHS-2 vegans (~46 g/d).

**Cause-specific, the picture is clearer:**

- **Ischaemic heart disease — the robust win.** [Meta-analysis, 13 cohorts, 844,175 participants](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9899747/): vegetarians CVD RR **0.85 (0.79-0.92)**, IHD RR **0.79 (0.71-0.88)**; vegans IHD RR **0.82 (0.68-1.00)**. Much of this is mediated by lower BMI and LDL.
- **Stroke — the countervailing harm.** [EPIC-Oxford, Tong 2019](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6724406/): vegetarians +20% total stroke incidence, driven by haemorrhagic stroke HR **1.43 (1.08-1.90)** (+43%). Separately, in extended AHS-2 at age 85, vegetarians showed higher stroke *mortality* HR **1.17 (1.02-1.33)** and dementia mortality HR **1.13 (1.00-1.27)** — a mortality endpoint, not the same as EPIC's incidence, but pointing the same direction. Plausibly mediated by low B12 / high homocysteine and low cholesterol.
- **Cancer — modest reduction.** [Dinu 2017 meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26853923/): vegetarians total cancer RR **0.92 (0.87-0.98)**, vegans **0.85 (0.75-0.95)**. [UK Biobank](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9281076/): vegetarian overall cancer HR **0.87 (0.79-0.96)**. Absolute reductions are small and confounding by BMI/screening/SES is hard to exclude.
- **Type-2 diabetes — mostly BMI-mediated.** [2025 dose-response meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40789787/): vegan RR **0.65 (0.42-1.00)**. But in EPIC-Oxford the vegetarian/vegan benefit attenuated to near-null after BMI adjustment, implying it operates chiefly through lower body weight, not a diet-specific mechanism.
- **Fracture — a real long-term morbidity cost.** [Tong 2020](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7613518/): vegans BMI-adjusted hip-fracture HR **2.31 (1.66-3.22)** and total-fracture HR **1.43 (1.20-1.70)** vs meat-eaters, linked to low calcium (vegan intake ~582-610 mg/d) and low vitamin D.

**Causal vs healthy-user bias.** No RCT exists for hard longevity endpoints, so attribution rests on observational data. The biases are large enough to produce effects of this magnitude on their own:

- Vegetarians differ systematically in education, smoking, alcohol, and activity. [EPIC-Heidelberg](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10421322/) found that adjusting for smoking, adiposity, and alcohol erased the statistical significance of most animal-food/mortality associations.
- The AHS-2 benefit is larger in men than women. The sex heterogeneity is real but weakly interpretable — women's lower baseline event rates and reduced power explain much of it, so treat it as a caution, not as evidence against a diet effect.

**Diet quality vs the label.** [PDI-index metas](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40018753/): highest-vs-lowest healthful plant index (hPDI) all-cause mortality HR **0.86 (0.81-0.92)**; unhealthful plant index (uPDI, refined grains/sugar/juice) HR **1.20 (1.11-1.31)**. [Million Veteran Program](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/public-health-nutrition/article/degree-of-adherence-to-plantbased-diet-and-total-and-causespecific-mortality-prospective-cohort-study-in-the-million-veteran-program/91A237B3950086867063974662ED82C8): hPDI extreme deciles HR **0.64**, uPDI HR **1.41**. Caveat on reading this across to vegans: these cohorts are overwhelmingly *omnivorous*, scored on a plant-food gradient — a high-hPDI eater is not a vegan, and no PDI study isolates vegans. The honest framing: within mostly-omnivore populations, plant-food *quality* tracks mortality more strongly than plant-food *quantity*. That is a plausible mechanism for why vegan cohorts diverge, not a measured result in vegans.

## Energy & productivity (long-term)

**Bottom line: a well-supplemented vegan diet does not measurably impair cognition, mood, or energy in healthy adults, and does not impair physical performance when energy, protein, creatine, iron, and B12 are adequate.** The risks are specific nutrients, most fully correctable.

**The failure modes and what fixes them:**

- **B12 — the one non-negotiable.** No plant source. [52% of unsupplemented vegans deficient](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11124153/); deficiency causes fatigue, neurological/cognitive symptoms, and macrocytic anemia. Fixed by 50-100 µg/day or 2000 µg/week. Important nuance: in people who are *not* deficient, adding B12 does nothing — [12-RCT meta-analysis, n=6,276](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8000524/) found near-zero effects on executive function (g=0.06), memory, global cognition, and depression (SMD −0.049). The benefit is correcting deficiency, not a nootropic.
- **Iron.** Vegetarians/vegans have substantially lower ferritin — [meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27880062/): −29.71 µg/L overall, −61.88 in men, −13.50 in women — but not consistently higher anemia rates. Low-normal ferritin can cause fatigue even without anemia, especially in menstruating women. Vegans partly [up-regulate non-heme absorption](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12189169/) as an adaptation; vitamin C co-ingestion helps. Supplement on bloodwork, not blindly.
- **Omega-3 DHA/EPA — a status deficit of uncertain functional cost.** Vegans have markedly lower tissue DHA. [Crossover RCT](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9684969/): baseline serum DHA 0.75% (vegan) vs 1.94% (omnivore); 250 mg/day algal DHA raised vegans +124% but only to 1.74%, still below omnivore baseline, with all groups in the lowest omega-3-index band. Whether this residual gap has cognitive consequences in adults is unproven — [omega-3 cognition RCTs are mostly null](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S156816372200277X) (only 43.6% of 78 trials positive). Developmental (pregnancy/infancy) DHA need is a separate, better-established concern.
- **Creatine — modest, mostly unsettled in the young.** Absent from plant foods; vegetarians/vegans have lower muscle and likely brain creatine. [Meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9999677/): memory SMD 0.29 overall, but driven by older adults (0.88) with no effect in the young (0.03). An early large vegetarian benefit ([Rae 2003](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/9049698_Oral_creatine_monohydrate_supplementation_improves_brain_performance_A_double-blind_placebo-controlled_cross-over_trial), working memory and reasoning, p<0.0001) [did not robustly replicate](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12916-023-03146-5). Muscle repletion (ergogenic) is well established; cognitive payoff in young vegans is not.
- **Iodine — most-overlooked, and fast-moving.** Vegans are the group at highest deficiency risk. [Boston study](https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/96/8/E1303/2833831): median urinary iodine 78.5 µg/L (vegan) vs 147.0 (vegetarian); [others](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10551477/) find vegans lowest, below the WHO optimum. Overt thyroid dysfunction (TSH/T4) usually isn't different, but [vegans show higher thyroglobulin](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10611878/), a subclinical-insufficiency marker. Fix with 150 µg/day; do not rely on seaweed (wildly variable, can over-supply).
- **Vitamin D — easily corrected, largely not vegan-specific.** Vegans have the [lowest 25(OH)D](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/public-health-nutrition/article/plasma-concentrations-of-25hydroxyvitamin-d-in-meat-eaters-fish-eaters-vegetarians-and-vegans-results-from-the-epicoxford-study/13C1A2796ADA3A318D4F3B7C105D9D9C), but sun exposure and pigmentation dominate over diet. Lichen-derived D3 fully corrects it.
- **Zinc — measurable status deficit, rarely symptomatic.** Phytate cuts absorption; vegans show lower serum zinc despite adequate intake. A [2025 trial](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261561425001645) restored antiviral interferon response in zinc-deficient vegans. Clinical energy/cognition symptoms are rarely documented in Western vegans. Soaking/sprouting/fermenting cuts phytate.
- **Choline — theoretical.** Lower in plant foods, but there is [essentially no direct vegan intake/deficiency data](https://veganhealth.org/choline/) and under-consumption is a general-population problem (only ~10% of Americans meet the AI). Strongest concern is maternal choline in pregnancy.

**Performance.** [Systematic review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10647346/): vegetarian/vegan athletes match or slightly exceed omnivores on VO2max and endurance, with no strength/power penalty ([peak torque not different](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5133111/)). But every dataset is cross-sectional with strong self-selection (vegan athletes are unusually motivated and carbohydrate-loaded). Read this as *no evidence the diet impairs performance when nutrition is adequate*, not as a benefit of veganism.

**Residual gaps that supplements only partly close:** tissue DHA (stays lowest even on modest algal doses) and, plausibly, brain creatine. Neither has a proven functional cost in healthy adults. A [2026 older-adult cross-sectional study](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0963996926001298) reported higher cognitive-impairment odds in vegans (+33%), but it is prone to reverse causation and unmeasured B12 status, and conflicts with null findings in younger, better-controlled samples. Not evidence the diet harms cognition.

## Short-term transition

**Most "first weeks" effects are transient adaptation, not deficiency.** True micronutrient deficiencies do not appear early for nutrients with large body stores.

- **Gut adapts fastest.** [David 2014, Nature](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3957428/): microbiome shifts ~1 day after food reaches the colon, reverts ~2 days after stopping. Gas/bloating peaks week 1 and resolves by weeks 3-8 — [bean-feeding studies](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3228670/): flatulence complaints fall from 35% (wk 1) to ~3% (wk 8).
- **B12 cannot deplete in weeks.** Hepatic stores buffer [~3-6+ years](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK114329/) (worked example: ~6.3 years). The [Stanford twin RCT](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10690456/) found serum B12 unchanged after 8 weeks fully vegan. You still supplement day one because the drawdown is undetectable and there is no plant source.
- **Iron indices stay stable ~8 weeks.** [Hunt & Roughead 1999](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10232635/): ~70% lower non-heme absorption but compensatory up-regulation; ferritin/hemoglobin insensitive over 8 weeks. Menstruating women and those starting low can still deplete over months.
- **Iodine is the exception — it can fall below requirement within weeks.** Dropping dairy and iodized salt removes the main sources fast and the thyroid's reserve is small. [Veganuary study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10708059/): iodine intake fell ~131 µg (below the UK RNI) within 4 weeks.
- **Early fatigue is more likely a transition cost** — under-eating, low protein per volume, blood-sugar swings, or expectation — than nutrient depletion, since the stores timeline rules out acute B12/iron loss. People with pre-existing marginal iron/B12 are the exception and should be screened.
- **Cardiometabolic gains appear fast (the good side).** [Stanford twin RCT, corrected version](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10690456/): 8 weeks vegan vs omnivore improved LDL, fasting insulin, and weight. Both study diets were dietitian-designed whole-food diets excluding refined sugar/starch, so results do not generalize to a self-directed or junk-food vegan diet.

**Day-one supplement stack** (from the [Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics 2016 position](https://www.jandonline.org/article/S2212-2672(16)31192-3/abstract) plus practical dosing):

- **B12 — mandatory:** ~50-250 µg/day, or ~1,000-2,000 µg twice weekly.
- **Iodine:** ~150 µg/day (a defined supplement, not seaweed).
- **Vitamin D:** 1,000-2,000 IU/day if sun exposure is limited.
- **Algal DHA/EPA:** ~250-500 mg/day (ALA→DHA conversion is only ~0-4%).
- **Iron, zinc, selenium:** test (ferritin) rather than blindly dose — routine iron risks overload in men and post-menopausal women; 1-2 Brazil nuts/day cover selenium (soil-variable).

B12 and iodine are the two that matter from day one, for different reasons: B12 because there is no plant source, iodine because intake crashes fast.

## Methodology & caveats

- **No RCT exists for longevity endpoints.** Everything on mortality is observational and cannot fully remove healthy-user bias, SES confounding, or residual smoking effects — biases large enough to produce the observed effect sizes on their own.
- **Vegan subgroups are small and imprecise.** Vegan mortality CIs cross 1 in every single cohort. Much "vegan" evidence is really vegetarian evidence extrapolated.
- **Endpoints differ.** Some figures are incidence, others cause-specific mortality; they are directionally consistent here but not interchangeable.
- **Cross-sectional traps.** Athlete-performance and older-adult cognition findings carry heavy self-selection and reverse-causation risk.
- **Serum markers lag.** Serum B12 and ferritin are insensitive early markers; MMA/holotranscobalamin and repeat testing detect depletion sooner.
- **Diet quality is the hidden variable.** "Plant-based," "strictly vegan," and "healthy" are not interchangeable. Most of the divergence between good and bad outcomes runs through what the diet is built from.
