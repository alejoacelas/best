# Meal replacements: what fraction of my diet?

## Verdict

Long-term ceiling: **about 25-50% of daily calories** (1-2 of 3-4 eating occasions) from a complete formula, keeping a whole-food base indefinitely. Near-total replacement is fine only as a defined short phase (up to ~12 weeks) and, at very low calories, under medical supervision. The reason: 25-50% is the only dose with year-plus RCT support; above ~50% the evidence thins and the satiety, fiber-diversity, and diet-skill concerns compound. This is an evidence-bounded default, not a tested threshold — no trial has ever randomized long-term users to 50% vs 75%, so "above ~50% the evidence thins" is the honest claim, not "above 50% is unsafe."

## Why that number

**Formula meal replacements are genuinely micronutrient-complete against the RDA — but "complete" means adequacy, not optimum, and coverage is a label target verified in few products.** Engineered total-diet-replacement formulas (Soylent, Huel) are formulated to hit ~100% of the ~26 essential vitamins and minerals per 2,000 kcal — Soylent delivers 20% per serving, five servings to 100% ([Healthline](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/soylent-meal-replacements)); Huel Powder states all 26 essentials ([Frontiers pilot](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2022.945622/full)). The RDA is the intake meeting 97-98% of healthy people's needs to *avoid deficiency* — not an optimal or performance standard. These percentages are formulation targets, analytically verified in only a handful of products. The EU has a real legal definition of a "nutritionally complete" total diet replacement (Regulation (EU) 609/2013, [EFSA](https://www.efsa.europa.eu/en/applications/nutrition/regulationsandguidance)); the US has no equivalent general "complete food" category, so most US "complete nutrition" language is marketing.

**The only interventional blood-biomarker evidence is thin and points slightly up, not down.** A 4-week Huel pilot ([Frontiers 2022](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2022.945622/full)) saw iron, B12, vitamin D, selenium, and hemoglobin rise; but vitamin A (2.04→1.75), vitamin E (31.7→22.0 µmol/L), and potassium fell (all stayed in normal range). It was open-label, single-arm, 30 enrolled / 20 completed, funded by Huel, with one Huel-employee author and three Aelius Biotech co-founders/shareholders. Participants self-restricted calories (BMI fell ~6%), so gains are confounded with energy restriction and healthy-user bias, and 4 weeks cannot detect slow-accumulating gaps.

The real long-term limiters:

- **Protein quality, not quantity.** Individual plant proteins score below the high-quality DIAAS line: soy 91, whey 85 (high-quality); pea 70, oat 57, rice 47 (no quality claim, <75) ([Herreman et al. 2020](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7590266/)). Pea and rice are complementary (pea supplies lysine, rice the sulfur AAs), so a correctly-ratioed blend reaches parity; soy-isolate formulas (Soylent) are closest to animal protein. Rice-heavy or single-source formulas are weakest.
- **Phytonutrient diversity — the clearest gap.** "Complete" catalogs known nutrients. It makes no claim about the polyphenol/phytochemical matrix of a varied whole-food diet, because no RDA exists for those compounds ([Healthline dietitian review](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/best-meal-replacement)). This is a gap in the *definition*, not a proven harm — no long-term sole-source outcome trials exist.
- **Fiber is adequate in grams but narrow in type.** Huel Powder is 8-10 g/serving, mostly oats and flax ([Huel](https://huel.com/pages/the-huel-powder-formula-explained)); ready-to-drink formats can be ~6 g/bottle and fall short of 25-38 g/day if used as sole intake. "Grams" hides the narrow fiber portfolio that feeds a diverse microbiome.
- **Heavy metals in plant-based powders.** The [Clean Label Project 2024-25 study](https://cleanlabelproject.org/wp-content/uploads/CleanLabelProject_ProteinStudyWhitepaper_010625.pdf) (160 products) found 47% over California Prop 65 thresholds, with plant-based averaging 5x the cadmium and 3x the lead of whey, and chocolate ~110x the cadmium of vanilla. Caveat: Prop 65 limits are far stricter than federal food-safety levels, so "exceeds Prop 65" is not "unsafe dose," and industry disputes the methodology ([NutraIngredients](https://www.nutraingredients.com/Article/2025/01/10/clean-label-project-releases-controversial-protein-powder-report/)).
- **Added-vitamin forms.** Bioavailability varies by form (L-methylfolate vs folic acid; magnesium citrate vs oxide); cheaper products often don't disclose. A category-level risk, not a proven per-product deficit — no form-by-form brand audit was located.

**The "ultra-processed" objection is weaker than it looks for these products.** Meal replacements are NOVA group 4, and UPF diets drive passive overeating: +508 kcal/day and +0.9 kg over 2 weeks in the [Hall 2019 inpatient RCT](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7946062/) (n=20). But Hall's stimuli were energy-dense, hyper-palatable junk food, not portion-defined shakes — the overeating mechanism (energy density, texture) may not transfer. NOVA lumps nutritionally-optimized formulas with junk ([UPF paradox](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12964146/)), so the label alone is weak grounds to cap the fraction. Relatedly, the common "processing lowers the thermic effect" argument is not clean: one small crossover ([PMC7469013](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7469013/), n=8) found the meal replacement had *higher* thermic effect (+7.76%, p=0.048). Too small to generalize, but it flags the anti-processing case as empirically mixed.

**Satiety and diet-skill are the durable reasons to keep a whole-food base:**

- **Chewing drives satiety.** Chewing reduces hunger and intake and raises GLP-1/CCK/PYY ([Miquel-Kergoat 2015](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0031938415300317)); liquids empty faster and trigger weaker distension. Most data are acute single-meal; long-term weight effects are modest. A solid/bar-format replacement, or keeping one chewed meal a day, mitigates this.
- **Diet-skill atrophy.** Formulas don't build the cooking and eating habits maintenance needs; dietitians frame them as adjunct/kick-start ([WebMD](https://www.webmd.com/diet/what-to-know-about-meal-replacement-shakes)). DiRECT and clinical protocols deliberately reintroduce food to rebuild skills. This is behavioral, not physiological addiction, and rests on expert opinion more than RCT.

## Short-term vs long-term

**Short term (days to weeks):** expect transient GI symptoms — bloating, flatulence, altered stools — as the gut meets isolated fibers (inulin/FOS give mild symptoms at 7.5-20 g/day, worse above 30 g; [tolerance review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9776669/)). Largely self-limiting, though evidence for true adaptation is inconsistent. Purpose-formulated high-protein, low-GI formulas blunt post-meal glucose from the first serving — but that is a protein/carb-composition effect against a high-GI comparator, shown in a manufacturer-authored acute crossover ([PMC11188454](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11188454/); 3 of 4 authors Abbott-affiliated). A macronutrient-matched formula shows *no* glucose advantage. "More energy/focus" in early weeks is mechanistic inference (flatter glucose swings), not a measured cognitive outcome.

**Long term (months to a year):** partial replacement holds up. Replacing 1-2 meals/day produces about **1.44 kg more loss at 1 year** than an isocaloric food diet ([meta-analysis, 23 RCTs, 7,884 adults](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6849863/)), rising to 2-6 kg with structured support; Heymsfield's pooling found +2.4-2.6 kg sustained to 1 year with lower dropout ([Int J Obes 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12704397/)). The effect is portion/calorie control and adherence, not metabolic magic. Adverse events showed no signal of harm where measured — but only 2 of 23 trials reported adverse-event data, so this is under-reporting, not demonstrated safety, and the literature is heavily industry-funded.

**Near-total, long term:** unstudied. EFSA states there are [no data backing total-diet-replacement safety past three months](https://www.efsa.europa.eu/en/efsajournal/pub/3957). Even the aggressive protocols treat it as a phase: DiRECT ran 825-853 kcal/day formula for 12-20 weeks, *then* staged food reintroduction, achieving [46% type-2 diabetes remission vs 4%](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29221645/). Very-low-calorie regimens add real risks — ~11% develop gallstones within 6 months ([matched cohort](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3921672/)), plus cold intolerance, hair loss, fatigue — driven by the deficit and rapid loss, not the formula itself.

**Ramp:** substitute one meal/day first, raise total fiber gradually, hydrate. No RCT tests the optimal schedule; this is inferred from fiber physiology, clinical staging, and manufacturer guidance.

## How to do it well

- **Replace the low-effort, low-variety meals** (rushed breakfast, desk lunch). Keep dinner and at least one meal/day whole-food, chewed, and varied.
- **Cap at ~1-2 formulas/day long-term.** Go higher only for a defined stretch; go near-total only under medical supervision and never at very-low-calorie for casual convenience.
- **Prefer soy-isolate or a correctly-ratioed pea+rice blend** over rice-heavy or single-source plant formulas.
- **Get phytonutrients and fiber diversity from the whole-food side** — colorful vegetables, fruit, legumes, whole grains. The formula won't supply them.
- **Prefer vanilla over chocolate and non-organic over organic** if minimizing heavy-metal exposure from plant powders (per Clean Label Project's directional finding; magnitudes disputed).
- **Watch:** early GI symptoms (ramp fiber slowly), and total calories — don't accidentally undereat, which is where gallstone and fatigue risks come from.
- **Cost is favorable:** Soylent ~$1.82-1.91 and Huel ~$1.95-2.35 per ~400 kcal serving vs a reported ~$4.31 US home-cooked meal ([commercial pricing, not peer-reviewed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soylent_(meal_replacement))). Home-cooking cost varies enormously; a bare formula omits the variety people pay for.

## Methodology & caveats

Built from a fan-out literature search plus an adversarial review that fact-checked each figure against its source and flagged over-claims. High-confidence spine (verified verbatim): the meta-analysis numbers, DIAAS values, Clean Label Project figures, Hall 2019, EFSA's 3-month statement, DiRECT, and the gallstone rate.

Standing caveats:

- **Industry funding is pervasive.** Heymsfield (Slim-Fast), the Huel pilot (Huel), and the glycemic crossover (Abbott) are all sponsor-linked. Treat favorable effect sizes as upper bounds.
- **Healthy-user and UPF-debate biases.** Trials enroll overweight/obese adults seeking weight loss, not normal-weight people substituting for convenience — generalizing to "replace meals indefinitely" is extrapolation. The UPF harm evidence used junk-food stimuli, not portion-defined formulas.
- **The 25-50% ceiling is triangulated, not tested.** Year-plus evidence exists only at 1-2 meals/day; safety data stop at 12 weeks for near-total use. No trial has compared long-term replacement fractions head to head. The safe fraction rises with a complete, higher-fiber formula and a whole-food remainder that supplies chewing, fiber diversity, and phytonutrients.
