# Year-Round Light & Circadian Setup for Bipolar II — Research Notes for a Psychiatrist Conversation

These are decision-support notes to bring to your psychiatrist, not medical advice and not a treatment plan you should run solo. The goal is full-cycle functioning across the whole year — keeping your light and circadian input more *consistent* rather than letting it collapse every winter and surge every spring — while respecting that bright light is a real antidepressant with a real, if low, ability to flip you upward. The framing here deliberately goes past the cautious "light is dangerous in bipolar" first pass: the evidence supports careful, monitored experimentation. But every dose, timing, and stop-rule below is something to *agree and supervise* with your prescriber, ideally with a mood stabilizer on board and a written switch plan in hand.

A note on honesty: the bipolar-specific evidence base is small (the main meta-analyses pool roughly 4–7 RCTs and ~190–260 patients, mostly mixing Bipolar I and II without reporting the breakdown). Several "obvious" claims soften or reverse under scrutiny. Where that happens, it's flagged.

---

## The one insight that changes the plan

**Lever 1 — timing: midday is the well-validated, gentle default; morning is the standard but slightly more activating. Don't assume "more light, earlier" is better.**

For unipolar winter depression (SAD), the standard prescription is bright light first thing in the morning. For *bipolar* depression the picture is more nuanced, and this is the single most important "beyond-first-pass" correction:

- The best single trial — Sit et al. 2018, a 6-week double-blind RCT, N=46 Bipolar I+II, all on a mood stabilizer — used **7,000 lux at midday (12:00–14:30)** with slow titration and produced **68.2% vs 22.2% remission (adjusted OR ~12.6) and zero mood-polarity switches**. [RCT] That makes midday a genuinely strong, low-switch option.
- **But "midday is safer *and* better than morning" does not hold up.** The belief that midday is *safer* traces to a single 9-woman 2007 case series (3 of 4 women on morning light developed mixed states). [observational, weak] The only head-to-head trial — LuBi 2024, N=38 — actually found **morning produced *greater* clinical improvement than midday (CGI p=.035)**, with equal, rare, reversible switches (one per arm). [RCT] And the 2025 ISBD guideline lists **morning (7–9 am) as the primary timing and midday (12:00–14:30) as the acceptable alternative**, chosen mainly for practicality and for anyone with a switch history. [guideline]

So the honest read for your psychiatrist: **start midday because it has the single best placebo-controlled RCT and is gentle, then treat timing as a parameter to optimize.** If midday response is inadequate after 4–6 weeks and you've had no activation, a supervised trial of morning light is reasonable — it may be more potent. What actually keeps light safe is not the clock so much as the *dose-escalation + mood-stabilizer coverage + monitoring* below.

**Lever 2 — the deeper one: the real target is rhythm *stability* across the year, not just more lux in winter.**

Bipolar disorder behaves substantially like a disorder of circadian and social-rhythm regulation. Disruption of sleep/wake, meal, activity, and social-contact timing *precedes and predicts* episodes of **both** poles. [theory; observational] The flagship intervention, Interpersonal & Social Rhythm Therapy (IPSRT), in a 175-patient Bipolar I RCT **significantly lengthened time to a new episode (HR ~0.34)**, and the *degree* of rhythm regularization predicted lower recurrence — pointing to regularity as a plausibly causal protective lever, not just a correlate. [RCT] In bipolar-*spectrum* samples that include BD-II and cyclothymia, lower social-rhythm regularity prospectively predicted shorter time to depressive, hypomanic, and manic onset. [observational]

The destabilizer in your situation is well-characterized too: large international data tie the **springtime *rate of increase* in sunlight** (not latitude or total light) to younger bipolar onset. [observational] At 51–57°N, daylight swings from ~7.5–8 h at the winter solstice to ~16.5–17.5 h in summer — one of the steepest inhabited photoperiod swings, and BD-II carries the **highest seasonal/SAD prevalence of the mood disorders (~31% vs ~19% in BD-I)**. [observational]

The strategic implication: **artificially flatten the year.** Hold a constant bright-morning / dark-evening contrast and a rock-steady wake time *every week, every season*, so your clock "sees" roughly the same day in December as in June — rather than chasing winter with big light jumps and letting summer's long light drag your rhythm around. Consistency is the protective signal; raw lux is the tool, not the goal.

## The ideal setup, parameterized

Think of this as five layers stacked on a single anchor. Build only the ones you'll actually sustain — a boring routine you hold every week beats an elaborate one you abandon.

### 0. Master anchor — fixed wake time, 7 days a week, year-round
Pick one wake time you can hold in **both July and January** (e.g. 07:00) and get out of bed within ~15 min of it regardless of season, weekend, or how you slept. This is the highest-leverage, best-evidenced lever — it stabilizes the clock from the output end and blunts the summer urge to wake at 04:30 with the sun and the winter urge to sleep past a dark dawn. Track it with the **SRM-5**: log the clock time of five daily anchors (out of bed, first human contact, start of main activity, dinner, to bed), aiming for each within a ~30–45 min daily window. This is the same instrument that mediated IPSRT's effect and doubles as an early-warning dashboard.

### 1. Daytime bright-light dose (the antidepressant tool)
- **Device:** UV-filtered white box, **5,000–10,000 lux**, screen ≥12×14 in, placed **~30–40 cm (12–16 in)** from the eyes, slightly above eye level angled down; eyes open, glancing toward (not staring at) it while doing a task. [guideline]
- **Timing:** **start midday, ~12:00–14:30** (gentlest, best single RCT). Morning (7–9 am) is the more-potent, slightly-more-activating option to consider later under supervision. [guideline / RCT]
- **Dose & titration (the load-bearing safety feature):** Week 1 = **15 min/day**; +15 min/week → **30 / 45 / 60 min** by week 4 at 7,000 lux. At 10,000 lux a **~30 min/day** target is roughly dose-equivalent. Confirm exact minutes for your device with your psychiatrist. [RCT / guideline]
- **Distance discipline:** light obeys the inverse-square law — a box rated 10,000 lux at 30 cm gives only ~2,500 lux at 60 cm and ~1,100 lux at 90 cm. Mark a fixed spot so you sit at the same close distance daily, and buy only lamps that state lux **at a named distance** (marketing often quotes lux at the bulb surface). [theory]
- **Expected timeline:** some improvement in 1–2 weeks; fuller response/remission by 4–6 weeks. [guideline]

### 2. Dawn simulation — the gentle wake-anchor rung
A sunrise light that ramps over the last **30–45 min** of sleep to a peak of **~250–300 lux**, ending at your fixed wake time. In SAD trials it matched or even beat a 10,000-lux box (Avery 2001: dawn > box on remission; Terman 2006: 49.5% vs 57.1% response). [RCT] Its peak is 20–40× dimmer than a light box and most of it lands through closed eyelids before waking, so it's plausibly a **lower-switch-risk** stimulus — but note this is mechanistic inference: **no RCT has tested dawn simulators in bipolar specifically** (Terman 2006 included just 5 BD-II patients, unanalyzed). [theory] Keep the ramp to ~30–45 min: a 2-hour ramp reliably caused early-morning awakening (7/9 patients), and fragmented sleep is itself a switch trigger.

Its strongest evidence-based role for you is **wake-time anchoring**, not as a sufficient treatment for an actual depressive episode — a 250-lux device is well below the 5,000–10,000 lux "active" antidepressant dose, so for a real winter dip, the midday box is the better-evidenced escalation.

*Devices that reach a research-grade ramp:* Lumie Bodyclock **Shine 300** or **Luxe 700FM**, or Philips SmartSleep **HF3520** (the entry-level fixed-ramp units don't reach ~250+ lux). Place within ~50 cm of the pillow.

### 3. Home-lighting engineering — the "lumenate" high-lumen ambient layer
The core problem is a **10–100× light deficit**: ordinary rooms sit at **~100–500 lux** while even an overcast UK winter sky delivers **1,000–10,000+ lux** (one field study: outdoors ~8× indoor, 1,175 vs 179 lux median). [observational] Closing that gap with whole-room light has real support: the **BROAD trial** (Sandkühler 2022, N=62 SAD) installed up to ~70 daylight LED bulbs (~**100,000 lumens**) in a room people occupied ≥6 h/day, and it **matched a 10,000-lux box**, with an exploratory dose-response (brighter room → more improvement). [RCT] Its big advantage for *consistency* is that it removes the "sit still for 30 min" burden.

Practical build, in the room you use most:
- Lift baseline room light from ~150–300 lux toward **600–1,500+ lux** with **8–15 tunable smart bulbs at ~1,500–1,600 lm each** (100W-equivalent), or a few daylight LED panels.
- Rough math: `lux ≈ (total lumens × ~0.4) / floor area (m²)`, so ~30,000–50,000 lm in a ~12 m² room ≈ 1,000–1,600 lux average.
- **Kelvin schedule:** 4,000–5,000K (up to 6,000K) at high brightness from morning to late afternoon, then auto-ramp to **2,700–3,000K and dim** in the evening. Automate it so consistency doesn't depend on willpower.

Two honesty caveats: the lumenate/BROAD evidence is for **unipolar SAD, not bipolar**, so a continuously very-bright room is a larger *uncontrolled* light dose than a timed box — raise the trade-off explicitly with your psychiatrist. And blue-enriched light partly substitutes for raw lux (Meesters 2011: 750 lux blue-enriched ≈ 10,000 lux for SAD), which is why getting the *evening* Kelvin wrong is a genuine destabilizer.

### 4. Evening dark / blue-block — protect the "night" signal (with a real evidence caveat)
The mechanism is solid: the brain reads "night" from the *absence* of short-wavelength (~450–480 nm) light on melanopsin cells, and ordinary room light is enough to disrupt it — **<200 lux before bed delayed melatonin onset by ~1h45m** and ~50% suppression occurs below 30 lux (Gooley 2011). [RCT] Sensitivity varies **>50-fold between people** (Phillips 2019), so if your mood tracks light strongly you may sit at the sensitive end — treat this as a personal experiment. [RCT]

**The honest caveat (this is where the popular story overstates the evidence):** the famous amber-glasses anti-mania result (Henriksen 2016, YMRS −14.1, d≈1.86) came from a tiny (N=24), **rater-unblinded** acute-mania inpatient trial — and it **did not replicate** in the larger, blinded **Ottawa 2024 trial (N=42), which found no benefit** (group×time p≈0.93). The best outpatient bipolar trial (Esaki 2020) **missed its primary sleep endpoint (p=.13)**; the only reproducible effect was a **circadian phase advance**, not symptom or sleep improvement. Pooled actigraphy gains from blue-blockers are small and non-significant. And prophylactic/year-round use is essentially **untested** (the dedicated maintenance trial, BLUES, hasn't reported). So: the *bright-days* half of "bright days + dark evenings" is well-grounded; the *dark-evenings-fix-mania* half rests on shaky, partly-refuted data.

What this means for a **depression-dominant BD-II person at high latitude** specifically:
- Use the **gentle daily version**, not aggressive 14-hour darkness. **Over-darkening can tip toward depression** — Henriksen saw reversible depressive symptoms in 2 patients by days 6–7, and healthy controls reported "uncomfortably low energy." For someone whose main burden *is* winter low mood, an 18:00 "virtual darkness" block could worsen the very state you fear.
- **Daily default:** true amber/orange wrap glasses (block to ~530 nm — e.g. Uvex SCT-Orange, LowBlueLights/Swannies; **not** clear "computer glasses," which block only ~6–43% of blue) on **~2 h before bed**, plus dimming the room to **<30 lux** (ideally <10) at the eye in the final hour. Start the window **later (19:00–20:00) and shorter**, not at 18:00.
- **Escalation (only for clear hypomanic signs / racy nights / travel):** extend the amber window earlier and through the night — this is one of your first self-managed responses to an early-warning sign, alongside protecting sleep and contacting your clinician.
- **Winter rule:** don't add *more* evening darkness to compensate for winter — for a depression-prone winter the risk is too much dark. Hold the evening protocol **identical** to summer and fix the deficit on the **morning/bright** side.

### 5. Outdoors, supplements, and the seasonal-transition plan
- **Outdoors daily, even grey:** a 20–30 min walk delivers 1,000–10,000+ lux for free vs ~200 lux indoors. If optimizing circadian timing, do it in the morning; the structured antidepressant light stays at midday. [expert-opinion]
- **Vitamin D:** at 51–57°N the skin makes essentially no vitamin D from **October to March** (a 5–6 month "vitamin D winter"; ~40% of the population drops below 25 nmol/L). UK advice: **10 µg (400 IU)/day** through autumn/winter, baseline for everyone. Safe self-supplementation ceiling **100 µg (4,000 IU)/day**. Ask the GP to test **serum 25(OH)D** before winter; correct deficiency under clinician guidance. Frame this as **deficiency correction, not a mood treatment** — its evidence as an antidepressant is weak. [guideline]
- **Low-dose melatonin as a *chronobiotic* (discuss first):** this is a different drug-use than a sleeping pill. **0.5 mg** (not 3–5 mg) taken in the **early evening**, ~9–11 h before mid-sleep, produces a maximal **phase advance** (clock earlier — the usual winter goal); morning melatonin shifts the clock the *wrong* way. [RCT for the PRC] In bipolar maintenance, melatonergic agents show at best a modest signal against the *depressive* pole and the largest trial was negative — so this is a reasonable low-risk adjunct hypothesis, not an established tool. Bring the explicit "0.5 mg early-evening chronobiotic" framing to your psychiatrist. [meta-analysis]
- **Transition shocks:** the BST clock changes (late March, late October) and the steep spring light ramp are mini photoperiod jolts. Shift your wake time **gradually (~15 min/day over 3–4 days)** across each, be deliberate about evening light restriction in early spring, and pre-schedule a **March–April check-in** — historically the highest-risk pivot for spring (hypo)mania. Use blackout curtains + eye mask in summer so a 04:45 sunrise can't creep your rhythm earlier.
- **Do NOT self-run sleep deprivation / triple chronotherapy.** Wake therapy is fast and potent but carries one of the highest switch risks of all and must be inpatient/closely supervised. Mention it to your psychiatrist as an option only if standard light is insufficient.

## Safety & monitoring

This section is the part to actually agree and write down with your prescriber. The reassuring trial switch numbers (≈1% with light vs ≈1% control; OR ~1.30, NS) are low **precisely because** the trials built in these safeguards and excluded the highest-risk patients — they are not evidence that you can skip them. [meta-analysis, CONFIRMED]

**Pre-flight gate (all must be true before starting):**
- Not currently or recently hypomanic/manic, no mixed features, **not rapid cycling.** These are contraindications, not cautions — this is the one population where the cautious "light is dangerous" framing is genuinely correct. (Rapid cycling was the single biggest amplifier in a 799-patient review: ~18.8% switch vs the ~4% placebo base rate.) [observational / guideline]
- **Mood-stabilizer coverage** in place. For BD-I, ISBD requires a stable antimanic agent **2–4 weeks before** starting. **For BD-II, coverage is clinician-discretion per ISBD — not mandatory**, but advisable and the basis of nearly all the safety data. Never start during a medication change or gap. [guideline]
- **Eye check** and a med review for photosensitizers (St John's wort, some antibiotics/antipsychotics; lithium itself is *not* photosensitizing). Use a UV-filtered box.

**Titration schedule:** start **15 min/day** (7.5 if you're sensitive); advance **15 min/week only if no activation signs**, toward 45–60 min by ~week 4. If any week brings a warning sign, **hold or drop a step** — don't advance.

**Daily charting (2 min):** hours slept + "did I need *less* sleep?", plus a 0–10 energy/irritability/agitation rating. **Weekly:** PHQ-9, GAD-7, and a **Young Mania Rating Scale (YMRS)** self-check. **Pre-commit a numeric YMRS/activation threshold** with your psychiatrist as the stop line.

**Early-warning signs (chart sleep, not just mood):** the cardinal prodrome is **reduced need for sleep** — sleeping less without feeling tired. A run of good energy that *includes* sleeping less is the **stopping signal**, not "the light working." Also: racing/crowded thoughts, pressured speech, a surge of new projects/goal-directed activity, new irritability or inner restlessness, and — importantly — **worsening depression or rising suicidal ideation** (a mixed/agitated reaction, also a stop signal, not only euphoria).

**Stop-or-cut rule (act on the FIRST sign, same day):** stop the light or drop to the previous lower duration immediately, and notify your psychiatrist. In the dose-escalation trial, dose-related hypomanic symptoms **resolved within ~3 days of dose reduction** — so the response to early signs is usually to *cut the dose*, not necessarily abandon the treatment. If symptoms don't settle within a few days of stopping, that's an **urgent** contact, not wait-and-see.

**Where the Bipolar-II evidence is thin vs extrapolated:**
- The headline light RCTs (Sit 2018, LuBi) **pooled BD-I and BD-II** in small samples (N=46, N=38) and rarely report subtype-specific switch rates. The reassuring numbers are "promising in mixed bipolar samples," not BD-II-proven.
- The strongest rhythm RCT (IPSRT/Frank 2005) and the insolation/onset dataset (Bauer) are **BD-I**. The best BD-II-relevant rhythm evidence (Shen 2008) is **observational, not interventional**.
- **Two BD-II nuances pull in opposite directions:** the feared endpoint is hypomania, not full mania (less catastrophic — part of why careful experimentation is defensible), *but* rapid cycling and mixed/agitated features are **more common** in BD-II and in women, and those are exactly the high-risk modifiers. So "BD-II is automatically safer" is not true.
- **Dawn simulation and the lumenate/whole-room approach have no bipolar safety trials at all** — the "gentler" reasoning is mechanistic inference.
- **Evening blue-block:** antimanic benefit is partly refuted under blinding; sleep benefit is mostly null; prophylactic use is untested. Treat it as low-risk rhythm/phase insurance, not a proven symptom tool — and watch the over-darkening → depression risk.

NICE rates light-therapy efficacy for seasonal depression "uncertain" and it isn't routinely on the NHS — so this whole setup is an **evidence-supported experiment**, not a guideline-endorsed first-line treatment.

## If you only do five things

1. **Hold one wake time, 7 days a week, all year** (the single best-evidenced lever) and log the five SRM-5 anchors daily.
2. **Run a UV-filtered 10,000-lux box at a fixed midday time**, starting 15 min/day and adding 15 min/week toward ~30 min — **only on a mood stabilizer and with psychiatrist sign-off**, at a marked fixed distance.
3. **Chart sleep-need and mood daily + YMRS weekly**, with a pre-agreed numeric stop line; treat *less sleep without tiredness* as the stop signal.
4. **Keep evenings gently dark year-round** — amber glasses ~2 h before bed (start 19:00–20:00, not 18:00) and room <30 lux — and don't *add* darkness in winter.
5. **Take 10 µg (400 IU) vitamin D through Oct–Mar** (test 25(OH)D first) and **get outdoors daily even when grey**.

## Questions to ask the psychiatrist

- Given my **BD-II**, do you want **mood-stabilizer coverage** in place before I start light, and for how long beforehand?
- **Midday or morning** to start — and what would make you escalate me from midday to morning, or vice versa?
- What exact **lux / minutes / titration** do you want for *my* device, and what **YMRS/activation number** is my hard stop line?
- What's the **written switch/rescue plan** — at the first sign of reduced sleep need, do I cut the dose, hold, or stop, and when do I contact you vs go urgent?
- Is the **whole-room high-lumen ("lumenate") all-day setup** acceptable as a larger uncontrolled dose, or should I stick to a timed box?
- Thoughts on **0.5 mg early-evening melatonin as a chronobiotic** (not a sleeping-pill dose) for winter phase drift?
- Can we **test 25(OH)D** and agree a winter vitamin D plan?
- Should we **pre-schedule a March–April check-in** for the spring light surge, my likely highest-risk pivot?
- Are any of my current meds **photosensitizing**, and do I need an **eye exam** first?
