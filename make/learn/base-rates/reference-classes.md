# Reference classes

Base-rate passes live or die on the class you compare against, so the classes get
defined once here and passes cite them instead of re-deriving. This is a *family*,
not a single class — the useful move is often to run one decision against several
cuts and read the differences.

## The spine (shared by every cut)

**People with large realized impact accrued through a compounding career.** Three
deliberate choices in that phrase:

- **Realized, not ex-ante** — membership is by what actually happened, not how good
  the bet looked. (A pass may note the ex-ante view separately, but you get in by
  outcome.)
- **Compounding career, not a single decision** — the impact has to come from a
  process that builds on itself over years. That's the point: we're trying to
  learn repeatable factors of a life, and a single heroic draw (a Petrov or an
  Arkhipov — real impact, but one roll of the dice) teaches us about luck, not
  about how to live. Those cases are out of the spine by design.
- **Impact first, sign second** — scale of impact gets you in; certainty the sign
  is *positive* is a filter we apply or not (below), never an entry gate. Someone
  plausibly net-negative but undeniably high-impact (Musk) is *in* the spine,
  flagged. It's interesting precisely to watch how such a person made a decision.

## The axes we cut along

Pick the cut(s) that transfer to the decision at hand; compare across them when
they'd differ.

- **Sign** — *all* (scale only) vs. *confidently-positive* ("successful by
  utilitarian lights"). The strict cut is the aspirational class; the loose cut is
  the honest measurement. Report both when they'd diverge.
- **Era / cohort** — *all-time* (historical greats included) vs. *living, under
  ~40* (my near-peers). These often look different — modern life-context shifts the
  base rate — and for many decisions I want both, side by side.
- **Mechanism / type** — *enablers* (multiply others: platforms, tools,
  institutions, capital), *thinkers* (move the world through ideas — Mill, Cowen),
  *builders*, *philanthropists*. Not committed to one filter; the type cut is
  itself informative, because a founder and a philosopher may make the *same*
  decision in opposite ways.

## Why several classes, not one

The comparison is the finding. If founders almost all have children but the great
philosophers mostly didn't, that contrast says more than either rate alone — it
tells you which *part* of success the pattern is attached to. So a pass names its
cut(s) explicitly and, where classes diverge, shows the divergence rather than
averaging it away.

## Defining a class by phenotype, not label

When the class is a condition or trait with no clean roster (formal diagnosis is
absent, speculative, or under-applied to the functional cases), define membership by
a **signature** — a weighted list of markers — and score each candidate's *match*
by confidence rather than asserting a binary. See `bipolar-ii/reference-class.md`
for a worked example built from a personal symptom profile.

## Classes referenced so far
*(grows as passes reuse cuts)*
- `high-impact / all` — the spine, sign not filtered (includes contested giants).
- `high-impact / confidently-positive` — the strict "successful utilitarian" cut.
- `thinkers / all-time` — impact through ideas (Mill, and the canonical philosophers).
- `living / under-40` — currently-alive near-peers.
- `bipolar-phenotype` — matched by signature, not diagnosis; outcome measured in
