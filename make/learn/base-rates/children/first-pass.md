# Children — base-rate pass

## At a glance

| | |
|---|---|
| **Question** | Whether to have children (as a man), and when. |
| **Classes used** | `high-impact/all` + `/confidently-positive` (builders, enablers, philanthropists) vs. `thinkers/all-time` (impact through ideas). Compounding-career spine; sign not gated, so Musk is in. See `../reference-classes.md`. |

## The statistics — one decision, two classes, opposite answers

**Builders / enablers / philanthropists** (`high-impact/all`): 23/28 = **82%** have
children; restrict to those past the decision, or to confidently-positive sign, and
it rises to **~100%**. The childless few are almost all just young — Patrick & John
Collison, Buterin, MacAskill (the `living/under-40` cut, still deciding).

**Great thinkers** (`thinkers/all-time`): 24/36 = **67%** never fathered a child
(**75%** left no surviving line); among pre-1850 canonical philosophers it's
**~80–90%**. Newton, Locke, Hume, Kant, Smith, Leibniz, Spinoza, Pascal, Bentham,
Nietzsche, Kierkegaard, Wittgenstein — all childless. Mill, the utilitarian
himself, married at 44 and had none.

That's a **~4–5× swing in childlessness between two classes both unambiguously
"successful."** The contrast is the finding.

## What the contrast means

- **The pattern attaches to *how* you have impact, not to impact itself.**
  Building/enabling at scale co-occurs with family; the life of pure ideas trades
  against it. "Do successful people have kids?" has no answer until you say *which
  kind* — and you sit between the two cuts that most disagree.
- **But the thinker gap is partly a cohort artifact, not pure devotion.** The
  childless cluster is concentrated in 1600–1850, when an independent scholar
  without a chair or inheritance often couldn't afford to marry (Spinoza ground
  lenses; Hume and Kant lived frugally). Modern thinkers with institutional careers
  mostly *did* have children — Russell, Rawls, Einstein, Marx, Darwin. Strip the
  era confound and the honest residue is a real, self-attested **devotion** effect
  for the most monastic figures (Kierkegaard broke his engagement "for the work";
  Newton, Kant, Tesla built celibate lives around study), on top of **selection**
  into a solitary vocation. For a modern person with a stable career, the thinker
  discount is milder than the raw 67–75% looks.
- **Sign barely matters here.** Keeping contested giants in (Musk) or filtering to
  confidently-positive only moves the builder rate by a rounding error — the
  "whether = yes" for builders survives either way.

## How people in the class actually approached it

The base rate gives *whether*; these are the *hows* — three strategies and a warning.

**Have them young, then pay or integrate.** Buffett had three by his mid-20s and
was, by his own family's account, absent — "It was preventable. It was definitely
95% my fault," he said of the marriage it cost, decades later. Darwin married at 29,
had ten, but ran his science *from* a full house at Down rather than absenting
himself — the coexistence version of the same early start. (He drafted a literal
"Marry / Not Marry" ledger — "better than a dog anyhow" under Marry — before
concluding "Marry — Marry — Marry Q.E.D.")

**Delay for the work, then wish you hadn't waited.** Gates (first child at 40) and
Graham (~48) both worked obsessively first, and both recant it. Gates: "Don't wait
as long as I did." Graham: "the most freedom I had before kids, I never used. I paid
for it in loneliness, but I never used it." This is the cut closest to a modern
high-output man's *default* — and both exemplars flag the default as erring *too
late*.

**Pronatalist conviction.** Musk treats birthrate collapse as a bigger civilizational
risk than climate change and urges high-ability people especially to reproduce (~14
children, decoupled from any single household). Altman is the softer, AI-inflected
version: fatherhood "rewired all my priorities."

**The warning the analytic cases share.** The men who reasoned hardest beforehand
report the reasoning *mispredicted the experience*. Graham dreaded kids, then: "It
was like someone flipped a switch." Altman: "a neurochemical change… it happened so
fast." Darwin's cold pros/cons gave way to "one gets, as one grows older, to care
more for them than for anything in this world." An analytic base-rate frame should
hold this humbly — the decision's inputs may not survive contact with the outcome.
(Off the record: Cowen — stepfather, writes on demographics, silent on his own
choice; Collison — married 2022, no known kids, no stated view. The two most
intellectually adjacent to the question say least about their own answer.)

## Caveats that bite (why "what they did" ≠ "what you should do")

- **Survivorship** — both tables are winner pools: correlation inside survivors, not
  proof kids helped or cost impact.
- **The male window misleads the couple's window** — "median ~35, common into the
  40s" is a *male* number; in a partnered decision the binding constraint is usually
  the woman's fertility, far tighter. The delay exemplars (Gates 40, Graham 48) had
  resources — surrogacy, younger partners — a typical frontier lacks. This is the
  one place the base rate can actively mislead.
- **Era confounds both** the timing (secular rise in age-at-first-child) and the
  thinker gap (above).
